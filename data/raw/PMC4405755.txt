</div></section></section><section aria-label="Article content"><section class="body main-article-body"><section class="abstract" id="abstract1"><h2>Abstract</h2>
<p>The mechanisms triggering disuse muscle atrophy remain of debate. It is becoming evident that mitochondrial dysfunction may regulate pathways controlling muscle mass. We have recently shown that mitochondrial dysfunction plays a major role in disuse atrophy of soleus, a slow, oxidative muscle. Here we tested the hypothesis that hindlimb unloading-induced atrophy could be due to mitochondrial dysfunction in fast muscles too, notwithstanding their much lower mitochondrial content. Gastrocnemius displayed atrophy following both 3 and 7 days of unloading. SOD1 and catalase up-regulation, no H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> accumulation and no increase of protein carbonylation suggest the antioxidant defence system efficiently reacted to redox imbalance in the early phases of disuse. A defective mitochondrial fusion (Mfn1, Mfn2 and OPA1 down-regulation) occurred together with an impairment of OXPHOS capacity. Furthermore, at 3 days of unloading higher acetyl-CoA carboxylase (ACC) phosphorylation was found, suggesting AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) pathway activation. To test the role of mitochondrial alterations we used Tg-mice overexpressing PGC-1α because of the known effect of PGC-1α on stimulation of Mfn2 expression. PGC-α overexpression was sufficient to prevent (i) the decrease of pro-fusion proteins (Mfn1, Mfn2 and OPA1), (ii) activation of the AMPK pathway, (iii) the inducible expression of MuRF1 and atrogin1 and of authopagic factors, and (iv) any muscle mass loss in response to disuse. As the effects of increased PGC-1α activity were sustained throughout disuse, compounds inducing PGC-1α expression could be useful to treat and prevent muscle atrophy also in fast muscles.</p></section><section id="sec1"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Key points</h2>
<ul class="list" style="list-style-type:disc">
<li><p>Skeletal muscle atrophy occurs as a result of disuse. Although several studies have established that a decrease in protein synthesis and increase in protein degradation lead to muscle atrophy, little is known about the triggers underlying such processes.</p></li>
<li><p>A growing body of evidence challenges oxidative stress as a trigger of disuse atrophy; furthermore, it is also becoming evident that mitochondrial dysfunction may play a causative role in determining muscle atrophy.</p></li>
<li><p>Mitochondrial fusion and fission have emerged as important processes that govern mitochondrial function and PGC-1α may regulate fusion/fission events.</p></li>
<li><p>Although most studies on mice have focused on the anti-gravitary slow soleus muscle as it is preferentially affected by disuse atrophy, several fast muscles (including gastrocnemius) go through a significant loss of mass following unloading.</p></li>
<li><p>Here we found that in fast muscles an early down-regulation of pro-fusion proteins, through concomitant AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) activation, can activate catabolic systems, and ultimately cause muscle mass loss in disuse. Elevated muscle PGC-1α completely preserves muscle mass by preventing the fall in pro-fusion protein expression, AMPK and catabolic system activation, suggesting that compounds inducing PGC-1α expression could be useful to treat and prevent muscle atrophy.</p></li>
</ul></section><section id="sec2"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Introduction</h2>
<p>The molecular mechanisms that govern the imbalance between pathways controlling protein synthesis and degradation in disuse muscle atrophy remain unclear, notwithstanding the large amount of work done (Bonaldo &amp; Sandri, <a href="#b5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b5">2013</a>). Investigations of multiple models of disuse in animals have shown an up-regulation of genes related to protein degradation and stress response as well as a down-regulation of those related to transcription and translation, and energy metabolism (Lecker <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b30">2004</a>; Bodine, <a href="#b2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b2">2013</a>).</p>
<p>Debate is ongoing regarding whether and to what extent redox signalling can actually be the trigger of such phenomena (Pellegrino <em>et al</em>. 2011<em>a</em>,<em>b</em>; Powers <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b42">2012</a>). A growing body of evidence challenges oxidative stress as a trigger of disuse atrophy (Ikemoto <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b19">2002</a>; Koesterer <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b25">2002</a>; Servais <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b48">2007</a>; Brocca <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b6">2010</a>; Desaphy <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b11">2010</a>; Glover <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b14">2010</a>; Kuwahara <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b28">2010</a>; Pellegrino <em>et al</em>. 2011<em>b</em>; Powers <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b42">2012</a>; Cannavino <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b7">2014</a>). We have recently reported that, in soleus muscle, notwithstanding an early persistent redox imbalance, a metabolic programme controlling muscle mass, rather than oxidative stress, played a major role in triggering hindlimb unloading (HU) muscle atrophy. A down-regulation of peroxisome proliferative activated receptor-γ coactivator 1α (PGC-1α) expression and protein content was, in fact, shown to activate catabolic systems by promoting ubiquitin ligase and autophagy gene expression through FoxO3 disinhibition (Cannavino <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b7">2014</a>).</p>
<p>Although most studies on mice have focused on the anti-gravitary, slow soleus muscle as it is preferentially affected by disuse atrophy, several fast muscles (including gastrocnemius) go through a significant loss of mass following unloading (Kyparos <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b29">2005</a>; Brocca <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b6">2010</a>; Desaphy <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b11">2010</a>). As fast muscles in mice have mostly a glycolytic metabolism and express low levels of PGC-1 (Lin <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b32">2002</a>), the metabolic programme supporting disuse-induced muscle mass loss in soleus might not be relevant in fast muscles.</p>
<p>Data regarding PGC-1α during disuse in fast muscles are inconsistent: increases (Wagatsuma <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b52">2011</a>), decreases (Mazzatti <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b36">2008</a>) as well as no change (Nagatomo <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b38">2011</a>) of PGC-1α levels have been reported. However, alterations of mitochondrial biogenesis (Liu <em>et al</em>. 2012<em>b</em>), mitochondrial respiration (Yajid <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b53" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b53">1998</a>) and mitochondrial dynamics (Wagatsuma <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b52">2011</a>; Liu <em>et al</em>. 2012<em>b</em>) have been shown after prolonged unloading in fast muscles. Moreover, in our previous study we demonstrated that antioxidant treatment during unloading did not prevent gastrocnemius atrophy, suggesting that oxidative stress is not the major cause of disuse atrophy also in fast muscle (Brocca <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b6">2010</a>).</p>
<p>A recent report has highlighted the importance of mitochondrial dynamics in cell and animal physiology. Mitochondria constantly fuse and divide, and an imbalance of these two processes dramatically alters overall mitochondrial morphology and function (Chen &amp; Chan, <a href="#b8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b8">2005</a>). Mitochondrial fusion and fission have emerged as important processes that govern mitochondrial function (Detmer &amp; Chan, <a href="#b12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b12">2007</a>; Hoppins <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b17">2007</a>). The mitofusins, Mfn1 and Mfn2, are located on the mitochondrial outer membrane and are involved in early steps in membrane fusion (Koshiba <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b26">2004</a>; Meeusen <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b37">2006</a>; Song <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b49">2009</a>). The dynamin-related protein optic atrophy 1 (OPA1) is associated with the inner membrane and is essential for inner membrane fusion (Meeusen <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b37">2006</a>; Song <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b49">2009</a>). In addition to its well-recognized function of controlling mitochondrial morphology, mitochondrial fusion clearly protects mitochondrial function (Detmer &amp; Chan, <a href="#b12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b12">2007</a>). Mice lacking Mfn1/2 function in skeletal muscle exhibit both mitochondrial dysfunction and profound muscle atrophy (Chen <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b9">2010</a>).</p>
<p>Mfn2 is a key target of the nuclear coactivator PGC-1α. Maintenance of a normal expression of Mfn2 is critical for the stimulatory effect of PGC-1α on mitochondrial membrane potential (Soriano <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b50" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b50">2006</a>) and PGC-1α may regulate mitochondrial fusion/fission events and hence mitochondrial function (Soriano <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b50" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b50">2006</a>). The latter phenomena could reduce ATP generating capacity, which in turn could shift signalling pathways toward catabolism through activation of 5’AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK). AMPK, a cellular kinase, senses the energy level of the cell and causes atrogin-1 and muscle-specific ring finger protein-1 (MuRF-1) induction via a FoxO3-dependent mechanism (Tong <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b51" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b51">2009</a>) determining muscle loss.</p>
<p>The goal of the present study was to clarify whether mytochondrial dysfunction, recently indicated to play a major role in soleus (Cannavino <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b7">2014</a>), could trigger disuse-induced muscle atrophy also in a fast muscle, the gastrocnemius, which has different metabolism, much lower mitochondrial content (Jackman &amp; Willis, <a href="#b22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b22">1996</a>; Berchtold <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b1">2000</a>; Lin <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b32">2002</a>), low PGC-1α expression (Lin <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b32">2002</a>) and different redox balance response to disuse (Brocca <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b6">2010</a>). We show that: (1) HU resulted in the early down-regulation of pro-fusion protein expression and mitochondrial dysfunction; (2) energy stress activated the protein degradation pathway through AMPK activation; and (3) gastrocnemius atrophy was totally prevented by muscle-specific over-expression of PGC-1α through stimulation of Mfn2 expression.</p></section><section id="sec3"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Methods</h2>
<section id="sec4"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Ethical approval</h3>
<p>Experiments were approved by the Italian Health Department and complied with the Italian guidelines for the use of laboratory animals, which conform to the European Community Directive published in 1986 (86/609/ECC).</p></section><section id="sec5"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Animal care and hindlimb unloading</h3>
<p>Six-month-old male C57BL/6 mice (Charles River Laboratories, Wilmington, MA, USA) and transgenic mice overexpressing PGC-1α (TgPGC-1α) in skeletal muscle, as previously described (Lin <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b32">2002</a>), were used. Mice were unloaded for 3, 7 and 14 days as previously described (Brocca <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b6">2010</a>). Briefly, animals were suspended individually in special cages by thin string tied at one end to the tail and at the other end to the top of the cage; the length of the string was adjusted to allow the animals moving freely on the forelimbs, while the body was inclined at 30–40 deg from the horizontal plane. All mice had water and food <em>ad libitum</em>. The ground C57BL/6 and TgPGC-1α mice were maintained free in single cages and killed after 3, 7 or 14 days. The animals of all experimental groups were killed at 10.00 h after 2 h without food by cervical dislocation to allow removal of gastrocnemius muscle. Muscles were immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at −80°C.</p></section><section id="sec6"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Experimental plan</h3>
<p>Not all analyses were performed on both WT and TgPGC-1α mice at all times (3, 7 and 14 days), but the timing of HU, the strain of the animals and the analyses performed were chosen according to a precise rationale. WT mice were given 3 and 7 days of HU to analyse the time course of adaptations of muscle mass, intracellular pathways controlling muscle mass, redox status, mitochondrial impairment and AMPK pathway. As the experiment on WT mice suggested that mitochondrial dysfunction could be involved in causing gastrocnemius atrophy, to test such a conclusion and study the mechanisms underlying the potential prevention of muscle mass loss, TgPGC-1α mice were subjected to HU for 3 days. The time was chosen based on the observations that intracellular signalling pathways controlling muscle mass were previously shown to go through clear adaptations at that time in soleus (Cannavino <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b7">2014</a>) and in gastrocnemius (experiments on WT mice of this study), and that gastrocnemius was atrophic following 3 days of HU (Fig.<a href="#fig01" class="usa-link">1</a>). TgPGC-1α mice underwent 14 days of HU because we wanted to check that PGC-1 α protein levels were maintained during chronic disuse in transgenic mice and that the positive effect on muscle size found at 3 days of HU was maintained also after prolonged disuse.</p>

<p>CSA of individual muscle fibres was determined in the mid-belly region of gastrocnemius muscles as previously described (Brocca <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b6">2010</a>). Briefly, muscle serial transverse sections (10 μm thick) were stained with haematoxylin-eosin. Images of the stained sections were captured from a light microscope (Leica DMLS) and transferred to a personal computer using a video camera (Leica DFC 280). Fibre CSA was measured with Image J analysis software (NIH, Bethesda, MD, USA) and expressed in micrometres squared.</p></section><section id="sec8"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Analysis of myosin heavy chain (MHC) isoform content</h3>
<p>MHC isoform content was determined using an electrophoretic approach previously described in detail (Brocca <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b6">2010</a>). Briefly, about 6 μg of each muscle sample were dissolved in lysis buffer (20 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> Tris-HCl, 1% Triton X100, 10% glycerol, 150 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> NaCl, 5 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> EDTA, 100 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> NaF, 2 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> NaPPi, 1× inhibitors protease phosphatase (Protease Inhibitor Cocktail, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) and 1 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> PMSF). The lysates were loaded onto 8% polyacrylamide SDS-PAGE gels. Electrophoresis was run for 2 h at 200 V and then for 24 h at 250 V; the gels were stained with Coomassie Blue. Densitometric analysis of MHC bands was performed to assess the relative proportion of the MHC isoforms (Pellegrino <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b39" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b39">2003</a>)</p></section><section id="sec9"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Western blot analysis</h3>
<p>Frozen muscle samples were pulverized and immediately re-suspended in a lysis buffer (20 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> Tris-HCl, 1% Triton X100, 10% glycerol, 150 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> NaCl, 5 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> EDTA, 100 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> NaF and 2 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> NaPPi supplemented with 1× protease, phosphatase inhibitors (Sigma-Aldrich) and 1 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> PMSF). The homogenate obtained was centrifuged at 18000 <em>g</em> for 20 min at 4°C and the supernatant stored at −80°C until ready to use. Equal amounts of muscle samples were loaded on gradient precast gels purchased from Bio-Rad (AnyKd; Hercules, CA, USA). Proteins were electro-transferred to PVDF membranes at 35 mA overnight. The membranes were probed with specific primary antibodies (see below). Thereafter, the membranes were incubated in HRP-conjugated secondary antibody. The protein bands were visualized by an enhanced chemiluminescence method. The content of each protein investigated was assessed by determining the brightness–area product of the protein band as previously described (Gondin <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b15">2011</a>).</p></section><section id="sec10"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">OxyBlot analysis</h3>
<p>Muscle samples previously stored at −80°C were pulverized and homogenized at 4°C in an antioxidant buffer containing protease inhibitors, 25 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> imidazole and 5 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> EDTA, pH 7.2, adjusted with NaOH as previously described in detail (Brocca <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b6">2010</a>). The lysate was left for 20 min on ice and the homogenate obtained was centrifuged at 18000 <em>g</em> for 20 min at 4°C. Protein concentration was determined on the supernatant using the RC DCTM protein assay kit (BioRad). The supernatant was stored at −80°C until ready to use.</p>
<p>Protein carbonylation level was detected using an OxyBlot kit (AbNova, Taipei City, Taiwan), which provides reagents for sensitive immunodetection of these carbonyl groups. The carbonyl groups in the protein side chains are derivatized to 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazone (DNP hydrazone) by reaction with 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazine (DNPH); 6 μg of the DNP-derivatized protein samples were separated by PAGE (15% SDS polyacrylamide gels) and then blotted for 2 h at 100 V to a nitrocellulose membrane. Membranes obtained were stained with Ponceau Red and then scanned. Membranes were incubated with primary antibody, specific to the DNP moiety of the proteins, and subsequently with an HRP–antibody conjugate directed against the primary antibody (secondary antibody: goat anti-rabbit IgG). Blots were developed by using an enhanced chemiluminescence method. Positive bands emitting light were detected by short exposure to photographic films. Protein oxidation was quantified by defining the oxidative index (OI), i.e. the ratio between densitometric values of the OxyBlot bands and those stained with Ponceau Red.</p></section><section id="sec11"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Gene expression analysis</h3>
<p>Total RNA, from skeletal samples, was extracted using the Promega SV Total RNA isolation kit; the concentration of RNA was evaluated by using a NanoDrop instrument (Thermo Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA) and 300 ng was reverse-transcribed with SuperScript III reverse transcriptase (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA, USA) to obtain cDNA. The cDNA was analysed by real-time PCR (RT-PCR; AB 7500) with the SYBR Green or TaqMan PCR kit (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA, USA) and the data were normalized to hypoxanthine-guanine phosphoribosyl transferase rRNA. Oligonucleotide primers used for RT-PCR are listed below.</p></section><section id="sec12"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Primers</h3>

<p>Frozen muscle samples were pulverized in a steel mortar with liquid nitrogen to obtain a powder that was immediately re-suspended in the assay buffer provided with the kit (Abcam). After protein quantification, samples were removed of all proteins. A standard curve with known H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration was generated following the supplied protocol. Samples and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> standards were incubated at room temperature for 10 min in HRP and OxiRed Probe that reacted with H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to produce red fluorescence (Ex/Em = 535/587 nm) and measured with a micro-plate reader (Tecan Infinite 200 Pro) (Cannavino <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b7">2014</a>).</p></section><section id="sec15"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">OXPHOS capacity</h3>
<p>Maximal ADP-stimulated mitochondrial respiration (‘OXPHOS capacity’) was determined in permeabilized muscle fibres by high-resolution respirometry (Oxygraph 2-K; Oroboros Instruments, Innsbrück, Austria) (Salvadego <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b46" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b46">2013</a>). Fibre bundles were separated using sharp forceps in ice-cold preservation solution (BIOPS; Oroboros Instruments). Muscle samples in solution plus 10% (w/v) fatty acid-free BSA and 30% (v/v) DMSO were quickly frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at −80°C until analysis within 1 month. For analysis, muscle samples were quickly thawed by immersion in the preservation solution at room temperature, washed and permeabilized in saponin (50 μg ml<sup>–1</sup>) for 30 min at 4°C. Fibres were subsequently washed in a mitochondrial respiration solution (MiR05; Oroboros Instruments) and weighed before adding to a 3 ml chamber in the high-resolution respirometer and incubated with MiR05 at 37°C. The O<sub>2</sub> concentration in the chamber was kept above 270 μ<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> throughout the experiment, to avoid O<sub>2</sub> diffusion limitation. Intermittent reoxygenation steps were performed by adding a 200 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> solution into the medium containing catalase.</p>
<p>O<sub>2</sub> consumption in the chamber was measured polarographycally following a sequential administration of substrates. Maximal ADP-stimulated mitochondrial respiration (state 3 respiration) was measured after the addition of ADP (5.0 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span>) as phosphate acceptor with malate (4 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span>), glutamate (10 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span>) and succinate (10 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span>) as substrates. The addition of cytochrome c (10 μ<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span>) had no additive effects (&lt;10%) on respiration, thereby confirming the integrity of the outer mitochondrial membrane. Mitochondrial respiration values were then normalized per citrate synthase (CS) content, taken as an estimate of mitochondrial mass.</p></section><section id="sec16"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Statistical analysis</h3>
<p>Data are expressed as mean ± SEM. Statistical significance of the differences between means was assessed by one-way ANOVA followed by Student–Newman–Keuls test. A probability of less than 5% was considered significant (<em>P</em> &lt; 0.05).</p></section></section><section id="sec17"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Results</h2>
<section id="sec18"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">CSA was reduced in gastrocnemius in the early phases of HU</h3>
<p>CSA of skeletal muscle fibres was analysed in the mid-belly region of gastrocnemius in control and unloaded mice. The measurements were performed on cryosections stained with haematoxylin–eosin. Gastrocnemius muscle fibres went through 14 and 12% of atrophy, respectively, at 3 and 7 days of HU (Fig.<a href="#fig01" class="usa-link">1</a>). As the portion of muscle analysed contained almost solely MHC isoform IIB (Control MHC-IIB 91 ± 1.5%, MHC-IIX 5.5 ± 1.1% MHC-IIA 3.5 ± 0.8%), all fibres analysed were considered type IIB.</p></section><section id="sec19"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">The antioxidant defence system efficiently reacted to the initial reactive oxygen species (ROS) increase</h3>
<p>To examine the effects of HU on redox imbalance, we studied the expression of SOD1 and catalase, the two major enzymes of ROS scavenging, H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> content and protein carbonylation. Gastrocnemius showed an early SOD1 and catalase up-regulation evident at 3 and 7 days of HU compared with control (Fig.<a href="#fig02" class="usa-link">2</a><em>D</em>). H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> concentration (Fig.<a href="#fig02" class="usa-link">2</a><em>A</em>) and protein carbonylation (Fig.<a href="#fig02" class="usa-link">2</a><em>B</em>) levels were not different from control at any of the experimental times analysed.</p>

<p>To examine the effects of HU on expression of mitochondrial biogenesis-related genes, the mRNA transcript and protein level of PGC-1α, playing a central role in mitochondrial biogenesis, were studied. PGC-1α mRNA and protein expression was unchanged both at 3 and at 7 days of HU (Fig.<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">3</a>). Similarly, no changes in levels of protein in mitochondrial complexes (Fig.<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">3</a><em>D</em>) (Complexes I–V) at either time analysed were found.</p>

Levels of DRP1 protein did not change at either time analysed (Fig.<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">3</a><em>C</em>).</p>
<p>However, a mismatch between the increase in CS protein level and its activity was observed on day 3 of HU (Fig.<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">3</a><em>E</em>,<em>F</em>). CS protein and activity levels dropped significantly on day 7 of HU. OXPHOS capacity was assessed based on maximal ADP-stimulated state 3 mitochondrial respiration (normalized per CS content), taken as an estimate of mitochondrial mass. As mitochondrial fusion protects mitochondrial function (Detmer &amp; Chan, <a href="#b12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b12">2007</a>), we also analysed expression of the pro-fusion proteins Mfn1, Mfn2 and OPA1. Importantly, a significantly lower level of such proteins was detected in HU animals compared to controls (Fig.<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">3</a><em>H</em>).</p>
<p>The latter results indicate alteration to the mitochondrial network, which could potentially lead to energy stress. Because when mitochondria are fragmented FoxO3 is activated via AMPK in myofibres and induces the expression of atrogin-1 and MuRF-1 (Romanello &amp; Sandri, <a href="#b43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b43">2010</a>), we studied the ratio of the active (phosphorylated) and total form of AMPK and its downstream acetyl-CoA carboxylase (ACC) by Western blotting. At 3 days of HU, no significant changes were observed in AMPK activation whereas a significant increase of ACC activation was found (Fig.<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">4</a>).</p>

<p>To elucidate the role of Mfn2 expression in disuse atrophy, muscle-specific transgenic mice for the peroxisome PGC-1α were used as PGC-1α is known to enhance expression of pro-fusion proteins (Soriano <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b50" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b50">2006</a>; Zorzano, <a href="#b55" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b55">2009</a>).</p>

<p>Although several studies have attempted to elucidate the signalling pathways involved in triggering atrophy during disuse of limb muscles, the exact mechanisms responsible for disuse-induced muscular atrophy are currently debated and there is still no therapy to treat muscle atrophy. Our previous findings have indicated that a metabolic programme might be the major phenomenon triggering muscle atrophy in HU mice (Cannavino <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b7">2014</a>). We demonstrated, in fact, that a fall in the levels of PGC-1α can be responsible for the activation of proteolytic pathways through FoxO3 disinhibition, leading to the conclusion that deregulation in PGC-1α is the molecular switch that triggers protein breakdown. The latter work was performed on soleus muscle, a slow muscle with high oxidative metabolism, mitochondrial content and PGC-1α expression. As fast muscles rely mainly on glycolytic metabolisms, have much lower mitochondrial content (Jackman &amp; Willis, <a href="#b22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b22">1996</a>; Berchtold <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b1">2000</a>; Lin <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b32">2002</a>), and express PGC-1α at low levels (Lin <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b32">2002</a>), a metabolic programme might not be as relevant in gastrocnemius as in soleus. In the present study, we tested the hypothesis that also in fast muscles disuse-induced muscle atrophy could be due to a metabolic programme.</p>
<p>Gastrocnemius muscle went through atrophy following both 3 and 7 days of HU, confirming, and extending to earlier times, the results of previous studies, which showed significant gastrocnemius mass loss with long-term disuse (Kyparos <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b29">2005</a>; Brocca <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b6">2010</a>; Desaphy <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b11">2010</a>). Although the loss of CSA appeared to plateau between 3 and 7 days, note that, following 14 days of HU, CSA of type IIB fibres from gastrocnemius was 25% lower than in controls, indicating that disuse atrophy in gastrocnemius actually progresses beyond 7 days. However, the observation of a plateau is intriguing. It is consistent with some previous observations (Isfort <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b21">2002</a>) and at variance with others (Bodine, <a href="#b2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b2">2013</a>). The rate of muscle mass loss might vary according to the time course of the adaptations of the intracellular signalling pathways controlling muscle mass, which in turn might vary from pathway to pathway.</p>
<section id="sec25"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Oxidative stress and disuse atrophy in a fast muscle</h3>
<p>The present study supports the idea that even in fast muscle oxidative stress might not be the main phenomenon in triggering disuse muscle atrophy (Ikemoto <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b19">2002</a>; Koesterer <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b25">2002</a>; Servais <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b48">2007</a>; Brocca <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b6">2010</a>; Desaphy <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b11">2010</a>; Glover <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b14">2010</a>; Kuwahara <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b28">2010</a>; Pellegrino <em>et al</em>. 2011<em>b</em>; Powers <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b42">2012</a>; Cannavino <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b7">2014</a>). Redox imbalance occurred early into HU in gastrocnemius consistently with the up-regulation of SOD1 and catalase (Fig.<a href="#fig02" class="usa-link">2</a>). The lack of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> accumulation together with the absence of carbonylation both at 3 and at 7 days of HU (Fig.<a href="#fig02" class="usa-link">2</a>) indicates that, in the early phase of disuse, the antioxidant defence system efficiently reacts to the initial ROS increase. This compensatory increase in antioxidant activity is maintained over time, in agreement with the absence of carbonylation found at 14 days of HU (Brocca <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b6">2010</a>). Given that we have previously shown that antioxidant treatment during unloading did not prevent gastrocnemius atrophy, the data suggest that oxidative stress might not be the major phenomenon of disuse atrophy in fast muscle (Brocca <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b6">2010</a>).</p></section><section id="sec26"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Imbalance between mitochondrial fission and fusion and AMPK activation in fast muscles following HU</h3>
<p>In contrast to what was observed in soleus (Cannavino <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b7">2014</a>), unloaded gastrocnemius showed normal PGC-1α levels (Fig.<a href="#fig02" class="usa-link">2</a>) and no alterations in mitochondrial complex expression, suggesting different mechanisms for fast muscle atrophy. Previous findings regarding PGC-1α during disuse in fast muscle are contradictory. In fact, higher (Wagatsuma <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b52">2011</a>), lower (Mazzatti <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b36">2008</a>) and unchanged (Nagatomo <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b38">2011</a>) PGC-1α levels have been reported. Furthermore, mitochondrial complexes did not drastically change with disuse in fast muscle, and only a reduction of complex I expression was found after 28 days of unloading in gastrocnemius muscle (Liu <em>et al</em>. 2012<em>b</em>). This notwithstanding, several lines of evidence suggest that mitochondrial alterations could be good candidates in triggering atrophy even in fast muscle. In fact, alteration in mitochondrial biogenesis (Liu <em>et al</em>. 2012<em>b</em>) and mitochondrial respiration (Yajid <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b53" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b53">1998</a>) have been found after chronic disuse in fast skeletal muscle.</p>
<p>Mitochondria play critical roles in the life and death of cells. Mitochondrial dynamics are regulated by coordinated fusion and fission cycles performed by a complex molecular machinery. Mitochondrial dynamics maintain normal mitochondrial function by degrading damaged mitochondria and generating new mitochondria. The mitochondrion is vital to cellular energy and metabolism, being the site of generation of most ATP. Derangements in fusion–fission have been proposed as a mechanism underlying the formation of dysfunctional aberrant mitochondria (Yoon <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b54" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b54">2006</a>) and causing muscle atrophy through activation of AMPK (Romanello <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b43">2010</a>).</p>
<p>Fusion–fission derangement can be due to an excessive activation of fission. In contrast to observations in soleus (Cannavino <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b7">2014</a>), unchanged DRP1 mRNA levels (Fig.<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">3</a>) suggest that in the early phases of gastrocnemius atrophy there is no excessive fission activation.</p>
<p>Mitochondrial fusion was assessed based on the expression of three large GTPases that require the coordinated fusion of the outer and inner membranes: Mfn1, Mfn2 and OPA1. A defective mitochondrial fusion, revealed by a down-regulation of Mfn1, Mfn2 and OPA1, occurred (Fig.<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">3</a>), in agreement with previous reports in fast muscle during early (Wagatsuma <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b52">2011</a>) and chronic disuse (Liu <em>et al</em>. 2012<em>b</em>). Mitochondrial fusion can protect mitochondrial function (Detmer &amp; Chan, <a href="#b12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b12">2007</a>). Consistently, we found an impairment of OXPHOS capacity assessed as the maximal ADP-stimulated state 3 mitochondrial respiration (Fig.<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">3</a>). Together with a mismatch between the increase in CS protein level and its activity, the latter suggests a compensatory mechanism to an intrinsic oxidative defect in fast twitch muscle.</p>
<p>The decrease in mitochondrial biogenesis and impaired mitochondrial function could lower ATP production, generate energy imbalance and activate protein catabolism. The energy level of the cell is, in fact, sensed by AMPK, a cellular kinase, which can cause atrogin-1 and MuRF-1 induction via a FoxO3-dependent mechanism (Tong <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b51" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b51">2009</a>). Interestingly, an early up-regulation of ACC, a kinase downstream of AMPK, was observed (Fig.<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">4</a>), indicating activation of AMPK. The latter results suggest that mitochondrial alteration could be important in determining muscle size during disuse in fast muscles.</p>
<p>Several signalling kinases, including AMPK, have been implicated in activating PGC-1α transcription in response to various stimuli (Irrcher <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b20" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b20">2008</a>). This might explain why, following HU, PGC-1α level is unchanged.</p></section><section id="sec27"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">PGC-1α over-expression prevents HU-induced muscle atrophy and imbalance in mitochondrial fusion and fission</h3>
<p>To test the role of mitochondrial alterations we used transgenic mice overexpressing PGC-1α. In fact, it has been shown that the Mfn2 gene is controlled by PGC-1α and PGC-1β in conjunction with oestrogen-related receptor-α (Soriano <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b50" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b50">2006</a>; Liesa <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b31">2008</a>) and PGC-1α stimulates Mfn2 expression (Soriano <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b50" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b50">2006</a>; Zorzano, <a href="#b55" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b55">2009</a>). We subjected transgenic mice overexpressing PGC-1α to 3 days of HU. The duration of HU was chosen based on the observation that in soleus maximal induction of catabolic genes occurred 3 days after unloading (Cannavino <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b7">2014</a>). Skeletal muscle overexpressing PGC-α displayed complete resistance to loss of mass following 3 days of HU and this positive effect on muscle size was fully maintained also after prolonged disuse (14 days) (Fig.<a href="#fig06" class="usa-link">6</a>), consistent with the high and steady protein levels of PGC-1α (Fig.<a href="#fig05" class="usa-link">5</a>).</p>
<p>To test the mechanism underlying this effect on muscle size, we analysed the main pathways controlling muscle mass. The ubiquitine proteasome system (UPS) and autophagy are the two major proteolytic systems responsible for muscle wasting. Among several markers of UPS, two E3 ubiquitin ligases, MAFbx/Atrogin-1 and MuRF1 (also known as atrogenes), have been widely used as they are highly expressed in many conditions of muscle wasting (Bodine <em>et al</em>. 2001<em>a</em>; Cohen <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b10">2009</a>).</p>
<p>MuRF1 and atrogin-1 mRNA expression at 3 days (Fig.<a href="#fig07" class="usa-link">7</a>) was dramatically increased, indicating UPS activation in the early phases of disuse atrophy. Disuse also induced expression of genes involved in autophagy (Beclin1 and p62) (Fig.<a href="#fig07" class="usa-link">7</a>). It has been recently understood that an increase in activity of the autophagy system can have a role in muscle atrophy (Sandri, <a href="#b47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b47">2010</a>). The latter findings indicate that, at least in the early stages of disuse, gastrocnemius mass loss is supported by activation of both the UPS and autophagy systems, consistent with previous observations (Bodine <em>et al</em>. 2001<em>a</em>; Ikemoto <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b19">2002</a>; Haddad <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b16">2006</a>; Kline <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b24">2007</a>).</p>
<p>Signalling through the phosphatidyl-inositol 3-kinase (PI3K/AKY/mTOR) pathway controls protein synthesis and muscle hypertrophy (Rommel <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b45" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b45">2001</a>). In gastrocnemius muscle, HU did not result in any significant alterations in the phosphorylation states of the anabolic pathway after 3 days (Fig.<a href="#fig07" class="usa-link">7</a>). Little and contradictory information is available on the impact of disuse on activation of the protein synthesis signalling pathway in fast muscles. Although our results are not in agreement with those of Bodine <em>et al</em>. (2001<em>b</em>), who found a decrease in the protein synthesis signalling pathway in atrophied gastrocnemius muscle at 14 days of unloading, they are in line with the concept that protein synthesis regulation differs among muscle types (Hornberger <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b18">2001</a>). Indeed, several lines of evidence indicate an increase or no changes in protein synthesis in fast muscles in different disuse models, including HU. An increase in translation factors (p70S6K, eEF-2 and eIF-2a) has been reported in the atrophied fast EDL muscle at 7 days of denervation (Hornberger <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b18">2001</a>). Loss of gastrocnemius mass independent of a decrease in protein synthesis has been reported after unilateral hindlimb immobilization (Krawiec <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b27">2005</a>). Finally, in line with our results, Liu <em>et al</em>. (2012<em>a</em>) showed a biphasic protein translational signalling response of gastrocnemius to HU. Specifically, the authors found that the initial unloading phase was associated with increased repression of 4EBP1 (eukaryotic translation initiation factor 4E-binding protein 1) on translation initiation factor eIF4E without alteration of the Akt/mTOR/p70S6K/S6 pathway, while prolonged disuse (14 days) was associated with activation of translational signalling, including activation of the Akt/mTOR/p70S6K/S6 pathway and diminished repression of 4EBP1 on eIF4E.</p>
<p>The findings discussed so far prompted the hypothesis that in fast muscles an early down-regulation of pro-fusion proteins at 3 days of HU, through concomitant AMPK activation, can activate the ubiquitin proteasome pathway and autophagy, and ultimately cause muscle mass loss in disuse.</p>
<p>The latter hypothesis was confirmed by the observation that overexpression of PGC-α was sufficient to prevent (i) the decrease of pro-fusion proteins (Mfn1, Mfn2 and OPA1) (Fig.<a href="#fig08" class="usa-link">8</a>), (ii) activation of the AMPK pathway (Fig.<a href="#fig08" class="usa-link">8</a>), (iii) the inducible expression of MuRF1 and atrogin1 and the autophagic factors (Fig.<a href="#fig07" class="usa-link">7</a>); and (iv) any muscle mass loss in response to disuse.</p></section><section id="sec28"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Conclusions</h3>
<p>The present results and our recent previous study (Cannavino <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b7">2014</a>) strongly support the idea that a metabolic programme can play a major role in triggering disuse muscle atrophy both in slow and in fast muscles. However, the metabolic programme would exert its effects through different pathways in slow, soleus and fast, gastrocnemius muscles. In soleus the primary phenomenon would be a fall of PGC-1α expression and content, which would cause induction of atrogenes through FoxO3 disinhibition (Cannavino <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b7">2014</a>), whereas in gastrocnemius the primary phenomenon would be a down-regulation of pro-fusion proteins, which in turn would cause energy stress and induction of atrogenes through AMPK activation (Fig.<a href="#fig09" class="usa-link">9</a>). It is known that during muscle wasting the enhanced FoxO activity results in increased transcription of Mul1, a mitochondrial E3 ligase, which ubiquitinates and targets Mfn2 for degradation through the UPS, resulting in mitochondrial dysfunction (Lokireddy <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b35">2012</a>). Interestingly, slow-twitch muscle has small amounts of Mul1 but high Mfn2 protein levels whereas fast-twitch muscles express higher levels of Mul1 and low levels of Mfn2 (Lokireddy <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b35">2012</a>). The latter phenomenon probably exposes fast muscles to a rapid degradation of Mfn2 with subsequent alteration of mitochondrial fusion, which precedes the fall of PGC-α levels that occurs in the late stages of disuse.</p>

Interestingly, the higher efficacy of resistance training in preventing muscle mass loss compared to endurance training could be due to the fact that although both types of exercise increase PGC-1α expression, at a given exercise volume the higher the intensity the higher the effect (Egan <em>et al</em>. <a href="#b13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="b13">2010</a>).
