</div></section></section><section aria-label="Article content"><section class="body main-article-body"><section class="abstract" id="abstract1"><h2>ABSTRACT</h2>
<p>As part of the Microbial Tracking-2 study, 94 fungal strains were isolated from surfaces on the International Space Station, and whole-genome sequences were assembled. Characterization of these draft genomes will allow evaluation of microgravity adaption, risks to human health and spacecraft functioning, and biotechnological applications of fungi.</p></section><section id="s1"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">ANNOUNCEMENT</h2>
<p>Fungi are potential sources of nutrients and bioactive compounds during long-term spaceflight but also could affect astronaut health through both opportunistic infections and system biofouling (<a href="#B1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B1">1</a>, <a href="#B2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B2">2</a>). As part of a study characterizing fungal responses to the space environment, we report the draft genomes of 94 fungal strains that were isolated from the International Space Station (ISS), representing 10 ascomycetous and 1 basidiomycetous species.</p>
<p><em>Aspergillus</em> species are environmental fungi and opportunistic pathogens (<a href="#B3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B3">3</a>). <em>Aspergillus unguis</em> is a member of the ISS microbiome (<a href="#B4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B4">4</a>) and produces industrially important compounds (<a href="#B5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B5">5</a>). <em>Aureobasidium pullulans</em> is a black fungus that was previously isolated from the ISS water filtration system (<a href="#B6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B6">6</a>) and Mars mission spacecraft-associated surfaces (<a href="#B7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B7">7</a>).</p>
<p><em>Cladosporium</em> species are dominant fungal contaminants in indoor air (<a href="#B8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B8">8</a>, <a href="#B9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B9">9</a>). <em>Cladosporium sphaerospermum</em> and <em>Cladosporium cladosporioides</em> were detected multiple time on the ISS, and their properties in microgravity were studied (<a href="#B10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B10">10</a>, <a href="#B11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B11">11</a>).</p>
<p><em>Fusarium veterinarium</em> is a recently described species within the <em>Fusarium oxysporum</em> complex, the species of which are ubiquitous in soil, are known human/plant pathogens (<a href="#B12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B12">12</a>), and were isolated both from surfaces and from infected <em>Zinnia hybrida</em> plants aboard the ISS (<a href="#B13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B13">13</a>, <a href="#B14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B14">14</a>). <em>Fusarium annulatum</em>, which has been isolated from plant and human tissues on Earth, has not been reported previously in space (<a href="#B15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B15">15</a>).</p>
<p><em>Penicillium</em> species produce important bioactive compounds and can contaminate food and cause secondary infections (<a href="#B16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B16">16</a>). Previously detected on the ISS or Mir (<a href="#B2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B2">2</a>, <a href="#B11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B11">11</a>) are <em>Penicillium citrinum</em>, a common soil and indoor species (<a href="#B17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B17">17</a>), <em>Penicillium rubens</em>, from which penicillin was isolated (<a href="#B18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B18">18</a>), and <em>Penicillium corylophilum</em>, which is commonly found in damp buildings (<a href="#B19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B19">19</a>). <em>Penicillium palitans</em>, which has been reported in cheese (<a href="#B20" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B20">20</a>) and also in a wide range of habitats, including Antarctica (<a href="#B21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B21">21</a>), has not been reported previously in space.</p>
<p><em>Rhodotorula mucilaginosa</em> is a ubiquitous environmental (<a href="#B22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B22">22</a>) and human commensal yeast and opportunistic pathogen (<a href="#B23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B23">23</a>) that is found in aquatic and built environments, including bathrooms and dishwashers (<a href="#B24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B24">24</a>, <a href="#B25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B25">25</a>). It is the most commonly isolated yeast on the ISS (<a href="#B26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B26">26</a><a href="#B27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B27">–</a><a href="#B28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B28">28</a>).</p>
<p>Sample collection and fungal isolation steps were described elsewhere (<a href="#B26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B26">26</a>). For five flight missions, eight surfaces aboard the ISS were sampled with moistened polyester wipes (<a href="#tab1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>). Upon return to Earth, the wipes were agitated in sterile phosphate-buffered saline, which was concentrated using an InnovaPrep CP150 concentrating pipette, and suitable aliquots were spread onto nutrient media (<a href="#tab1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>). Fungal isolates were restreaked on potato-dextrose agar (PDA), and genomic DNA was extracted using the ZymoBIOMICS MagBead DNA kit according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Whole-genome shotgun sequencing libraries were prepared with an Illumina Nextera DNA Flex library preparation kit (<a href="#B29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B29">29</a>) and were sequenced on the NovaSeq 6000 paired-end 2 × 150-bp platform with a S4 flow cell. After quality filtering and trimming with FastQC v0.11.7 (<a href="#B30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B30">30</a>) and fastp v0.20.0 (<a href="#B31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B31">31</a>), genomes were assembled using SPAdes v3.11.1 (<a href="#B32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B32">32</a>). Assembly quality was assessed with QUAST v5.0.2 (<a href="#B33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B33">33</a>). Fastp included screening for 512 adapters; otherwise, default settings were used for all steps.</p>
