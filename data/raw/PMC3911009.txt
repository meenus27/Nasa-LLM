</div></section></section><section aria-label="Article content"><section class="body main-article-body"><section class="abstract" id="abstract1"><h2>Abstract</h2>
<p>The roles of various core components, including α/β/γ-type small acid-soluble spore proteins (SASP), dipicolinic acid (DPA), core water content, and DNA repair by apurinic/apyrimidinic (AP) endonucleases or nonhomologous end joining (NHEJ), in <em>Bacillus subtilis</em> spore resistance to different types of ionizing radiation including X rays, protons, and high-energy charged iron ions have been studied. Spores deficient in DNA repair by NHEJ or AP endonucleases, the oxidative stress response, or protection by major α/β-type SASP, DPA, and decreased core water content were significantly more sensitive to ionizing radiation than wild-type spores, with highest sensitivity to high-energy-charged iron ions. DNA repair via NHEJ and AP endonucleases appears to be the most important mechanism for spore resistance to ionizing radiation, whereas oxygen radical detoxification via the MrgA-mediated oxidative stress response or KatX catalase activity plays only a very minor role. Synergistic radioprotective effects of α/β-type but not γ-type SASP were also identified, indicating that α/β-type SASP's binding to spore DNA is important in preventing DNA damage due to reactive oxygen species generated by ionizing radiation.</p></section><section id="sec1"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">INTRODUCTION</h2>
<p>Spores of <em>Bacillus subtilis</em> have been used extensively as biological indicators for industrial purposes such as sterilization or decontamination. Spores have also been shown to be suitable dosimeters for probing terrestrial and extraterrestrial ionizing radiation in environmental and astrobiological studies (<a href="#B1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B1">1</a><a href="#B2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B2">–</a><a href="#B3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B3">3</a>; reviewed in references <a href="#B4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B4">4</a>, <a href="#B5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B5">5</a>, <a href="#B6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B6">6</a>, and <a href="#B7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B7">7</a>). Ionizing radiation can damage cellular components through direct transfer of radiation energy into biomolecules (e.g., DNA, RNA, proteins) and indirectly by generating reactive oxygen species (ROS) from the radiolysis of intracellular H<sub>2</sub>O (<a href="#B8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B8">8</a><a href="#B9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B9">–</a><a href="#B11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B11">11</a>). The biological effects of ionizing radiation are thought to arise from the formation of single- and double-strand breaks (SSB and DSB) in cellular DNA and clustered DNA damage, e.g., two or more closely spaced lesions, including abasic sites, base lesions, SSB, and DSB. The biological effects of ionizing radiation depend on the quality and the dose of radiation and on the cell type (<a href="#B9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B9">9</a><a href="#B10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B10">–</a><a href="#B11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B11">11</a>). Linear energy transfer (LET) represents the energy lost per unit distance as an ionizing particle travels through a material, and it is used to quantify the effects of ionizing radiation on biological specimens (<a href="#B5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B5">5</a>, <a href="#B10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B10">10</a><a href="#B11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B11">–</a><a href="#B13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B13">13</a>). High-LET radiation sources include protons, and high-energy-charged (HZE) particles give densely ionizing radiation, since they lose their energy in a small distance and thus cause dense ionization along their tracks and can give localized multiple DNA-damaging events. Low-LET radiation sources, such as X rays, give sparsely ionizing radiation, since they produce ionizations sparsely along their track and, hence, almost homogeneously within a cell. The biological effects of high-LET radiation are in general much higher than those of low-LET radiation of the same energy (<a href="#B10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B10">10</a>, <a href="#B12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B12">12</a>, <a href="#B13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B13">13</a>). This is because high-LET radiation deposits most of its energy within the volume of one cell and the damage to DNA is therefore larger (<a href="#B9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B9">9</a><a href="#B10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B10">–</a><a href="#B11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B11">11</a>). This is attributable to the formation of clusters of damage that result in two or more DNA lesions along the tracks of high-LET radiation sources, i.e., the physical path of the applied ionizing radiation source in a cell.</p>
<p>Previous studies have indicated that in both pro- and eukaryotic cells or under highly scavenging conditions mimicking those for ROS scavenging in the cell, one-fourth of the lesions induced in DNA by low-LET radiation can be ascribed to direct effects increasing up to 80% for high-LET particles (<a href="#B9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B9">9</a>, <a href="#B11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B11">11</a>, <a href="#B12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B12">12</a>). Hydrogen peroxide (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) and hydroxyl radicals (HO·) are major oxidizing species produced by the radiolysis of water, and superoxide ions (O<sub>2</sub>·<sup>−</sup>) are formed in the presence of dissolved oxygen (<a href="#B12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B12">12</a>, <a href="#B13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B13">13</a>). Lethal and mutagenic effects induced by ionizing radiation are thought to be the result of DNA damage caused during the course of irradiation (<a href="#B11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B11">11</a>, <a href="#B14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B14">14</a><a href="#B15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B15">–</a><a href="#B16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B16">16</a>). Spore DNA resides in the innermost spore compartment, the core, and dormant spores of <em>B. subtilis</em> possess a complex arsenal of protective attributes in the core, in particular a low core water content as well as abundant novel core constituents such as (i) the calcium chelate of dipicolinic acid (Ca-DPA), which comprises ∼25% of core dry weight, and (ii) small, acid-soluble spore proteins (SASP) of the α/β type, which bind spore DNA and protect it from many types of damage, including UV photoproducts, apurinic/apyrimidinic (AP) sites, and oxidative lesions (<a href="#B17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B17">17</a>, <a href="#B18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B18">18</a>, <a href="#B47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B47">47</a>; reviewed in references <a href="#B4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B4">4</a>, <a href="#B6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B6">6</a>, and <a href="#B19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B19">19</a>). Spore DNA is saturated with a group of unique proteins called α/β-type SASP, which are encoded by multiple genes and synthesized only during sporulation in the developing spore. These α/β-type SASP are nonspecific DNA-binding proteins that bind to random-sequence double-strand DNA and comprise approximately 5% of total spore protein (reviewed in references <a href="#B6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B6">6</a> and <a href="#B18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B18">18</a>). The high levels of α/β-type SASP in spores are sufficient to saturate the spore DNA, and the DNA within this nucleoprotein complex is protected from a variety of environmental insults (<a href="#B4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B4">4</a>, <a href="#B7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B7">7</a>).</p>
<p>Germinating spores also can detoxify ROS using enzymes such as catalase (KatX) and superoxide dismutase (SodA), although these enzymes play no role in dormant spore resistance to oxidizing agents, most likely because enzymes in the spore core are inactive due to the core's low water content (<a href="#B20" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B20">20</a><a href="#B21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B21">–</a><a href="#B23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B23">23</a>). Mechanisms to prevent damage caused by ROS, in particular damage to DNA, include protective DNA-binding proteins as well as enzymes such as alkyl hydroperoxide reductases, catalases, and superoxide dismutases, which can destroy the oxidizing agents alkyl hydroperoxides, hydrogen peroxide, and superoxide, respectively. A study of the role of oxidative stress responsive proteins such as the DNA-binding protein MrgA, alkyl hydroperoxide reductase, catalases, and superoxide dismutases in the resistance of <em>B. subtilis</em> spores to oxidative stress caused by paraquat and hydrogen peroxide exposure showed that inactivation of genes coding for the protective enzymes or MrgA had no effect on the spores' heat or hydrogen peroxide resistance (<a href="#B21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B21">21</a>). Spore DNA damage can also be repaired during germination via a number of different pathways such as direct reversal, nonhomologous end joining (NHEJ, encoded by YkoV and YkoU), base and nucleotide excision repair (BER and NER, respectively), mismatch repair, recombination-mediated repair, and translesion synthesis (<a href="#B24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B24">24</a><a href="#B25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B25">–</a><a href="#B28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B28">28</a>; reviewed in references <a href="#B6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B6">6</a> and <a href="#B29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B29">29</a>). Two AP endonucleases, ExoA and Nfo, have also been implicated in the repair of AP sites caused by oxidizing agents in germinating <em>B. subtilis</em> spores (<a href="#B24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B24">24</a>). However, there has been no systematic study of the relationship between the spore core protective features (i.e., α/β/γ-type SASP, DPA, and core hydration), the oxidative stress response (by MrgA), ROS detoxification (via SodA or KatX), and DNA repair (by NHEJ or BER) in spores' ionizing radiation resistance, and this is the subject of the present work.</p></section><section id="sec2"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">MATERIALS AND METHODS</h2>
<section id="sec2-1"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Bacterial strains, growth, sporulation, and spore purification.</h3>
<p>The <em>B. subtilis</em> strains used in this work are listed in <a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>, and all are isogenic with the wild-type strain PS832. Spores were obtained by cultivation under vigorous aeration at 37°C for 7 days in double-strength liquid Schaeffer sporulation medium (<a href="#B30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B30">30</a>), and spores were purified and stored as described previously (<a href="#B31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B31">31</a><a href="#B32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B32">–</a><a href="#B33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B33">33</a>). When appropriate, chloramphenicol (5 μg/ml), neomycin (10 μg/ml), spectinomycin (100 μg/ml), erythromycin (1 μg/ml), or tetracycline (10 μg/ml) was added to the medium. Spore preparations consisted of single spores with no detectable clumps and were free (&gt;99%) of growing cells, germinated spores, and cell debris, as seen in the phase-contrast microscope (<a href="#B32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B32">32</a><a href="#B33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B33">–</a><a href="#B34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B34">34</a>).</p>

</div></section></section><section id="sec2-2"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Measurement of spore resistance to ionizing radiation.</h3>
<p>Preparation of spore samples for radiation exposure has been described in detail previously (<a href="#B33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B33">33</a>, <a href="#B34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B34">34</a>). In brief, suspensions of spores of the different <em>B. subtilis</em> strains (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>) were prepared in sterile distilled water to a final concentration of 1 × 10<sup>8</sup> spores per ml. Triplicate samples of spores in water (100 μl) were individually exposed to three different types of ionizing radiation: X rays (200 keV/15 mA), protons (with energy of 218 MeV, LET of 0.4 keV/μm, and a range of 301 mm in water), and high-energy-charged iron ions (Fe; with energy of 500 MeV/nucleon, LET of 200 keV/μm, and a range of 99 mm in water). X ray, proton, and Fe irradiations were conducted at the German Aerospace Center, Institute of Aerospace Medicine (DLR-ME), in Cologne, Germany, at the University of Florida Proton Therapy Institute (UF-PTI), Jacksonville, Florida, USA, and at the heavy ion medical accelerator (HIMAC) facility at the National Institute for Radiological Sciences (NIRS) in Chiba, Japan, respectively. Details on the irradiation geometry of the NIRS-HIMAC, DLR-ME, and UF-PTI facilities, beam monitoring, dosimetry, and dose calculations have been described (<a href="#B34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B34">34</a><a href="#B35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B35">–</a><a href="#B37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B37">37</a>).</p></section><section id="sec2-3"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Numerical and statistical analysis.</h3>
<p>Spore survival was determined from appropriate dilutions in distilled water as colony-forming ability after incubation overnight at 37°C on nutrient broth (NB) agar plates (Difco, Detroit, MI, USA) (<a href="#B33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B33">33</a>, <a href="#B34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B34">34</a>, <a href="#B36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B36">36</a>). The surviving fraction of <em>B. subtilis</em> spores was determined from the ratio <em>N</em>/<em>N</em><sub>0</sub>, where <em>N</em> is the number of CFU of the irradiated sample and <em>N</em><sub>0</sub> that of the nonirradiated controls. Spore inactivation curves were obtained as described previously (<a href="#B34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B34">34</a>). Data are reported as <em>D</em><sub>10</sub>-values, the dose of ionizing radiation killing 90% of the initial spore population (<a href="#B34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B34">34</a>). All data are expressed as averages ± standard deviations (<em>n</em> = 3). The significance of the differences in the survival rates and relative sensitivities were determined by analysis of variance (ANOVA), using SigmaPlot software Version 12.0 (Systat Software GmbH, Erkrath, Germany). Values were evaluated in multigroup pairwise combinations, and differences with <em>P</em> values of &lt;0.05 were considered statistically significant (<a href="#B33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B33">33</a>, <a href="#B34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B34">34</a>, <a href="#B36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B36">36</a>).</p></section></section><section id="sec3"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">RESULTS</h2>
<p>The importance of the two types of SASP, the DNA-binding α/β type and non-DNA-binding γ type, alone and in combination with various other protective spore core components, including core water content and DPA, has been assessed to establish their roles as radioprotectants. <em>B. subtilis</em> spores have several additional potential mechanisms for minimizing damage to spore DNA including BER via AP endonucleases (by ExoA and Nfo), DNA double-strand break repair (via NHEJ by YkoU and V), and radical detoxification (superoxide dismutase SodA and major spore catalase KatX), as well as via the oxidative stress response (DNA-binding protein MrgA); spores of strains lacking these various gene products have been also included in this study. Wild-type and mutant spores (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>) were subjected to three different types of ionizing radiation, X rays, protons, and high-energy Fe ions.</p>
<section id="sec3-1"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Role of spore core components in ionizing radiation resistance.</h3>
<p>Spore structures (e.g., the spore coat and cortex) and components in the spore core (e.g., Ca-DPA, α/β-type SASP) have previously been demonstrated to protect spores from inactivation after exposure to various sporicidal treatments such as exposure to UV radiation, hydrogen peroxide, or heat (reviewed in references <a href="#B4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B4">4</a>, <a href="#B6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B6">6</a>, and <a href="#B7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B7">7</a>). Spores lacking various spore core components were irradiated with X rays (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1A</a>), protons (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1B</a>), or Fe ions (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1C</a>). In order, from most to least sensitive, were spores of mutants in <em>sspA</em> (encoding one of two major α/β-type SASP [<a href="#B38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B38">38</a>]) &gt; <em>sspB</em> (encoding the less abundant major α/β-type SASP [<a href="#B38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B38">38</a>]) &gt; <em>dacB</em>, previously shown to exhibit a 1.6-fold-increased core water content (52 to 65%) compared to that of wild-type spores (34 to 41%) (<a href="#B22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B22">22</a>, <a href="#B23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B23">23</a>) &gt; <em>sleB</em> plus <em>spoVF</em> (which allow isolation of stable spores that lack Ca-DPA [<a href="#B39" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B39">39</a>]) &gt; <em>sspE</em> (encoding the only γ-type SASP; termed γ<sup>−</sup> spores), which is equivalent to the wild type (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1</a>).</p>

<p>Spores of strains defective in ROS detoxification, the oxidative stress response DNA-binding protein MrgA, or DNA repair were exposed to X rays, protons, and Fe ions, and their sensitivities were compared (<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3</a>). Spores deficient in radical detoxification (<em>sodA</em> or <em>katX</em> spores) were no more sensitive than wild-type spores to any type of ionizing radiation tested (<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3</a>), as expected given the minimal activity of enzymes in the spore core. Spores lacking a major oxidative-stress-protective protein (<em>mrgA</em> spores) were significantly more sensitive than wild-type spores to X rays and Fe ions, but not to protons, which could be due to the different spectra of lesions induced by the three types of radiation (<a href="#B9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B9">9</a>, <a href="#B11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B11">11</a>, <a href="#B15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B15">15</a>) (<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3</a>). Spores lacking BER (<em>exoA nfo</em> spores) and double-strand break repair (<em>ykoVU</em> spores) were both significantly more sensitive to all types of radiation than wild-type spores (<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3</a>). Comparison of the spore radiation resistance in wild-type and α<sup>−</sup>β<sup>−</sup> backgrounds showed that in response to X rays only the <em>ykoVU</em> spores showed an additive effect with the loss of α/β-type SASP (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4A</a>). In response to proton exposure, the effects of the <em>katX</em>, <em>mrgA</em>, <em>exoA nfo</em>, and <em>ykoVU</em> mutations (but not <em>sodA</em>) were all potentiated in the α<sup>−</sup>β<sup>−</sup> background (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4B</a>). In response to Fe ions, only the <em>exoA nfo</em> (BER-deficient) and <em>ykoVU</em> (NHEJ-deficient) mutant spores demonstrated a significantly enhanced sensitivity in the α<sup>−</sup>β<sup>−</sup> background (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4C</a>).</p>

<p>In regard to the characteristic resistance to ionizing radiation, spores of the Gram-positive bacterium <em>B. subtilis</em> have often been used to evaluate sterilization efforts in areas of food preservation, medical sterilization, and decontamination of potential biohazardous materials (<a href="#B3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B3">3</a>, <a href="#B4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B4">4</a>, <a href="#B6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B6">6</a>). Ionizing radiation is known to cause damage to numerous targets within the cell, including DNA, due both to (i) direct interaction of the radiation with the target molecule and to (ii) indirect effects due to the production of reactive species such as oxygen radicals (<a href="#B9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B9">9</a>, <a href="#B10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B10">10</a>, <a href="#B12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B12">12</a>, <a href="#B13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B13">13</a>, <a href="#B15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B15">15</a>). Ionizing radiation induces a large variety of damages to DNA bases and through interaction with the sugar moiety can cause formation of DNA strand breaks, either SSB or DSB. DNA strand breaks were reported to be the major lesions in the genome of spores exposed to ionizing radiation (reviewed in references <a href="#B4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B4">4</a> and <a href="#B7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B7">7</a>), caused either directly by the highly energetic photons or accelerated particles or indirectly via interaction of radiation-induced free radicals, e.g., ROS, with the DNA (<a href="#B9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B9">9</a>, <a href="#B13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B13">13</a>, <a href="#B15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B15">15</a>, <a href="#B16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B16">16</a>). The results of several radiobiological studies at accelerators and in space experiments lead to the assumption that at least two qualitatively different lesions are formed depending on the type and energy of the applied ionizing radiation. The spore's structure and chemical composition play major roles in spore resistance (<a href="#B6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B6">6</a>, <a href="#B7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B7">7</a>, <a href="#B18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B18">18</a>). The spore's general structure is very different from that of a growing cell, with a number of features and constituents unique to spores (<a href="#B4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B4">4</a>). Several of these features have been shown to be involved in spore resistance to some chemicals and physical treatments, but little is known of their role in spore resistance to ionizing radiation. Spore DNA is saturated with a group of SASP that bind to DNA largely on the outside of the DNA helix and straighten and stiffen the DNA while changing the DNA to an A-like helix (reviewed in reference <a href="#B6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B6">6</a> and references therein). As a consequence, DNA properties in spores are dramatically different from those in vegetative cells, e.g., spore DNA complexed with SASP is much more resistant to chemical attack, thermal degradation, and UV radiation (<a href="#B6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B6">6</a>, <a href="#B7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B7">7</a>, <a href="#B17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B17">17</a>, <a href="#B22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B22">22</a>, <a href="#B40" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B40">40</a>), strongly suggesting that SASP may also be important for spore resistance to ionizing radiation, as indicated in earlier studies testing mutants lacking a single SASP (<a href="#B17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B17">17</a>, <a href="#B33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B33">33</a>).</p>
<p>The current work has shown that protective components in the spore core are important determinants of spore resistance to X ray, proton, and heavy Fe ion bombardment with the order of importance being as follows: α/β-type SASP ≫ core dehydration &gt; DPA. In contrast, the single major γ-type SASP does not bind to DNA and plays no protective role in spore ionizing radiation resistance, as has also been found when spore resistance to many other agents has been tested (<a href="#B23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B23">23</a>, <a href="#B37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B37">37</a>; reviewed in reference <a href="#B6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B6">6</a>) (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1</a> and <a href="#F2" class="usa-link">2</a>). In the dormant state, spores remain metabolically inactive, and thus substantial DNA, protein, tRNA, and ribosome damage may accumulate, and the spores are incapable of repairing and/or degrading such damaged DNA and proteins (<a href="#B6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B6">6</a>, <a href="#B7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B7">7</a>, <a href="#B18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B18">18</a>). Consequently, damage to DNA, proteins, or other essential components of bacterial spores poses a unique problem, since such repair of such damage does not occur until the processes of germination and outgrowth, when spores reactivate and prepare to return to vegetative growth (<a href="#B6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B6">6</a>, <a href="#B7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B7">7</a>, <a href="#B28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B28">28</a>). Repair of DSB and base modification/loss via NHEJ and BER appear to be important mechanisms for spore resistance to ionizing radiation, and the MrgA-mediated oxidative stress response (with effects in wild-type spores for X-ray stress and in α<sup>−</sup>β<sup>−</sup> spores for proton radiation resistance) plays a lesser role. In contrast, ROS detoxification by catalase or superoxide dismutase appears to play a very minor role, if any, in spore resistance to ionizing radiation, as is also the case in spore resistance to oxidizing agents such as hydrogen peroxide and superoxide (<a href="#B20" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B20">20</a>, <a href="#B21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B21">21</a>) (<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3</a> and <a href="#F4" class="usa-link">4</a>). Clearly the data indicate that spore DNA is a major target of lethal ionizing radiation damage, based on the observation that the major factors contributing significantly to spore survival (<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3</a> and <a href="#F4" class="usa-link">4</a>) in response to different types of ionizing radiation are known to be important in either DNA protection in spores or DNA repair in spore germination and outgrowth. The findings in this communication thus complement the results from microarray experiments demonstrating upregulation of stress-related regulons responding to DNA damage (SOS response) and oxidative stress (PerR regulon) during germination of spores exposed to space conditions, including the effects of galactic cosmic radiation (<a href="#B41" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B41">41</a>).</p>
<p>Spores appear to have two possible ways to minimize deleterious effects of ionizing radiation: (i) by protecting dormant spore DNA from damage in the first place (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1</a> and <a href="#F2" class="usa-link">2</a>) and (ii) by ensuring detoxification and repair of direct and indirect damage during spore germination (<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3</a> and <a href="#F4" class="usa-link">4</a>). Because ionizing radiation causes such a wide spectrum of direct and indirect DNA damage, it will be interesting to determine the contributions of additional error-free and error-prone DNA repair pathways (e.g., mismatch repair, translesion synthesis, and homologous recombination [<a href="#B29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B29">29</a>, <a href="#B42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B42">42</a><a href="#B43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B43">–</a><a href="#B44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B44">44</a>]), to gain further detailed insights into spore resistance to ionizing radiation.</p></section><section id="ack1" class="ack"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">ACKNOWLEDGMENTS</h2>
