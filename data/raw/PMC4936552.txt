<p>Mathematical modeling coupled with direct measurement of Ca<sup>2+</sup> and ROS dynamics suggest that ROS-assisted calcium-induced calcium release propagates stress-induced Ca<sup>2+</sup> waves in plants.</p></section><section class="abstract" id="abstract2"><h2>Abstract</h2>
<p>Plants exhibit rapid, systemic signaling systems that allow them to coordinate physiological and developmental responses throughout the plant body, even to highly localized and quickly changing environmental stresses. The propagation of these signals is thought to include processes ranging from electrical and hydraulic networks to waves of reactive oxygen species (<a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>) and cytoplasmic Ca<sup>2+</sup> traveling throughout the plant. For the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave system, the involvement of the vacuolar ion channel TWO PORE CHANNEL1 (TPC1) has been reported. However, the precise role of this channel and the mechanism of cell-to-cell propagation of the wave have remained largely undefined. Here, we use the fire-diffuse-fire model to analyze the behavior of a Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave originating from Ca<sup>2+</sup> release involving the TPC1 channel in Arabidopsis (<em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em>). We conclude that a Ca<sup>2+</sup> diffusion-dominated calcium-induced calcium-release mechanism is insufficient to explain the observed wave transmission speeds. The addition of a <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-triggered element, however, is able to quantitatively reproduce the observed transmission characteristics. The treatment of roots with the <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> scavenger ascorbate and the NADPH oxidase inhibitor diphenyliodonium and analysis of Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave propagation in the Arabidopsis <em>respiratory burst oxidase homolog D</em> (<em>AtrbohD</em>) knockout background all led to reductions in Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave transmission speeds consistent with this model. Furthermore, imaging of extracellular <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> production revealed a systemic spread of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> release that is dependent on both <em>AtRBOHD</em> and <em>TPC1</em>. These results suggest that, in the root, plant systemic signaling is supported by a <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-assisted calcium-induced calcium-release mechanism intimately involving <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> production by AtRBOHD and Ca<sup>2+</sup> release dependent on the vacuolar channel TPC1.</p></section><hr class="headless">
<p>Plants possess a rapid systemic signaling network that enables whole-plant responses to localized stimuli. Examples include wounding (<a href="#bib34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib34">Miller et al., 2009</a>), pathogen attack (<a href="#bib13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib13">Dempsey and Klessig, 2012</a>), and osmotic shock (<a href="#bib9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib9">Christmann et al., 2007</a>). The system(s) involved in the plant-wide propagation of these signals has been proposed to be mediated by a diverse set of molecular regulators, including changes in membrane potential and ion fluxes (<a href="#bib16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib16">Felle and Zimmermann, 2007</a>; <a href="#bib65" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib65">Zimmermann et al., 2009</a>; <a href="#bib38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib38">Mousavi et al., 2013</a>), hydraulics in the vasculature (<a href="#bib9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib9">Christmann et al., 2007</a>; <a href="#bib15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib15">Farmer et al., 2014</a>), and reactive oxygen species (<a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>; <a href="#bib34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib34">Miller et al., 2009</a>). A novel addition to this suite of systemic signals was described by <a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Choi et al. (2014)</a>, in which a wave-like propagation of increased cytoplasmic Ca<sup>2+</sup> moved throughout the plant in response to localized salt stress. This signal propagated through the roots at a velocity of 396 ± 28 μm s<sup>−1</sup> and induced the expression of a range of stress-response genes in the leaves. Unlike systemic signals described previously, this Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave preferentially propagates through the root cortical and endodermal cells rather than the vasculature (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Choi et al., 2014</a>). Furthermore, the velocity of the signal is slower than the reported speed of 800 to 1,400 μm s<sup>−1</sup> seen with the other proposed systemic signals, such as the <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> wave or electrical coupling (<a href="#bib34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib34">Miller et al., 2009</a>; <a href="#bib38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib38">Mousavi et al., 2013</a>).</p>
<p><a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Choi et al. (2014)</a> revealed that the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave could be blocked by the application of pharmacological agents (La<sup>3+</sup> and Ruthenium Red) known to inhibit calcium-release pathways and that disruption of the <em>TWO PORE CHANNEL1</em> (<em>TPC1</em>) gene results in a substantial drop in wave speed (<a href="#tblI" class="usa-link">Table I</a>). Furthermore, overexpression of <em>TPC1</em> increased the speed of the wave by a factor of 1.7 (<a href="#tblI" class="usa-link">Table I</a>). TPC1 also appears to be involved in systemic wound-related Ca<sup>2+</sup> increases (<a href="#bib66" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib66">Kiep et al., 2015</a>), yet for many stress responses, whole-plant Ca<sup>2+</sup> signaling dynamics appear unchanged in <em>TPC1</em> mutants (<a href="#bib47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib47">Ranf et al., 2008</a>).</p>

<div class="p text-right font-secondary"><a href="table/tblI/" class="usa-link" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Open in a new tab</a></div></section><p><em>TPC1</em> in Arabidopsis (<em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em>) encodes the slow vacuolar (<a href="#def2" class="usa-link">SV</a>) channel (<a href="#bib27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib27">Hedrich and Neher, 1987</a>; <a href="#bib43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib43">Peiter et al., 2005</a>) that has been shown to be permeable to cations such as K<sup>+</sup> but also to Ca<sup>2+</sup> (<a href="#bib60" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib60">Ward and Schroeder, 1994</a>; <a href="#bib21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib21">Gradogna et al., 2009</a>). The <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">SV</a> channel has been proposed to mediate Ca<sup>2+</sup> release from the vacuole (<a href="#bib60" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib60">Ward and Schroeder, 1994</a>; <a href="#bib2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib2">Allen and Sanders, 1996</a>; <a href="#bib45" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib45">Pottosin et al., 2009</a>), although this idea has become controversial in recent years (<a href="#bib26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib26">Hedrich and Marten, 2011</a>). The original electrophysiological characterization of the <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">SV</a> channel showed that it could be modulated by Ca<sup>2+</sup> levels (<a href="#bib27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib27">Hedrich and Neher, 1987</a>), and now structural and genetic analyses of the channel indicate the presence of Ca<sup>2+</sup>-binding EF hand domains on the cytosolic face of the channel that aid channel activation (<a href="#bib22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib22">Guo et al., 2016</a>; <a href="#bib32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib32">Kintzer and Stroud, 2016</a>; for review, see <a href="#bib26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib26">Hedrich and Marten, 2011</a>), coupled with a novel Ca<sup>2+</sup>-sensitive regulatory domain on its vacuolar lumenal face that shifts the channel’s voltage activation toward more positive potentials as vacuolar Ca<sup>2+</sup> levels rise (<a href="#bib4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib4">Beyhl et al., 2009</a>; <a href="#bib11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib11">Dadacz-Narloch et al., 2011</a>; <a href="#bib22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib22">Guo et al., 2016</a>). The recently published structure of TPC1 (<a href="#bib22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib22">Guo et al., 2016</a>; <a href="#bib32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib32">Kintzer and Stroud, 2016</a>) indicates several possible sites of phosphorylation offering further scope for regulation. Indeed, <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">SV</a> channel activity is known to be regulated by a host of cytosolic factors (for review, see <a href="#bib26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib26">Hedrich and Marten, 2011</a>). However, in general, the precise role of TPC1 in potentially mediating stress-induced Ca<sup>2+</sup> changes, and also which regulatory mechanisms may act to modulate TPC1 channel gating in such responses, remain unclear. For example, while some of the genetic components of the wave-like propagation of stress information by Ca<sup>2+</sup> (such as TPC1; <a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Choi et al., 2014</a>; <a href="#bib66" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib66">Kiep et al., 2015</a>) have been identified, the molecular mechanisms mediating the spread of this wave have yet to be fully elucidated. Therefore, we used mathematical models to provide insight into the possible modes to generate this wave and to produce testable predictions for empirical validation of these models. We conclude that a simple calcium-induced calcium-release (<a href="#def3" class="usa-link">CICR</a>) mechanism is unlikely. To support the speeds of wave transmission seen in vivo, we suggest that a <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-assisted <a href="#def3" class="usa-link">CICR</a> mechanism involving TPC1 and <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> production by the NADPH oxidase Arabidopsis RESPIRATORY BURST OXIDASE HOMOLOG D (AtRBOHD) may be required.</p>
<section id="s1"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">RESULTS</h2>
<section id="s2"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Models Based on <a href="#def3" class="usa-link">CICR</a> Do Not Recapitulate the Experimentally Determined Speed of Ca<sup>2+</sup> Wave Propagation under Physiological Conditions</h3>
<p>The experimental investigation of <a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Choi et al. (2014)</a> highlighted a number of different Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave speeds in roots in response to chemical perturbations and in mutant lines of Arabidopsis in response to local NaCl stress application (<a href="#tblI" class="usa-link">Table I</a>). For instance, increasing the abundance of TPC1 using a 35S promoter resulted in a significantly increased wave speed. Our approach to begin to dissect this system, therefore, has been to evaluate whether there are parameters for a mathematical model of Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave propagation that can reproduce the empirically determined wild-type velocity and whether those same parameters, but with an increased number of TPC1 channels, also can capture the speed in the <em>TPC1</em> overexpression line. Failure to do so would indicate that further elements and/or other mechanisms need to be considered in the model. This approach of testing whether a model is consistent with the available data has the advantage of not requiring experimental values for all parameters (which might be extremely difficult, if not impossible, to determine).</p>
<p>Given the direct effect that TPC1 has on the propagation of the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave, we first considered whether a <a href="#def3" class="usa-link">CICR</a> mechanism mediated by TPC1 could be involved. As discussed in the introduction, TPC1’s ability to directly mediate the release of vacuolar Ca<sup>2+</sup> is not clear (<a href="#bib26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib26">Hedrich and Marten, 2011</a>), and its role could well be indirect. However, one key strength of the modeling strategy is that it does not make any statement about the identity of the channels responsible for mediating the release of Ca<sup>2+</sup>, just that it is dependent on TPC1 action.</p>
<p><a href="#def3" class="usa-link">CICR</a> can be described mathematically through the fire-diffuse-fire model (<a href="#bib31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib31">Keizer et al., 1998</a>; <a href="#bib44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib44">Ponce-Dawson et al., 1999</a>; <a href="#bib56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib56">Timofeeva and Coombes, 2003</a>; <a href="#bib10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib10">Coombes et al., 2004</a>). This framework describes the process in which a quantity of Ca<sup>2+</sup>, σ<sub>i</sub>, released through a channel at position <em>x</em><sub>i</sub> and at time <em>t</em><sub>i</sub> diffuses through the cytosol and activates the next channel at position <em>x</em><sub>i+1</sub>. This mechanism has the capacity to create a wave of Ca<sup>2+</sup> that is regenerated each time a channel opens.</p>
<p>Approximating wave propagation through roots as a one-dimensional system, the concentration of Ca<sup>2+</sup>, <em>u</em>(<em>x</em>,<em>t</em>), can be determined using the diffusion equation:</p>

<p>where <em>D</em> is the diffusion constant of Ca<sup>2+</sup>, σ is the release strength, and τ<sub>R</sub> is the time for which the channel remains open. Channels are modeled as point sources that are characterized by Dirac delta functions, δ(<em>x</em>), in space and by the product of two Heaviside step functions, <em>H</em>(<em>t</em>), in time, to capture the open time. A channel at a position, <em>x</em><sub>i</sub>, on the vacuole is assumed to open when the cytosolic Ca<sup>2+</sup> concentration at that position is greater than the threshold value <em>u</em><sub>c.</sub> This gating value represents the mathematical description of either the concentration-dependent binding kinetics of a ligand-gated channel or the requisite buildup of ionic charge needed to open a voltage-gated channel.</p>
<p>We assume that the release of Ca<sup>2+</sup> from the vacuole occurs quickly compared with the time for diffusion between channels, so that we can treat a release as a point source in time (τ<sub>R</sub> = 0). For a system of equally spaced channels with separation <em>d</em>, the velocity of the resulting wave is given by <a href="#bib41" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib41">Pearson and Ponce-Dawson (1998)</a>:</p>
<table class="disp-formula p" id="e2"><tr>
<td class="formula"><img class="graphic" src="https://cdn.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/blobs/4907/4936552/8ea700389a99/PP_PP201600215R1_equ2.jpg" loading="lazy" alt="graphic file with name PP_PP201600215R1_equ2.jpg"></td>
<td class="label">(2)</td>
</tr></table>
<p>Within a cell, the vacuole (with the ubiquitously expressed channel TPC1) typically occupies 50% to 90% of the interior volume (<a href="#bib33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib33">Macklon et al., 1996</a>), whereas plasmodesmata, presumably lacking TPC1, connect cells, leading to two distinct regions for the propagation of the wave in the model (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1A</a>). In the first, the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave is propagated through the cytoplasm surrounding the vacuole. In the case of approximately equally distributed TPC1 channels on the tonoplast, the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave has a velocity given by Equation 2. In the second region, the wave must move between neighboring cells. If the mechanism behind transmission in the cytoplasm is a self-propagating wave of <a href="#def3" class="usa-link">CICR</a> mediated by TPC1, then presumably this mechanism will reduce to passive diffusion over a distance <em>d</em><sub>v</sub> (the cell wall region) between the cytoplasm in adjacent cells through plasmodesmata. We assumed that this plasmodesmal coupling poses no obstacle to diffusion, so that the model has the best chance of propagating a wave at maximal speed. In this case, Ca<sup>2+</sup> moving from a cell takes a time, <em>T</em>, to activate the first TPC1 channel on the tonoplast of the neighboring cell. The concentration profile for a single release evaluated at the time of activation of the next channel (when <em>u</em> = <em>u</em><sub>c</sub>) then satisfies the expression</p>

<td class="label">(3)</td>
</tr></table>
<p>We assume here that the activation of the first channel in the neighboring cell is dominated by Ca<sup>2+</sup> released from the closest channel in the previous cell. Within this model, the velocity of a Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave is determined by the velocities across these two regions. The spatial and temporal resolution of the experiments measuring these speeds (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Choi et al., 2014</a>) is insufficient to robustly distinguish transmission through each cell versus the cell-to-cell component crossing <em>d</em><sub>v</sub>, even using the high spatial and temporal resolution afforded by confocal microscopy. Therefore, the transit speed between the two regions cannot be separated and so is averaged in the empirical measurements. For a cell of length <em>L</em>, this average speed is given by</p>

<td class="formula"><img class="graphic" src="https://cdn.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/blobs/4907/4936552/fcb84b6df352/PP_PP201600215R1_equ4.jpg" loading="lazy" alt="graphic file with name PP_PP201600215R1_equ4.jpg"></td>

<p>The time taken to cross the cell wall region (Eq. 3) and the velocity in the cytoplasm surrounding each vacuole region (Eq. 2) both depend on the parameter ratio σ/<em>u</em><sub>c</sub>. This dependency implies that, in order to decrease the time spent crossing the cell wall region, the cell must change the release properties of the channels, but this has a corresponding effect on how quickly the wave crosses the cytoplasm in the vacuolar region. We used the measured velocity in the wild type, 395 μm s<sup>−1</sup>, the measured density of TPC1 channels in mesophyll cells, <em>d</em> = 1 μm (<a href="#bib46" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib46">Pottosin and Schönknecht, 2007</a>), and a commonly used value for the diffusion rate of Ca<sup>2+</sup> in the cytosol, <em>D</em> = 20 μm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> (<a href="#bib1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib1">Allbritton et al., 1992</a>), to calculate an estimate for this unknown parameter ratio, σ/<em>u</em><sub>c</sub>. We then used this value to determine if the model can recapitulate the velocity in the TPC1 overexpressor (oxTPC1), 679 ± 73 μm s<sup>−1</sup> (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Choi et al., 2014</a>).</p>

(2005)</a> measured the average numbers of TPC1 channels in vacuolar membrane patches in both the wild type and the overexpressor line. With the average channel density measured by <a href="#bib46" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib46">Pottosin and Schönknecht (2007)</a>, we used the channel number data to estimate the channel separation. For the TPC1 overexpressor, we obtained a channel separation, <em>d</em>, of about 0.6 μm. Using this channel separation estimate and the ratio σ/<em>u</em><sub>c</sub> calculated from the wild-type data, we calculated the expected velocity in the TPC1 overexpressor for a range of cell lengths and intervacuolar distances, <em>d</em><sub>v</sub>, covering those found in the regions of the Arabidopsis root where empirical wave speed determinations have been performed (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1B</a>).</p>
<p>We see that the model can only recapitulate the velocity in the overexpressor when <em>d</em><sub>v</sub> is sufficiently small. This result did not depend on our choice of diffusion constant (<a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.16.00215/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Fig. S1A</a>) but could be affected by changes in the channel separation, <em>d</em> (<a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.16.00215/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Fig. S1B</a>). Since our chosen value for <em>d</em> came from TPC1 channel density data from mesophyll cells and the wave travels through the root, different expression levels of TPC1 could result in different densities of TPC1 on the vacuolar membrane. We tested for such potential differences with quantitative PCR (<a href="#def4" class="usa-link">QPCR</a>) analysis of roots and shoots dissected from plants grown as for the Ca<sup>2+</sup> imaging. This analysis showed no statistically significant difference between root and shoot expression in the wild type (<a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.16.00215/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Fig. S2</a>). In the overexpressor, shoots showed a 1.9-fold higher transcript level than the roots of the same plants, so any change in actual channel density between roots and shoots will likely be small.</p>
<p>Measurements of cell wall thickness in leaves (<a href="#bib35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib35">Moghaddam and Wilman, 1998</a>) and imaging of tonoplast intrinsic proteins in root cells (<a href="#bib62" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib62">Werner et al., 2003</a>; <a href="#bib28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib28">Hunter et al., 2007</a>; <a href="#bib19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib19">Gattolin et al., 2009</a>; <a href="#bib23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib23">Guo et al., 2014</a>) suggest typical values of <em>d</em><sub>v</sub> in the range of 1 to 2 μm, with values of 10 μm and even 60 μm in some cases. Due to the variability in such parameters drawn from the literature, we used the GFP signals in the images from the Ca<sup>2+</sup> measurement data to visualize the cytoplasm of the cells used for Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave speed measurement and so define where the vacuoles within each cell likely ended. We then measured the distance between the vacuole (where the yellow fluorescent protein [YFP] signal was less than 2 <span class="font-variant-small-caps">sd</span> above background) in one cell to the vacuole in the adjacent cell using ImageJ (<a href="#bib52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib52">Schneider et al., 2012</a>). As the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave speed is constant throughout the root at approximately 400 µm s<sup>−1</sup> in the wild type (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Choi et al., 2014</a>), we concentrated on making these measurements in images taken at the 1,000-µm region from the root tip to allow direct comparison with the data in <a href="#fig3" class="usa-link">Figures 3</a> and <a href="#fig4" class="usa-link">4</a>, which focus on Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave responses at this point in the root. Using this approach, average cytoplasmic lengths between vacuoles in adjacent cells were 4.4 ± 1.2 µm (epidermis; mean ± <span class="font-variant-small-caps">sd</span>, <em>n</em> = 29), 5.4 ± 1.5 µm (cortex; <em>n</em> = 38), and 4.9 ± 1.4 (endodermis; <em>n</em> = 41). It is important to note that vacuoles are dynamic organelles, so these measurements should be viewed as time-averaged values of vacuolar behavior, as they are taken from random images across multiple independent experiments.</p>

<p>The analysis outlined above assumes that TPC1 is distributed uniformly across the vacuolar membrane. Clustering of signaling components may provide a mechanism for selective amplification and channeling of signals that could, in theory, contribute to increased signal propagation rates. Indeed, the observed distribution of channels (<a href="#bib43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib43">Peiter et al., 2005</a>) suggests that TPC1 may show a degree of clustering. <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> Production via RBOHD Is Sufficient to Activate Calcium Release and Wave Propagation at Observed Rates</h3>
<p>To explain the observed Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave speeds, we hypothesized that <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> may have a role to play. Rapid propagation of a systemic increase in <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> produced by the NADPH oxidase AtRBOHD has been reported in response to stress signals (<a href="#bib57" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib57">Torres et al., 2002</a>; <a href="#bib34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib34">Miller et al., 2009</a>; <a href="#bib64" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib64">Zhang et al., 2009</a>). RBOHD is expressed ubiquitously throughout Arabidopsis plants and is localized to the plasma membrane of cells (<a href="#bib51" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib51">Sagi and Fluhr, 2006</a>). It is responsible for the production of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> such as hydrogen peroxide in the apoplast and is involved in a range of signaling processes, such as in defense (<a href="#bib57" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib57">Torres et al., 2002</a>) and in the opening and closing of stomata (<a href="#bib64" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib64">Zhang et al., 2009</a>). Furthermore, it has been shown to be essential to the propagation of the <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> waves by <a href="#bib34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib34">Miller et al. (2009)</a>. The RBOH family of proteins contain two EF hand calcium-binding domains as well as multiple potential phosphorylation sites, which enable their activation in response to raised cytosolic Ca<sup>2+</sup> (<a href="#bib51" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib51">Sagi and Fluhr, 2006</a>). Indeed, the dynamics of changes in <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> and Ca<sup>2+</sup> also are known to interact in the processes of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-induced Ca<sup>2+</sup> release and Ca<sup>2+</sup>-induced <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> release (<a href="#bib20" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib20">Gilroy et al., 2014</a>).</p>
<p>A conceptual model for <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> wave propagation was produced by <a href="#bib14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib14">Dubiella et al. (2013)</a>, where Ca<sup>2+</sup> acting via the calcium-dependent protein kinase family member 5 stimulates <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> production from the plasma membrane NADPH oxidase RBOHD and <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> is able to trigger Ca<sup>2+</sup> influx across the plasma membrane into cells via <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-regulated plasma membrane Ca<sup>2+</sup> channels (<a href="#bib48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib48">Richards et al., 2014</a>). This provides a self-propagation mechanism in which <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> triggers plasma membrane Ca<sup>2+</sup> influx into the cell, potentially triggering <a href="#def3" class="usa-link">CICR</a> via TCP1 at the tonoplast. The cellular Ca<sup>2+</sup> rise in turn activates RBOHD, which then produces more apoplastic <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>. <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> could potentially travel through the apoplast and trigger the same process in the neighboring cell.</p>
<p>This <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-dependent model can be described mathematically to quantitatively analyze whether it is consistent with the observed stress-related Ca<sup>2+</sup> waves. <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-induced <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> release can be described by Equation 1, where <em>u</em> is now the concentration of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> in the apoplast. <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> does not activate a neighboring RBOHD directly but via triggering increased Ca<sup>2+</sup> in the cytoplasm, which in turn activates RBOHD. The critical threshold parameter <em>u</em><sub>c</sub> incorporates the indirect nature and complexity of this activation process in one variable and so provides a mathematical means through which changes in Ca<sup>2+</sup> behavior can feed back on <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> propagation without needing to parameterize the detailed molecular mechanisms behind this process. If higher levels of Ca<sup>2+</sup> release are being maintained in a cell (e.g. because of <em>TPC1</em> overexpression), then we would expect the RBOHDs to be activated more quickly, which can be represented in the model by a decreased <em>u</em><sub>c</sub>, irrespective of the precise molecular mechanism responsible for the Ca<sup>2+</sup>-dependent activation.</p>
<p><a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> diffusion in the apoplast may be predicted to be faster than that of Ca<sup>2+</sup> in the cytoplasm, as highly active cytoplasmic Ca<sup>2+</sup> buffering and sequestration mechanisms limit the cytoplasmic movement of this ion (<a href="#bib1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib1">Allbritton et al., 1992</a>); although it is important to note that the levels of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> in the cell wall also are thought to be tightly regulated by a balance between their generation systems and <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-scavenging mechanisms (<a href="#bib29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib29">Kärkönen and Kuchitsu, 2015</a>). RBOHDs also exist at a higher density than TPC1 (<a href="#bib43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib43">Peiter et al., 2005</a>; <a href="#bib25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib25">Hao et al., 2014</a>). This puts the propagation of the <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> wave into the continuous regime (<a href="#bib41" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib41">Pearson and Ponce-Dawson, 1998</a>); therefore, the velocity of the <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-assisted wave has a different functional form than from a purely <a href="#def3" class="usa-link">CICR</a>-driven process, in which it propagates as a saltatory wave (<a href="#bib10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib10">Coombes et al., 2004</a>):</p>

<p>To empirically test the prediction of a <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-assisted mechanism for Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave propagation, we attempted to dissect the involvement of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> in the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave by treating the root with the <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> scavenger ascorbate and the NADPH oxidase inhibitor <a href="#def5" class="usa-link">DPI</a>. In the oxTPC1 background, a Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave was still evident after pretreatment with <a href="#def5" class="usa-link">DPI</a>, but the rate of transmission was slowed to 134.4 µm s<sup>−1</sup> (<a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.16.00215/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Fig. S4</a>).</p></section><section id="s6"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Imaging of the Extracellular <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> Wave Using OxyBurst Green-Bovine Serum Albumin</h3>
<p>Implicit in the <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-assisted <a href="#def3" class="usa-link">CICR</a> model of Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave propagation is that the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave should be accompanied by a similarly propagating wave of apoplastic <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> production. Although the presence of a <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> wave has been inferred from the wave-like activation of the <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-dependent transcriptional response (<a href="#bib34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib34">Miller et al., 2009</a>), direct measurement of the wave-like propagation of an apoplastic <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> signal has proven technically challenging. We used the <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-sensing fluorescent dye OxyBurst Green H<sub>2</sub>HFF conjugated to bovine serum albumin (<a href="#def6" class="usa-link">BSA</a>) to exclude this <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> sensor from the cytoplasm of the root. OxyBurst becomes more fluorescent upon oxidation and so provides a measure of the kinetics of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> increases (<a href="#bib36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib36">Monshausen et al., 2007</a>, <a href="#bib37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib37">2009</a>). Therefore, we monitored OxyBurst fluorescence intensity before and after local NaCl stimulation of the root tip at points distant from the site of NaCl stimulation to try to capture the spread of a putative wave of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> production. As OxyBurst shows an irreversible increase in fluorescence upon oxidation, the constitutive background <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> production by the root led to a slow increase in signal with time prior to treatment (<a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">Fig. 5, A and B</a>; <a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.16.00215/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Movie S1</a>). Addition of growth medium to the root tip (control) led to a slight increase in OxyBurst signal measured 3,000 µm away from the root tip site of local medium addition (<a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">Fig. 5, A and B</a>; <a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.16.00215/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Movie S1</a>), which may represent a small response to the mechanical signal generated by medium addition (<a href="#bib36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib36">Monshausen et al., 2007</a>). However, when NaCl was added to the tip, this distal region showed a rapid and significantly larger increase in OxyBurst fluorescence (<a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">Fig. 5, A and B</a>; <a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.16.00215/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Movie S2</a>). Calculation of the speed of movement of the signal triggering this response was made by monitoring the time for a significant increase (greater than 2 <span class="font-variant-small-caps">sd</span> above prestimulated levels) in the mean OxyBurst signal monitored at 3,000 and 5,000 µm from the root apex in 14 replicate experiments. This analysis indicated a propagation speed of 374 µm s<sup>−1</sup>.</p>

<p>We have applied the fire-diffuse-fire model to explore the mechanisms behind the plant-wide transmission of stress-induced Ca<sup>2+</sup> waves, focusing on the salt-induced wave of the root. Our analyses suggest that a simple <a href="#def3" class="usa-link">CICR</a>-based mechanism relying upon a self-reinforcing Ca<sup>2+</sup> release from the vacuole via TPC1-dependent Ca<sup>2+</sup> efflux and diffusion of Ca<sup>2+</sup> between cells is unlikely to account for the observed velocity of wave movement. Extending this <a href="#def3" class="usa-link">CICR</a> mechanism for Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave transit through the cell with Ca<sup>2+</sup>-dependent apoplastic <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> production to couple between cells, however, is able to support the observed velocities.</p>
<p><a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> have been implicated repeatedly in systemic signaling (<a href="#bib3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib3">Alvarez et al., 1998</a>; <a href="#bib30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib30">Karpinski et al., 1999</a>; <a href="#bib34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib34">Miller et al., 2009</a>; <a href="#bib55" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib55">Suzuki et al., 2013</a>), and while it is tempting to identify this and the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave we have studied here as two sides of the same coin, there are important differences that have yet to be explained. First, the <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> wave in response to wounding travels at 1,400 μm s<sup>−1</sup> (<a href="#bib34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib34">Miller et al., 2009</a>), much faster than the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave we studied here. This difference might be because the wound-induced <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> wave propagates through the vascular tissue, whereas the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave appears to be restricted to the cortex and endodermis (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Choi et al., 2014</a>). The vasculature is well suited for long-distance transmission of signals (<a href="#bib58" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib58">van Bel et al., 2014</a>), particularly electrical signals. Alternatively, the triggering stimulus also may impact on propagation speed/mechanism. Indeed, the velocity of the wound-related <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> wave is very similar to that of electrical responses to wounding (<a href="#bib38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib38">Mousavi et al., 2013</a>), suggesting that <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> and electrical signals may act together in the wounding response (<a href="#bib58" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib58">van Bel et al., 2014</a>). It is striking, however, that the signal velocity in the <em>rbohD</em> mutant is compromised in response to either salt stress (this study) or wounding (<a href="#bib34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib34">Miller et al., 2009</a>), hinting at some possibly shared core components of the transmission machinery. Indeed, both the NaCl-triggered Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave propagation (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Choi et al., 2014</a>) and the wound-induced systemic Ca<sup>2+</sup> increases (<a href="#bib66" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib66">Kiep et al., 2015</a>) appear to be dependent on the <em>TPC1</em>, reinforcing the idea of conserved elements of the propagation system.</p>
<p>The activity of <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">SV</a> channels is known to be inhibited by <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> and activated by reducing agents (<a href="#bib7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib7">Carpaneto et al., 1999</a>; <a href="#bib53" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib53">Scholz-Starke et al., 2004</a>; <a href="#bib45" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib45">Pottosin et al., 2009</a>), and this may at first sight appear contradictory to the <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>/TPC1-related propagation mechanism modeled above. However, since <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> is generated within the apoplast by RBOHDs, it would initially be able to rapidly gate <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-activated channels in the plasma membrane. The <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> would then need to cross the plasma membrane and the cytoplasm before interacting with TPC1. It can take 0.9 s to develop a stable gradient of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> across plasma membranes (<a href="#bib5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib5">Bienert et al., 2006</a>), although the maximum theoretical rate could be much faster (<a href="#bib59" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib59">Vestergaard et al., 2012</a>). Given the speed of the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave in the wild type, it should take only approximately 2.5 ms (TPC1 channel separation over the wave speed) to activate each TPC1 in sequence; thus, it is unlikely that the <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> would have time to trigger a direct effect on TPC1’s ability to mediate the initial propagation phase of the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave. The slower <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-triggered inactivation of TPC1, therefore, may have an important role to play in terminating the wave once it has moved through the cell. <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-based TPC1 inactivation also could lead to a refractory period where the channel is unable to support further Ca<sup>2+</sup> increases, leading to the wave-like progression of the Ca<sup>2+</sup> increase.</p>
<p>Inherent to the model presented above is rapid triggering of TPC1 by <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-dependent cytosolic Ca<sup>2+</sup> increases. Although the <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">SV</a> channel has long been identified as being activated by cytosolic Ca<sup>2+</sup> (<a href="#bib27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib27">Hedrich and Neher, 1987</a>), large concentrations of Ca<sup>2+</sup> are required (<a href="#bib22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib22">Guo et al., 2016</a>). The ability of the <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">SV</a> channel to directly release Ca<sup>2+</sup> also is a topic of some controversy (<a href="#bib26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib26">Hedrich and Marten, 2011</a>), as large vacuolar Ca<sup>2+</sup> concentrations inhibit the activation of TPC1 (<a href="#bib4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib4">Beyhl et al., 2009</a>; <a href="#bib26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib26">Hedrich and Marten, 2011</a>; <a href="#bib22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib22">Guo et al., 2016</a>). The <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">SV</a> channel is known to be regulated by many other factors (for review, see <a href="#bib26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib26">Hedrich and Marten, 2011</a>). For example, recent structural data have indicated a number of potential phosphorylation sites on TPC1 (<a href="#bib32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib32">Kintzer and Stroud, 2016</a>), and the beet (<em>Beta vulgaris</em>) <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">SV</a> channel is activated by calmodulin (<a href="#bib45" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib45">Pottosin et al., 2009</a>). Such alternative regulatory mechanisms provide a wide range of other means for the activation of this channel as part of the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave transmission process. TPC1 also could be only indirectly responsible for the release of Ca<sup>2+</sup> from the vacuole, possibly by controlling changes in membrane voltage that activate other channels (<a href="#bib42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib42">Peiter, 2011</a>). Our modeling approach does not make any assumptions about TPC1’s role in mediating Ca<sup>2+</sup> release, merely requiring that the activation of TPC1 leads to Ca<sup>2+</sup> release, whether directly or indirectly.</p>
<p>The knockout of <em>TPC1</em> that slows Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave transmission approximately 40-fold only delays the Ca<sup>2+</sup> response at the site of direct NaCl treatment by 30 s (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Choi et al., 2014</a>), suggesting that TPC1 may have a more pronounced role in transmission than in the initial direct stress response, consistent with the lack of an obvious Ca<sup>2+</sup> dynamics phenotype in response to stress in <em>tpc1-2</em> when measured at the whole-plant level (<a href="#bib47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib47">Ranf et al., 2008</a>).</p>
<p>The model presented in <a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">Figure 6</a> also identifies two pools of Ca<sup>2+</sup> that are likely to be important for generating the cytosolic Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave, apoplastic, with influx through plasma membrane channels, and vacuolar, with influx to the cytosol, through TPC1 or a TPC1-related channel on the tonoplast. Thus, extracellular Ca<sup>2+</sup> levels in the wall and lumenal Ca<sup>2+</sup> levels in the vacuole may be other key regulators of Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave propagation, especially as TPC1 is known to be responsive to both cytosolic and lumenal vacuolar Ca<sup>2+</sup> levels as described above. While a role for extracellular Ca<sup>2+</sup> signals is well established (e.g. in the stomatal response; <a href="#bib24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib24">Han et al., 2003</a>; <a href="#bib39" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib39">Nomura et al., 2008</a>; <a href="#bib61" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib61">Weinl et al., 2008</a>), the potential for signaling-related dynamic changes of vacuolar lumenal Ca<sup>2+</sup> levels remains poorly understood.</p>

<figcaption><p>Conceptual model of the propagation of the salt stress-induced Ca<sup>2+</sup>/<a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> waves. <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> (green arrows) are produced in RBOHD (green circles) and diffuse through the apoplast, activating <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-sensitive Ca<sup>2+</sup> channels in the plasma membrane (light blue ellipse). These channels release Ca<sup>2+</sup> into the cytosol (blue arrows) that activate TPC1 proteins (yellow circles), which, directly or indirectly, mediate Ca<sup>2+</sup> release from the vacuole. Combined, this Ca<sup>2+</sup> activates further RBOHD proteins, giving rise to a self-propagating <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>/Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave. Passage between cells may be mediated by either diffusion of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> through the apoplast or Ca<sup>2+</sup> through the plasmodesmata.</p></figcaption></figure><p>Our direct imaging of a spreading extracellular <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> increase shown in <a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">Figure 5</a> provides a strong supporting element for the <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-assisted <a href="#def3" class="usa-link">CICR</a> model of wave propagation. The dynamics of a <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> wave have been largely inferred from, for example, the timing and spatial patterning of the activation of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-responsive promoters (<a href="#bib34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib34">Miller et al., 2009</a>). However, we have now been able to show that an extracellular <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> increase accompanied the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave moving at approximately 400 µm s<sup>−1</sup>, suggesting a propagating <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> wave. This <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> wave is dependent on AtRBOHD and TPC1, consistent with the <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-assisted Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave model where <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> trigger Ca<sup>2+</sup> increases that then propagate and trigger further distal <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> responses. It is important to note here that although this wave of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> increase appears to be moving at approximately the same velocity as the Ca<sup>2+</sup> increase, this measurement of rate must be interpreted with caution. The OxyBurst Green-<a href="#def6" class="usa-link">BSA</a> sensor is designed to be excluded from the cell wall. Thus, this imaging technique relies on the diffusion of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> from the surface of the root to the medium and so likely incorporates a delay in appearance as the <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> move through the apoplast and accumulate in the medium. This caveat is especially relevant as the NaCl-triggered Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave, and by implication the <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-related events associated with it, are initiated at the cortex and endodermis, so <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> would need to transit the epidermal apoplast to begin to leave the root. Therefore, although we can say that an extracellular <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> increase accompanies the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave, defining whether the <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> appear before or concurrent with the Ca<sup>2+</sup> increases must await the development of improved extracellular <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> imaging technology. However, the inhibition of the propagation of the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave by treatment with <a href="#def5" class="usa-link">DPI</a> or ascorbate (<a href="#fig3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3</a>) and in the <em>atrbohD</em> mutant (<a href="#fig4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4</a>) suggests that the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave is at least dependent on <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> production. The residual wave transmission seen with <a href="#def5" class="usa-link">DPI</a> could reflect an incomplete inhibition of RBOH activity by this pharmacological agent or the action of a parallel, <a href="#def5" class="usa-link">DPI</a>-resistant <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-generating mechanism as part of the wave propagation mechanism.</p>
<p>Our combination of mathematical modeling and experimental analysis supports the following model for the propagation of the systemic signal in response to salt. Locally, the application of salt triggers the movement of ions between various compartments in the cell, and in particular, the concentration of Ca<sup>2+</sup> in the cytoplasm increases. This signal leads to the activation of RBOHD via its EF hand domains and via phosphorylation by Ca<sup>2+</sup>-binding kinases (<a href="#bib14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib14">Dubiella et al., 2013</a>). As illustrated in <a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">Figure 6</a>, local production of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> by RBOHD is predicted to activate plasma membrane Ca<sup>2+</sup> channels. <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-sensitive Ca<sup>2+</sup> channel activities have been monitored at the electrophysiological level in Arabidopsis root cells (<a href="#bib17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib17">Foreman et al., 2003</a>; <a href="#bib12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib12">Demidchik and Maathuis, 2007</a>; <a href="#bib40" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib40">Ordoñez et al., 2014</a>), and <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-responsive candidates such as the annexins (<a href="#bib48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib48">Richards et al., 2014</a>) have been identified at the molecular level, although their possible roles in Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave transmission remain to be explored. This Ca<sup>2+</sup> contributes to the activation of TPC1, resulting, directly or indirectly, in the release of more Ca<sup>2+</sup> from the vacuole. <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> and Ca<sup>2+</sup> diffuse within the apoplast and cytoplasm, respectively, activating neighboring channels until all the involved signaling proteins within a cell have been activated. The signaling molecules are able to diffuse between cells, <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> through the apoplast and Ca<sup>2+</sup> through the plasmodesmata, where signaling in the next cell is activated. In the TPC1 overexpressor, the quantity of TPC1 channels is increased (<a href="#bib43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib43">Peiter et al., 2005</a>), resulting in more Ca<sup>2+</sup> being released and faster activation of RBOHDs and, therefore, a faster wave. Within the <em>tpc1-2</em> mutant background, RBOHD and the plasma membrane Ca<sup>2+</sup> channel form a linked propagation system. The observation that high concentrations of ascorbate can abolish the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave suggests that Ca<sup>2+</sup> alone is insufficient to propagate the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave; therefore, in the <em>rbohD</em> mutant, it is possible that other sources of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> act to give rise to the lower velocity wave.</p>
<p>While the modeling work presented here assumes <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> propagation through the apoplast, we cannot exclude the possibility that Ca<sup>2+</sup> diffusing through the plasmodesmata is responsible for transmitting the signal between adjacent cells, or indeed that the two species act in tandem (<a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">Fig. 6</a>). A TPC1-mediated <a href="#def3" class="usa-link">CICR</a> wave acting alone seems unlikely, and that motivated us to look at <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> propagation, but the requirement for a <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-activated plasma membrane Ca<sup>2+</sup> channel means there also could be sources of Ca<sup>2+</sup> influx within the plasmodesmatal plasma membrane itself that could contribute to a rapid transmission event. Unfortunately, the resolution of current imaging techniques is unable to distinguish these possibilities.</p>
<p><em>TPC1</em> and <em>AtRBOHD</em> are expressed ubiquitously throughout the plant, so their distribution does not provide an obvious explanation for why the salt-induced Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave preferentially transits through the cortical and endodermal cell layers. Similarly, candidates for the putative <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-activated Ca<sup>2+</sup>-release channels, such as annexins and SKOR (<a href="#bib18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib18">Garcia-Mata et al., 2010</a>; <a href="#bib48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib48">Richards et al., 2014</a>), also are expressed throughout the root (<a href="#bib6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib6">Birnbaum et al., 2003</a>). Thus, identifying the <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-regulated channels involved with this signaling process, as well as other components that provide regulatory roles, represents a key challenge for the future that may provide important insights into the tissue-specific pattern of the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave.</p></section><section id="s8"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">MATERIALS AND METHODS</h2>
<section id="s9"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Plant Growth</h3>
<p>Arabidopsis (<em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em>) ecotype Columbia-0 (<a href="#def7" class="usa-link">Col-0</a>), mutants in <em>AtRBOHD</em> and <em>TPC1</em> in the <a href="#def7" class="usa-link">Col-0</a> background, and the <em>TPC1</em> overexpression line (in the <a href="#def7" class="usa-link">Col-0</a> background) were germinated and grown under sterile conditions on a thin layer of gel (approximately 2 mm thick) containing one-half-strength Epstein’s medium with 10 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> Suc and 0.5% (w/v) Phytagel (Sigma-Aldrich) under 16 h of light/8 h of dark at 22°C for 6 d as described previously (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Choi et al., 2014</a>). <em>AtrbohD</em> was kindly provided by Dr. Andrew Bent (University of Wisconsin-Madison), and <em>oxTPC1</em> and <em>tpc1-2</em> were provided by Edgar Peiter (Martin-Luther-Universität) and Dale Sanders (John Innes Centre) and transformed with Yellow Cameleon Nano-65 GFP Ca<sup>2+</sup> bioreporter as reported previously (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Choi et al., 2014</a>).</p></section><section id="s10"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Ratio Imaging</h3>
<p>Confocal ratio imaging was performed as described by <a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Choi et al. (2014)</a>. Briefly, 7-d-old seedlings were grown as described above and imaged using a 20×/0.75 Plan-Apochromat objective on a Zeiss LSM510 confocal microscope, with 458-nm excitation, 458-nm primary dichroic, and 462- to 505-nm (CFP) and 526- to 537-nm (cpVenus/FRET) emission selected using the microscope’s Meta detector. Images were analyzed using the Image Calculator in the FIJI analysis package (<a href="#bib67" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib67">Schindelin et al., 2012</a>), and ratio data from the sequential confocal images were converted to a pseudocolored kymogram using MultiExperiment Viewer software version 10.2 (<a href="#bib50" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib50">Saeed et al., 2003</a>). Due to uncertainties in the applicability of in vitro calibration of this reporter to in vivo responsiveness, data are presented as raw ratios of YFP (cpVenus) to CFP signal intensities. Increasing YFP-CFP ratio signal represents an increase in Ca<sup>2+</sup> levels.</p></section><section id="s11"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Measuring Extracellular <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> with OxyBurst Green-<a href="#def6" class="usa-link">BSA</a>
</h3>
<p>Extracellular <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> production was measured essentially as described by <a href="#bib37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib37">Monshausen et al. (2009)</a>. A total of 200 µg mL<sup>−1</sup> OxyBurst Green (dihydro-2′,4,5,6,7,7′-hexafluorofluorescein)-<a href="#def6" class="usa-link">BSA</a> (Life Technologies) dissolved in growth medium was added to the root approximately 10 min prior to treatment at the root tip. A ROI of 50 × 50 µm was then imaged either 3,000 or 5,000 µm from the root tip using a 20×/0.75 Plan-Apochromat objective on a Zeiss LSM780 confocal microscope, using 458-nm primary dichroic, 458-nm excitation, and 526- to 537-nm emission using the microscope’s Meta detector.</p></section><section id="s12"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Inhibitor Treatments</h3>
<p>For inhibitor treatments, a small window (approximately 500 μm × 500 μm) was made in the gel in the middle region of the root, shootward of the root tip, using the tip of a syringe needle as a knife to carefully remove the gel using a dissecting microscope. Ten microliters of 25 to 200 μ<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span>
<a href="#def5" class="usa-link">DPI</a> (NADPH oxidase inhibitor) or potassium ascorbate (<a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ROS</a> scavenger) made up in one-half-strength Epstein’s medium with 10 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> Suc was added to the gel window 30 min prior to salt treatment of the root tip. Control plants were treated similarly, but with 25 μL of one-half-strength Epstein’s medium with 10 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> Suc in the window. To prevent the gel from drying out, these samples were kept in a humid petri dish prior to confocal imaging.</p></section><section id="s13"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Total RNA Isolation and Quantitative Real-Time PCR Analysis</h3>
<p>For <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">QPCR</a> analysis, root and shoot samples of 6-d-old seedlings of wild-type <a href="#def7" class="usa-link">Col-0</a> and <em>oxTPC1</em> were harvested in liquid nitrogen. Total RNA was then isolated from 50- to 200-mg tissue samples using the RNeasy Plant Mini Kit (Qiagen) followed by removing residual genomic DNA using the TURBO DNase Kit (RNase-free DNase I; Ambion) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Total RNA (1–2 μg) was reverse transcribed into first-strand complementary DNA in a 40-µL reaction (25–50 ng total RNA µL<sup>−1</sup>) with ProtoScript II reverse transcriptase (New England Biolabs). <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">QPCR</a> analysis was done using an Applied Biosystems 7500 Real Time PCR system, and analysis was performed with the 7500 Software version 2.3 (Applied Biosystems). The Arabidopsis <em>UBQ10</em> gene was used as an internal reference for standardization as described previously (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Choi et al., 2014</a>). First-strand complementary DNA proportional to 10 ng of genomic DNA-free starting total RNA was combined with 200 n<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> of each primer and 7.5 µL of 2× EvaGreen <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">QPCR</a> Master Mix with ROX passive reference dye (Biotium) in a final volume of 15 µL. <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">QPCR</a> was performed on a 96-well optical PCR plate (ABgene) using the following parameters: one cycle of 15 min at 95°C; 40 cycles of 20 s at 95°C, 30 s at 58°C, and 15 s at 65°C; and one cycle of dissociation from 58°C to 95°C with 0.5°C increments. Quantitation of the expression of <em>AtTPC1</em> was calculated using the comparative threshold cycle method as described previously (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Choi et al., 2014</a>). The <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">QPCR</a> primers used are AtUBQ10-qPCR-F (<em>UBQ10</em> forward primer, 5′-CACACTCCACTTGGTCTTGCGT-3′), AtUBQ10-qPCR-R (<em>UBQ10</em> reverse primer, 5′-TGGTCTTTCCGGTGAGAGTCTTCA-3′), AtTPC1-qPCR-F(i) (<em>TPC1</em> forward primer, 5′-GCTCTATTGGCGTACAGGTCTTTG-3′), and AtTPC1-qPCR-R(j) (<em>TPC1</em> reverse primer, 5′-GAAGAGTGTGACCATTCCATTGG-3′).</p></section><section id="s14"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Mathematical Modeling</h3>
<p>A full simulation of the <a href="#def3" class="usa-link">CICR</a> model, illustrated in <a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Figure 1A</a>, uses Equation 1 assuming a fast Ca<sup>2+</sup> release.
