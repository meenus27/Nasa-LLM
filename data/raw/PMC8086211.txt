<p>Whole-genome sequences were generated from 96 bacterial strains of 14 species that were isolated from International Space Station surfaces during the Microbial Tracking 2 study. Continued characterization of this closed habitat's microbiome enables tracking of the spread and evolution of secondary pathogens, which is vital for astronaut health.</p></section><section id="s1"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">ANNOUNCEMENT</h2>
<p>The International Space Station (ISS) is currently the only long-term human habitat in space. Microgravity disrupts human immune function (<a href="#B1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B1">1</a>), and close monitoring of the ISS microbiome for increased pathogenicity is thus an ongoing critical task. Here, we report the draft genomes of 96 bacterial strains that were isolated from the ISS (<a href="#tab1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>). Eleven of the 14 species found are common members of the human microbiome, and most can act as opportunistic human pathogens.</p>

</div></section><p>Four species from the phylum <em>Actinobacteria</em> were isolated, from the genera <em>Pseudoclavibacter</em>, <em>Kocuria</em>, and <em>Micrococcus</em>, all of which are common in both environmental and human microbiomes (<a href="#B2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B2">2</a><a href="#B3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B3">–</a><a href="#B5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B5">5</a>). <em>Kocuria palustris</em> and <em>Micrococcus luteus</em> are opportunistic pathogens (<a href="#B5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B5">5</a>, <a href="#B6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B6">6</a>). <em>M. luteus</em> can survive in a dormant state under extreme oligotrophic conditions (<a href="#B7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B7">7</a>) and has increased growth and increased biomass yield in microgravity (<a href="#B8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B8">8</a>).</p>
<p>Multiple coagulase-negative staphylococci (CoNS) were also isolated. CoNS are normal components of human skin flora (<a href="#B9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B9">9</a>) and are often resistant to antibiotics because of their ability to form biofilms (<a href="#B10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B10">10</a>); all CoNS in this report are documented opportunistic pathogens (<a href="#B11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B11">11</a><a href="#B12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B12">–</a><a href="#B15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B15">15</a>).</p>
<p>Three species from the phylum <em>Proteobacteria</em> were identified. <em>Acinetobacter pittii</em> is a less common nosocomial pathogen that causes pneumonia and meningitis in intensive care patients (<a href="#B16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B16">16</a>), <em>Pseudomonas fulva</em> is a commensal plant endophyte (<a href="#B17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B17">17</a>) that can also infect immunocompromised patients (<a href="#B18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B18">18</a>, <a href="#B19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B19">19</a>), and <em>Pseudomonas granadensis</em> is a recently discovered soil bacterium (<a href="#B20" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B20">20</a>).</p>
<p>Two other species in this report that are not associated with the human microbiome are <em>Cytobacillus horneckiae</em>, a Gram-positive UV-resistant endospore-former that was isolated from a clean room at the Kennedy Space Center (<a href="#B21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B21">21</a>), and <em>Methylobacterium organophilum</em>, a facultative methylotroph (<a href="#B22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B22">22</a>).</p>
<p>All strains reported here were collected aboard the ISS over the course of five flight missions between June 2017 and December 2018 (<a href="#tab1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>). Premoistened polyester wipes were used to collect samples from eight predetermined surfaces during each flight (see <a href="#tab1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a> for locations). After transport to Earth, the wipes were agitated in sterile phosphate-buffered saline, and the buffer was concentrated with an InnovaPrep CP-150 concentrator. Concentrates were plated onto Reasoner’s 2A (R2A) agar (25°C for 7 days), potato dextrose agar (PDA) (25°C for 7 days), and blood agar (37°C for 2 days) using appropriate concentrations for microbial isolation. Isolated colonies were restreaked on tryptic soy agar (TSA) (25°C), and genomic DNA was extracted using the ZymoBIOMICS DNA MagBead kit according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Libraries for whole-genome shotgun sequencing (WGS) were prepared using the Illumina Nextera DNA Flex library preparation kit as in previous studies (<a href="#B23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B23">23</a>) and were sequenced using the NovaSeq 6000 S4 flow cell paired-end 2 × 150-bp platform.</p>
<p>Sequencing reads were quality filtered and trimmed, and adapter sequences were removed, using FastQC v0.11.7 (<a href="#B24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B24">24</a>) and fastp v0.20.0 (<a href="#B25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B25">25</a>). Scaffolds were assembled with SPAdes v3.11.1 (<a href="#B26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B26">26</a>). QUAST v5.0.2 (<a href="#B27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B27">27</a>) was used to determine assembly quality, including the number of contigs, genome size, and <em>N</em><sub>50</sub> value. Default settings were used for all steps except for fastp, which included 512 adapters screening. OrthoANIu (<a href="#B28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B28">28</a>) was used to confirm the species identity for each strain against the species type strain sequence, with a minimum average nucleotide identity of 95% for identification. Genomes were annotated using the NCBI Prokaryotic Genome Annotation Pipeline (<a href="#B29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B29">29</a>).</p>
