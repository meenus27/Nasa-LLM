</div></section></section><section aria-label="Article content"><section class="body main-article-body"><section class="abstract" id="abstract1"><h2>Abstract</h2>
<p>Auxin is transported in plants with distinct polarity, defined by transport proteins of the PIN-formed (PIN) family. Components of the complex trafficking machinery responsible for polar PIN protein localization have been identified by genetic approaches, but severe developmental phenotypes of trafficking mutants complicate dissection of this pathway. We utilized a temperature sensitive allele of <em>Arabidopsis thaliana SCD1</em> (<em>stomatal cytokinesis defective1</em>) that encodes a RAB-guanine nucleotide exchange factor. Auxin transport, lateral root initiation, asymmetric auxin-induced gene expression after gravitropic reorientation, and differential gravitropic growth were reduced in the roots of the <em>scd1-1</em> mutant relative to wild type at the restrictive temperature of 25°C, but not at the permissive temperature of 18°C. In <em>scd1-1</em> at 25°C, PIN1- and PIN2-GFP accumulated in endomembrane bodies. Transition of seedlings from 18 to 25°C for as little as 20 min resulted in the accumulation of PIN2-GFP in endomembranes, while gravitropism and root developmental defects were not detected until hours after transition to the non-permissive temperature. The endomembrane compartments that accumulated PIN2-GFP in <em>scd1-1</em> exhibited FM4-64 signal colocalized with ARA7 and ARA6 fluorescent marker proteins, consistent with PIN2 accumulation in the late or multivesicular endosome. These experiments illustrate the power of using a temperature sensitive mutation in the gene encoding SCD1 to study the trafficking of PIN2 between the endosome and the plasma membrane. Using the conditional feature of this mutation, we show that altered trafficking of PIN2 precedes altered auxin transport and defects in gravitropism and lateral root development in this mutant upon transition to the restrictive temperature.</p>
<section id="kwd-group1" class="kwd-group"><p><strong>Keywords:</strong> SCD1, gravitropism, lateral root, auxin transport, PIN2, <em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em></p></section></section><section id="S1"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Introduction</h2>
<p>The process of auxin transport drives many aspects of plant development, including primary root elongation, lateral root development, and asymmetric growth in response to gravity and light gradients (<a href="#B49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B49">Muday and DeLong, 2001</a>; <a href="#B66" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B66">Vieten et al., 2007</a>; <a href="#B73" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B73">Zazimalova et al., 2010</a>). Auxin (indole-3-acetic acid; IAA) is transported long distances in two polarities in two root cell layers (<a href="#B49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B49">Muday and DeLong, 2001</a>). Cell to cell IAA transport is mediated by AUX/LAX influx carriers and by efflux carriers of the PIN and ABCB families (<a href="#B73" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B73">Zazimalova et al., 2010</a>). Directionality is controlled by asymmetric localization of PIN proteins at the plasma membrane (<a href="#B69" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B69">Wisniewska et al., 2006</a>; <a href="#B2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B2">Adamowski and Friml, 2015</a>). In <em>Arabidopsis</em> roots, PIN1 is localized to the stele and implicated in auxin transport toward the root apex (<a href="#B17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B17">Galweiler et al., 1998</a>), which is now called rootward transport and this auxin flow is required for lateral root development (<a href="#B58" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B58">Reed et al., 1998</a>; <a href="#B38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B38">Lavenus et al., 2013</a>). In contrast, PIN2 is localized to the epidermal and cortical cell layers of roots and mediates shootward transport from the root apex (<a href="#B51" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B51">Muller et al., 1998</a>), which is integral to root elongation and gravitropism (<a href="#B57" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B57">Rashotte et al., 2000</a>). PIN3 is localized to the columella cells at the root tip, where the angle of the gravity vector is perceived, and in the vascular cylinder, consistent with functions in gravitropism and root development, respectively (<a href="#B16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B16">Friml et al., 2002</a>; <a href="#B26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B26">Harrison and Masson, 2007</a>).</p>
<p>The polar distribution of the PIN proteins is dynamic (<a href="#B3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B3">Barbosa and Schwechheimer, 2014</a>; <a href="#B68" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B68">Weller et al., 2017</a>). Targeting of PIN proteins to the membrane in response to stimuli may be an important mechanism to allow changes in auxin transport and dependent growth processes (<a href="#B53" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B53">Paciorek et al., 2005</a>; <a href="#B3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B3">Barbosa and Schwechheimer, 2014</a>). PIN proteins travel through the endosome to the plasma membrane after <em>de novo</em> synthesis through a highly regulated process (<a href="#B31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B31">Jasik et al., 2013</a>, <a href="#B32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B32">2016</a>) involving the RAB and ARF families of small G proteins. GNOM, a well-studied ARF-GEF and target of the drug Brefeldin A, is critical in targeting of PIN1 and PIN2 between the endosome and the plasma membrane (<a href="#B21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B21">Geldner et al., 2001</a>, <a href="#B23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B23">2004</a>, <a href="#B20" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B20">2003</a>). Although genetic approaches have identified pathways for targeting PIN proteins (<a href="#B2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B2">Adamowski and Friml, 2015</a>), non-conditional mutants with impaired auxin transport, such as <em>gnom</em>, have profound developmental phenotypes (<a href="#B20" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B20">Geldner et al., 2003</a>). This complicates determination of whether altered auxin transport causes developmental phenotypes or the converse.</p>
<p>This study used a conditional <em>scd1-1</em> mutant to test whether auxin transport trafficking defects precede growth or developmental phenotypes. The SCD1 protein has a DENN domain, found exclusively in RAB-GEFs (<a href="#B72" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B72">Yoshimura et al., 2010</a>; <a href="#B46" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B46">Marat et al., 2011</a>). SCD1 and an associated protein, SCD2, have been shown to complex with a RabE1 at the exocyst in <em>Arabidopsis</em> roots (<a href="#B47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B47">Mayers et al., 2017</a>), where it plays a role in exocytosis and recycling of membrane proteins. Three <em>scd1</em> mutant alleles have been described: <em>scd1-1</em>, <em>scd1-2</em>, and <em>scd1-3</em> (<a href="#B14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B14">Falbel et al., 2003</a>). <em>scd1-2</em> and <em>scd1-3</em> are loss of function mutants with altered guard cell development, and profound root growth and developmental phenotypes. However, the temperature sensitive <em>scd1-1</em> allele, displays these phenotypes only at the restrictive temperature of 25°C (<a href="#B14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B14">Falbel et al., 2003</a>).</p>
<p>This study utilizes the unique features of a temperature sensitive <em>scd1-1</em> mutant allele to demonstrate that the growth and developmental effects in <em>Arabidopsis</em> roots at the restrictive temperature are tied to altered auxin transport protein targeting. The developmental and auxin transport defects can be induced by transition to the restrictive temperature and reversed by return to the permissive temperature. We report the effects of these treatments on polar auxin transport and dependent physiological processes including lateral root development, gravity response, and root elongation. At the restrictive temperature, PIN2 fused to GFP accumulates in endomembrane vesicles in <em>scd1</em>-1, which is consistent with a role of SCD1 in controlling PIN endomembrane targeting. Auxin transport and endomembrane accumulation of a PIN2 reporter were examined both in <em>scd1-1</em> grown continuously at the restrictive temperature or in seedlings transitioned briefly to this temperature. The endomembrane bodies in which the PIN2 fusion protein is localized also accumulate other proteins that are linked to the late multivesicular endosome and the endosome dye FM4-64 after an extended incubation. These results provide support for the hypothesis that SCD1 protein functions to regulate the movement of PIN2 to and from the plasma membrane and that these transient changes in localization occur more rapidly than changes in auxin transport and dependent developmental processes.</p></section><section id="S2"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Results</h2>
<section id="S2.SS1"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Root Elongation and Lateral Root Development Are Temperature-Sensitive in <em>scd1-1</em>
</h3>
<p>If <em>scd1-1</em> has a defect in targeting of auxin transport proteins, then alterations in auxin transport-dependent root responses, including primary root elongation, gravitropism, and lateral root development, would be expected. Root growth and development of <em>scd1-1</em> were compared to its parental line, Columbia <em>glabrous-1</em> [(Col(g)], at 18°C and at 25°C. At 18°C, Col(g) and <em>scd1-1</em> had similar primary root elongation rates and lateral root numbers (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Figure 1</a>). At 25°C <em>scd1-1</em> primary roots were substantially shorter than Col(g) (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Figure 1B</a>), and the number of lateral roots were dramatically reduced (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Figures 1B,C</a>). We performed a two-way ANOVA to determine which of these differences were statistically significant across this time course. In both root elongation and lateral root development, <em>scd1-1</em> was significantly impaired relative to Col(g) at both temperatures with <em>p</em> &lt; 0.01 for all comparisons.</p>

<p>Polar transport of the hormone auxin from cell to cell is controlled by the asymmetric localization of the PIN family of IAA efflux carriers (<a href="#B24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B24">Grunewald and Friml, 2010</a>; <a href="#B73" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B73">Zazimalova et al., 2010</a>; <a href="#B2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B2">Adamowski and Friml, 2015</a>). These carrier proteins are directed to specific plasma membrane domains through dynamically regulated membrane trafficking and recycling pathways (<a href="#B19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B19">Geldner, 2009</a>; <a href="#B24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B24">Grunewald and Friml, 2010</a>; <a href="#B3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B3">Barbosa and Schwechheimer, 2014</a>). Internal and external environmental cues also affect the localization of the PIN proteins through regulation of the flow of PIN proteins between the endosome and plasma membrane (<a href="#B53" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B53">Paciorek et al., 2005</a>; <a href="#B31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B31">Jasik et al., 2013</a>, <a href="#B32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B32">2016</a>; <a href="#B56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B56">Rakusova et al., 2015</a>), as well as between the plasma membrane and the lytic vacuole (<a href="#B34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B34">Kleine-Vehn et al., 2008a</a>).</p>
<p>In this study we utilized a temperature-sensitive <em>scd1-1</em> mutant to uncover the dynamics of auxin transport targeting and how these reversible changes in protein targeting control auxin transport and dependent process. SCD1 contains a DENN domain found in proteins with RAB-directed GEF activity and in proteins that interact with clathrin (<a href="#B55" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B55">Patino-Lopez et al., 2008</a>; <a href="#B60" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B60">Sato et al., 2008</a>; <a href="#B48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B48">McMichael et al., 2013</a>). <em>SCD1</em> null mutants have revealed the function of this protein as a RAB-GEF that participates in both endocytosis and exocytosis (<a href="#B47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B47">Mayers et al., 2017</a>).</p>
<p>The effects of the temperature-sensitive <em>scd1-1</em> mutant on root development was examined at the permissive and restrictive temperatures of 18 and 25°C, respectively. We found that <em>scd1-1</em> exhibits a suite of phenotypes only at restrictive temperature, which include impaired root elongation and gravity response, reduced lateral root formation, and inhibition of formation of asymmetric auxin-induced gene expression across a gravity-stimulated root. These phenotypes were also induced when plants were grown at the permissive temperature and then transitioned to the restrictive temperature and could be reversed when plants were transitioned from the restrictive temperature back to the permissive temperature. As all of these phenotypes are dependent on auxin transport, we also measured both shootward and rootward transport and found that both are impaired at the restrictive temperature.</p>
<p>The ability to reversibly manipulate lateral root development in the <em>scd1-1</em> mutant provided unique insight into how this process is regulated and revealed the critical time point when SCD1 and auxin transport are needed for lateral root development. A temperature-switch assay was performed resulting in the shift of seedlings from the permissive temperature to the non-permissive temperature or vice versa over 10 days. These data support the hypothesis that SCD1 is needed for lateral root development between days 2 and 6. Transition from the permissive to non-permissive temperature also reduces auxin transport in <em>scd1-1</em> (but not Col(g)) showing reductions in the long distance rootward transport within 8 h after transition. A previous report suggested that there is specific regulation of auxin transport in this time window with a pulse of auxin moving from the shoot to the root at 3–5 days, which correlated with the emergence of the first lateral root (<a href="#B5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B5">Bhalerao et al., 2002</a>). Our ability to reversibly alter auxin transport by turning off or on SCD1 function support the model of an auxin pulse that drives root formation that occurs within a precise developmental window.</p>
<p>The effects of the <em>scd1-1</em> mutation on auxin transport and gravitropism were evident after both long and short-term exposure to the non-permissive temperature, with changes in auxin transport evident preceding gravitropic differences. When grown continuously at 25°C or within 8 h after transfer from the permissive 18°C temperature, <em>scd1-1</em> seedlings showed reduced shootward <sup>3</sup>H-IAA transport and accumulation of auxin-induced gene expression at the root tip, consistent with impaired auxin transport away from the site of application. Similarly, continuous growth at 25°C or a shift from 18 to 25°C for 8 h showed a significant decrease in the gravitropic curvature in <em>scd1-1</em> compared to wild type. Auxin-induced gene expression, visualized via DR5-GFP fluorescence (<a href="#B50" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B50">Muday and Rahman, 2008</a>; <a href="#B18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B18">Geisler et al., 2014</a>) was rapidly enhanced on the lower side of Col(g) roots after gravitropic reorientation, but was impaired in <em>scd1-1</em> seedlings relative to wild type only in plants grown at 25°C. These data support the hypothesis that a functional SCD1 protein is needed to maintain polar and lateral auxin transport and the physiological processes dependent upon dynamic changes in appropriate auxin distribution. Consistent with this model, altered PIN2-GFP trafficking is even more rapidly perturbed in <em>scd1-1</em> than auxin movements and gravity response.</p>
<p>Because PIN2 is required for rootward transport and root gravitropism (<a href="#B51" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B51">Muller et al., 1998</a>; <a href="#B57" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B57">Rashotte et al., 2000</a>; <a href="#B7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B7">Chen et al., 2002</a>), we compared its localization in the <em>scd1-1</em> mutant and wild type using a PIN2-GFP reporter. At 25°C, PIN2 is normally localized to the plasma membrane on the shootward end of cells of the lateral root cap and epidermis of the developing root of wild type seedlings (<a href="#B51" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B51">Muller et al., 1998</a>). In the <em>scd1-1</em> mutant, PIN2-GFP was not restricted to the plasma membrane but was also localized in endomembrane bodies. <a href="#B47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B47">Mayers et al. (2017)</a> showed that <em>scd1-2</em> has altered PIN2-GFP trafficking during treatment with brefeldin A, a drug which blocks intracellular endosome and trans-Golgi transport. However, we find that in <em>scd1-1</em> at the restrictive temperature, PIN2-GFP accumulates in the absence of BFA.</p>
<p>The trafficking defect is not detectable in <em>scd1-1</em> roots grown at 18°C, but when switched from 18 to 25°C, the PIN2 trafficking defect becomes apparent within minutes. Since changes in auxin transport and root growth and development are not observed for 8–12 h, this suggests that these responses are sequential and that PIN mislocalization is not a secondary result of compromised root development at 25°C. The linkage is most evident in terms of gravity response. PIN2 controls shootward auxin transport that is required for gravitropism (<a href="#B51" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B51">Muller et al., 1998</a>; <a href="#B57" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B57">Rashotte et al., 2000</a>; <a href="#B7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B7">Chen et al., 2002</a>). Upon transition of <em>scd1-1</em> to the non-permissive temperature, PIN2-GFP endomembrane accumulation is evident with less than hour. In Col(g) a transition from 18 to 25°C increases shootward transport by more than fivefold within 5 h, leading to a more than twofold greater transport in Col(g) than in <em>scd1-1</em>. Treatment of roots for 12 h at the non-permissive temperature impaired gravity response in <em>scd1-1</em> to levels consistent with constitutive growth at this non-permissive temperature. Together these results support the timeline where PIN2 targeting is impaired, reducing auxin transport and then dependent physiological processes.</p>
<p>We also detected l PIN1-GFP within endomembrane bodies in <em>scd1-1</em>. However, other fluorescent fusions to the auxin transport proteins PIN3, ABCB19, and AUX1, were not observed in any endomembrane compartments. As all of these proteins are trafficked and recycled, and the <em>scd1-1</em> mutation most strongly affects PIN1 and PIN2 and dependent physiological processes, there appears to be selectivity of SCD1 targets toward PIN2 and PIN1, which is consistent with other studies that reveal distinct regulatory and trafficking machinery between auxin transporters (<a href="#B3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B3">Barbosa and Schwechheimer, 2014</a>; <a href="#B68" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B68">Weller et al., 2017</a>). Other reports have indicated that ABCB19 and AUX1 localization are regulated by different trafficking mechanisms than those that regulate PIN1 and PIN2 (<a href="#B36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B36">Kleine-Vehn et al., 2006</a>; <a href="#B64" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B64">Titapiwatanakun et al., 2008</a>). The results are based on differences in the trafficking of these proteins in response to brefeldin A and FM4-64 uptake (<a href="#B64" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B64">Titapiwatanakun et al., 2008</a>).</p>
<p>We find that in <em>scd1-1</em> at 25°C, PIN2-GFP accumulates in the late or multivesicular endosome. Impaired uptake of the endosomal marker dye FM4-64 has previously been observed in <em>scd1-1</em>, suggesting that SCD1 participates in endocytosis via clathrin coated vesicles (<a href="#B48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B48">McMichael et al., 2013</a>). To determine whether FM4-64 accumulates in the PIN2 endomembrane bodies observed here, the roots of <em>scd1-1</em> containing PIN2-GFP grown at 18°C and transferred to 25°C were treated with FM4-64. Although initially there was little co-localization, within 20 min significant overlap was observed. These results are consistent with PIN2 being trapped in <em>scd1</em> mutants in a late or multivesicular endosome or pre-vacuolar compartment on its way to or from the plasma membrane through the endosome.</p>
<p>To confirm that the endomembranes in which PIN2-GFP accumulates in <em>scd1-1</em> are late endosomes, we crossed multiple endosomal markers into this mutant and performed colocalization analyses. In <em>scd1-1</em> at 25°C, but not 18°C, we observed PIN2-GFP colocalization with ARA7 and to a lesser extent with ARA6. ARA6 and ARA7 are RAB5 GTPases contained in late multivesicular endosomes, with related, but not identical trafficking activities (<a href="#B65" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B65">Ueda et al., 2004</a>; <a href="#B4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B4">Beck et al., 2012</a>; <a href="#B13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B13">Ebine et al., 2012</a>; <a href="#B54" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B54">Paez Valencia et al., 2016</a>). ARA7 function is required for vesicle transport between the pre-vacuolar compartment (PVC) and the vacuole (<a href="#B39" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B39">Lee et al., 2004</a>; <a href="#B25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B25">Haas et al., 2007</a>; <a href="#B33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B33">Jia et al., 2013</a>) and ARA6 is associated with transport between the PVC and the plasma membrane, where it may regulate trafficking from the endosome to the plasma membrane (<a href="#B12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B12">Ebine et al., 2011</a>, <a href="#B13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B13">2012</a>). PIN2 has also been localized to ARA7 vesicles in <em>Arabidopsis</em> roots after treatment with endosidin 16, which inhibits apical membrane trafficking (<a href="#B43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B43">Li et al., 2017</a>).</p>
<p>The colocalization of PIN2 with both ARA7 and ARA6 Rab-related GTPases is not surprising considering that compartments bearing ARA7 and ARA6 have been previously shown to partially overlap, despite evidence that they are distinct endosomal compartments (<a href="#B65" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B65">Ueda et al., 2004</a>; <a href="#B4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B4">Beck et al., 2012</a>; <a href="#B13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B13">Ebine et al., 2012</a>). The PVC, where both ARA6 and ARA7 are found, is a distinct organelle in plants that facilitates the transport of materials from the endosomes to the vacuole (<a href="#B8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B8">Contento and Bassham, 2012</a>). Using immunogold and confocal microscopy, plasma membrane proteins that pass through the PVC have been shown to be directed to the vacuole for degradation (<a href="#B25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B25">Haas et al., 2007</a>; <a href="#B67" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B67">Viotti et al., 2010</a>). Thus, association of PIN2 with the PVC in <em>scd1-1</em> suggests that PIN2 in the PVC is likely targeted to the vacuole for degradation. This could explain the reduced domain of PIN2-GFP staining seen in the mutants at the restrictive temperature, coincident with the changes in columella cells. However, there is also evidence that suggests that PIN2 can be retrieved from the PVC and recycled (<a href="#B35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B35">Kleine-Vehn et al., 2008b</a>; <a href="#B61" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B61">Spitzer et al., 2009</a>). Indeed, PIN1, PIN2, and AUX1 are targeted to the vacuole through the multi-vesicular body (MVB), and RabF2A co-localizes in the MVB with PIN1-GFP (<a href="#B61" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B61">Spitzer et al., 2009</a>).</p>
<p>The localization of PIN2-GFP in late endosomal compartments in <em>scd1-1</em> is consistent with reports that PIN protein turnover involves both cycling between the endosome and plasma membrane and vacuolar degradation, depending on environmental conditions (<a href="#B53" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B53">Paciorek et al., 2005</a>; <a href="#B35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B35">Kleine-Vehn et al., 2008b</a>; <a href="#B24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B24">Grunewald and Friml, 2010</a>; <a href="#B31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B31">Jasik et al., 2013</a>). For example, when roots are reoriented relative to the gravity vector, enhanced endocytosis of PIN2 at the apical membrane followed by degradation through sorting to a proteolytic endosomal compartment resulted in asymmetric distribution of PIN2, redirected auxin flow, and root bending (<a href="#B1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B1">Abas et al., 2006</a>). The mis-localization and loss of membrane PIN2 in <em>scd1-1</em> explains the observed reductions in auxin transport and the observed phenotypes.</p>
<p>The efficient targeting of auxin transport proteins to the plasma membrane is required for appropriate auxin transport and dependent physiological processes. In this paper we have shown that the conditional <em>scd1-1</em> mutant, with a defect in a gene that functions as a RAB-GEF (<a href="#B47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B47">Mayers et al., 2017</a>), has altered localization of PIN2, with accumulation of this protein in endomembrane bodies. The PIN2-GFP containing endomembrane bodies in <em>scd1-1</em> showed dye accumulation and fluorescent protein reporter expression consistent with a late endosomal/prevacuolar character. Consistent with this altered protein localization, polar auxin transport and gravity response are impaired in <em>scd1-1</em>. The kinetics of the alterations in these responses upon transition to the non-permissive temperature demonstrate that altered trafficking of PIN2 is observed within minutes of transition to this temperature, while auxin transport and gravity defects are measured hours later, and root developmental defects can be quantified days after transition. These findings provide direct evidence that disruption of the targeting machinery which disrupts PIN2 protein localization precedes reductions in auxin transport and changes in auxin transport-dependent physiological processes.</p></section><section id="S4"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Materials and Methods</h2>
<section id="S4.SS1"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Growth of Mutant and Transgenic Seedlings</h3>
<p>Seeds were sterilized (75% ethanol, 0.01% Triton X-100) for 2 min, followed by a 95% ethanol rinse. The culture medium was 0.8% (w/v) agar (MP Biomedical), 1X MS nutrients (macro and micro salts: MSP01-50LT, Caisson Labs, Inc.), vitamins (1 μg/mL<sup>–1</sup> thiamine, 1 μg/mL<sup>–1</sup> pyridoxine HCL, and 0.5 μg/mL<sup>–1</sup> nicotinic acid), 1.5% sucrose (w/v), and 0.05% MES (w/v); pH adjusted to 6.0 with 1M KOH. The seeds were placed in the dark at 4°C for 48 h before being moved to the permissive (18°C) or the restrictive (25°C) temperatures. The seedlings were grown vertically under continuous light at an intensity of 100 μmol m<sup>–2</sup>s<sup>–1</sup> for 5 or 7 days at the specified temperature.</p>
<p>The Arabidopsis <em>scd1-1</em> mutant (<a href="#B14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B14">Falbel et al., 2003</a>) was crossed with a number of fluorescent protein fusions all driven by their endogenous promoters: PIN2-GFP (<a href="#B71" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B71">Xu and Scheres, 2005</a>), PIN1-GFP (<a href="#B27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B27">Heisler et al., 2005</a>), PIN3-GFP (<a href="#B37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B37">Laskowski et al., 2008</a>), MDR1(ABCB19)-GFP (<a href="#B70" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B70">Wu et al., 2007</a>), AUX1-YFP (<a href="#B63" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B63">Swarup et al., 2008</a>), and the auxin responsive promoter driven GFP reporter, DR5-GFP (<a href="#B52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B52">Ottenschläger et al., 2003</a>; <a href="#B42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B42">Lewis et al., 2011</a>). The F2 generation was screened for lines homozygous for mutant phenotype and for reporter GFP expression. <em>scd1-1</em> containing PIN2-GFP was also crossed with reporters that localize to specific endomembrane compartments, with these organelle specific reporters listed in <a href="#TS1" class="usa-link">Supplementary Table S1</a>.</p></section><section id="S4.SS2"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Characterization of <em>scd1-1</em> Root Length and Lateral Root Development</h3>
<p>Seedlings of similar size were placed on fresh agar plates on the 5th day after growth at 18 or 25°C. Lateral roots initiation events corresponding to stages VI and VII of primordia (<a href="#B44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B44">Malamy and Benfey, 1997</a>) and emerged lateral roots (defined as any root that broke through the epidermis of the primary root) were quantified using a dissecting microscope.</p>
<p>To examine lateral root formation in seedlings that were moved between temperature conditions, Col(g) and <em>scd1-1</em> seedlings were grown at 18 or 25°C continuously or transferred from 18 to 25°C at 2, 4, or 6 days. Parallel experiments were performed where seedlings were grown at 25°C continuously or transferred from 25 to 18°C at the indicated times. The number of lateral root initiation events and the number of emerged lateral roots were quantified by DIC microscopy using the Zeiss AxioObserver inverted microscope with a Plan Apochromatic 40x objective and the reported data is from 20 seedlings.</p></section><section id="S4.SS3"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">IAA Transport Assays</h3>
<p>Pulse-chase rootward (acropetal) IAA transport assays were performed using a previously published method (<a href="#B41" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B41">Lewis and Muday, 2009</a>) in Col(g) seedlings at 9 and 6 days at 18 and 25° C, respectively. <em>scd1-1</em> seedlings were assayed at 10 days for both 18°C and 25°C to more closely match the length of <em>scd1-1</em> and Col(g). An agar droplet (1.25% agar w/v) containing 1 μM tritiated IAA was placed at the root shoot junction and incubated at 18 or 25°C for 10 min. The seedlings were transferred to a new agar plate and exposed to a “chase” droplet of unlabeled 1 μM IAA. After 50 min, 2 mm sections were excised beginning at the base of the droplet toward the root tip. Three to six segments were recovered from all plants, and each segment was counted separately, and IAA levels quantitated using a Beckman LS6500 liquid scintillation counter. The average and SE of 19–25 seedlings is reported in all transport assays.</p>
<p>Shootward IAA transport was measured by applying 500 nM tritiated IAA (<sup>3</sup>H-IAA) to root tips (<a href="#B41" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B41">Lewis and Muday, 2009</a>) of 5 day seedlings grown at 18 or 25°C. For temperature switch experiments, seedlings were transferred to the appropriate temperature 4, 8, and 24 h before assay. Agar droplets containing 500 nM <sup>3</sup>H-IAA were applied to the root tips, and seedlings were placed into the indicated temperatures under yellow filters to prevent auxin degradation (<a href="#B62" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B62">Stasinopoulos and Hangarter, 1990</a>). After 2 h, the droplets were removed from the root tip. A 5 mm segment was excised 2 mm from the site of <sup>3</sup>H-IAA application, and radioactivity was measured.</p>
<p>Shootward IAA transport was also measured indirectly using the artificial DR5 promoter driving expression of green fluorescent protein (GFP) (<a href="#B52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B52">Ottenschläger et al., 2003</a>). Droplets containing 1 μM unlabeled IAA in 1.25% agar were applied to the root tips of 5-day old seedlings grown at 18 or 25°C for the designated times. GFP fluorescence was visualized using a Zeiss 710 LSCM (Jena, Germany) using the argon laser with excitation at 488 nm. Images of root tips were captured using 80% laser power with 40x magnification, a pinhole of 2.64 airy units (AU), 560 gain and an emission spectrum of 493-656 nm. Images were exported to Adobe Photoshop, and the distance of GFP fluorescence in the epidermal layers was measured using the line tool.</p></section><section id="S4.SS4"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Gravitropic Response Assays</h3>
<p>Five-day old Col(g) and <em>scd1-1</em> seedlings grown at 18°C were placed in diffuse light at 20–22 μmol m<sup>–2</sup> s<sup>–1</sup> and re-oriented 90°. Images were captured for low temporal resolution analysis at time 0 and at 2 h intervals until 8 h after re-orientation, with a final image 24 h after re-orientation. Images were captured using an Epson Perfection 3200 Photo Perfection flatbed scanner (Nagano, Japan) with 16-bit grayscale and 800 dpi resolution. Images were exported to ImageJ to measure degree of curvature.</p>
<p>The gravitropic root response was also observed using high temporal resolution imaging using an AVT Marlin camera (Stadtroda, Germany). Root curvature after gravity stimulation was measured by a MATLAB based custom image analysis software (<a href="#B40" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B40">Lewis et al., 2007</a>) and is the average and standard error of 12 seedlings of each genotype.</p></section><section id="S4.SS5"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Asymmetric Auxin Induced Gene Expression After Gravitropic Reorientation</h3>
<p>Col(g) and <em>scd1-1</em> seedlings crossed with the proDR5-GFP construct were imaged after roots were reoriented 90° relative to the gravity vector. Seedlings were placed on agar plates on the 5th day after growth at 18 or 25°C. After 1 h, seedlings were placed in diffuse light at 20–22 μmol m<sup>–2</sup>s<sup>–1</sup> and reoriented 90°. Images were captured at time 0 and at 2 h intervals until 8 h after re-orientation. Seedlings were stained with propidium iodide (PI) to visualize root structure and mounted in dH<sub>2</sub>0 on slides. The root tips were visualized with the 40x objective of a Zeiss 710 LSCM using the argon laser at an excitation wavelength of 488nm and a pinhole of 1.0 AU. GFP emission was collected between 492 and 547 nm, while PI was collected at 567–735. All images were collected at identical gain and laser power and exported to Adobe Photoshop for analysis. Representative images of more than 5 roots at each time point and from each genotype were shown.</p></section><section id="S4.SS6"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Localization of Auxin Transport Protein-Fluorescent Protein Fusions</h3>
<p>Col(g) and <em>scd1-1</em> seedlings containing PIN2-GFP, PIN1-GFP, PIN3-GFP, ABCB19-GFP, and AUX1-YFP (protein fusions driven by endogenous promoters) were grown for 7 days at a constant 18 or 25°C. Seedlings were mounted in dH<sub>2</sub>O and maintained at the indicated temperatures using a Julabo F25 temperature stage (Seelbach, Germany). Images were captured using a 63x water objective with or without a 1.5x optical zoom on a Zeiss 710 LSCM, using the argon laser for excitation at 488 nm with a pinhole of 1.0AU. PIN2-GFP, PIN1-GFP, PIN3-GFP, and MDR1-GFP were imaged with an emission spectrum of 500–525 nm, while AUX1-YFP was imaged with an emission spectrum of 519–621 nm.</p>
<p>For temperature shift experiments, wild-type and <em>scd1-1</em> were grown at 18°C for 7 days. Seedlings were transferred to microscope slides and then placed on the temperature-controlled stage at 25°C. Samples were imaged as described above at various times after transfer. For each reporter construct more than 5 roots were analyzed at each treatment, with representative images shown.</p></section><section id="S4.SS7"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">PIN2-GFP and FM4-64 Analysis</h3>
<p>Seedlings grown at 25°C were incubated in 5 μM FM4-64 to visualize endosomes (<a href="#B6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B6">Bolte et al., 2004</a>) for either 5 or 20 min, as indicated. The roots were rinsed in dH<sub>2</sub>O and imaged at various times after staining. The GFP and FM4-64 signals were visualized using a multi-track setting, as described above. No FM4-64 fluorescence was detected in the GFP channel. The FM4-64 signal was imaged using 80% laser power, a pinhole of 1.0 AU, 510 gain, and an emission spectrum of 632–759 nm. No GFP signal was detected in the FM4-64 channel. Individual images for each fluorophore and an overlay are shown with magenta for FM4-64, green for PIN2-GFP, and white for any pixels with both FM4-64 and PIN2. The red signal in these images was converted to magenta in Photoshop, changing the hue saturation. To better identify spatial colocalization independent of intensity, a thresholding function was applied to individual channels in Photoshop or in the Zeiss Zen colocalization module. We then prepared a thresholded overlay in which pixels are indicated as magenta for FM4-64, green for PIN2-GFP, and white for any pixels with both FM4-64 and GFP. To examine the change in colocalization of PIN2-GFP and FM4-64 over time, the number of PIN2-GFP, FM4-64, or both, signals in each cell with a PIN2-GFP labeled endomembrane compartment was quantified, and the percentage was determined for 740 cells from 20 roots. In the temperature shift experiments, <em>scd1-1</em> seedlings grown at 18°C for 7 days were transferred to 5 mM FM4-64 for 5 min, rinsed briefly, and then imaged at intervals of 2 min on a temperature-controlled stage at 25°C.</p></section><section id="S4.SS8"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Colocalization of PIN2 and Endosomal Markers</h3>
<p>Col(g) and <em>scd1-1</em> seedlings containing PIN2-GFP were crossed with fluorescently labeled endomembrane marker lines. These constructs and their localization patterns are summarized in <a href="#TS1" class="usa-link">Supplementary Table S1</a>. The seedlings were grown for 7 days at 25°C. Seedling roots were visualized using the Zeiss 710 laser scanning confocal microscope. GFP and RFP signals were resolved in dual channel mode with excitation at 488 and 594 nm, and emission wavelengths of 493–582 and 599–754 nm, respectively. For all samples, we verified the absence of RFP signal in the GFP channel and vice versa, using single-labeled samples. For colocalization analysis, samples were examined using Zeiss Zen software. The threshold for RFP or GFP was determined within each image, using regions that had only signal from one fluorophore. We selected regions of interest (ROI) of consistent size, focusing on individual endomembrane compartments (<a href="#B15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B15">French et al., 2008</a>) from at least 4 roots each of Ara6 and Ara7 reporters, measuring 21 and 17 endomembranes, respectively. Colocalization was quantified using both Pearson’s coefficients (weighted colocalization coefficients) and Manders’ overlap coefficients (<a href="#B45" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B45">Manders et al., 1993</a>; <a href="#B11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B11">Dunn et al., 2011</a>).</p>
<p>Lines containing both PIN2-GFP and other RFP reporters were examined in dual channel mode as described above. PIN2-GFP SYP61-CFP transgenics were imaged using spectral imaging and linear unmixing at an excitation wavelength of 458 nm and a 464–648 nm filter.</p></section></section><section id="S5"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Data Availability Statement</h2>
