<div class="ra xbox p" role="complementary" aria-label="Related or updated information about this article"><div>See commentary "<a href="/articles/PMC4036006/" class="usa-link">Plant salt stress status is transmitted systemically via propagating calcium waves</a>" on page 6126.</div></div>
</div></section></section><section aria-label="Article content"><section class="body main-article-body"><section class="abstract" id="abstract1"><h2>Significance</h2>
<p>This work documents a previously unreported plant-wide signaling system based on the rapid, long-distance transmission of Ca<sup>2+</sup> waves. In the root these waves move through the cortical and endodermal cell layers at speeds of up to 400 µm/s, i.e., traversing several cells per second. This Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave system correlates with the triggering of molecular responses in distant parts of the plant upon perception of localized (salt) stress. Such propagating Ca<sup>2+</sup> waves provide a new mechanism for the rapid integration of activities throughout the plant body.</p>
<section id="kwd-group1" class="kwd-group"><p><strong>Keywords:</strong> Ca<sup>2+</sup> signaling, Yellow Cameleon, Two Pore Channel 1</p></section></section><section class="abstract" id="abstract2"><h2>Abstract</h2>
<p>Their sessile lifestyle means that plants have to be exquisitely sensitive to their environment, integrating many signals to appropriate developmental and physiological responses. Stimuli ranging from wounding and pathogen attack to the distribution of water and nutrients in the soil are frequently presented in a localized manner but responses are often elicited throughout the plant. Such systemic signaling is thought to operate through the redistribution of a host of chemical regulators including peptides, RNAs, ions, metabolites, and hormones. However, there are hints of a much more rapid communication network that has been proposed to involve signals ranging from action and system potentials to reactive oxygen species. We now show that plants also possess a rapid stress signaling system based on Ca<sup>2+</sup> waves that propagate through the plant at rates of up to ∼400 µm/s. In the case of local salt stress to the <em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em> root, Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave propagation is channeled through the cortex and endodermal cell layers and this movement is dependent on the vacuolar ion channel TPC1. We also provide evidence that the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave/TPC1 system likely elicits systemic molecular responses in target organs and may contribute to whole-plant stress tolerance. These results suggest that, although plants do not have a nervous system, they do possess a sensory network that uses ion fluxes moving through defined cell types to rapidly transmit information between distant sites within the organism.</p></section><hr class="headless">
<p>Plants are constantly tailoring their responses to current environmental conditions via a complex array of chemical regulators that integrate developmental and physiological programs across the plant body. Environmental stimuli are often highly localized in nature, but the subsequent plant response is often elicited throughout the entire organism. For example, soil is a highly heterogeneous environment and the root encounters stimuli that are presented in a patchy manner. Thus, factors including dry or waterlogged regions of the soil, variations in the osmotic environment, and stresses such as elevated levels of salt are all likely to be encountered locally by individual root tips, but the information may have to be acted on by the plant as a whole.</p>
<p>In animals, long-range signaling to integrate activities across the organism occurs through rapid ionic/membrane potential-driven signaling through the nervous system in addition to operating via long-distance chemical signaling. Plants have also been proposed to possess a rapid, systemic communication network, potentially mediated through signals ranging from changes in membrane potential/ion fluxes (<a href="#r1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r1">1</a>–<a href="#r3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r3">3</a>) and levels of reactive oxygen species (ROS) (<a href="#r4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r4">4</a>, <a href="#r5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r5">5</a>) to altered hydraulics in the vasculature (<a href="#r6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r6">6</a>). Even so, the molecular mechanisms behind rapid, systemic signaling in plants and whether such signals indeed carry regulatory information remains largely unknown. Suggestions that Ca<sup>2+</sup> channels play a role in signals that occlude sieve tube elements (<a href="#r7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r7">7</a>), or that mediate systemic electrical signaling (<a href="#r2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r2">2</a>) in response to remote wounding, highlight Ca<sup>2+</sup>-dependent signaling events as a strong candidate for mediating some of these long-range responses. Similarly, cooling of roots elicits Ca<sup>2+</sup> increases in the shoot within minutes (<a href="#r8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r8">8</a>), suggesting systemic signals can elicit Ca<sup>2+</sup>-dependent responses at distal sites within the plant. However, despite extensive characterization of Ca<sup>2+</sup> signals (reviewed in ref. <a href="#r9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r9">9</a>), their roles in a possible plant-wide communication network remain poorly understood. Therefore, to visualize how Ca<sup>2+</sup> might act in local and systemic signaling, we generated <em>Arabidopsis</em> plants expressing the highly sensitive, GFP-based, cytoplasmic Ca<sup>2+</sup> sensor YCNano-65 (<a href="#r10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r10">10</a>). We observed that a range of abiotic stresses including H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, touch, NaCl, and cold shock triggered Ca<sup>2+</sup> increases at the point of application. However, NaCl also elicited a Ca<sup>2+</sup> increase that moved away from the point of stress application. Propagation of this Ca<sup>2+</sup> increase was associated with subsequent systemic changes in gene expression. We also report that this salt stress-induced long-distance Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave is dependent on the activity of the ion channel protein Two Pore Channel 1 (TPC1), which also appears to contribute to whole-plant stress tolerance.</p>
<section id="s1"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Results</h2>
<section id="s2"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Changes in Cytoplasmic Ca<sup>2+</sup> Occur in Response to Abiotic Stress.</h3>
<p><em>Arabidopsis</em> seedlings were grown in a thin layer of gel and a ∼500 µm × 500 µm region of the gel covering the root apex was removed to allow localized application of treatments to the root tip (<a href="#s10" class="usa-link"><em>Materials and Methods</em></a>). Consistent with previous studies (reviewed in ref. <a href="#r9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r9">9</a>), when a range of abiotic stresses including touch, cold, ROS (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>), and NaCl were locally applied to the root tip, we detected rapid and transient Ca<sup>2+</sup> increases at the site of treatment that showed stimulus-specific spatial/temporal characteristics (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S1</a> and <a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Movies S1–S4</a>). However, we observed that, among these treatments, NaCl treatment also led to a propagating Ca<sup>2+</sup> increase moving away from the point of stress application (<a href="#fig01" class="usa-link">Figs. 1</a> and <a href="#fig02" class="usa-link">2</a> and <a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/sm05.mov" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Movie S5</a>). Treatment with 200 mM sorbitol (osmotic control for the 100 mM NaCl stress) did not elicit Ca<sup>2+</sup> changes under these conditions (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S1<em>E</em></a>), suggesting such Ca<sup>2+</sup> changes were being specifically elicited by the salt stress.</p>

<p>Local application of salt stress to the root tip induced an increase in Ca<sup>2+</sup> that propagated shootward from the site of NaCl stimulation (<a href="#fig01" class="usa-link">Figs. 1</a> and <a href="#fig02" class="usa-link">2</a>). This salt stress-induced elevation in Ca<sup>2+</sup> was largely constrained to the cortical and endodermal cell layers (<a href="#fig01" class="usa-link">Fig. 1 <em>A</em> and <em>B</em></a>). Thus, changes were not seen in the epidermal cells even though this tissue is known to generate stimulus-evoked Ca<sup>2+</sup> elevations (e.g., ref. <a href="#r11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r11">11</a>). In addition, when a lateral root tip was stimulated, a Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave also moved through the cortex/endodermis, traveling to the main root and then bidirectionally rootward and shootward (<a href="#fig01" class="usa-link">Fig. 1<em>B</em></a> and <a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/sm06.mov" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Movie S6</a>). This Ca<sup>2+</sup> increase eventually traversed to the aerial part of the plant, moving through the hypocotyl (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF2" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S2</a> and <a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/sm07.mov" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Movie S7</a>). Quantitative analysis of the timing of Ca<sup>2+</sup> increase in various regions along the root (<a href="#fig02" class="usa-link">Fig. 2<em>A</em></a>) coupled with kymograph-based measurements of rates of Ca<sup>2+</sup> increase (<a href="#fig02" class="usa-link">Fig. 2<em>C</em></a>) indicated that the wave front of these Ca<sup>2+</sup> changes moved at a steady 395.7 ± 27.8 µm/s (±SD) (<a href="#fig02" class="usa-link">Fig. 2 <em>A</em> and <em>C</em></a>), taking ∼1 min to traverse the entire length of the root system and ∼2 min to spread across the entire length of the <em>Arabidopsis</em> seedlings used in this study.</p></section><section id="s4"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Root Tip Salt Stress Triggers Systemic Molecular Responses in Shoot Tissue.</h3>
<p>We next used a battery of molecular markers (<a href="#t01" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>) and quantitative real-time PCR (qPCR) analysis to ask whether the long-distance Ca<sup>2+</sup> increase we observed in response to this root-applied NaCl stress was accompanied by a systemic shoot response (<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">Fig. 3<em>A</em></a>). As expected, increased transcript levels of stress-related genes such as <em>RAB18</em>, <em>DREB2A</em>, <em>TCH2</em>, and <em>ZAT12</em>, and the salt stress response genes <em>SOS1</em> and <em>CBL10</em> (<a href="#r12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r12">12</a>) were detectable in the directly salt-stressed root tissues within 1–2 min of treatment (<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">Fig. 3</a> and <a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF3" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S3<em>A</em></a>). These rapidly induced genes showed subsequent oscillations in levels, sharing a fall at 10 min and subsequent increase over the 60 min of analysis (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF3" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S3<em>A</em></a>). However, local salt treatment of the root tip also led to an increase in transcript abundance of a number of stress markers in the distant aerial parts of the plant. For example, by 10 min <em>RAB18</em>, <em>DREB2A</em>, <em>TCH2</em>, <em>TCH3</em>, <em>PIP2;3</em>, <em>ZAT12</em>, <em>LOX3</em>, and <em>SID2</em> levels were significantly increased in the shoot (<em>P</em> &lt; 0.05, <em>t</em> test; <a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">Fig. 3<em>A</em></a> and <a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF3" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S3<em>B</em></a>). These genes generally showed a peak in transcript level at 10 min that had begun to fall by 30–60 min. Induction of markers such as <em>SOS1</em>, <em>SOS2/CIPK4</em>, <em>SOS3/CBL4</em>, and <em>CBL10</em> was significant only after 30 min. The vacuolar channel <em>TPC1</em> was notable in showing a rapid, transient transcript accumulation within 1 min in the shoot (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF3" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S3<em>C</em></a>). In contrast, NaCl treatment led to an equally rapid but sustained decrease in <em>TPC1</em> transcript levels in the salt-stressed roots.</p>

Therefore, the propagation of the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave and induction of systemic molecular responses are unlikely to reflect direct response to salt that had moved from the site of application either through the gel or within the plant.</p>
<p>To test whether the transcriptional changes elicited by local salt stress of the root required the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave, we asked whether pharmacological inhibitors that blocked transmission of the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave also blocked distal transcriptional responses. Local application of LaCl<sub>3</sub> (putative plasma-membrane Ca<sup>2+</sup>-channel blocker) or Ruthenium Red (RuRed) (putatively targeting internal Ca<sup>2+</sup> release) between the sites of stress application and Ca<sup>2+</sup> signal detection (<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4<em>A</em></a>) inhibited transmission of the Ca<sup>2+</sup> signal past this point (<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4</a>). However, if the NaCl stress was applied shootward of the LaCl<sub>3</sub> treatment, the propagation of the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave still moved shootward at 397.5 ± 42.1 μm/s (±SEM) (<em>P</em> &lt; 0.05, <em>t</em> test) from the point of NaCl application (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF5" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S5</a>). This result indicates that the position of the LaCl<sub>3</sub>, rather than simply its presence in the root system, appeared to be a key factor in blocking the progression of the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave.</p>

A prolonged suppression of <em>LOX3</em> and <em>SOS1</em> transcript levels were a notable exception, and so responses of these markers in La<sup>3+</sup> inhibitor experiments must be made with caution (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF7" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S7</a>).</p></section><section id="s5"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Long-Distance Ca<sup>2+</sup> Signaling Is Dependent on the Vacuolar TWO PORE CHANNEL 1.</h3>
<p>In animals, Ca<sup>2+</sup> waves are often supported by processes involving calcium-induced calcium release (CICR) (<a href="#r13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r13">13</a>). The slow vacuolar (SV) channel is a large-conductance, voltage-dependent ion channel (reviewed in ref. <a href="#r14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r14">14</a>) that is permeable to Ca<sup>2+</sup> (<a href="#r15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r15">15</a>) and has been proposed to mediate plant CICR (<a href="#r15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r15">15</a>–<a href="#r17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r17">17</a>), although this idea remains controversial (<a href="#r14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r14">14</a>). The <em>Arabidopsis</em> SV channel is encoded by a single gene, <em>TWO PORE CHANNEL 1</em> (<em>TPC1</em>) (<a href="#r18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r18">18</a>). In <em>Arabidopsis</em>, loss-of-function mutants of <em>TPC1</em> show disrupted stomatal signaling and abscisic acid (ABA) regulation of germination (<a href="#r18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r18">18</a>) and the gain-of-function <em>fou2</em> mutant exhibits developmental effects thought to be linked to aberrant defense signaling (<a href="#r19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r19">19</a>). We therefore tested the hypothesis that the SV channel might play a role in sustaining the salt-induced Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave using <em>tpc1</em> mutants. Previous work measuring Ca<sup>2+</sup> changes in a <em>TPC1</em> knockout expressing the luminescent Ca<sup>2+</sup> indicator aequorin indicated no clear disruption of a range of stress-induced Ca<sup>2+</sup> changes (<a href="#r20" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r20">20</a>). Consistent with this report, we found that salt stress-induced Ca<sup>2+</sup> elevations were not grossly altered in <em>tpc1-2</em> (a protein null) (<a href="#r18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r18">18</a>) when monitored at the direct site of stress application, although they did exhibit a slightly delayed onset (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S1 <em>D</em> and <em>E</em></a>, and <a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/sm08.mov" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Movie S8</a>). However, the speed of transmission of the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave in <em>tpc1-2</em> was significantly reduced to 15.5 ± 1.9 µm/s (±SD) (<em>P</em> &lt; 0.05, <em>t</em> test; <a href="#fig02" class="usa-link">Fig. 2</a>).</p></section><section id="s6"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">TWO PORE CHANNEL 1 Mutants Affect Transcript Levels of Stress-Related Genes.</h3>
<p>qPCR analysis of our panel of stress markers showed that, in response to root tip NaCl treatment, shoot induction of genes such as <em>TCH2</em> and <em>ZAT12</em> was significantly attenuated in the <em>tpc1-2</em> mutant (<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">Fig. 3<em>C</em></a> and <a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF8" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S8</a>), a pattern confirmed in the knockdown lines <em>tpc1-1</em> (36% reduction in <em>TPC1</em> transcript; <a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF8" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S8</a>) and <em>tpc1-4</em> (21% reduction in <em>TPC1</em> transcript; <a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF8" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S8</a>). Interestingly, La<sup>3+</sup> treatment had a complex interaction with NaCl induction of <em>TCH2</em> in these backgrounds, leading to a partial recovery of transcript levels. This observation suggests that feedback between vacuolar and plasma membrane Ca<sup>2+</sup> fluxes is likely an important element in the transcriptional response of at least <em>TCH2</em> to salt stress.</p>
<p>qPCR analysis also indicated that <em>TPC1</em> is the most rapidly systemically up-regulated gene we tested, with 10-fold increase in transcript abundance in the shoot within 1–2 min of root salt stress (<em>P</em> &lt; 0.05, <em>t</em> test; <a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">Fig. 3<em>A</em></a> and <a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF3" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S3<em>C</em></a>). This observation raises the possibility of a self-reinforcing signaling system with Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave-related rapid induction of <em>TPC1</em> amplifying and extending subsequent Ca<sup>2+</sup> responses. Consistent with such ideas, markers that were suppressed in <em>tpc1</em> mutants such as <em>ZAT12</em> and <em>TCH2</em> (<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">Fig. 3<em>C</em></a> and <a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF8" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S8</a>) were constitutively increased above WT in a <em>TPC1</em> overexpression line (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF9" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S9<em>A</em></a>). This up-regulation of transcript levels was suppressed by La<sup>3+</sup> treatment (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF9" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S9<em>B</em></a>), suggesting altered control of Ca<sup>2+</sup> homeostasis may link <em>TPC1</em> levels to transcriptional regulation. Consistent with these ideas, the speed of propagation of the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave in the TPC1 overexpression line was 679 ± 73 µm/s, 1.7-fold faster than WT but was still sensitive to inhibition by LaCl<sub>3</sub> (<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">Fig. 3<em>D</em></a>).</p></section><section id="s7"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">TWO PORE CHANNEL 1 Expression Level Affects Whole-Plant Salt Sensitivity.</h3>
<p>The overexpression line <em>TPC1 10.21</em> (<a href="#r18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r18">18</a>), which showed 73- to 130-fold increase in transcript level (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF9" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S9<em>C</em></a>), was more resistant to NaCl stress based on seedling growth and chlorophyll levels after growth on 100 mM NaCl for 7 d (<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">Fig. 3<em>E</em></a>). Thus, the up-regulation of systemic molecular changes and faster Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave signaling observed in the <em>TPC1</em> transgenic lines correlate with alterations in whole-plant salt stress resistance.</p></section></section><section id="s8"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Discussion</h2>
<p>In plants, environmental stimuli including wounding or heat stress have been proposed to elicit long-distance signaling involving mechanisms such as plasma membrane depolarization and self-propagating increases of ROS (<a href="#r1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r1">1</a>–<a href="#r5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r5">5</a>, <a href="#r21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r21">21</a>, <a href="#r22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r22">22</a>). Our results suggest Ca<sup>2+</sup>-dependent signaling also plays a role in this process of systemic signaling in response to salt stress. Thus, local salt stress of the root tip leads to the spreading of a Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave that propagates preferentially through cortical and endodermal cells to distal shoot tissues (<a href="#fig01" class="usa-link">Figs. 1</a> and <a href="#fig02" class="usa-link">2</a>). This Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave moves with a velocity of ∼400 µm/s (<a href="#fig02" class="usa-link">Fig. 2<em>A</em></a>), i.e., traversing several cells per second. Local treatment with LaCl<sub>3</sub> that blocked the transmission of the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave (<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4</a>) also inhibited subsequent systemic molecular responses (<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">Fig. 3<em>C</em></a> and <a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF6" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S6</a>). Thus, these patterns of transmission and response are consistent with a model in which the rapidly propagating Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave serves as a long-distance signal that triggers systemic responses to local salt stress of the root system. It is important to note that such La<sup>3+</sup> treatment itself could inhibit transcription of some genes (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF7" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S7</a>). This observation implies that a basal level of La<sup>3+</sup>-sensitive (likely Ca<sup>2+</sup>-dependent) signaling between root and shoot may be required to sustain nonstimulated levels of some genes. In addition, although such blocking of the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave could inhibit systemic induction of stress markers such as <em>TCH2</em>, <em>ZAT12</em> (<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">Fig. 3<em>C</em></a>), <em>TCH3</em>, and <em>PIP2;3</em> (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF6" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S6</a>) in response to localized salt stress treatment, a similar treatment could not block up-regulation of <em>RAB18</em> and <em>DREB2A</em> (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF6" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S6</a>). These observations suggest additional systemic signals are likely operating in parallel to the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave.</p>
<section id="s9"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Role of the Vacuolar Ion Channel TPC1 in Propagation of the Ca<sup>2+</sup> Wave.</h3>
<p>We found that the rate of propagation of the systemic Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave was reduced from 395.7 ± 27.8 µm/s in WT to 15.5 ± 1.9 µm/s in the loss-of-function <em>tpc1-2</em> mutant (<a href="#fig02" class="usa-link">Fig. 2</a>), representing a ∼25-fold reduction in speed. <em>TPC1</em> encodes the vacuolar SV ion channel that is sensitive to cytosolic Ca<sup>2+</sup> levels and to those in the lumen of the vacuole (<a href="#r23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r23">23</a>), suggesting feedback regulation of this channel by alterations in cytosolic and vacuolar Ca<sup>2+</sup> levels could play a role in Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave propagation. However, it remains unclear whether TPC1 acts itself as a vacuolar Ca<sup>2+</sup> release channel that sustains the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave (<a href="#r14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r14">14</a>). Thus, TPC1 could be operating more indirectly, through, e.g., altering tonoplast potential and so gating an independent vacuolar Ca<sup>2+</sup> conductance. However, effects of <em>tpc1</em> mutants on both reducing Ca<sup>2+</sup> changes and inhibiting the induction of systemic molecular responses such as increase in <em>TCH2</em> and <em>ZAT12</em> transcript abundance (<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">Fig. 3<em>C</em></a> and <a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF8" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S8</a>) are very similar to those of blocking Ca<sup>2+</sup> changes with La<sup>3+</sup>. These observations are consistent with TPC1 actively maintaining the propagation of the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave through an effect on Ca<sup>2+</sup> fluxes. In support of these ideas, the <em>TPC1</em> overexpression line showed Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave propagation rates that were 1.7-fold faster than WT (<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">Fig. 3<em>D</em></a>). The overexpression line also exhibited constitutive elevation of marker genes such as <em>TCH2</em> and <em>ZAT12</em> that appear normally dependent for up-regulation on the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave elicited by local salt stress. This constitutive induction was inhibited by the channel blocker La<sup>3+</sup> (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF9" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S9<em>B</em></a>), suggesting a link between transcriptional changes modulated by TPC1 and Ca<sup>2+</sup>-mediated events. Taken together, these results support a model in which local NaCl stress elicits a long-distance Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave-mediated signaling system that triggers systemic molecular responses. Furthermore, comparison of WT, <em>tpc1-2</em>, and plants overexpressing <em>TPC1</em> grown on 100 mM NaCl-containing plates indicated that, by 7 d, the overexpression line was significantly more resistant to this level of salt stress as inferred from plant size and chlorophyll content (<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">Fig. 3<em>E</em></a>). These observations suggest that the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave system mediated by <em>TPC1</em> may also be contributing to the whole-plant resistance to salt stress. However, it is important to note that, due to the extended time of salt stress needed to elicit this visible phenotype, the link between Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave signaling and this whole-plant physiological response must remain tentative.</p>
<p>qPCR analysis (<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">Fig. 3<em>A</em></a> and <a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF3" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S3<em>C</em></a>) indicated that <em>TPC1</em> was the most rapidly up-regulated gene we tested in the shoot, showing up to 10-fold increase in transcript abundance within 1–2 min of root salt stress. We also observed rapid changes in transcript abundance for some other genes in qPCR analyses, such as rapid down-regulation of <em>TCH2</em> (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1319955111/-/DCSupplemental/pnas.201319955SI.pdf?targetid=nameddest=SF3" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Fig. S3</a>). The timescale of these alterations suggest either extremely fast changes in transcription or rapid alterations in mRNA stability/turnover elicited by the salt stress signal.</p>
<p>In addition to such rapid alterations in transcript level, posttranscriptional regulation is also likely playing a role in modulating the extent of Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave propagation through <em>TPC1</em>. This idea is because this channel is ubiquitously expressed across tissue types, yet the root Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave is restricted to the cortex and endodermal cell layers (<a href="#fig01" class="usa-link">Fig. 1</a>). Although it remains to be discovered whether the action of TPC1 and transmission of the Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave is related to other proposed systemic signaling systems such as membrane depolarization (<a href="#r2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r2">2</a>) or ROS-dependent events (<a href="#r5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r5">5</a>), TPC1 has been proposed to be posttranslationally regulated by ROS (<a href="#r24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r24">24</a>). This observation raises the possibility of a point of interaction between the ROS and Ca<sup>2+</sup>-dependent systemic signaling systems. Transmission of systemic ROS responses is thought to be Ca<sup>2+</sup>-independent (<a href="#r5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r5">5</a>), suggesting a model where Ca<sup>2+</sup> changes could lie downstream of ROS. However, the relationship between ROS and Ca<sup>2+</sup> is complex with Ca<sup>2+</sup> triggering ROS production (<a href="#r25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r25">25</a>) and ROS eliciting Ca<sup>2+</sup> increases (<a href="#r25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r25">25</a>, <a href="#r26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r26">26</a>). Thus, the relationship of TPC1, Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave, and ROS is an important area for future study.</p>
<p>TPC1 is found in all land plants (<a href="#r14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r14">14</a>) and the function of this channel appears to be conserved at least between monocots to dicots (<a href="#r27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r27">27</a>). This ubiquity of TPC1 suggests a possibly widespread and general function of this channel in plant biology, hinting at the potentially widespread nature of Ca<sup>2+</sup> wave signaling in plants.</p></section></section><section id="s10"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Materials and Methods</h2>
<section id="s11"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Plant Growth and Transformation.</h3>
<p>Seeds of <em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em> ecotype Columbia 0 and the mutants used in this study (<em>tpc1-1</em>, <em>tpc1-2</em>, <em>tpc1-4</em>, <em>OxTPC1/TPC1 10.21</em>) were surface sterilized and germinated on half-strength Epstein’s medium containing 10 mM sucrose and 0.5% (wt/vol) phytagel (Sigma-Aldrich), pH 5.7, as described previously (<a href="#r28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r28">28</a>). Plants were grown under a long-day (LD) cycle of 16 h light/8 h dark at 22 °C. Ten-day-old seedlings were transplanted to SunshineRedi-earth soil (Sun Gro Horticulture) and were grown under cool white fluorescent lights (75–88 µE⋅m<sup>−2</sup>⋅s<sup>−1</sup>) at 22 °C under the LD light cycle. Plant transformation was carried out essentially as in ref. <a href="#r29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r29">29</a> using midlogarithmic cultures (<em>A</em><sub>600</sub> = 0.6) of <em>Agrobacterium tumefaciens</em> strain GV3101 harboring the binary vector YCNano-65/pEarleyGage100 that encoded YCNano-65 (see below for details of this vector), resuspended in half strength Epstein’s medium containing 5% (wt/vol) sucrose and 0.05% (vol/vol) Silwet-L77 (Lehle Seeds). Transformed plants were covered with a clear plastic cover overnight. A second transformation on the same plants was carried out 1 wk later. Plants were then grown under LD conditions until seed set and harvest. Selection of transformants expressing YCNano-65 was made using an epifluorescence dissecting microscope (Nikon SMZ1500 Stereoscope) using a GFP filter set (excitation, 450–490 nm; dichroic mirror, 495 nm; emission, 500–550 nm). Multiple independent lines were selected, selfed, and the T2 or greater generation used for subsequent imaging. All results were confirmed on multiple independent lines. All YCNano-65–expressing lines used showed equivalent growth and development to their untransformed counterparts. <em>tpc1-1</em>, <em>tpc1-2</em>, <em>tpc1-4</em>, and <em>OxTPC1</em> were kindly provided by Edgar Peiter (Martin-Luther-Universität Halle-Wittenberg, Halle, Germany) and Dale Sanders (John Innes Centre, Norwich, UK).</p></section><section id="s12"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Abiotic Stress and Inhibitor Treatment.</h3>
<p><em>Arabidopsis</em> seedlings expressing the YCNano-65 FRET sensor were grown on a thin layer of gel (∼2 mm thick) containing half-strength Epstein’s medium supplemented with 0.5% (wt/vol) phytagel (Sigma-Aldrich) and 10 mM sucrose, under LD conditions at 22 °C for 6 d. A small window (∼500 µm × 500 µm) was removed from the gel at the tip of the root to expose the apical ∼50 µm of the root to allow precise application of 100 mM NaCl (salt stress), 100 µM H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (ROS stress), chilled medium at 4 °C (cold stress), or 200 mM <span class="font-variant-small-caps">d</span>-sorbitol (control equivalent to the osmotic stress from 100 mM NaCl). Touch stress was applied using contact with a glass micropipette as previously described (<a href="#r30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r30">30</a>). For inhibitor treatments, a small window (∼500 µm × 500 µm) was also made in the gel in the middle region of the root, shootward of the root tip window. Ten microliters of 25 µM LaCl<sub>3</sub> (putative plasma membrane channel blocker) or RuRed (putative antagonist of internal sites of Ca<sup>2+</sup> release) were added either in the root tip gel window or to the middle gel window (see diagram in <a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4<em>A</em></a>) 30 min before salt treatment of the root tip as indicated for each experiment. <em>Arabidopsis</em> seedlings pretreated with pharmaceutical inhibitors were kept in a humid Petri dish until confocal imaging.</p></section><section id="s13"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Confocal Image Analysis of in Vivo Ca<sup>2+</sup> Changes.</h3>
<p>Stable transgenic lines expressing YCNano-65 under the control of CaMV35S promoter were grown under sterile conditions in a thin layer (∼2 mm) of half-strength Epstein’s medium containing 0.5% (wt/vol) phytagel and 10 mM sucrose on a no. 1.5 cover glass (24 × 50 mm; Fisher Scientific) for 6 d under LD conditions at 22 °C. Ratio confocal imaging of Ca<sup>2+</sup> changes was then performed with an LSM510 Meta laser-scanning confocal microscope (Carl Zeiss) using a Plan-Neofluar 10×/0.3 or 20×/0.75 Plan-Apochromat objective as described previously (<a href="#r31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r31">31</a>). The YCNano-65 Ca<sup>2+</sup> sensor was excited with the 458-nm line of an argon laser and CFP (462–505 nm) and cpVenus (FRET, 526–537 nm) emission detected using a 458-nm primary dichroic mirror and the microscope system’s Meta detector. Ca<sup>2+</sup> levels were determined from the ratio of the Ca<sup>2+</sup>-dependent FRET signal to the CFP signal normalized to the FRET-to-CFP ratio from before stress treatment. Quantitative analysis of Ca<sup>2+</sup> dynamics (from the ratiometric Ca<sup>2+</sup>-FRET signal) was performed using iVision-Mac (version 4.0.16) image-processing software (BioVision Technologies) with a custom script for the calculation of the ratiometric FRET signal from region of interests (ROIs). Briefly, quantitative analysis of YCNano-65 data (e.g., <a href="#fig02" class="usa-link">Fig. 2</a>) was calculated from the ROIs (4 µm long × 51.2 µm wide, with 1,280 pixels of data) scanned along the root, generating 116 sequential data points covering a total of 464 µm of root length per image. The extracted quantitative ratio numerical data from the sequential confocal images were displayed as a pseudocolor-coded matrix using MultiExperiment Viewer MeV software (version 10.2; ref. <a href="#r32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r32">32</a>).</p>
<p>Ratio values were converted to absolute [Ca<sup>2+</sup>]<sub>cyt</sub> (in nanomolar concentration) using the equation <span xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><img class="inline" src="https://cdn.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/blobs/2a2e/4035928/7ce40ec037e8/pnas.1319955111i1.jpg" loading="lazy" id="gra5" alt="Inline graphic"></span> (<a href="#r33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r33">33</a>), where <em>R</em> is the experimentally monitored FRET/CFP ratio, <em>n</em> is the Hill coefficient (1.6), and the <em>K</em><sub>d</sub> for Ca<sup>2+</sup> is 64.8 nM (<a href="#r10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r10">10</a>). The ratio from Ca<sup>2+</sup>-saturated reporter (<em>R</em><sub>max</sub>, 5.33 ± 1.43) was determined by incubating samples with 50% (vol/vol) ethanol and the minimum ratio (<em>R</em><sub>min</sub>, 0.40 ± 0.04) by incubation with 10 mM EGTA according to ref. <a href="#r31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r31">31</a>. The values derived from this in vivo calibration should be taken as approximations due to difficulties inherent in obtaining a robust <em>R</em><sub>max</sub> and <em>R</em><sub>min</sub> value and uncertainty in the precise <em>K</em><sub>d</sub> of YCNano-65 in the plant cytoplasmic environment.</p></section><section id="s14"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Monitoring Diffusion Through the Gel.</h3>
<p>To test for the rate of small-molecule diffusion through the growth medium gel, 50 µM fluorescein was added to a window at the tip of the root as for NaCl local treatment. Fluorescein movement was then monitored with the LSM510 using a 20×/0.75 Plan-Apochromat objective. Fluorescein signal was excited with the 488-nm line of the argon laser and emission collected using an HFT 488/543/633 dichroic beam splitter and 500- to 530-nm emission filter. Images were acquired every 4 s for 270 s.</p></section><section id="s15"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Cloning of the YCNano-65 FRET Sensor.</h3>
<p>For the 35S:YCNano-65 Ca<sup>2+</sup> FRET sensor construct, the YCNano-65 FRET cassette fragment from YCNano-65/pcDNA3.1 plasmid (a kind gift from Takeharu Nagai, Osaka University, Osaka) (<a href="#r10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r10">10</a>) was isolated by digesting with EcoRI and cloned into the EcoRI site of the pENTR11 gateway entry vector (Invitrogen). The resulting YCNano-65/pENTR11 construct was introduced into the plant binary vector pEarleyGate100 (<a href="#r34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r34">34</a>) by Gateway cloning using LR Clonase II (Invitrogen) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. pEarlyGate 100 has a CaMV35S promoter to drive constitutive expression in plant. The resulting YCNano-65/pEarleyGate100 construct was verified by HindIII endonuclease digestion patterns and DNA sequencing and transformed into <em>Agrobacterium tumefaciens</em> strain GV3101 using electroporation.</p></section><section id="s16"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Salt Treatment, Total RNA Isolation, and qPCR Analysis.</h3>
<p>For qPCR analysis, the root tip region of 10-d-old seedlings of WT Col_0, <em>tpc1-1</em>, <em>tpc1-2</em>, <em>tpc1-4</em>, <em>OxTPC1</em> was covered with an ∼5-mm strip of KimWipe wetted with growth medium and acclimated for at least 1 h. Approximately 20 µL of medium with or without 100 mM NaCl or 25 µM LaCl<sub>3</sub> was then added to the KimWipe strip to achieve precise local application as described for each experiment. For the LaCl<sub>3</sub> blockade experiments described for <a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">Fig. 3</a>, the 25 µM LaCl<sub>3</sub> was added to a similar KimWipe strip covering the midregion of the root 30 min before salt stress was applied at the root tip.
