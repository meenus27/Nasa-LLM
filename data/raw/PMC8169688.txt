</div></section></section><section aria-label="Article content"><section class="body main-article-body"><section class="abstract" id="Abs1"><h2>Abstract</h2>
<p id="Par1">Using a ground-based model to simulate spaceflight [21-days of single-housed, hindlimb unloading (HLU) combined with continuous low-dose gamma irradiation (LDR, total dose of 0.04 Gy)], an in-depth survey of the immune and hematological systems of mice at 7-days post-exposure was performed. Collected blood was profiled with a hematology analyzer and spleens were analyzed by whole transcriptome shotgun sequencing (RNA-sequencing). The results revealed negligible differences in immune differentials. However, hematological system analyses of whole blood indicated large disparities in red blood cell differentials and morphology, suggestive of anemia. Murine Reactome networks indicated majority of spleen cells displayed differentially expressed genes (DEG) involved in signal transduction, metabolism, cell cycle, chromatin organization, and DNA repair. Although immune differentials were not changed, DEG analysis of the spleen revealed expression profiles associated with inflammation and dysregulated immune function persist to 1-week post-simulated spaceflight. Additionally, specific regulation pathways associated with human blood disease gene orthologs, such as blood pressure regulation, transforming growth factor-Î² receptor signaling, and B cell differentiation were noted. Collectively, this study revealed differential immune and hematological outcomes 1-week post-simulated spaceflight conditions, suggesting recovery from spaceflight is an unremitting process.</p>
<section id="kwd-group1" class="kwd-group"><p><strong>Subject terms:</strong> Gene regulation in immune cells, Immunology, Biomarkers, Computational biology and bioinformatics, Gene regulatory networks</p></section></section><section id="Sec1"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Introduction</h2>
<p id="Par2">While the majority of spaceflight studies focus on the physiological responses during or immediately after flight, readaptation to nominal gravitational environments is also an important factor in astronaut health. This is particularly important for future exploratory missions to the Moon (0.166 g) or Mars (0.38 g) involving long-duration exposures to microgravity (0 g) and elevated doses of ionizing irradiation. Both the adaptation to spaceflight, and the readaptation to planetary gravitational environments, involve multiple physiological systems. Currently, short and the long-term consequences of prolonged exposure to deep spaceflight environments are largely unknown.</p>
<p id="Par3">There are several consequences to living in low-Earth orbit. These include edema due to fluid and cardiovascular deconditioning<sup><a href="#CR1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR1">1</a></sup>, myelin degeneration<sup><a href="#CR2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR2">2</a></sup>, disorientation and motor control deficits<sup><a href="#CR3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR3">3</a>,<a href="#CR4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR4">4</a></sup>, visual deficits<sup><a href="#CR5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR5">5</a></sup>, endocrine and hormonal alterations<sup><a href="#CR6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR6">6</a></sup>, muscle tone wasting<sup><a href="#CR7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR7">7</a></sup>, and bone loss due to lack of mechanical loading<sup><a href="#CR8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR8">8</a></sup>. Upon return to Earth, there is a period of readaptation that includes acute motion sickness, unsteadiness, imbalance due to gravity-induced vestibular fluid shifts<sup><a href="#CR9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR9">9</a></sup>, and blood pressure changes<sup><a href="#CR10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR10">10</a>â€“<a href="#CR12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR12">12</a></sup>. Most notably, space anemia<sup><a href="#CR13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR13">13</a>,<a href="#CR14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR14">14</a></sup> was re-classified as post-flight anemia, because of inconsistencies reported in key hematological parameters, including red blood cell (RBC) counts and mean corpuscular volumes (MPV). This was attributed to the timing of blood sample collections occurring at post-flight return versus in-flight retrieval<sup><a href="#CR10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR10">10</a>,<a href="#CR14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR14">14</a>,<a href="#CR15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR15">15</a></sup>. Mission duration can also affect post-flight recovery, whereby longer missions result in prolonged recovery post-mission<sup><a href="#CR16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR16">16</a></sup>. This becomes an important risk factor to consider for exploratory missions to Mars. Indeed, understanding how immune and hematological cells compensate in response to the changing gravitational environment becomes particularly important as crew currently rehabilitate to Earthâ€™s gravity for multiple months post-flight<sup><a href="#CR17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR17">17</a></sup>. Yet, this timeframe may be problematic on planetary bodies with reduced gravity. Therefore, a better understanding of blood differentials and their transcriptional regulatory patterns are essential for successful development of countermeasures to be used on future exploratory missions to the lunar surface and Mars.</p>
<p id="Par4">Ground-based models of spaceflight are developed to easily explore the impacts of the spaceflight environment on physiology. Indeed, we previously reported that a 21-day, protracted exposure of 0.04 Gy low dose/low dose rate gamma radiation (LDR), combined with hindlimb unloading (HLU), can have neurological impacts on bloodâ€“brain barrier integrity, the oxidative stress response, and neuroplasticity<sup><a href="#CR18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR18">18</a>â€“<a href="#CR22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR22">22</a></sup>. Here, we expand on this work, using RNA-sequencing of splenic tissues and complete blood cell (CBC) counts to characterize relevant immune and hematological pathways engaged after 1-week readaptation following simulated spaceflight conditions.</p></section><section id="Sec2"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Results</h2>

<p id="Par10">Recovery post-spaceflight is systemically taxing and often leaves a lasting impression on human health. Circulating RBC and WBC interact with all physiological systems; therefore, a systems biology approach was applied to assess the effects of the immune and hematological outcomes post-simulated spaceflight (simSpace). Since astronaut collections are difficult to acquire, ground-based animal models are a useful alternative that provides valuable insight into these outcomes. Therefore, spleens and blood of mice were analyzed by RNAseq and hematological analyses, respectively, at 7-days post-simSpace. Previous studies in our lab have developed a simSpace model of low-dose radiation (LDR, 0.04 Gy) and hindlimb unloading (HLU) that mimics astronauts in-orbit on the International Space Station (ISS)<sup><a href="#CR18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR18">18</a></sup>. Analyses at 7-days post-simSpace provide insight into the immediate early responses similarly experienced by crew during Earth readaptation, a critical time point of equalizing homeostasis. Hematological profiling revealed minimal disparities in WBC differentials, however RBC displayed a wide variation in morphology and disrupted function, including reduced overall numbers and hemoglobin levels, suggestive of anemia. Transcriptomic analysis of the spleen, which is the filter for circulating blood cells and a secondary lymphoid organ, displayed genes involved in signal transduction, metabolism, cell cycle, chromatin organization and DNA repair pathways are altered. These analyses also revealed pathways connected to dysfunctional immunity and inflammation, including <em>Cd19, Alox5, Trem1,</em> and <em>H2-DMb2</em> expression. Genes also implicated in blood disorders, including <em>Bcl11a</em>, <em>Nedd4l</em>, and <em>Aspn</em>, with common pathways involved in regulation of blood pressure, protein modifications, transforming growth factor regulation, and negative regulation of developmental pathways were identified. Thus, understanding immediate early immune and hematological pathways engaged post-simulated spaceflight sheds light on suitable timepoints for therapeutic intervention to promote homeostasis and restoration.</p>
<p id="Par11">DEG from spleens collected at 7-days post-simSpace were compared to single exposures of post-LDR or -HLU alone, which showed negligible overlap in genes (Fig. <a href="#Fig1" class="usa-link">1</a>Aâ€“C), indicating distinct transcriptomic profiles are generated following each condition. Similar outcomes were also observed in our previous report that analyzed brain tissues at 4-months post-exposure<sup><a href="#CR18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR18">18</a></sup>, indicating LDR, HLU, and simSpace independently trigger divergent pathways post-exposure. Interestingly, post-HLU appears to produce a large number of transcriptional changes compared to post-LDR alone, indicating altered gravity may cause pronounced physiological impairments. However, different doses and types of irradiation experienced on deep space missions would also cause distinct results<sup><a href="#CR24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR24">24</a></sup>; therefore, future studies are aimed to delineate these transcriptional profiles as well. In addition, combined exposures of post-simSpace (HLUâ€‰+â€‰LDR) compared to single exposures of post-HLU or post-LDR alone also created increased expression profile variability, which may account for reduced DEG observed in post-simSpace (Fig. <a href="#Fig1" class="usa-link">1</a>D). A complete list of DEG for each condition is provided in <a href="#MOESM1" class="usa-link">supplemental information</a>. We next wanted to determine DEG induced post-simSpace and found 33 genes were downregulated and 95 genes were upregulated, compared to controls (Fig. <a href="#Fig2" class="usa-link">2</a>). Of note, no significant differences in overall body<sup><a href="#CR18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR18">18</a></sup> or spleen tissue weights (data not shown) were observed, supporting either minimal physiological effects occurred during simSpace or recovery post-simSpace has reached homeostasis.</p>
<p id="Par12">Reactome analysis identified five major functional pathways that were affected, including metabolism, cell cycle, chromatin organization, DNA repair, and signal transduction (independent pathways from metabolism, cell cycle, chromatin organization, and DNA repair) (Fig. <a href="#Fig3" class="usa-link">3</a>). The majority of DEG were grouped as signal transduction genes, including a large fold upregulation of the cation transporter, <em>Slc22a4</em>. In fact, multiple <em>Slc</em> genes were upregulated post-simSpace, which are transporters of small molecules, including sodium/potassium and cation elimination, suggesting systemic electrolyte imbalance and potential dehydration. Of note, these ion channels are also mechanosensitive and regulate signaling pathways via physical forces they receive<sup><a href="#CR25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR25">25</a></sup>. Therefore, more studies are required to better understand the contribution of these mechanosensitive transporter signaling pathways during- and post-simSpace.</p>
<p id="Par13">We and others have identified the oxidative stress response as a major pathway engaged during spaceflight<sup><a href="#CR26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR26">26</a>â€“<a href="#CR28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR28">28</a></sup>, while metabolic syndromes and ageing are also linked to oxidative stress<sup><a href="#CR29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR29">29</a></sup>. In this study, multiple genes involved in metabolism were upregulated, including the insulin signaling receptor, <em>Igf2r</em>. Additionally, glycoprotein forming genes, <em>B4galt2</em> and <em>B4galt3</em> were both upregulated, suggesting heightened glycoprotein synthesis. Cell cycle pathways were also altered, including upregulation of genes <em>Sccca1, Kif24, Myh10,</em> and <em>Tor1aip1</em>. These DEG are involved in mitosis and meiosis via microtubule formation and cytokinesis, suggesting increased cellular turnover and division post-simSpace. Chromatin organization and remodeling genes <em>Chd3</em> and <em>Ncoa3</em> downregulation were also identified. Furthermore, <em>Swsap1, Ift57,</em> and <em>Ippk</em> genes were upregulated, indicating DNA repair mechanisms occur post-simSpace. Collectively, multiple DEG pathways were actively engaged at 7-days post-simSpace, indicating a dynamic and constant healing process seeking homeostasis.</p>
<p id="Par14">Immune-related genes were grouped according to their cellular function. Myeloid cell (monocyte and granulocyte)-related genes included downregulation of <em>Card11, Sf3b1</em>, <em>H2-DMb2, Vps39, Ccdc50</em>, and <em>Pcm1</em>; while <em>SphK1</em>, <em>Trem1</em>, and <em>Alox5</em> were upregulated (Fig. <a href="#Fig4" class="usa-link">4</a>D). <em>H2-DMb2</em> is part of the MHC complex involved in antigen presentation to lymphocytes<sup><a href="#CR30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR30">30</a></sup>. Downregulation of this gene would imply impaired antigen presentation and deficient immunological activation; however, this may be an important mechanism post-simSpace to dull the immune response, as recovery should be devoid of self-antigenic stimulation. Furthermore, downregulation of <em>Vps39</em> (involved in fusion of endosomes/lysosomes) suggests phagocytosis impairment<sup><a href="#CR31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR31">31</a></sup>. In line with this, impairment of neutrophil phagocytosis is observed up to 3-days post-spaceflight<sup><a href="#CR32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR32">32</a>,<a href="#CR33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR33">33</a></sup>, suggesting a possible role for downregulated <em>Vps39</em> in this process. The transcription factor NFÎºB activates a wide variety of signaling pathways involved in immune activation, inflammation, and cytokine production<sup><a href="#CR34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR34">34</a></sup>. <em>Card11</em> and <em>Ccdc50</em> are both regulators of NFÎºB signaling. Their expression levels are downregulated, indicating dysregulated immunity. In addition, <em>Alox5</em> is typically upregulated by the proinflammatory cytokine, GM-CSF, involved in the generation of emergency myelopoiesis and activates matured myeloid cells into a pro-inflammatory state<sup><a href="#CR35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR35">35</a></sup>. Therefore, <em>Alox5</em> upregulation suggests a proinflammatory environment predominates post-simSpace. In line with this, <em>Trem1</em> is upregulated and is involved in amplifying inflammation of neutrophils and monocytes<sup><a href="#CR36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR36">36</a></sup>. Indeed, <em>Trem1</em> inhibition has been successfully shown to prevent <em>mir-155</em>-driven lung inflammation and injury<sup><a href="#CR37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR37">37</a></sup>, septic shock<sup><a href="#CR38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR38">38</a></sup>, and neuroinflammation<sup><a href="#CR39" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR39">39</a></sup>. <em>Trem1</em> is expressed in neutrophils, which are granulocytes<sup><a href="#CR40" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR40">40</a></sup>. Elevated granulocytes and granulocyte-to-lymphocyte ratio (GLR) has been identified as a biomarker for inflight immune monitoring<sup><a href="#CR26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR26">26</a>,<a href="#CR41" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR41">41</a></sup>, therefore, it is plausible that <em>Trem1</em> induction may also be a determinant for inflight inflammation. Acute inflammation is beneficial in the face of immunological challenges, such as infections or tumors, yet chronic persistence, specifically post-exposures can be detrimental to the host. Resolution of inflammation does not appear to occur at 7-days post-simSpace, suggesting the potential disease development<sup><a href="#CR42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR42">42</a></sup>. Furthermore, although correlation of <em>Alox5</em> and <em>Trem1</em> expression levels and severity of inflammation is not clear, monitoring <em>Alox5</em> and <em>Trem1</em> upregulation may be useful biomarkers to measure chronic inflammation in future spaceflight studies.</p>
<p id="Par15">Lymphocytes play an important role in cell-mediated and humoral immunity. Notable DEG altered post-simSpace, included downregulation of <em>Sla, Cd19, Pikap1, Kbtbd8, Pcm1, Card11</em>, and <em>Cccdc50;</em> and upregulation of <em>Sphk1</em> and <em>Zfp341</em> (Fig. <a href="#Fig4" class="usa-link">4</a>D). Of note, <em>Sla</em> negatively regulates TcR signaling, which may contribute to issues during positive selection and non-specific antigen-immunity<sup><a href="#CR43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR43">43</a></sup>. Indeed, T cell function is impaired during- and post-spaceflight<sup><a href="#CR44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR44">44</a>,<a href="#CR45" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR45">45</a></sup>, therefore supporting identification of gene pathways involved in this process for future studies. Additionally, <em>Cd19</em> and <em>Pik3ap1,</em> are involved in B cell phenotypes, including development and BcR signaling<sup><a href="#CR46" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR46">46</a>,<a href="#CR47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR47">47</a></sup>, therefore downregulation suggests deficiency in humoral immunity. Certainly, B cells are impacted by ground-based deep space simulation models<sup><a href="#CR24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR24">24</a></sup> and post-spaceflight<sup><a href="#CR48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR48">48</a></sup>. Further, activation of splenocytes collected 3-days post-flight produced a robust elevation of the inflammatory chemokine MIP-1Î±, further supporting a proinflammatory state post-spaceflight<sup><a href="#CR44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR44">44</a></sup>. Yet, the overall contribution of immune responses on whole-organism physiology cannot be fully concluded until longitudinal phenotypic studies are performed, as mission timeframes vary, ground-model conditions and exposure timeframes are non-standardized, and post-mission/model tissue collections differ, which can all result in different interpretations. Nonetheless, altered immunity is recognized across multiple in-flight, at readaptation, and in ground-based models of spaceflight, suggesting a need to delineate all potential mechanisms involved, and strongly supports the need for personalized medicine for future crewmembers.</p>
<p id="Par16">CBC indicated no differences in WBC counts, which may be due to either group-housing effects on cell distribution<sup><a href="#CR49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR49">49</a></sup> or homeostasis is established at this time point post-simSpace. There were, however, significant reductions in overall RBC numbers and HGB levels (Fig. <a href="#Fig5" class="usa-link">5</a>A,B). These results suggest at 7-days post-simSpace anemic conditions persist, which may explain post-flight etiology during return to Earthâ€™s gravity in crewmembers<sup><a href="#CR9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR9">9</a>â€“<a href="#CR11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR11">11</a></sup>. Other parameters showed no significant differences including, MCHC and HCT (Fig. <a href="#Fig5" class="usa-link">5</a>D,E). However, significantly increased levels of mean corpuscular hemoglobin (MCH), RBC distribution width (RDW), and MCV (Fig. <a href="#Fig5" class="usa-link">5</a>C,F,G). Collectively, abnormal RBC shape suggest anemic outcomes occurred post-simSpace. Furthermore, both elevated or reduced values of MCH, RDW, and MCV indicate abnormal size and volume of RBC, which impacts hemoglobin function and oxygen carrying capacity, contributing to anemic states<sup><a href="#CR50" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR50">50</a>,<a href="#CR51" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR51">51</a></sup>. An elevated RDW can also contribute to cardiovascular and cancer risk<sup><a href="#CR52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR52">52</a></sup>, highlighting the necessity to normalize these biomarkers during post-flight readaptation. Interestingly, elevated RDW may be caused by elevated inflammation<sup><a href="#CR53" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR53">53</a></sup>, which is also observed in our model, therefore targeting inflammation early post-flight may avert cardiovascular- or cancer-related risks. In line with this, RDW was the only hematological marker that remained elevated at 30-days post-simSpace, while reaching homeostasis at 270-days post-simSpace (data not shown), suggesting the importance of regulating this biomarker in particular for future spaceflight studies.</p>
<p id="Par17">Since RBC do not contain a nucleus and thus genetic material, the majority of blood disease/disorder genes are likely pooled from DNA-containing immature RBC or reticulocytes, immature platelets or reticulated platelets, WBC, and other cells of the spleen, including mesothelial cells and smooth muscle cells<sup><a href="#CR54" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR54">54</a></sup>. Using Malacards Human Disease Database, outstanding blood disease/disorder orthologs including, upregulated <em>Tmem184a, Daam2, Ippk, Aspn, RhD</em>, and <em>Igf2r;</em> and downregulated <em>Nedd4l</em> and <em>Bcl11a</em> were identified (Fig. <a href="#Fig5" class="usa-link">5</a>H). For one, an elevated <em>RhD</em> (Rh factor antigen) was noted post-simSpace, which may have been induced due to RBC loss, as Rh(null) phenotypes are associated with hemolytic anemia<sup><a href="#CR55" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR55">55</a></sup>. As it is unclear if anemia was induced during- or post-simSpace in our model, and due to the complex definition of space/post-flight anemia, more studies are required. Nonetheless, while determining the timeframe of anemia onset is important, we propose the potential for prolonged and pronounced anemia manifestation in astronauts on partial gravity surfaces of the Moon and Mars, following long duration, deep space travel. Therefore, identifying biomarkers to assist with future countermeasure developments to help safeguard astronauts on future deep space missions is critical. Interestingly, pathway analysis implicated <em>Bcl11a, Aspn</em>, and <em>Nedd4l</em> are interconnected and regulate a wide variety of pathways including, blood pressure, B cell differentiation, and TGF-Î²R signaling (Fig. <a href="#Fig5" class="usa-link">5</a>I). Due to fluidic shifting and reduced normal loading in spaceflight, astronauts returned to Earth are at an increased risk of low blood pressure when standing<sup><a href="#CR12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR12">12</a></sup> and arterial stiffening<sup><a href="#CR56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR56">56</a></sup>. Therefore, targeting these interconnected genes may provide a robust countermeasure suited to reverse both immune and cardiovascular disparities during readaptation. We recognize complex pathways depend on multiple genes for function. Therefore, differential gene expression of a few may not disrupt biological phenotypes, and as such additional studies are required to assess functional consequences. Furthermore, physiological responses depend on multiple gene expressions within collective pathways; therefore, a thorough survey of the pan-transcriptomic landscape is required to construct reliable countermeasures.</p>
<p id="Par18">In brief, this study identified key DEG and DEG pathways engaged in systemic blood circulation via spleen transcriptome and CBC differential analyses at 7-days post-simSpace. Primary responses include persistent inflammation and anemia are protracted up to 7-days post-simSpace. Limitations of this study include restricted cross-translation to humans, lack of baseline and longitudinal results, and absence of galactic cosmic ray radiation testing parameters. Nonetheless, future space biology queries can be generated from this study. Furthermore, this study highlights the requisite of systemic homeostasis upon return to Earthâ€™s gravity and describes options for recourse during reduced gravity experiences. In brief, comprehensive and dynamic outcomes of the immune and hematological systems occur 7-days post-protracted simulated spaceflight.</p></section><section id="Sec8"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Methods</h2>
<section id="Sec9"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Experimental conditions</h3>
<p id="Par19">Six-month-old, female <em>C57BL/6J</em> mice (Jackson Laboratory) were acclimatized in standard habitats at 20 Â°C with a 12 h:12 h light:dark cycle for 7 days. Following acclimatization, animals were housed one per cage and assigned to one of four groups: (1) control (<em>n</em>â€‰=â€‰6); (2) hindlimb unloaded (HLU) (<em>n</em>â€‰=â€‰3); (3) low-dose irradiated (LDR, total dose 0.04 Gy) (<em>n</em>â€‰=â€‰6); and (4) simSpace, combination of hindlimb unloaded and low-dose irradiated (HLUâ€‰+â€‰LDR) (<em>n</em>â€‰=â€‰6) for 21 days. Low-dose/low-dose rate was delivered using <sup>57</sup>Co plates (a total dose of 0.04 Gy at 0.01 cGy/h) placed 7 cm below the cages, with 1 plate per 2 cages for whole-body irradiation. Uniformity of dose wasâ€‰Â±â€‰5%, as previously described<sup><a href="#CR18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR18">18</a>â€“<a href="#CR22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR22">22</a></sup>. Post-exposure animals were group housed (3 per cage) for 7-days. Commercial pellet chow (5LG4, LabDiet<sup>R</sup>) and hydrogel were available ad libitum<em>.</em> Health status, water and food intake were daily monitored. After 7-day post-simSpace, mice were CO<sub>2</sub> euthanized and spleens were collected and frozen. RNA was isolated from whole, frozen spleens and samples were subjected to whole transcriptome shotgun sequencing (RNA-sequencing)<sup><a href="#CR18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR18">18</a></sup>. Datasets were deposited to the NASA GeneLab Data Systems (GLDS)-211 and were analyzed using the NASA pipeline for determination of differentially expressed genes (DEG)<sup><a href="#CR23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR23">23</a></sup>. All methods were performed in accordance with the guidelines recommended in the Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals<sup><a href="#CR57" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR57">57</a></sup> and was approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC) at Loma Linda University (Protocol number 8130028). This study is reported in accordance with ARRIVE guidelines<sup><a href="#CR58" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR58">58</a></sup>.</p></section><section id="Sec10"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Complete blood count differential analysis</h3>
<p id="Par20">Mice were euthanized with 100% CO<sub>2</sub> at day seven after the simulated spaceflight period of 21-days. Whole blood was collected via cardiac punch in [K2]-ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA) coated syringes immediately following euthanasia and evaluated using the ABC Vet Hematology Analyzer (Heska Corp., Waukesha, WI, USA). Parameters characterized include red blood cell (RBC), platelet (PLT), and white blood cell (WBC) counts, hemoglobin concentration (HGB) and hematocrit (HCT, percentage of whole blood consisting of RBC). Mean corpuscular volume (MCV, mean volume per RBC), mean corpuscular hemoglobin (MCH; mean weight of hemoglobin per RBC), mean corpuscular hemoglobin concentration (MCHC; concentration of hemoglobin per RBC), RBC distribution width (RDW; width of the RBC histogram produced by cell number x cell size), and the mean platelet volume (MPV; volume per cell) were also reported, along with absolute counts of granulocytes, monocytes, and lymphocytes (Ã—â€‰10<sup>6</sup> cells/ml).</p></section><section id="Sec11"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Tissue collection and nucleic acid extraction</h3>
<p id="Par21">Mice were humanely CO<sub>2</sub> euthanized followed by immediate exsanguination by cardiac puncture.</p>
<p id="Par22">Spleens were isolated and placed in a sterile cryovial, snap frozen in liquid nitrogen and kept at âˆ’â€‰80 Â°C. Spleens were homogenized with CKMix (Bertin Instruments, Montigny-le-Bretonneux, France) and a Minilys homogenizer (Bertin Instruments). AllPrep DNA/RNA/miRNA Universal Kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany) extracted RNA and DNA according to the manufacturerâ€™s instructions. RNA and DNA concentrations were measured using a Qubit 3.0 Fluorometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA) and stored at âˆ’â€‰80 Â°C.</p></section><section id="Sec12"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">RNA sequencing</h3>
<p id="Par23">To construct RNA-sequencing libraries, an Ovation Mouse RNA-Seq System 1â€“96 (NuGEN Technologies, Redwood City, CA, USA) was used per manufacturerâ€™s instructions. RNA integrity was determined using a 2100 Bioanalyzer (Agilent) with RIN valuesâ€‰&gt;â€‰8. 100 ng of total RNA was used as input. cDNA (first and second strands) was synthesized from total RNA spiked with ERCC ExFold RNA Spike-In Mix 1 (Life Technologies, Carlsbad, CA, USA) at the appropriate ratio. Products were sheared using Covaris S220 Focused-ultrasonicator (Covaris Inc., Woburn, MA, USA) to obtain fragment sizes between 150â€“200 bp. Followed by end-repair, adaptor index ligation and strand selection. For multiplexing, barcodes with unique indices out of 96, were used per sample. Custom InDA-C primer mixture SS5 Version5 for mice (NuGEN Technologies) was used for strand selection. Libraries were amplified by PCR (17 cycles) on a Mastercycler Pro (Eppendorf) and purified with RNAClean XP Agencourt beads (Beckman Coulter, Pasadena, CA, USA). Libraries were sequenced on a HiSeq 4000 (Illumina, Mira Loma, CA, USA) to generate 15â€“30 M 75-bp single end reads per sample. Raw data are available at NASA GeneLab (genelab.nasa.gov, accession GLDS-211)<sup><a href="#CR23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR23">23</a></sup>.</p></section><section id="Sec13"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Differential expression and pathway enrichment analysis</h3>
<p id="Par24">With Cutadapt<sup><a href="#CR59" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR59">59</a></sup> using the Trim Galore! wrapper, sequencing reads were trimmed. All reads were mapped with STAR<sup><a href="#CR60" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR60">60</a></sup> and expressions were quantified and imported with RSEM<sup><a href="#CR61" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR61">61</a></sup> and tximport<sup><a href="#CR62" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR62">62</a></sup> respectively, into DESeq2 (R Bioconductor)<sup><a href="#CR63" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR63">63</a></sup>. Log<sub>2</sub> fold-change (0.263) and adjusted <em>p</em>-value cutoffs (0.05) were used. All groups were compared using the Wald test and the likelihood ratio test was used to generate the F statistic p-value. Reactome version 67<sup><a href="#CR64" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR64">64</a></sup> was used to detect enriched murine pathways. Volcano plots were generated using NASAâ€™s GeneLab tool<sup><a href="#CR23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR23">23</a></sup>. Murine spleen DEG that were orthologous to human blood gene-related diseases, as defined by GeneCards Human Gene Database and MalaCards Human Disease Databases, were identified. Using the Network Analyst Global EnrichNetwork tool, with parameters including the gene ontology: biological process (GO:BP) enrichment analysis database, large graph layout, and single node display (<a href="https://www.networkanalyst.ca/NetworkAnalyst/Secure/vis/ListEnrichment.xhtml" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">https://www.networkanalyst.ca/NetworkAnalyst/Secure/vis/ListEnrichment.xhtml</a>), the blood gene-related gene list was mapped to determine common pathways.</p></section><section id="Sec14"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Statistical analysis</h3>
<p id="Par25">Differential expression analysis was performed in R DESeq2. All groups were compared using the Wald test and the likelihood ratio test was used to generate the F statistic p-value. A one-way ANOVA with post-hoc (Sidak) test was performed for the calculated coefficient of gene expression variation per gene. An unpaired, parametric <em>t</em> test with Welchâ€™s correction was performed for all CBC parameters. A GitHub data analysis notebook was created and can be found here, <a href="https://github.com/jgalazka/GLDS-211_Paul_2021" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">https://github.com/jgalazka/GLDS-211_Paul_2021</a>.</p></section></section><section id="sec15"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Supplementary Information</h2>
