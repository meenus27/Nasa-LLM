These WGS enable the identification of genes responsible for synthesizing compounds with biological implications.</p></section><section class="abstract" id="abstract2"><h2>ABSTRACT</h2>
<p>The whole-genome sequences (WGS) of 28 isolates from the International Space Station were generated and identified as <em>Rhodotorula mucilaginosa</em>, a pigmented yeast that has been classified as an emerging human pathogen in recent times. These WGS enable the identification of genes responsible for synthesizing compounds with biological implications.</p></section><section id="s1"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">ANNOUNCEMENT</h2>
<p><em>Rhodotorula mucilaginosa</em> of phylum <em>Basidiomycota</em> is found in soil, air, food, stool, and other environments (<a href="#B1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B1">1</a>) and produces carotenoids, making it easily identifiable by its distinctive pink, yellow, orange, or red colonies (<a href="#B2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B2">2</a>). Carotenoids are important for various biological activities, including vitamin A biosynthesis, enhancement of the immune system, reduction of the risk of various diseases (<a href="#B3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B3">3</a>), and protection from radiation (<a href="#B4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B4">4</a>). For these reasons, <em>R. mucilaginosa</em> carotenoids are used as food additives and hold pharmaceutical potential (<a href="#B5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B5">5</a>). <em>R. mucilaginosa,</em> which was previously considered to be nonpathogenic, has now been classified as an emerging pathogen (<a href="#B6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B6">6</a>, <a href="#B7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B7">7</a>) and has been shown to colonize central venous catheters, causing fungemia due to biofilm formation (<a href="#B8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B8">8</a>).</p>
<p>Among the 28 recognized members of the genus <em>Rhodotorula</em> (<a href="#B9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B9">9</a>), <em>R. mucilaginosa</em> is the most common species isolated from the environment (<a href="#B7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B7">7</a>) and the most abundant yeast isolated from surfaces of the International Space Station (ISS) (<a href="#B10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B10">10</a>). The ability of this yeast to produce biofilms makes it very important to study ISS strains since the harsh conditions of the ISS (microgravity and radiation) were shown to enhance antimicrobial resistance and biofilm formation (<a href="#B11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B11">11</a>, <a href="#B12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B12">12</a>). Due to their ability to form biofilms and colonize life support systems, such as water tanks and pipes containing clean water, characterization of whole-genome sequences (WGS) of <em>R. mucilaginosa</em> would allow for the development of countermeasures to eradicate this potential threat.</p>
<p>Samples were collected from ISS surfaces using premoistened polyester wipes (<a href="#B10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B10">10</a>). Each sample was aseptically transferred into 200 ml of phosphate-buffered saline, vigorously shaken, and concentrated using an InnovaPrep (Drexel, MO) CP-150 concentrated pipette. A 100-μl aliquot from each sample was plated onto potato dextrose agar (PDA) with 100 μg/ml chloramphenicol (25°C; 7 days). A single colony was obtained and restreaked onto PDA plates (25°C; 7 days), and a single colony was collected for DNA extraction. Genomic DNA was extracted by using a ZymoBIOMICS DNA MagBead kit (Zymo, Irvine, CA).</p>
<p>To acquire the WGS of these 28 fungal strains, shotgun libraries were prepared using the Illumina Nextera Flex protocol (<a href="#B13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B13">13</a>). Paired-end sequencing was performed on a NovaSeq 6000 S4 flowcell paired-end (PE) 2 × 150-bp platform. Quality analysis was performed with FastQC (v0.11.7) (<a href="#B14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B14">14</a>) to validate the quality of the raw sequencing data. For quality control, adapter trimming and quality filtering were performed using the software fastp (v0.20.0) (<a href="#B15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B15">15</a>), and then the cleaned sequences were assembled using SPAdes (v3.11.1) (<a href="#B16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B16">16</a>). Three functions of fastp were used, namely, correction of mismatches in overlapped regions of paired-end reads, trimming of autodetected adapter sequences, and quality trimming at the 5′ and 3′ ends. SPAdes ran using an option to reduce the number of mismatches and short indels in the final contigs, the automatic read coverage cutoff value, and the default values of k-mer sizes. To assess the assembly quality, the number of contigs, <em>N</em><sub>50</sub> values, median coverage, and the genome size were calculated using QUAST (v5.0.2) (<a href="#B17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B17">17</a>) (<a href="#tab1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>). The G+C content ranged between 60.53% and 60.55%. All other statistics are given in <a href="#tab1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>.</p>
