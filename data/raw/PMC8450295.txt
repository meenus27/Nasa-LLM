<p>Measurements of telomere length in skeletal muscle stem cells (MuSCs), a rare cell population within muscles, provide insights into cellular dysfunction in diseased conditions. Here, we describe a protocol (<span class="text-underline">cryo</span>section <span class="text-underline">mu</span>scle <span class="text-underline">q</span>uantitative <span class="text-underline">f</span>luorescent <em><span class="text-underline">i</span>n <span class="text-underline">s</span>itu</em><span class="text-underline">h</span>ybridization) using skeletal muscle cryosections for assessments of telomere length in MuSCs, in their native environment. Using a free software, telomere length measurements are assessed on a single-cell level. We also provide methodology to perform data analyses in several ways.</p>
<p>For complete details on the use and execution of this protocol, please refer to <a href="#bib7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib7">Tichy et al. (2021)</a>.</p>

<p>Measurements of telomere length in skeletal muscle stem cells (MuSCs), a rare cell population within muscles, provide insights into cellular dysfunction in diseased conditions. Here, we describe a protocol (<span class="text-underline">cryo</span>section <span class="text-underline">mu</span>scle <span class="text-underline">q</span>uantitative <span class="text-underline">f</span>luorescent <em><span class="text-underline">i</span>n <span class="text-underline">s</span>itu</em><span class="text-underline">h</span>ybridization) using skeletal muscle cryosections for assessments of telomere length in MuSCs, in their native environment. Using a free software, telomere length measurements are assessed on a single-cell level. We also provide methodology to perform data analyses in several ways.</p></section><section id="sec1"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Before you begin</h2>
<section id="sec1.1"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Experimental design and considerations</h3>
<p id="p0035">Reductions in telomere length in muscle stem cells (MuSCs) have been recently discovered in chronic muscle injury conditions, including Duchenne muscular dystrophy (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Tichy et al., 2017</a>, <a href="#bib7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib7">2021</a>), which results in a more severe disease phenotype (<a href="#bib6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib6">Sacco et al., 2010</a>). Many common methods for conducting telomere length assessments in other cell and tissue types requires the isolation of primary cells, which may disrupt the tissue environment, requires large numbers of cells to obtain enough genomic DNA for appraisal [Telomere Restriction Fragment/TRF analysis (<a href="#bib5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib5">Mender and Shay, 2015</a>)], or only provides the average telomere length over the entirety of the cell population [Telomere:single copy gene QPCR methodology (<a href="#bib1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib1">Cawthon, 2002</a>)] . The quantitative fluorescent in-situ hybridization (Q-FISH) technique, which allows for assessment of telomere length based on the fluorescence intensity of a hybridized probe, coupled with semi-automated telomere length assessments using a free software (Telometer), provides robust information that can eliminate such limitations. Here, we provide a protocol where our previously established and validated muscle stem cell-specific Q-FISH protocol for measuring telomeres in FACS-isolated MuSCs [MuQ-FISH (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Tichy et al., 2017</a>)], has been altered and adapted for use on cryosections of murine skeletal muscle (CRYO MuQ-FISH), leaving the MuSC niche intact. This method can be beneficial for telomere length quantitation of archival samples and may also be adapted to examine telomere lengths in additional muscle-resident cell types, as well as cell types from other tissues. This protocol is also compatible with skeletal muscle cryosections derived from species other than mice but testing and optimization should be conducted by the end user.</p>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><p><strong><em>Note:</em></strong> This protocol utilizes skeletal muscle tissue from research animals and human cohorts. Ensure that the use of research animals has been approved by the appropriate institutional animal care and use committee (IACUC) or equivalent oversight body. Additionally, procurement and use of tissues derived from human sources should follow the appropriate organizational rules regarding informed consent and be approved by the end user’s institutional review board. Sample collection for this protocol was done according to institutional guidelines and regulations of the University of Pennsylvania.</p></blockquote>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><p><strong><em>Note:</em></strong> For telomere data extraction from images, the following software must be installed on the analysis computer: java se (<a href="https://www.oracle.com/java/technologies/javase-downloads.html" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">https://www.oracle.com/java/technologies/javase-downloads.html</a>) and the freeware Telometer (<a href="https://demarzolab.pathology.jhmi.edu/telometer/download.html" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">https://demarzolab.pathology.jhmi.edu/telometer/download.html</a>). Install software according to instructions listed on the websites.</p></blockquote>
<p id="p0040">This protocol includes the following steps: 1) Skeletal muscle preparation, embedding, sectioning, 2) CRYO MuQ-FISH, 3) image collection, 4) analysis and statistics</p></section><section id="sec1.2"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Skeletal muscle preparation, embedding, and sectioning</h3>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><p><img class="inline" src="https://cdn.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/blobs/10d6/8450295/51ca064f7ff6/fx2.gif" loading="lazy" alt="Inline graphic"><strong>Timing: 2 days</strong></p></blockquote>
<ul id="olist0010" class="list" style="list-style-type:none"><li id="o0010">
<span class="label">1.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0050">Humanely euthanize mice according to an approved IACUC protocol methodology and institutional guidelines.</p>
</li></ul>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><p><strong><em>Note:</em></strong> This protocol uses male mice between 2 and 5 months of age. However, this protocol can be applied to samples derived from other ages and both sexes of mice.</p></blockquote>
<ul id="olist0015" class="list" style="list-style-type:none">
<li id="o0015">
<span class="label">2.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0055">Spray the lower half of mouse body with 70% ethanol.</p>
</li>
<li id="o0020">
<span class="label">3.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0060">Lay mouse on its back and completely remove skin from the back legs.</p>
</li>
<li id="o0025">
<span class="label">4.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0065">Dissect desired hindlimb skeletal muscles for assessment.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><p><strong><em>Note:</em></strong> For downstream assessments, it is ideal to compare the same types of muscles, rather than mixing different anatomical groups. We routinely utilize either the Tibialis anterior or the gastrocnemius muscles for telomere length measurements.</p></blockquote>
<ul id="olist0020" class="list" style="list-style-type:none"><li id="o0030">
<span class="label">5.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0070">Fix and process tissues for embedding.<ul id="olist0025" class="list" style="list-style-type:none">
<li id="o0035">
<span class="label">a.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0075">Place isolated muscles in 3–4 mL of cold (4°C) 4% paraformaldehyde in PBS per well in a 6-well plate.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0040">
<span class="label">b.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0080">Incubate tissue on a rocker at 4°C for at least 2 h.<blockquote class="text-italic"><div class="p">
<strong><em>Note:</em></strong> Tibialis anterior muscles up to 200 mg are fixed for 2 h, but larger muscles, such as the gastrocnemius, require a 3-h incubation for fixative to fully enter the tissue.</div></blockquote>
</div>
</li>
<li id="o0045">
<span class="label">c.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0085">Remove muscles and place in pre-chilled 30% sucrose in water (4°C) overnight (16 h) at 4°C.</div>
</li>
</ul>
</div>
</li></ul>
<ul id="olist0030" class="list" style="list-style-type:none"><li id="o0050">
<span class="label">6.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0090">Embed tissues in O.C.T. and freeze.<ul id="olist0035" class="list" style="list-style-type:none">
<li id="o0055">
<span class="label">a.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0095">Label 15 mm <strong>×</strong> 15 mm <strong>×</strong> 5 mm base molds with pertinent mouse information with an alcohol resistant marker and mark directionality of the muscle by labeling the terminal tendon location.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0060">
<span class="label">b.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0100">Fill base mold with O.C.T. to the point where it almost overflows from the inset. Do not allow bubbles to form. If they do, use a 100 μL micropipettor and tip to remove the bubbles.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0065">
<span class="label">c.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0105">Place fixed muscle tissue in the base mold and push it evenly to the bottom of the mold. The orientation should be as it was labeled previously.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0070">
<span class="label">d.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0110">Chill 2-methylbutane with dry ice or liquid nitrogen. Immerse muscles in base molds in chilled 2-methylbutane for &gt;30 s for TA muscles and &gt;1 min for gastrocnemius muscles.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0075">
<span class="label">e.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0115">Place chilled blocks on dry ice for 1 h to allow residual 2-methylbutane to evaporate.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0080">

</ul></section></section><section id="sec3"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Step-by-step method details</h2>
<section id="sec3.1"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Telomere, centromere, muscle stem cell, and nuclear staining</h3>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><p><img class="inline" src="https://cdn.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/blobs/10d6/8450295/51ca064f7ff6/fx2.gif" loading="lazy" alt="Inline graphic"><strong>Timing: 2 days</strong></p></blockquote>
<ul id="olist0050" class="list" style="list-style-type:none">
<li id="o0110">
<span class="label">1.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0235">Remove one slide from each desired sample set from −20°C and warm to room temperature (25°C).</p>
</li>
<li id="o0115">
<span class="label">2.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0240">Permeabilize sections by immersing slides in 0.5% Triton X-100 in PBS for 10 min in a coplin jar at room temperature (25°C).</p>
</li>
</ul>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><p><strong><em>Note:</em></strong> The coplin jar used in this protocol holds approximately 50 mL of solution and 8 slides. If more slides must be stained, we recommend using a tissue-tek staining dish and 24-place slide holder. This alternative setup requires 200–250 mL of solution volume for adequate coverage of the slides.</p></blockquote>
<ul id="olist0055" class="list" style="list-style-type:none"><li id="o0120">
<span class="label">3.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0245">Wash slides twice with PBS for 3 min each at room temperature (25°C).</p>
</li></ul>
<ul id="olist0060" class="list" style="list-style-type:none"><li id="o0125">
<span class="label">4.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0250">Perform heat-mediated antigen retrieval with TET buffer.<ul id="olist0065" class="list" style="list-style-type:none">
<li id="o0130">
<span class="label">a.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0255">Heat buffer to boiling in a microwave first and then transfer buffer to a coplin jar placed in a preheated steamer (95°–100°C).</div>
</li>
<li id="o0135">
<span class="label">b.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0260">Add slides to the coplin jar containing hot TET buffer (95°–100°C) and incubate for 10 min. Remove coplin jar containing the slides from the steamer and let it cool at room temperature (25°C) for 15 min.<blockquote class="text-italic"><div class="p">
<strong><em>Note:</em></strong> Take care when handling hot solutions.</div></blockquote>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><div class="p">
<img class="inline" src="https://cdn.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/blobs/10d6/8450295/83e6728c8b52/fx4.gif" loading="lazy" alt="Inline graphic"><strong>CRITICAL:</strong> Do not let the slides dry at any time following antigen retrieval, as doing so greatly increases autofluorescence of the tissue and will make distinguishing MuSCs difficult.</div></blockquote>
</div>
</li>
<li id="o0140">
<span class="label">c.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0265">Wash slides with PBS twice for 3 min each in a coplin jar at room temperature (25°C).</div>
</li>
</ul>
</div>
</li></ul>
<ul id="olist0070" class="list" style="list-style-type:none"><li id="o0145">
<span class="label">5.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0270">RNAse A treatment<ul id="olist0075" class="list" style="list-style-type:none">
<li id="o0150">
<span class="label">a.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0275">Remove slides from coplin jar and mark tissue boundaries with a hydrophobic/PAP pen.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0155">
<span class="label">b.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0280">Add sufficient RNase A (100 μg/mL) to cover tissue on the slide (100–200 μL) and cover the slide with a glass coverslip to prevent evaporation.<blockquote class="text-italic"><div class="p">
<img class="inline" src="https://cdn.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/blobs/10d6/8450295/83e6728c8b52/fx4.gif" loading="lazy" alt="Inline graphic"><strong>CRITICAL:</strong> RNase should be DNase-free. We routinely prepare RNase stock concentrations of 10 mg/mL, heat at 100°C for 10 min to destroy DNAses and then dilute to 100ug/mL in water and store at −20°C.</div></blockquote>
</div>
</li>
<li id="o0160">
<span class="label">c.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0285">Incubate slides at 37°C in a slide warmer or alternative heating source for 20 min.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0165">
<span class="label">d.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0290">Remove coverslip and transfer slides into coplin jars containing PBS. Wash slides twice for 3 min each at room temperature (25°C).</div>
</li>
</ul>
</div>
</li></ul>
<ul id="olist0080" class="list" style="list-style-type:none"><li id="o0170">
<span class="label">6.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0295">Telomere Staining<ul id="olist0085" class="list" style="list-style-type:none">
<li id="o0175">
<span class="label">a.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0300">Prepare hybridization buffer [adapted from (<a href="#bib4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib4">Matthiesen and Hansen, 2012</a>)] as described in the <a href="#sec2" class="usa-link">materials and equipment</a> section.<blockquote class="text-italic"><div class="p">
<strong><em>Note:</em></strong> The hybridization buffer requires extensive vortexing and pipetting to get all components completely into solution. Heating or alteration of pH is unnecessary. When all components are properly dissolved, the solution will be viscous.</div></blockquote>
</div>
</li>
<li id="o0180">
<span class="label">b.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0305">Prepare TelC-Alexa Fluor 647 telomere probe and optional Cy3-CENPB centromere probe.<ul id="olist0090" class="list" style="list-style-type:none">
<li id="o0185">
<span class="label">i.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0310">Dilute 5 nmol probe pellet provided by the supplier with 100uL formamide.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0190">
<span class="label">ii.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0315">Aliquot in 4 μL aliquots in 1.5 mL microcentrifuge tubes and store at −80°C.<blockquote class="text-italic"><div class="p">
<strong><em>Note:</em></strong> Probe is light sensitive. All steps proceeding the addition of probe should be done in the dark or low-light conditions.</div></blockquote>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><div class="p">
<strong><em>Note:</em></strong> Probes stored at −80°C are stable for at least several months.</div></blockquote>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><div class="p">
<strong><em>Note:</em></strong> We recommend using the Alexa Fluor 647-conjugated telomere probe, as it provides the least background in skeletal muscle cryosections.</div></blockquote>
</div>
</li>
</ul>
</div>
</li>
<li id="o0195">
<span class="label">c.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0320">Remove telomere probe from −80°C storage, thaw, and add probe at a 1/600 dilution to the hybridization buffer (1.67 μL/mL of buffer).<blockquote class="text-italic"><div class="p">
<strong><em>Optional:</em></strong> Centromere probe staining and analysis is not required, unless problems arise with abnormal nuclear architecture or DAPI staining intensities between samples. If centromere probe staining is desired, use Cy3-CENPB probe at 1/600 dilution and add it at the same time as the telomere probe above. The CENPB probe is prepared identically to the telomere probe.</div></blockquote>
</div>
</li>
<li id="o0200">
<span class="label">d.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0325">Heat probe(s) in hybridization buffer for 10 min at 67°C in a thermomixer or alternative heating source in the dark.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0205">
<span class="label">e.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0330">Add hybridization buffer containing probe to slides. We typically use 100 μL probe solution per slide. Cover slides with a glass coverslip to prevent evaporation during incubation.<blockquote class="text-italic"><div class="p">
<img class="inline" src="https://cdn.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/blobs/10d6/8450295/83e6728c8b52/fx4.gif" loading="lazy" alt="Inline graphic"><strong>CRITICAL:</strong> It is crucial that bubbles are kept to a minimum, as areas that are not evenly covered will not stain properly.</div></blockquote>
</div>
</li>
<li id="o0210">
<span class="label">f.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0335">Incubate slides in a Thermobrite slide warmer or equivalent heating source, according to <a href="#tbl1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0215">
<span class="label">g.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0340">Preheat wash buffers I, II, III, IV to 55°C in a water bath or equivalent heating source.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0220">
<span class="label">h.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0345">Gently remove coverslips from slides after hybridization, place slides in coplin jars, and incubate with 50 mL prewarmed wash buffer I for 5 min. Keep coplin jar at 55°C during the wash process. Repeat wash once.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0225">
<span class="label">i.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0350">Continue washing stepwise with 2 washes each (50 mL per wash) of wash buffer II, wash buffer III, and wash buffer IV, in that order, for 5 min each wash and at 55°C.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0230">
<span class="label">j.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0355">Wash slides once with PBS for 5 min at room temperature (25°C).</div>
</li>
</ul>
</div>
</li></ul>
<ul id="olist0095" class="list" style="list-style-type:none"><li id="o0235">
<span class="label">7.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0360">Muscle Stem Cell Staining<ul id="olist0100" class="list" style="list-style-type:none">
<li id="o0240">
<span class="label">a.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0365">Block sections in 50 mL antibody blocking buffer for 1 h at room temperature (25°C).</div>
</li>
<li id="o0245">
<span class="label">b.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0370">Incubate slides in cold (4°C) blocking buffer with VCAM antibody (1/100 dilution); stock antibody concentration 1 mg/mL, in a dark, humidified staining chamber overnight (16 h) at 4°C. We use 100 μL of antibody solution per slide. Cover slides with a coverslip to ensure even coverage of antibody solution.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0250">
<span class="label">c.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0375">Wash slides the following day in coplin jars with 50 mL PBS, twice, 5 min each at room temperature (25°C).</div>
</li>
<li id="o0255">
<span class="label">d.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0380">Incubate sections for 1 h with secondary antibody (Alexa Fluor 488 Donkey anti-goat IgG; 1/300 dilution) in antibody blocking buffer at room temperature (25°C). We use 100 μL of antibody solution per slide. Cover slides with a coverslip to ensure even coverage of antibody solution.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0260">
<span class="label">e.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0385">Wash slides three times for 5 min each with PBS at room temperature (25°C). PBS washes are carried out in coplin jars and using 50 mL of solution.</div>

<span class="label">f.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0390">Mount coverslips with Prolong gold with DAPI.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0270">

<blockquote class="text-italic"><p><img class="inline" src="https://cdn.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/blobs/10d6/8450295/83e6728c8b52/fx4.gif" loading="lazy" alt="Inline graphic"><strong>CRITICAL:</strong> The imaging must take place as soon as possible after staining, and imaging must be completed for all samples in as short a period of time as possible. Given the sensitivity of Q-FISH assays, minor changes in fluorescence intensities can result in skewed datasets. Even with antifade agents in the mounting medium, it is imperative to image cells of interest as quickly as possible, so we recommend completing imaging of all samples in 3 days or less.</p></blockquote>
<ul id="olist0105" class="list" style="list-style-type:none"><li id="o0275">
<span class="label">8.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0405">Identify VCAM+ cells present in tissue sections using a Nikon Eclipse NiU widefield epifluorescence microscope.<blockquote class="text-italic"><div class="p">

<blockquote class="text-italic"><div class="p">
<strong><em>Note:</em></strong> While VCAM1 is a good marker of MuSCs, it may also label other cells, such as endothelial cells. To prevent selecting the wrong cell types during image acquisition and analysis, we only image MuSCs in the satellite cell position (between the basal lamina and the sarcolemma). Additionally, staining with other non-muscle markers such as CD31 and CD45 (as described in <a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Tichy et al., 2017</a>) could be performed to exclude such populations from the analysis.</div></blockquote>
<ul id="olist0110" class="list" style="list-style-type:none">
<li id="o0280">

</li>
<li id="o0285">
<span class="label">b.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0415">Switch to a higher-powered 100<strong>×</strong> objective (i.e., Nikon plan apo100<strong>×</strong>/1.45 oil) for actual imaging and photographing cells of interest.</div>
</li>
</ul>
</div>

<span class="label">9.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0420">Identify the optimal exposure settings for DAPI, telomere Alexa Fluor 647 signal, and optional centromere Cy3 imaging. <a href="#sec7.1" class="usa-link">Troubleshooting 1</a>, <a href="#sec7.3" class="usa-link">Troubleshooting 2</a></p>
</li></ul>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><p><strong><em>Note:</em></strong> Several slides should be examined before determining the optimal exposure settings. It is undesirable to have oversaturated or undersaturated pixels in any of the images. In either situation, fewer regions of interest are identified.</p></blockquote>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><p><img class="inline" src="https://cdn.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/blobs/10d6/8450295/83e6728c8b52/fx4.gif" loading="lazy" alt="Inline graphic"><strong>CRITICAL:</strong> Once intensity settings are determined, the exposure settings should not be changed for the duration of the particular experiment.</p></blockquote>

<li id="o0300">
<span class="label">a.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0430">Identify focal planes containing the most telomere signals for every cell of interest.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0305">
<span class="label">b.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0435">A minimum of 35 VCAM+ cells should be imaged per biological sample.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0310">
<span class="label">c.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0440">Save multichannel images in raw file formats to maintain metadata. For Nikon Elements software, this is a .nd2 file.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0315">
<span class="label">d.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0445">Image as many cells as possible during scheduled equipment usage hours from the biological samples. <a href="#sec7.5" class="usa-link">Troubleshooting 3</a>
</div>
</li>
</ul>
</div>
</li></ul>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><p><strong><em>Optional:</em></strong> Obtaining z-stacks and flattening with an extended depth of focus algorithm will provide more telomere and optional centromere foci per cell, but a single focal plane with many foci in focus is sufficient to yield useable data for analysis.</p></blockquote>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><p><strong><em>Note:</em></strong> Imaging more VCAM+ cells will produce a better representation of the population of MuSCs throughout the muscle. Ensure that more than one muscle section is examined per slide.</p></blockquote>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><p><strong><em>Note:</em></strong> If imaging of all desired cells from all samples cannot be completed in one day, stagger the genotypes/treatments during imaging to ensure fading that occurs between imaging sessions will be similar between the groups.</p></blockquote>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><p><strong><em>Note:</em></strong> It is best to avoid imaging/assessing cells of interest that are positioned on top of or below other cells/nuclei, as telomere length quantitated from overlapping cells may confound the datasets.</p></blockquote>

<blockquote class="text-italic"><p><img class="inline" src="https://cdn.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/blobs/10d6/8450295/51ca064f7ff6/fx2.gif" loading="lazy" alt="Inline graphic"><strong>Timing: varies based on sample size. Several hours to several days.</strong></p></blockquote>
<p id="p0470">Telomere length is measured using a semi-automated ImageJ-based platform known as Telometer. Telometer provides step-by step instructions to analyze 16-bit monochrome images for telomere length. The premise involves creating a project that is saved on a local machine, identifying cells of interest, and measuring the intensity of telomeres, relative to the total DAPI intensity of that cell. The readout from Telometer produces both total sum intensity of telomeres, which can be normalized to DAPI intensity to account for differences in cell cycle position, as well as the mean intensity of telomeres, which can also be normalized to DAPI. Should nuclear architecture or staining be compromised, telomere measurements can instead be normalized to centromere intensities. In this fashion, Telometer steps are conducted twice. Once, where the telomere signal is assessed relative to DAPI, and then those same cells of interest are analyzed a second time, where the centromere intensities are measured instead of the telomere intensities. The ratio of these measurements would then be used for dataset readouts.</p>
<ul id="olist0140" class="list" style="list-style-type:none">
<li id="o0335">
<span class="label">12.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0475">Open Telometer software and create a new project in the “Admin” tab (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Figure 2</a>A).</p>
</li>
<li id="o0340">
<span class="label">13.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0480">Click the “Score” tab and follow the on-screen instructions for each step.</p>
</li>
<li id="o0345">
<span class="label">14.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0485">Select the Project name for your analysis grouping and the Preset objective (in this case 100<strong>×</strong> telomere) and subtract Cy3 background. The scoring tag adds metadata to the analysis. We do not usually alter this.</p>
</li>
<li id="o0350">
<span class="label">15.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0490">Select the Telomere 16-bit monochrome exported tiff image when prompted for opening the first image.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><p><strong><em>Note:</em></strong> Telometer defaults as the telomere signal being a Cy3 image. In this protocol, the telomere image is the Alexa Fluor 647 channel image, and it is this image that is opened first when prompted.</p></blockquote>
<ul id="olist0145" class="list" style="list-style-type:none">
<li id="o0355">
<span class="label">16.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0495">Next, open the DAPI 16-bit monochrome exported tiff image when prompted.</p>
</li>
<li id="o0360">
<span class="label">17.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0500">Normalize the Telomere image by clicking and forming a box of any size in an area absent of features and click “normalize” (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Figure 2</a>B).</p>
</li>
<li id="o0365">
<span class="label">18.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0505">Repeat normalization for the DAPI image when prompted (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Figure 2</a>C).</p>
</li>
<li id="o0370">
<span class="label">19.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0510">Continue following software prompts for creating masked images to narrow down regions in the image that contain telomere foci.</p>
</li>
<li id="o0375">
<span class="label">20.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0515">Threshold the masked image, if necessary, when prompted to obtain the greatest telomere foci in the image with the least background (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Figure 2</a>D).</p>
</li>
</ul>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><p><strong><em>Note:</em></strong> The Telometer software typically does a decent job with auto-thresholding, so the investigator does not often need to further threshold.</p></blockquote>
<ul id="olist0150" class="list" style="list-style-type:none">
<li id="o0380">
<span class="label">21.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0520">Manually draw a circle encompassing a nucleus of interest when prompted. Refer to the color image to determine which nuclei are clearly VCAM+ and designate the nucleus of interest. Selection of VCAM nuclei should only occur for cells in the satellite cell position (between the basal lamina and sarcolemma). Name the nucleus as desired (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Figure 2</a>E).</p>
</li>
<li id="o0385">
<span class="label">22.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0525">Select the “+ Add Nucleus” button. Repeat for additional VCAM+ cells in the image, if any. Click the “next” button (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Figure 2</a>F).</p>
</li>
</ul>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><p><strong><em>Optional:</em></strong> Telometer next gives the option to separate conjoined telomere foci (if counting telomere number) and manually remove halo artifacts from out of focus light. These functions are completely optional for the analysis.</p></blockquote>
<ul id="olist0155" class="list" style="list-style-type:none">
<li id="o0390">
<span class="label">23.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0530">Select “complete.” The data will be stored on your local machine. To view images at a later time that have been processed and analyzed, select the “View” option in the Telometer tab (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Figure 2</a>G).</p>
</li>
<li id="o0395">
<span class="label">24.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0535">Repeat step 15–23 for remaining images to analyze. Ensure that they are being saved in the proper project data file and appropriately named.</p>
</li>
<li id="o0400">
<span class="label">25.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0540">Export data to copy into excel (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Figure 2</a>H).<ul id="olist0160" class="list" style="list-style-type:none">
<li id="o0405">
<span class="label">a.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0545">In the “Export” tab of Telometer, select the project name for data export.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0410">
<span class="label">b.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0550">Click the “Scoring” button that has an arrow.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0415">
<span class="label">c.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0555">Select all data under “Scorings” and click the “Cells” button that has an arrow.</div>
</li>
<li id="o0420">
<span class="label">d.</span><div class="display-inline p" id="p0560">Select all data under “Cells” and copy and paste into Excel or other spreadsheet software. Ensure that you also note the headings for each column. <a href="#sec7.7" class="usa-link">Troubleshooting 4</a>
</div>
</li>
</ul>
</div>
</li>
<li id="o0425">
<span class="label">26.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0565">To analyze datasets using the Telomere sum option, refer to the last column of the dataset. It represents the telomere sum intensity divided by DAPI sum intensity formula∗100.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><p><strong><em>Optional:</em></strong> If telomere mean intensities are desired per cell instead of total (sum) intensities, calculate this value by dividing the datasets in the Telomere Mean column by its respective DAPI Sum.</p></blockquote>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><p><strong><em>Optional:</em></strong> If one desires to normalize to centromere intensity instead of DAPI intensity, the procedure for telomere analysis is repeated, but instead of using the Telomere images on prompt to open, the centromere images are opened instead. The nuclei should be named and selected similarly in the same order as the telomere analysis. Then the last column of data from the telomere analysis is divided by the last column of centromere data analysis. <a href="#sec7.9" class="usa-link">Troubleshooting 5</a></p></blockquote>
<ul id="olist0165" class="list" style="list-style-type:none"><li id="o0430">
<span class="label">27.</span><p class="display-inline" id="p0570">Graph datasets as desired in Graphpad prism. Data can be graphed as total intensity values, or data can be graphed as histograms to demonstrate minor shifts of telomere length within the populations (<a href="#fig3" class="usa-link">Figure 3</a>). <a href="#sec7.11" class="usa-link">Troubleshooting 6</a></p>
</li></ul>
<blockquote class="text-italic"><p><strong><em>Note:</em></strong> The telomere and centromere probes are probes that recognize mammalian DNA sequences and are functional in other species, including samples from rat and human. We have found that this protocol is compatible with muscle tissues of human and rat skeletal muscle cryosections (<a href="#fig4" class="usa-link">Figures 4</a> and <a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">5</a>). We also examined the adaptability of this protocol to several other tissue types (spleen, liver, kidney, testicle, and colon) and found this protocol does appropriately label telomeres in all tissues examined (<a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">Figure 5</a>), increasing the impact and usability of this method. However, in such tissues, it may be necessary to utilize thinner sections or more advanced microscopes (confocal microscopy), given the increased number of focal planes exhibiting telomere foci observed.</p></blockquote>

Scale bar: 10 μm.</p></figcaption></figure></section></section><section id="sec4"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Expected outcomes</h2>
<p id="p0580">This protocol demonstrates the methodology to process cryosections of skeletal muscle for telomere analysis. We utilized VCAM as a muscle stem cell marker (<a href="#bib2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib2">Liu et al., 2015</a>; <a href="#bib3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib3">Maesner et al., 2016</a>); however, this protocol can likely be applied to additional resident skeletal muscle populations, once appropriate markers are identified and tested for compatibility. Here, we utilized data from two different genotypes of mice that underwent chronic, repetitive injuries, to demonstrate different ways to represent the data (<a href="#fig3" class="usa-link">Figure 3</a>). Additionally, to confirm the validity of the CRYO MuQ-FISH method, we compared CRYO MuQ-FISH with our traditional MuQ-FISH method using isolated MuSCs. We found similar pattens of telomere shortening in a mouse model of Duchenne muscular dystrophy coupled with a telomere defect (<a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">Figure 6</a>).</p>

<p id="p0585">Comparisons of data between genotypes are conducted using unpaired student T-tests with Welch’s correction. Cells from at 3 mice or samples per experimental condition should be examined, with at least 35 cells imaged per mouse.</p></section><section id="sec6"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Limitations</h2>
<p id="p0590">This protocol utilizes the quantitative fluorescence in situ hybridization method for measuring telomere length. Because measurements of telomere length are based on fluorescent intensities, it is critical to image the slides as soon as possible, since fading will occur over time. Samples should not be imaged between different experiments, but instead they should only be imaged in the same batch in as short a time window as possible, since fluorescent intensities will be variable between experiments. Normalization must be done to respective control samples to combine datasets. Since MuSCs comprise a rare population of cells and are very small in size, a 100<strong>×</strong> or equivalent high-powered objective is necessary to visualize the telomere foci to get meaningful datasets.</p></section><section id="sec7"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Troubleshooting</h2>
<section id="sec7.1"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Problem 1</h3>
<p id="p0595">No Q-FISH telomere probe signal present following imaging (step 9).</p></section><section id="sec7.2"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Potential solution</h3>
<p id="p0600">Antigen retrieval time was insufficient or not completed with boiling buffer. Ensure that temperatures and times are completed as outlined in this protocol.</p>
<p id="p0605">Probe was bleached or stored improperly after being prepared. Always use a new aliquot of telomere/centromere probe from −80°C and ensure that it is protected from light throughout this protocol.</p>
<p id="p0610">Inappropriate dilution of probe was used, hybridization buffer was not correctly made, hybridization conditions were not followed. If probes are too dilute, increase the amount of the Q-FISH probes used in the protocol. Confirm that hybridization buffer is completely dissolved and that no air bubbles were present when hybridization occurred. Confirm the heating source during denaturation and hybridization is at the proper temperatures.</p></section><section id="sec7.3"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Problem 2</h3>
<p id="p0615">High background staining (step 9).</p></section><section id="sec7.4"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Potential solution</h3>
<p id="p0620">Do not let the tissue dry out at any step following permeabilization. Avoid Cy3/Alexa fluor red/orange dyes except for centromeric region staining.</p></section><section id="sec7.5"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Problem 3</h3>
<p id="p0625">Large numbers of samples that cannot be imaged in the required timeframe (step 10d).</p></section><section id="sec7.6"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Potential solution</h3>
<p id="p0630">While imaging samples in the same time window is optimal, if too many samples are present, a 3-day window may not be sufficient time to complete the acquisition, resulting in fading of the sample and alteration of the data. To overcome this caveat, it may be possible to normalize the intensity of a particular reference sample imaged on different days using fluorescent beads, such as those found in the InSpeck Microscopy Image Intensity Calibration Kit. These kits contain beads of varying but defined intensities from which a standard curve can be generated on each day within the same experiment.</p></section><section id="sec7.7"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Problem 4</h3>
<p id="p0635">Telometer is not displaying recorded datasets (step 25d).</p></section><section id="sec7.8"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Potential solution</h3>
<p id="p0640">Ensure that you are trying to view data in the appropriate project file. It may be necessary to use the “refresh” button to repopulate the project file, particularly if one is trying to view data that was accumulated since Telometer was first opened.</p></section><section id="sec7.9"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Problem 5</h3>
<p id="p0645">DAPI staining intensity is not consistent between experimental groups or nuclear architecture is dissimilar (step 26).</p></section><section id="sec7.10"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Potential solution</h3>
<p id="p0650">Conduct telomere analysis by normalizing to a centromeric probe. First do telomere analysis normalized to DAPI. Then repeat the Telometer analysis on the same cells using the centromere image instead of the telomere image. Ensure to label cells of interest in the same order between the telomere identification and the centromere identification. Finally, divide the Telomere sum/DAPI sum percent by the Centromere sum/DAPI sum. Utilize those values for graphing.</p></section><section id="sec7.11"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Problem 6</h3>
<p id="p0655">Inconsistent data between experiments (step 27).</p></section><section id="sec7.12"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Potential solution</h3>
<p id="p0660">Given that Q-FISH is an intensity-based measurement, experimental samples are ideally measured at the same time with the same exposure settings. If too many samples are present, fading can occur between sample sets that would confound downstream analysis. Thus, it is optimal to image all experimental groups as soon as possible, and comparisons between different experiments are not possible. Additionally, telomere probe in hybridization buffer should completely cover the sample without any air bubbles. While the values themselves may be different, due to inherent differences between experimental preparations, calculations of percent difference between groups can be useful in confirming that datasets are similar.</p></section></section><section id="sec8"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Resource availability</h2>
