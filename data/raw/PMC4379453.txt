</div></section></section><section aria-label="Article content"><section class="body main-article-body"><section class="abstract" id="abstract1"><h2>Abstract</h2>
<p id="P1">Bone health and cardiovascular function are compromised in individuals with type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM). The purpose of the present study was to determine whether skeletal vascular control mechanisms are altered during the progression of T2DM in the Zucker diabetic fatty (ZDF) rat. Responses of the principal nutrient artery (PNA) of the femur from obese ZDF rats with prediabetes, short-term diabetes, and long-term diabetes to endothelium-dependent (acetylcholine) and –independent (sodium nitroprusside) vasodilation, and KCl, norepinephrine and myogenic vasoconstrictor were determined <em>in vitro</em>. Few changes in the PNA vasomotor responses occurred in the pre-diabetic and short-term diabetic conditions. Endothelium-dependent and –independent vasodilation were reduced, and norepinephrine and myogenic vasoconstriction were enhanced in obese ZDF rats with long-term diabetes relative to lean age-matched controls. Differences in endothelium-dependent vasodilation of the femoral PNA between ZDF rats and controls were abolished by the nitric oxide synthase inhibitor <em>N</em><sup>G</sup>-nitro-L-arginine methyl ester. The passive pressure-diameter response of the femoral PNA was also lower across a range of intraluminal pressures with long-term T2DM. Regional bone and marrow perfusion and vascular conductance, measured <em>in vivo</em> using radiolabeled microspheres, were lower in obese ZDF rats with long-term diabetes. These findings suggest that the profound impairment of the bone circulation may contribute to the osteopenia found to occur in long bones with chronic T2DM.</p>
<section id="kwd-group1" class="kwd-group"><p><strong>Keywords:</strong> Diabetes, Bone blood flow, Endothelium, Vascular conductance, Zucker diabetic fatty rat</p></section></section><section id="S1"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Introduction</h2>
<p id="P2">The increase in fracture risk observed among patients with type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) has been suggested to be associated with corollaries of the disease, such as peripheral neuropathy, decreased physical fitness, vision loss, and poor balance (<a href="#R33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R33">Leslie, <em>et al.</em> 2012</a>, <a href="#R38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R38">Melton, <em>et al.</em> 2008</a>, <a href="#R54" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R54">Schwartz. 2003</a>). Emerging research also suggests a causal link between cardiovascular function and indices of bone health (<a href="#R15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R15">Farhat &amp; Cauley. 2008</a>, <a href="#R31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R31">Lampropoulos, <em>et al.</em> 2012</a>, <a href="#R44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R44">Parfitt. 2000</a>, <a href="#R47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R47">Prisby, <em>et al.</em> 2012</a>). Cardiovascular disease is a leading complication of T2DM (<a href="#R17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R17">Grundy, <em>et al.</em> 1999</a>, <a href="#R60" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R60">World Health Organization. 2013</a>), including impairment of endothelium-dependent vasodilation of large conduit arteries (<a href="#R21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R21">Hogikyan, <em>et al.</em> 1998</a>, <a href="#R36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R36">Makimattila, <em>et al.</em> 1999</a>, <a href="#R52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R52">Rossi, <em>et al.</em> 2005</a>, <a href="#R58" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R58">van de Ree, <em>et al.</em> 2001</a>).</p>
<p id="P3">Previous findings using the obese Zucker diabetic fatty (ZDF) rats (<a href="#R34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R34">Lesniewski, <em>et al.</em> 2008</a>), an animal model of T2DM, demonstrate that an impairment of endothelium-dependent vasodilation through the nitric oxide (NO) signaling mechanism effectively changes the balance of vasomotor control in skeletal muscle arterioles to favor vasoconstriction. Decreases in BMD have also been shown to occur in obese ZDF rats with long-term T2DM (<a href="#R48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R48">Prisby, <em>et al.</em> 2008</a>) and is one basis for the decrease in mechanical strength of long bones in the hindlimb. It was hypothesized that one contributing factor to this decrease in BMD and mechanical strength could be a reduction in bone and marrow blood flow and impairment of coupling mechanisms linking endothelium-dependent vasodilation to bone cell remodeling activity (<a href="#R48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R48">Prisby, <em>et al.</em> 2008</a>). For example, reduced blood flow to the bone and marrow of the femur occurs in aged rats (<a href="#R49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R49">Prisby, <em>et al.</em> 2007</a>), and this is accompanied by an impairment of the ability of the femoral principal nutrient artery (PNA) to vasodilate by way of a NO signaling mechanism (<a href="#R49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R49">Prisby, <em>et al.</em> 2007</a>). In addition, prior experiments with young and old exercise trained rats (<a href="#R12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R12">Dominguez, <em>et al.</em> 2010</a>) and ovariectomized rats (<a href="#R47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R47">Prisby, <em>et al.</em> 2012</a>) suggest a coupling of endothelium-dependent vasodilation to measures of bone volume. Therefore, the purpose of the present study was to determine whether control mechanisms of the skeletal resistance vasculature, including endothelium-dependent vasodilation, are altered during the progression of T2DM when BMD is both increasing and decreasing (<a href="#R48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R48">Prisby, <em>et al.</em> 2008</a>). We hypothesized that endothelium-dependent vasodilation of the femoral PNA would be higher in pre-diabetic 7-wk old obese ZDF rats when femoral BMD is greater than that in 7-wk old lean ZDF rats, and that endothelium-dependent vasodilation would be impaired in long-term diabetic 20-wk old obese ZDF rats when femoral BMD is reduced. Results from these vascular studies demonstrated that endothelium-dependent vasodilation of the PNA was diminished with long-term diabetes, along with other vascular mechanisms that serve to regulate bone perfusion. Consequently, a secondary purpose of this study was to determine the effects of frank T2DM on hindlimb bone and marrow blood flow and vascular conductance. We hypothesized that bone and marrow perfusion would be lower in long-term diabetes relative to that in 20-wk old lean ZDF animals.</p></section><section id="S2"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Materials and Methods</h2>
<section id="S3"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Animals</h3>
<p id="P4">All experimental procedures were approved by the University of Florida’s Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee and conformed to the National Institutes of Health <em>Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals</em> (Eighth edition, 2011). Lean (371: +/?) and obese (370: <em>fa</em>/<em>fa</em>) ZDF rats were obtained from Charles River Laboratories (Kingston, NY, USA). Obese ZDF rats are homozygous for the fatty (<em>fa</em>) gene (<a href="#R56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R56">Truett, <em>et al.</em> 1991</a>) and when fed the Purina 5008 diet manifest obesity and hyperlipidemia (<a href="#R32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R32">Leonard, <em>et al.</em> 2005</a>). Seven-wk old obese ZDF rats manifest hyperinsulinemia and mild hyperglycemia and have been classified as pre-diabetic. The strain develops more severe hyperglycemia by 13 wks of age (short-term diabetes) and becomes normo- or hypoinsulinemic by 20 wks of age (long-term diabetes) (<a href="#R14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R14">Etgen &amp; Oldham. 2000</a>, <a href="#R46" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R46">Peterson, <em>et al.</em> 1990</a>). Animals from the present study were provided a Purina 5008 diet and water ad libitum, maintained on a 12:12 light-dark cycle, and studied at 7 wks (lean, <em>n</em>=28; obese, <em>n</em>=29), 13 wks (lean, <em>n</em>=28; obese, <em>n</em>=28), and 20 wks (lean, <em>n</em>=51; obese, <em>n</em>=51) of age to correspond to a pre-diabetic, short-term diabetic, and long-term diabetic state in the obese ZDF rats, respectively.</p></section><section id="S4"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Isolated microvessels</h3>
<p id="P5">Animals were anesthetized with isoflurane (2%/O<sub>2</sub> balance), a thoracotomy was performed, and 2 mL of blood was withdrawn via cardiac puncture before excision of the heart. Immediately after euthanasia the hindlimbs were removed and placed into a dissecting bath containing 4°C physiological saline solution (PSS). Femoral PNAs, which regulate ~70% of the blood supply to the femoral bone marrow and cortex (<a href="#R4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R4">Bridgeman &amp; Brookes. 1996</a>), were isolated, cannulated, and pressurized with PSS at 60 cmH<sub>2</sub>O (44 mmHg) as previously described (<a href="#R12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R12">Dominguez, <em>et al.</em> 2010</a>, <a href="#R49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R49">Prisby, <em>et al.</em> 2007</a>). This pressure was selected based upon intravascular arterial pressures measured within similarly sized skeletal muscle resistance arteries of 43–46 mmHg (<a href="#R37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R37">Meininger, <em>et al.</em> 1984</a>).</p></section><section id="S5"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Experimental design</h3>
<p id="P6">Endothelium-dependent vasodilation of femoral PNAs (<em>n</em>=14–15/group) was assessed by the cumulative addition of acetylcholine (ACh, 10<sup>−9</sup>–10<sup>−4</sup> mol l<sup>−1</sup>). To determine the contribution of the NO signaling pathway to endothelium-dependent vasodilation, PNAs were incubated with the NO synthase (NOS) inhibitor <em>N</em><sup>G</sup>-nitro-L-arginine methyl ester (L-NAME, 10<sup>−5</sup> mol l<sup>−1</sup>) (<a href="#R12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R12">Dominguez, <em>et al.</em> 2010</a>, <a href="#R41" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R41">Muller-Delp, <em>et al.</em> 2002</a>, <a href="#R49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R49">Prisby, <em>et al.</em> 2007</a>), and the ACh dose-response was repeated. To investigate the contribution of prostaglandin signaling to endothelium-dependent vasodilation, PNAs were co-incubated with L-NAME and the cyclooxygenase (COX) inhibitor indomethacin (10<sup>−5</sup> mol l<sup>−1</sup>) (<a href="#R49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R49">Prisby, <em>et al.</em> 2007</a>), and the ACh dose-response was repeated. Endothelium-independent vasodilator responsiveness was assessed via the cumulative addition of the NO donor sodium nitroprussside (SNP, 10<sup>−9</sup>–10<sup>−4</sup> mol l<sup>−1</sup>). Lastly, maximal intraluminal diameter and medial wall thickness were determined after two 15 min incubations in Ca<sup>2+</sup>-free PSS supplemented with SNP (10<sup>−4</sup> mol l<sup>−1</sup>) to achieve complete smooth muscle relaxation. Medial wall thickness was recorded as the average of three distinct wall measurements made with the video caliper as previously described (<a href="#R55" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R55">Stabley, <em>et al.</em> 2013</a>).</p>
<p id="P7">In a separate set of rats (<em>n</em>=13–14/group), vasoconstrictor responses of PNAs to increasing concentrations of potassium chloride (KCl, 10–100 mmol l<sup>−1</sup>) were determined to investigate the contribution of voltage-gated Ca<sup>2+</sup> channels. Concentrations of NaCl and KCl in PSS were balanced such that bath osmolarity was maintained and the desired isotonic K<sup>+</sup> bath concentrations were achieved (<a href="#R13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R13">Donato, <em>et al.</em> 2005</a>). Vasoconstrictor responses to the cumulative addition of norephinephrine (NE, 10<sup>−9</sup>–10<sup>−4</sup> mol l<sup>−1</sup>) were recorded to measure the contribution of alpha adrenoreceptors (<a href="#R10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R10">Delp. 1999</a>). In a separate group of 20-wk old animals (lean, <em>n</em>=11; obese, <em>n</em>=10), the endothelium of PNAs was removed by passing 3–5 mL of air through the lumen as previously described (<a href="#R13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R13">Donato, <em>et al.</em> 2005</a>). Administration of a bolus dose of ACh (10<sup>−5</sup> mol l<sup>−1</sup>) was used to confirm the successful removal of the endothelium, and measurement of the PNA response to the cumulative addition of NE was determined.</p>
<p id="P8">Active myogenic responses to stepwise increases in intraluminal pressure were determined by raising the height of both fluid reservoirs in 15 cmH<sub>2</sub>O increments from 0 cmH<sub>2</sub>O to 135 cmH<sub>2</sub>O. Intraluminal pressure was then decreased in 15 cmH<sub>2</sub>O increments back to 0 cmH<sub>2</sub>O. Passive myogenic responses to stepwise increases in intraluminal diameter were determined using the steps employed for the active myogenic response describe above except that the vessel bathing solution was replaced with Ca<sup>2+</sup>-free PSS plus SNP (10<sup>−4</sup> mol l<sup>−1</sup>) as previously described (<a href="#R10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R10">Delp. 1999</a>).</p></section><section id="S6"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Peripheral quantitative computed tomography (pQCT)</h3>
<p id="P9">Tomographic scans were performed <em>ex vivo</em> on femoral mid-shafts and distal femora in PBS using a Stratec XCT Research-M device as previously reported (<a href="#R48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R48">Prisby, <em>et al.</em> 2008</a>). Reported measures of cortical BMD were derived from the femoral mid-shaft and reported total and cancellous BMD were derived from measures of the distal femora. For precision of the measurements, calibration of the Stratec XCT Research-M device was performed prior to scanning by use of a hydroxyapatite standard cone phantom. The distal metaphysis of the femur was scanned 4, 5, and 6 mm from the proximal plateau and the mid-diaphysis was scanned (on center) 8 mm from distal end of the lateral epicondyles. For both the distal metaphysis and mid-diaphysis, values from multiple slices were averaged. Scans were performed at 5 mm/s with voxel resolution of 0.07 × 0.07 × 0.5 mm. Additionally, analyses were performed using cut and peel modes of 3 and 2. According to the manufacturer’s data, machine precision is ±3.0 mg/cm<sup>3</sup> for cancellous bone and ±9.0 mg/cm<sup>3</sup> for cortical bone.</p></section><section id="S7"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Surgical procedures</h3>
<p id="P10">Blood flow was measured in a separate group of 20-wk old lean (<em>n</em>=12) and obese (<em>n</em>=12) ZDF rats as previously described (<a href="#R9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R9">Colleran, <em>et al.</em> 2000</a>, <a href="#R49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R49">Prisby, <em>et al.</em> 2007</a>). Animals were anesthetized with isoflourane (2–2.5%/O<sub>2</sub> balance) and a polyethylene catheter filled with heparinized saline solution (100 U/mL) was implanted in the ascending aorta via the right carotid artery. Blood (~300 µL) was collected from this catheter to determine plasma insulin and glucose concentrations. A separate polyethylene catheter was implanted into the caudal artery of the tail for reference blood sample withdrawals (<a href="#R11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R11">Delp &amp; Armstrong. 1988</a>). Each animal was allowed ≥3 hrs of recovery after wound closure and the cessation of anesthesia before experiments to measure tissue blood flow were performed. Previous research demonstrates that cardiovascular dynamics, regional blood flow, and acid-base status recover to normal levels within 3 hrs after anesthesia (<a href="#R16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R16">Flaim &amp; Zelis. 1980</a>).</p></section><section id="S8"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Blood flow determination</h3>
<p id="P11">Bone blood flow using radiolabeled microspheres was measured during quiet standing in animals with long-term diabetes and 20 wk old age-matched lean controls as previously described (<a href="#R9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R9">Colleran, <em>et al.</em> 2000</a>, <a href="#R49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R49">Prisby, <em>et al.</em> 2007</a>, <a href="#R55" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R55">Stabley, <em>et al.</em> 2013</a>). After completion of the blood flow experiment, animals were euthanized with Beuthanasia®-D Special (~0.6 mL/kg, Schering-Plough Animal Health, Union, NJ, USA) administered via the carotid artery catheter. Hindlimb bones were removed, cleared of muscle and tendon, and divided into regions as previously described (<a href="#R9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R9">Colleran, <em>et al.</em> 2000</a>, <a href="#R12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R12">Dominguez, <em>et al.</em> 2010</a>, <a href="#R49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R49">Prisby, <em>et al.</em> 2007</a>). Individual tissue and reference blood sample radioactivity were determined and individual tissue blood flow (mL/min/100 g) was calculated according to the reference sample microsphere method (<a href="#R24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R24">Ishise, <em>et al.</em> 1980</a>). Tissue vascular conductance (mL/min/100 g/mmHg) was calculated by dividing individual tissue blood flow by mean arterial pressure.</p></section><section id="S9"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Statistical analyses</h3>

When the PNAs were denuded of the endothelium, differences in NE-mediated vasoconstriction between obese and lean ZDF rats were abolished (<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3B</a>).</p>

In long-term obese ZDF rats, where the animals are more skeletally mature, total, cancellous and cortical BMD were lower than that in lean ZDF animals (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>).</p></section><section id="S15"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Relation between endothelium-dependent vasodilation and femoral BMD</h3>

<p id="P25">Previously work has shown a deterioration of both bone health and general cardiovascular function during the progression of T2DM (<a href="#R17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R17">Grundy, <em>et al.</em> 1999</a>, <a href="#R33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R33">Leslie, <em>et al.</em> 2012</a>, <a href="#R48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R48">Prisby, <em>et al.</em> 2008</a>). However, little is known regarding the effects of T2DM on the bone circulation. Thus, the primary purpose of the present study was to determine whether the progression of T2DM through a pre-diabetic, short-term diabetic and more chronic diabetic state alters mechanisms of vascular control in bone resistance arteries. The results show few adverse effects of the pre-diabetic and short-term diabetic conditions on the vasodilator or vasoconstrictor properties of the femoral PNA. However, with long-term diabetes the PNA shifts toward a more pro-vasoconstrictor phenotype. This is evidenced by a decrement in endothelium-dependent (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1C</a>) and –independent (<a href="#F2" class="usa-link">Fig. 2</a>) vasodilation, an enhanced noradrenergic (<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3A</a>) and myogenic (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4A</a>) vasoconstrictor responsiveness, as well as a decrease in the passive mechanical distensibility (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4B</a>) of the PNA. Consequently, a secondary purpose of the present study was to determine whether this shift toward a more pro-vasoconstrictor phenotype in the bone resistance vasculature diminishes <em>in vivo</em> bone and marrow perfusion and vascular conductance. The results demonstrate an impairment of bone and marrow blood flow (<a href="#F7" class="usa-link">Fig. 7A</a>) and vascular conductance (<a href="#F7" class="usa-link">Fig. 7B</a>) with long-term T2DM. Given the potential coupling of bone vascular signaling and blood flow with skeletal remodeling (<a href="#R15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R15">Farhat &amp; Cauley. 2008</a>, <a href="#R31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R31">Lampropoulos, <em>et al.</em> 2012</a>, <a href="#R44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R44">Parfitt. 2000</a>, <a href="#R47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R47">Prisby, <em>et al.</em> 2012</a>), the present results suggest that the profound impairment of the bone circulation could contribute to the osteopenia found to occur in long bones with chronic T2DM.</p>
<p id="P26">T2DM is a physiologically devastating disease whose deleterious effects on bone and the cardiovascular system are multifaceted. For example, the hyperglycemia and hyperlipidemia associated with T2DM can stimulate mitochondrial free radical production and alter redox balance to promote endothelial dysfunction in the vasculature (<a href="#R53" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R53">Schalkwijk &amp; Stehouwer. 2005</a>, <a href="#R59" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R59">van den Oever, <em>et al.</em> 2010</a>). Impaired endothelium-dependent vasodilation of conduit arteries is present in adolescents (<a href="#R42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R42">Naylor, <em>et al.</em> 2011</a>) and adults (<a href="#R5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R5">Bruno, <em>et al.</em> 2012</a>, <a href="#R21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R21">Hogikyan, <em>et al.</em> 1998</a>, <a href="#R28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R28">Kotb, <em>et al.</em> 2012</a>, <a href="#R36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R36">Makimattila, <em>et al.</em> 1999</a>) with T2DM, and has been shown to be associated with elevated oxidative stress and the uncoupling of endothelial NOS (<a href="#R43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R43">Pannirselvam, <em>et al.</em> 2002</a>), resulting in diminished NO bioavailability to induce vascular smooth muscle cell relaxation. Although T2DM-induced decreases in conduit artery endothelium-dependent vasodilation (<a href="#R36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R36">Makimattila, <em>et al.</em> 1999</a>, <a href="#R52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R52">Rossi, <em>et al.</em> 2005</a>) do not necessarily reflect changes in the microvasculature (<a href="#R39" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R39">Meyer, <em>et al.</em> 2008</a>), results from the present study also demonstrate endothelial dysfunction in the bone microcirculation with T2DM (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1C</a>). Further, the results suggest that this is due to an impairment of endothelial NO signaling, as indicated by the elimination of differences in endothelium-dependent vasodilation between PNAs from obese ZDF rats and age-matched lean controls when NOS inhibition is present.</p>
<p id="P27">The enhanced adrenergic vasoconstriction of the femur resistance vasculature in the present results (<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3A</a>) coincides with observations of greater adrenergic vasoconstriction in the forearm of humans with T2DM (<a href="#R20" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R20">Hogikyan, <em>et al.</em> 1999</a>). Such changes in vasoconstrictor responsiveness could be mediated through the α-adrenergic receptor signaling pathway in smooth muscle cells that elicits vasoconstriction, or the modulatory influence of α-adrenergic receptor signaling in endothelial cells that promotes vasodilation. Results in bone resistance arteries indicate the latter effect, since removal of the endothelium abolished differences in PNA responses to NE (<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3B</a>). Previous research with skeletal muscle resistance arteries has shown a similar endothelium-dependent effect on NE-mediated vasoconstriction with long-term diabetes (<a href="#R34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R34">Lesniewski, <em>et al.</em> 2008</a>). Together, the results of endothelium-dependent vasodilation and NE-mediated vasoconstriction point to a critical role for the endothelium in the transition of the bone resistance arteries to a more pro-vasoconstrictor phenotype with T2DM.</p>
<p id="P28">Alterations in the bone resistance vasculature with T2DM are not limited to changes in endothelial cell signaling, however. Endothelium-independent vasodilation to SNP is impaired (<a href="#F2" class="usa-link">Fig. 2</a>), active myogenic vasoconstriction is enhanced (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4A</a>), and the passive distensibility of the PNA is decreased with long-term diabetes (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4B</a>). Each of these changes would also contribute to greater vasoconstrictor responsiveness, which correspond to reductions in vascular conductance <em>in vivo</em> (<a href="#F7" class="usa-link">Fig. 7B</a>).</p>
<p id="P29">The decrements in SNP-mediated vasodilation (<a href="#F2" class="usa-link">Fig. 2</a>) of the PNA suggest stiffer bone arteries with long-term diabetes. One potential mechanism for enhanced stiffness is an increase in vascular calcification. Previous work has shown that there is an increased risk for arterial calcification in T2DM (<a href="#R29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R29">Kreines, <em>et al.</em> 1985</a>), and that vascular calcification in diabetes is associated with decreased muscle blood flow (<a href="#R8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R8">Christensen. 1968</a>). Several studies also demonstrate positive associations between vascular calcification and osteoporosis or low BMD (<a href="#R1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R1">Adragao, <em>et al.</em> 2009</a>, <a href="#R3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R3">Bandeira, <em>et al.</em> 2012</a>, <a href="#R7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R7">Choi, <em>et al.</em> 2009</a>, <a href="#R18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R18">Hak, <em>et al.</em> 2000</a>, <a href="#R22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R22">Hyder, <em>et al.</em> 2009</a>, <a href="#R50" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R50">Reddy, <em>et al.</em> 2008</a>). In particular, iliac artery vascular calcification is positively associated with lumbar spine and femoral neck osteoporosis in men with T2DM (<a href="#R3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R3">Bandeira, <em>et al.</em> 2012</a>).</p>
<p id="P30">The association between vascular alterations and bone loss are likely impacted by other factors beyond changes in arterial calcification. Evidence suggests an active role of the vasculature in coupling vascular signaling and bone remodeling (<a href="#R9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R9">Colleran, <em>et al.</em> 2000</a>, <a href="#R12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R12">Dominguez, <em>et al.</em> 2010</a>, <a href="#R47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R47">Prisby, <em>et al.</em> 2012</a>). For instance, the basic multicellular unit (BMU) that drives bone remodeling activity may be regulated via signals from core capillary endothelial cells to constituent osteoblasts and osteoclasts as the BMU advances through bone (<a href="#R44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R44">Parfitt. 2000</a>). The present results demonstrate that femur BMD is positively associated with endothelium-dependent vasodilation of the femoral PNA in the pre-diabetic (<a href="#F5" class="usa-link">Fig. 5A</a>), short-term diabetic (<a href="#F5" class="usa-link">Fig. 5B</a>), and long-term diabetic (<a href="#F5" class="usa-link">Figs. 5A</a>&amp;<a href="#F6" class="usa-link">6</a>) states. The association between endothelium-dependent vasodilation of the femoral PNA and bone volume has been previously demonstrated among young and old sedentary and exercise trained rats (<a href="#R12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R12">Dominguez, <em>et al.</em> 2010</a>), as well as among young and old ovariectomized and estrogen replaced animals (<a href="#R47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R47">Prisby, <em>et al.</em> 2012</a>). Important endothelium-derived signaling molecules involved in local vascular control, including NO, prostacyclin (PGI<sub>2</sub>), and prostaglandin E<sub>2</sub> (PGE<sub>2</sub>), have also been shown to be important in bone biology (<a href="#R35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R35">MacIntyre, <em>et al.</em> 1991</a>, <a href="#R45" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R45">Pead &amp; Lanyon. 1989</a>). For example, NO is a potent inhibitor of osteoclast driven bone resorption (<a href="#R35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R35">MacIntyre, <em>et al.</em> 1991</a>) and has a positive effect on osteoblastic differentiation (<a href="#R19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R19">Hikiji, <em>et al.</em> 1997</a>), while PGI<sub>2</sub> has been shown to be a powerful inhibitor of osteoclastic bone resorption (<a href="#R6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R6">Chambers &amp; Ali. 1983</a>) and PGE<sub>2</sub> has been reported to increase osteoblast and decrease osteoclast numbers (<a href="#R25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R25">Jee, <em>et al.</em> 1987</a>). Results from the present study indicate that NO signaling from the vasculature is the more important molecule in the apparent coupling of endothelium-dependent vasodilation to BMD, given that differences in endothelium-dependent vasodilation in PNAs from obese and lean ZDF rats were abolished by NOS inhibition alone (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Figs. 1 A&amp;C</a>).</p>
<p id="P31">An additional factor impacting the relation between the vasculature and bone health is bone perfusion. Bone blood flow facilitates the generation of differences in hydrostatic pressure between the medullary cavity and bone capillary efferents that drive centrifugal interstitial fluid flow (<a href="#R40" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R40">Montgomery, <em>et al.</em> 1988</a>). Resultant transcortical fluid flow stimulates osteocytes and osteoblasts (<a href="#R27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R27">Knothe Tate. 2003</a>) and subsequent bone formation (<a href="#R51" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R51">Riddle &amp; Donahue. 2009</a>, <a href="#R57" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R57">Turner. 1999</a>). Thus, the decreases in bone and marrow blood flow found in the present study (<a href="#F7" class="usa-link">Fig. 7A</a>) could also compromise important flow and pressure stimuli that affect bone formation.</p>
<p id="P32">The selection of an animal model of T2DM is important since no single model will permit the investigation of all disease-related questions (<a href="#R46" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R46">Peterson, <em>et al.</em> 1990</a>). For example, the Goto-Kakizaki rat is a valuable model for investigating effects of T2DM in the absence of a corresponding increase in adiposity (<a href="#R2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R2">Akash, <em>et al.</em> 2013</a>). Therein, the model fails to replicate the typical clinical T2DM phenotype that includes obesity. The Zucker obese rat is a valuable model of the metabolic syndrome (<a href="#R30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R30">Kurtz, <em>et al.</em> 1989</a>) with impaired glucose tolerance and insulin resistance, but it fails to demonstrate hyperglycemia (<a href="#R46" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R46">Peterson, <em>et al.</em> 1990</a>). The ZDF rat utilized in the present study was originally derived from the Zucker obese rat strain via select inbreeding of animals that spontaneously demonstrated unusually high blood glucose levels (<a href="#R46" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R46">Peterson, <em>et al.</em> 1990</a>). The ZDF rat displays a well-defined T2DM disease progression (<a href="#R14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R14">Etgen &amp; Oldham. 2000</a>, <a href="#R26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R26">Kawaguchi, <em>et al.</em> 1999</a>) with deleterious changes in obesity and blood lipid profiles (<a href="#R32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R32">Leonard, <em>et al.</em> 2005</a>) that correspond with human T2DM. Since glucose and insulin levels have important outcomes on bone health (<a href="#R23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R23">Inzerillo &amp; Epstein. 2004</a>) and resistance artery function (<a href="#R34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R34">Lesniewski, <em>et al.</em> 2008</a>), the present study was designed to leverage known changes in ZDF rat T2DM disease progression to investigate their effects on potential bone-vascular interactions.</p>
<p id="P33">In conclusion, the present study demonstrates that few vasomotor alterations occur in the bone resistance vasculature in the pre-diabetic and short-term diabetic states. However, the femoral PNA shifts toward a more pro-vasoconstrictor phenotype with long-term diabetes, as indicated by decreases in endothelium-dependent (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1C</a>) and –independent (<a href="#F2" class="usa-link">Fig. 2</a>) vasodilation, enhanced noradrenergic (<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3A</a>) and myogenic (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4A</a>) vasoconstrictor responsiveness, and a decrease in the passive mechanical distensibility (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4B</a>). The enhanced vasoconstrictor responsiveness of the bone resistance vasculature was evident <em>in vivo</em> from decreases in bone and marrow blood flow (<a href="#F7" class="usa-link">Fig. 7A</a>) and vascular conductance (<a href="#F7" class="usa-link">Fig. 7B</a>) with long-term T2DM. Given the potential coupling of bone vascular signaling molecules (<a href="#F5" class="usa-link">Figs. 5</a>&amp;<a href="#F6" class="usa-link">6</a>) and bone fluid dynamics with skeletal remodeling (<a href="#R15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R15">Farhat &amp; Cauley. 2008</a>, <a href="#R31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R31">Lampropoulos, <em>et al.</em> 2012</a>, <a href="#R44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R44">Parfitt. 2000</a>, <a href="#R47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R47">Prisby, <em>et al.</em> 2012</a>), the present results suggest that the impairment of the bone circulation could contribute to the bone loss found to occur during the progression of T2DM.</p></section><section id="SM"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Supplementary Material</h2>
