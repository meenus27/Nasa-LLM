</div></section></section><section aria-label="Article content"><section class="body main-article-body"><section class="abstract" id="Abs1"><h2>SUMMARY</h2>
<p id="P1">The evolutionary success of parasitoid wasps, a highly diverse group of
insects widely used in biocontrol, depends on a variety of life history
strategies in conflict with those of their hosts [<a href="#R1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R1">1</a>]. <em>Drosophila melanogaster</em>
is a natural host of parasitic wasps of the genus <em>Leptopilina</em>.
Attack by <em>L. boulardi</em> (<em>Lb</em>), a specialist wasp
to flies of the melanogaster group, activates NF-κB-mediated humoral and
cellular immunity. Inflammatory blood cells mobilize and encapsulate
<em>Lb</em> eggs and embryos [<a href="#R2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R2">2</a>–<a href="#R5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R5">5</a>].
<em>L. heterotoma</em> (<em>Lh</em>), a generalist wasp,
kills larval blood cells and actively suppresses immune responses. Spiked
virus-like particles (VLPs) in wasp venom have clearly been linked to its
successful parasitism of <em>Drosophila</em> [<a href="#R6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R6">6</a>], but VLP composition and their biotic
nature have remained mysterious. Our proteomics studies reveal that VLPs lack
viral coat proteins but possess a pharmacopoeia of (a) eukaryotic vesicular
transport system, (b) immunity, and (c) previously unknown proteins. These novel
proteins distinguish <em>Lh</em> from <em>Lb</em> VLPs; notably,
some proteins specific to <em>Lh</em> VLPs possess sequence similarities
with bacterial secretion system proteins. Structure-informed analyses of an
abundant <em>Lh</em> VLP surface/spike-tip protein, p40, reveal
similarities to the needle-tip invasin proteins SipD/IpaD of Gram negative
bacterial type 3 secretion systems that breach immune barriers and deliver
virulence factors into mammalian cells. Our studies suggest that
<em>Lh</em> VLPs represent a new class of extracellular organelles
and share pathways for protein delivery with both eukaryotic microvesicles and
bacterial surface secretion systems. Given their mixed prokaryotic/eukaryotic
properties, we propose the term Mixed Strategy Extracellular Vesicles (MSEVs) to
replace VLP.</p>

<section id="S2"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Sister <em>Leptopilina</em> species produce different VLPs</h3>
<p id="P3">Larvae of parasitic wasps of the <em>Leptopilina</em> genus feed
on <em>Drosophila</em> larval host tissues, and eclose into free-living
adults (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig 1 A</a>). The VLP-producing
generalist/specialist <em>L. heterotoma</em>
(<em>Lh</em>)/<em>L. boulardi</em> (<em>Lb</em>)
wasps differ greatly in their infection of their natural host <em>D.
melanogaster</em>, as seen in the anterior lobes of the fly larval lymph
glands. Hematopoietic progenitors are housed in the gland’s medulla
(marked by <em>Dome-Meso-GFP</em>) and mature hemocytes, in the cortex
(GFP-negative, <a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig 1 B</a>).
<em>Lb</em> 17 infection induces lamellocyte differentiation (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig 1 C</a>), while <em>Lh</em> 14 attack
causes cell loss in both the medulla and the cortex, and few cells survive
(<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig 1 D</a>). The differences in the
virulence of <em>Lh</em> versus <em>Lb</em> is attributed to
differences in the VLPs produced by these wasps [<a href="#R7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R7">7</a>] and the mechanism of
<em>Lh</em>-induced cell killing are not understood. VLPs from
<em>Lb</em> 17 and <em>Lh</em> 14 differ in morphology;
<em>Lb</em> 17 VLPs have fewer spikes and are somewhat larger than
<em>Lh</em> VLPs (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig 1 E, F</a>)
[<a href="#R8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R8">8</a>]. A peripheral
membrane lipid bilayer (~ 10 nm) surrounds <em>Lh</em> VLPs,
which lack the typical coat-like structure found in some viruses (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig 1 G</a>). Because of their key role in wasp parasitism
of <em>Drosophila</em> spp. [<a href="#R4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R4">4</a>, <a href="#R5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R5">5</a>], we hypothesized
that differences in the composition of spiked particles produced in the venom of
both wasps underlie these contrasting infection strategies.</p>

<em>Lh</em> VLPs are rich in eukaryotic microvesicular
proteins</h3>
<p id="P5">No proteins with significant homology to structural proteins of any known
virus, including polydnaviruses (PDVs) associated with ichneumonid and braconid
wasps, which prey on Coleoptera, Hymenoptera and Lepidoptera [<a href="#R14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R14">14</a>], were found in the
<em>Lh</em> proteome. The 161 proteins common to <em>Lh</em>
14 and <em>Lh</em> NY VLP proteomes were categorized as (1) conserved
eukaryotic proteins with core biological function (42%, Class 1); (2)
virulence/immunity-associated proteins (24%, Class 2); or (3) novel
sequences (34%, Class 3) (<a href="#F2" class="usa-link">Figs 2</a>,
<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">3 A</a>). Of the ~ 160 VLP
proteins, 25% are <em>Lh</em>-specific (i.e., they are not
expressed by <em>Lb</em> 17 [<a href="#R10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R10">10</a>]) and most (27/41, 66%) of these proteins are
novel (Class 3) (<a href="#F2" class="usa-link">Fig 2</a>). Class 1 sequences
contain orthologs of <em>Drosophila</em> and mammalian extra- and
intracellular vesicle (including microvesicle and exosome) components as well as
membrane proteins (<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig 3 A – C</a>).
The presence of transmembrane (e.g., Na/K pump, SERCA calcium pump) and vesicle
transport proteins (e.g., H<sup>+</sup>-ATPase, heat shock cognate 70,
Rab proteins, and soluble NSF attachment protein receptor) (<a href="#F2" class="usa-link">Figs 2</a>, <a href="#F3" class="usa-link">3 A –
C</a>) in the proteome suggest that <em>Lh</em> VLPs are not
viral but instead share functional properties with eukaryotic extracellular
organelles called microvesicles, produced by animals cells, and specialized to
transfer proteins between different cell types [<a href="#R15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R15">15</a>].</p>

of predicted activities of Class 2 proteins likely facilitates
<em>Lh</em> success across a broad range of
<em>Drosophila</em> spp.</p>
<p id="P8">The abundance of novel proteins in Class 3 of the proteome was

also shows similarity to phage tail tape measure proteins (data not shown). In
addition to the multiple gene families in <em>Lh</em> VLPs that are
common to <em>Lb</em> and <em>G1</em> (e.g., RhoGAPs and MEPs;
<a href="#F2" class="usa-link">Figs 2</a>, <a href="#F3" class="usa-link">3

<a href="#SD1" class="usa-link">Data S2</a>). Multiple

<em>Lh</em> VLPs suggests that gene duplications and
neofunctionalization underlie the powerful virulence strategy of
<em>Lh</em>.</p></section><section id="S5"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Novel endomembrane-active GTPases</h3>

novel Class 3 sequences. Overlapping protein motifs,
[<em>Bacillus</em> PF05103; fungal PF15577]

3 secretion system (T3SS) of the self-assembling bacterial flagella are thought
to be ancestral to the ones found in the needle/injectisome of pathogens
[<a href="#R26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R26">26</a>].</p>
<p id="P12">Our previous antibody staining and inhibition studies uncovered an
abundant 40 kDa surface protein of <em>Lh</em> VLPs
(“p40”) which is necessary for lamellocyte lysis [<a href="#R9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R9">9</a>]. Early in VLP biogenesis, p40 is
associated with the membranes of canals that emanate from the cytoplasm of
secretory cells of the venom gland, where it is synthesized. Once in the canal
lumen, p40 is then associated with membranous vesicles that are released from
secretory cells. The vesicles mature into spiked VLPs which carry p40 both on
their surfaces and spikes [<a href="#R9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R9">9</a>,
<a href="#R27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R27">27</a>]. The bacterial T3SS
domain from IpaD/SipD/BipD (<a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/protein/PRK15330" class="usa-link" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">PRK15330</a>, E = 7.60<sup>−05</sup>)
proteins was found in residues 39-146 of p40 (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig
4 D</a>). This assignment was made by peptide mapping, cloning and
expressing polyhistidine-tagged p40 in bacteria. In western blot experiments,
anti-p40 antibody recognized this bacterially-expressed protein (<a href="#SD1" class="usa-link">Fig S1 A</a>). As expected,
p40 is detected in wasp venom extracts (<a href="#SD1" class="usa-link">Fig S1 B</a>). We were unable to identify a putative
<em>Lb</em> 17 or G1 p40 ortholog (<a href="#F2" class="usa-link">Fig
2</a>) and, to our knowledge, p40 represents the first eukaryotic protein
with an IpaD/SipD-like domain.</p>
<p id="P13">IpaD-like proteins from
<em>Shigella/Salmonella/Burkholderia</em> spp. are tip proteins of
T3SS injectisomes, mediating contact and regulated delivery of effectors into
the host cytoplasm of non-phagocytic cells [<a href="#R28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R28">28</a>]. IpaD expression in mammalian macrophages
triggers apoptosis [<a href="#R29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R29">29</a>],
reminiscent of the TUNEL-positive death of fly macrophages upon
<em>Lh</em> infection [<a href="#R7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R7">7</a>]. Unlike the bacterial proteins, p40 is predicted to
encode a C-terminal transmembrane helix in addition to an N-terminal secretion
signal (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Figs 4 D</a>; <a href="#SD1" class="usa-link">S1 C</a>). p40’s
transmembrane domain (this study) and its extracellular localization in venom
gland canals [<a href="#R27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R27">27</a>] suggest
that p40 exits venom gland secretory cells in association with microvesicle-like
structures. This interpretation is in agreement with the extracellular vesicular
proteomic profile of VLPs (<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig 3</a>).</p>
<p id="P14">Given the unexpected parallels in their structures and surface/tip
localizations, we hypothesized that, like IpaD/SipD on the T3SS injectisome, p40
on VLP spikes facilitates invasive contact with the plasma membrane of
non-phagocytic lamellocytes to deliver VLP contents. To test this idea, we
carried out <em>ab initio</em> modeling of p40. The knowledge-based
energetics of the p40 model are similar to crystal structures of similar length
(ProSA Z-Score = −6.23). 82% of model residues are found
in expected local environments (3D Verify). Superimposition of the p40 model
against IpaD confirmed the T3SS protein-like fold in p40 (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Figs 4 E</a>; <a href="#SD1" class="usa-link">S1 C</a>) [<a href="#R30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R30">30</a>].</p>
<p id="P15">Surprisingly, high-scoring matches to this fold included the vertebrate
actin-binding proteins spectrin and plectin (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig 4
F</a>, superimposed with p40), further strengthening structural parallels
between p40 and IpaD/SipD. Searches for the most similar structures within the
N-terminal half of IpaD family proteins also returned actin-binding proteins
(talin, vinculin, α-catenin) [<a href="#R31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R31">31</a>]. These proteins are known to reprogram the actin
cytoskeleton leading to the profuse membrane ruffling observed in non-phagocytic
mammalian cell invasions by <em>Salmonella</em> and
<em>Shigella</em> [<a href="#R31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R31">31</a>].</p></section></section><section id="S7"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">DISCUSSION</h2>
<p id="P16">The composition of <em>Lh</em> VLPs is complex and interesting in
multiple respects but the most conspicuous observations are an absence of viral
structural proteins and the presence of conserved eukaryotic proteins with
microvesicular signature. Abundant <em>Lh</em>-unique proteins, including
currently novel proteins, have an unexpected diversity of domains, especially those
previously found exclusively in prokaryotic proteins. The mechanisms that
contributed to the evolution of VLP proteins (horizontal gene transfer or others
[<a href="#R32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R32">32</a>]) remain unknown.</p>
<p id="P17"><em>Lh</em> VLPs lack the defined symmetry and external coat found
in many true viruses including PDVs. Reminiscent of eukaryotic organelles,
precursors and mature VLPs exhibit heterogeneity in their shapes, sizes, and spike
numbers [<a href="#R9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R9">9</a>, <a href="#R33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R33">33</a>]. Moreover, it is noteworthy that, unlike PDVs
which are fully formed in the cells of their origin and then released by lysis or
budding [<a href="#R14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R14">14</a>],
<em>Leptopilina</em> VLPs assume their final shape outside the cells in
which at least some of their proteins and vesicular constituents are synthesized
[<a href="#R9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R9">9</a>, <a href="#R27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R27">27</a>]. Also, there is currently no evidence for the
presence of nucleic acids in VLPs, which further distinguishes them from
DNA-containing PDVs.</p>
<p id="P18">VLPs are unlike endosymbiotic bacteria of <em>Leptopilina</em> wasps
[<a href="#R34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R34">34</a>].
Antibiotic-treatment of <em>L. victoriae</em> (sister species of
<em>Lh</em> that make VLPs and carry
cytoplasmic-incompatibility-inducing <em>Wolbachia</em>) did not affect
genomic amplification of p40 or SmGTPase01 genes. Furthermore, VLP gene loci were
amplified not only from female <em>Lh</em> 14 genomes but also male wasp
genomes, even though VLPs are not produced in males. BrdU incorporation studies did
not support the possibility of DNA-based VLP replication in the venom gland (our
unpublished results). The non-replicating nature of the particles, genomic encoding
of VLP protein genes, and vesicular signature of their proteome strongly suggest
that <em>Lh</em> VLPs are neither viruses nor endosymbiotic bacteria but
instead represent a new class of genomically-encoded, microvesicle-like organelles.
Extracellular vesicles are produced by prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells and VLPs
carry a diversity of potential immune-suppressive proteins. We, thus, propose the
alternative moniker, MSEV (Mixed Strategy Extracellular Vesicles), to replace the
VLP term.</p>
<p id="P19">Virulence factors of parasitic wasps have diversified in response to the
variety and complexities of their hosts’ immune systems. With a broad host
range [<a href="#R3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R3">3</a>], <em>Lh</em>
wasps parasitize many <em>Drosophila</em> spp. whose own distinct immune
responses are supported by varying numbers and types of blood cells [<a href="#R35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R35">35</a>]. This may explain, first, the
diversity of putative virulence and cell death proteins with homologs across the
biological kingdoms [viral (diedel), bacterial (p40, Syd-like), and
eukaryotic (fire ant allergen, B-cell receptor-associated protein 31,
etc.)], and second, the presence of MSEV paralogs
[<em>Lh</em>-specific GTPase and <em>Lh-/Lb</em>-common
Rho GAP, MEPs, and diedel gene families] in the proteome. Multiple members
presumably perform redundant or overlapping cell-specific functions for rapid and
robust immune suppression, much like the IκB/Cactus-like ankyrin-repeat
proteins of distantly-related bracoviral and ichnoviral PDV proteins that block
NF-κB signaling [<a href="#R36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R36">36</a>, <a href="#R37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R37">37</a>].</p>
<p id="P20">The parallels between the 3D-structure and locations of p40 with the
well-characterized T3SS IpaD-like prokaryotic proteins are provocative and suggest
that p40 likely contributes to <em>Lh</em> MSEVs’ unique blood
cell-killing activities. T3SS assemblies are widespread and are used by bacteria to
infect plants and animals [<a href="#R26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R26">26</a>]. <em>Pseudomonas aeruginosa</em> use their own T3SS to
rapidly infect and kill adult flies. While cytotoxic to macrophages, <em>P.
aeruginosa</em> infection activates the NF-κB-dependent IMD
antimicrobial pathway [<a href="#R38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R38">38</a>].
It is thus possible that elements of the pathogenic bacterial systems have been
co-opted by wasps to attack the fly’s cellular immune system. In this
scenario, intracellular protein complexes within lamellocytes would be under direct
selective pressure to respond to MSEV-based T3SS-like virulence.</p>
<p id="P21">The presence of prokaryotic-like (particularly, T3SS/flagellar-like)
proteins, hints at the possibility that either MSEV spikes evolved from primordial
flagellar/needle-like structures or they share evolutionary history with such
structures. These findings also support a hypothesis proposed by Martin and
colleagues [<a href="#R39" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R39">39</a>] that the
eukaryotic endomembrane system may have arisen from bacterial outer membrane
vesicles. In this regard, characterization of the prokaryotic protein motifs that
comprise nearly 10% of the novel proteins outlined above will be especially
revealing. The molecular mechanisms by which MSEV proteins deplete and destroy its
well-characterized hosts’ immune system will suggest how virulence factors
are acquired by insect parasites, how these factors evolve, and how insects might
serve as reservoirs of disease. Answering these questions is likely to lead to new
cost-effective therapies for treating emerging infections and opportunistic
diseases.</p></section><section id="S8"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">STAR METHODS</h2>

<section id="S15"><h4 class="pmc_sec_title">Wasp infections, dissections, and imaging</h4>
<p id="P26">For experimental infections, cultures containing both male and
female mid to late second instar <em>D. melanogaster</em> larvae
were exposed to 10 – 12 mated, 1 week PE, <em>Lh</em> 14 or
<em>Lb</em> 17 female wasps [<a href="#R3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R3">3</a>] for 12 hours at 25°C. Wasps
were removed and larvae were allowed to recover in the medium for 5 hours at
25°C. Infected larvae were scooped from the medium, scored for age,
matched to <em>y w</em> control wasp-infected larvae, washed in
deionized water, washed in phosphate buffered saline (1X PBS, pH 7.2,
25°C), and dissected to obtain larval lymph gland tissue. Infection
was validated during dissection by the presence of wasp eggs (free floating
or attached onto larval gut tissue) and uninfected hosts were discarded.</p>
<p id="P27">Dissections and slide preparations were performed at 25°C.
Tissue was dissected on untreated glass slides by bleeding larvae and
pulling the cuticle bilaterally below the mouth hooks to leave the mouth
parts, lymph gland, and dorsal vessel. After air drying, excess tissue was
removed. Samples were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde, washed,
permeabilized (1X PBS with 0.3% Triton X-100), and washed again in
1X PBS. Counterstains were applied (Rhodamine-tagged Phalloidin at 0.5 mg/ml
and Hoechst 33258 at 0.02 mg/mL) and samples were washed and then mounted in
VectaShield.</p>
<p id="P28">Samples were imaged with a Zeiss 510 confocal at 40X. Laser settings
were based on GFP negative and uninfected control tissue and all images were
scanned using the same instrument settings. Images were processed with Zeiss
LSM image browser. Figures were compiled in Adobe Photoshop v12.0.4 and CC
2015.5 and Illustrator CC 2015.</p>
<p id="P29">Results shown in <a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig 1</a> were
validated in dissections of at least twenty animals (n = 20 lymph
glands) each, infected by either wasp. Results have also been validated by
infections and dissection of at least three other <em>D.
melanogaster</em> genotypes with replicates of at least five animals
(n = 5 lymph glands). All infection-related changes to lymph gland
morphologies were included in our conclusions (no data were excluded). No
strategies for randomization and/or stratification, blinding, or sample-size
estimation were utilized, or found to be necessary, for this study.</p></section><section id="S16"><h4 class="pmc_sec_title">VLP purification</h4>
<p id="P30">Three hundred each <em>Lh</em> 14 [<a href="#R3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R3">3</a>] and <em>Lh</em> NY
[<a href="#R9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R9">9</a>] female wasps
(see Wasp Strain section, above, for husbandry and housing details) of 1 to
2 weeks PE were sacrificed by submerging in 70% ethanol and rinsed
at least three times each in deionized water and 0.1X PBS (pH 7.2) at
4°C. Venom gland complexes were dissected in PBS by grasping the
ovipositor and gently pulling. Only intact, dissected venom gland complexes
with a VLP long gland, reservoir, and ovipositor were used in the
purification protocol; other body parts were discarded. Tissue was collected
in 1X PBS (4°C), gently crushed with a sterile pestle in
200μL of 1X PBS (4°C), shook vigorously in 300 μL 1X
PBS (4°C), pulse agitated, and added to the top of a 4°C
Nycodenz gradient. The gradient of 50 to 10% Nycodenz in 1X PBS (5
bands of 900 μL, each) was prepared in a clean, RNA-free Beckman 9
cm centrifuge tube and pre-chilled. Whole VLPs were separated from other
tissues into a single band by ultracentrifugation (20,000 RPM, 7°C,
80 mins). The VLP band was removed from the gradient, washed with 1X PBS
(4°C), pelleted (11,700 RPM, 7°C, 35 mins), resuspended in a
minimum of 1X PBS (4°C), and stored (4°C). Extraction of
VLPs from ~ 300 long gland complexes has consistently yielded
upwards of thousands of VLPs.</p></section><section id="S17"><h4 class="pmc_sec_title">Electron microscopy</h4>
<p id="P31">For cryo electron microscopy (EM), purified VLPs were pipetted onto
a holey carbon coated grid. Excess fluid was blotted (Whatman #1)
and the grid was plunge frozen (liquid ethane) and stored in liquid
nitrogen. Samples were visualized with a Technai G2 (200kV) at the New York
Structural Biology Center. Membrane surrounding <em>Lh</em> VLPs
(observed via cryo EM in <a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig 1</a>) was
present in all VLPs of more than two sample preparations and this result
confirmed previously-published findings from transmission electron
microscopy experiments [<a href="#R6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R6">6</a>,
<a href="#R9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R9">9</a>].</p>
<p id="P32">For scanning EM (SEM), purified VLPs (washed and re-suspended, PBS)
were fixed in glutaraldehyde (3% in 0.085M sodium cacodylate buffer,
overnight, 4°C), followed by cacodylate buffer (0.085M, 1 hr,
4°C). After washing (glass distilled water) and fixing in osmium
tetroxide (1% in 0.085M cacodylate buffer, 1 hr, 4°C), VLPs
were filtered onto polycarbonate membranes (0.1 μm pores). Filtered
samples were then dehydrated in serial ethanol washes (technical grade, to
70%) and stored overnight. The membranes were washed (amyl acetate),
dried, and mounted on pin stubs. Membranes were gold-palladium plated and
stored at 60°C until imaged on a Ziess Supra 55 SEM. Parameters for
SEM imaging of <em>Lh</em>, <em>Lv</em>, and
<em>Lb</em> VLPs were consistent for all samples and technical
replicates; data presented are representative of many particles from at
least two replicates per species’ VLP.</p></section><section id="S18"><h4 class="pmc_sec_title">MS/MS analysis of <em>Lh</em> VLP proteins</h4>
<p id="P33">Purified VLPs were separated on a 1-D SDS-PAGE gel as per standard
protocols. Bands were excised, destained, reduced, alkylated, and trypsin
digested. Peptides from combined additional lanes (L457) were also analyzed.
Peptides were extracted (Applied Biosystems POROS 20 R2 beads), cleaned-up
(C18 ZipTips), dried, and reconstituted in 2%
acetonitrile/0.1% formic acid. <em>Lh</em> 14 peptides were
trapped (Waters Symmetry® C<sub>18</sub> trap column (180 μm
× 100 mm, 5 μm particles)), washed, and separated on a
Waters BEH130 C<sub>18</sub> column (1.7 μm particle size) (Waters
NanoAcquity UPLC (Milford, MA)). <em>Lh</em> NY peptides were
separated on a Waters BEH130 C<sub>18</sub> column (75 μm ×
150 mm). The MS analysis was performed on an LTQ-Orbitrap (ThermoFisher,
CA).</p>
<p id="P34">The instrument RAW files were analyzed using Proteome Discoverer
(PD) 1.4.0.288 with a work template that contained a Target Decoy PSM
Validator node (peptide spectrum matches) with both Sequest and Mascot
algorithms. Mascot searches, independent of PD, were also conducted. Peptide
mass tolerance was set to 10 ppm, and the fragment mass tolerance was 0.6
Da. The enzyme was set to “trypsin” with two maximum missed
cleavage sites and the search was against VLPSwiss_20140319.fasta (1332969
entries). Carbamidomethylation of cysteine was specified as a fixed
modification. Deamidation of asparagine and glutamine and oxidation of
histidine, methionine and tryptophan were specified as variable
modifications. Methylation at aspartic acid residues was specified only for
Mascot analyses conducted without PD. The .msf output files were integrated
into Scaffold (version Scaffold_4.7.3, Proteome Software Inc.) which was
used to validate MS/MS based peptide and protein identifications.</p></section><section id="S19"><h4 class="pmc_sec_title">Identification of <em>Lh</em> VLP proteins</h4>
<p id="P35">VLP proteins (<a href="#SD2" class="usa-link">Tables S1</a>, <a href="#SD3" class="usa-link">S2</a>) reported here had at least two proteomic peptides,
identified at 99% or greater probability by either PD or solo Mascot
searches, that aligned to a target sequence. In addition, four proteins are
included in the list where only one peptide aligned at 99% more
probability (see below, Protein sequence annotations subsection).
Full-length target protein sequences were translated from RNA-Seq
<em>Lh</em> 14 (<a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/GAJC00000000.1" class="usa-link" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">GAJC00000000.1</a>) transcripts translated to ORFs
[<a href="#R10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R10">10</a>].</p>
<p id="P36">The number of aligned peptides varied slightly between PD and Mascot
analyses conducted without PD. The PD results have been deposited to the
ProteomeXchange Consortium via the PRIDE [<a href="#R42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R42">42</a>] partner repository (see <a href="#SD6" class="usa-link">KRT</a> for link).</p>
<p id="P37">In this report, VLP composition is based on the subset of proteins
common to both the <em>Lh</em> 14 and NY strains. The common,
full-length VLP protein ORFs (<a href="#SD2" class="usa-link">Tables S1</a>, <a href="#SD3" class="usa-link">S2</a>) were annotated via <strong>(A)</strong> primary
sequence analyses, and <strong>(B)</strong> structure-based analyses and
predictions for select proteins (described below). Note that the
<em>Lh</em> 14 dataset is larger and select <em>Lh</em>
14-unique sequences were preferentially included.</p></section><section id="S20"><h4 class="pmc_sec_title">Additional verification of protein sequences</h4>
<p id="P38">The VLP peptides were also searched against other proteomes (Uniprot
<em>D. melanogaster</em>, <em>A. mellifera</em>,
<em>H. sapiens</em>, viral, prokaryotic and archaea databases
[<a href="#R43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R43">43</a>]). The
full-length proteins identified from the RNA-Seq ORFs [<a href="#R10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R10">10</a>] were BLASTed against
<em>Lh</em> ESTs (NCBI LIBEST 028179 and 028205, [<a href="#R11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R11">11</a>, <a href="#R12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R12">12</a>]), providing an alternative method of ORF sequence
verification. This step also identified the full, or near full, length VLP
protein clones in our <em>Lh</em> EST collection (e.g.,
<em>Lh</em> VLP Sm &amp; LgGTPas01). Roughly 20% and
~ 45% of the common VLP proteins were identified in the

identification of absence/presence of expression of each <em>Lh</em>
VLP protein in <em>Lb</em> 17 and G1 abdominal transcriptomes (<a href="#F2" class="usa-link">Fig 2</a>).)</p></section><section id="S21"><h4 class="pmc_sec_title">Protein sequence annotations</h4>
<p id="P39">The automated, pipe-line annotation algorithms of (1) BLAST2GO, E
&lt;= 10<sup>−5</sup> (v2.7.0) [<a href="#R44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R44">44</a>], (2) InterProScan (v5.3–46)
[<a href="#R45" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R45">45</a>], and (3)
FastAnnotator [<a href="#R46" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R46">46</a>]
were used to characterize all (common set) VLP proteins identified from VLP
peptide alignments to <em>Lh</em> transcriptome ORFs (See above).
Multiple bioinformatic methods were used to avoid algorithm bias and
increase accuracy. Two related criteria were used as measures of reliability
of annotations: (a) E values (E &lt;= 10<sup>−5</sup>);

<p id="P43">A high-quality structural model was created for SmGTPase01 using a

Loopy [<a href="#R68" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R68">68</a>] and SCWRL4

<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig 4 C</a>; the remaining details of
the model will be published elsewhere.</p>
<p id="P44">The SmGTPase01 co-factor, Mg2+, was placed in the active

[<a href="#R71" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R71">71</a>]. GTP was
placed and checked for energy minimization in the SmGTPase01 active site
with Autodock Vina [<a href="#R72" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R72">72</a>]. The cofactor and substrate placements were confirmed
with predictions from COACH [<a href="#R73" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R73">73</a>], BSP-SLIM [<a href="#R74" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R74">74</a>], and 3D Ligand [<a href="#R75" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R75">75</a>].</p>

previously characterized) were met. Confidence in quantitative bioinformatics

protein model analyses, p-values for enrichments) is based on the algorithms
intrinsic to these methods and is described in the corresponding primary
references. Where possible, more than one computational approach (supported by
different algorithms and metaservers) was used to strengthen interpretation by
avoiding biases arising from a single computational methodology. No additional
statistical analyses or methods to determine assumption credibility and/or

here. The first three sequences of ~ 300 residues, are SmGTPases,
while the following five sequences of ~ 500 residues are LgGTPases.
Among the eight family members shown here, the percent similarities within
the N-terminal regions range from ~ 25 to 60% and average
pairwise similarity over their full lengths is 45%. Only seven
insertion/deletion sites exist in this 222 amino acid alignment and large
blocks of identity (&gt; 80% of the alignment length) are
present. Highly similar secretion signal peptides are predicted at the
N-termini of all GTPases (not shown).</p>
<p id="P67"><strong>(B)</strong> The five most-similar ORFs found in the NCBI TSA
transcriptome for G1 (<a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/GAIW00000000.1" class="usa-link" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">GAIW00000000.1</a>) are shown aligned with
<em>Lh</em> VLP Sm/LgGTPase01s. Like the <em>Lh</em> VLP
GTPases, several putative G1 GTPases have predicted secretion signal
peptides (not shown). Potential G1 homologs demonstrate &gt;=
31% and 35% identity (E &lt;=
3e<sup>−03</sup> and 4e<sup>−59</sup>) to
<em>Lh</em> VLP SmGTPase01 and LgGTPase01, respectively (given
75% alignment coverage).</p>
<p id="P68"><strong>(C)</strong> The five most-similar ORFs found in the NCBI TSA
transcriptome for <em>Lc</em> (<a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/GAXY00000000.2" class="usa-link" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">GAXY00000000.2</a>) are shown aligned
with <em>Lh</em> VLP Sm/LgGTPase01s. Similar to <em>Lh</em>
and G1 GTPases, multiple <em>Lc</em> GTPases have predicted signal
sequence peptides. The <em>Lc</em> transcripts identified show
similarity in the N-terminal G domain, but are generally much shorter than
the <em>Lh</em> VLP GTPases. <em>Lh</em> VLP SmGTPase01
shows &lt;= 34% identity to <em>Lc</em>
transcripts (&gt;= 75% alignment criterion, E
&gt;= 2e<sup>−34</sup>) and LgGTPase01 shows
&lt;= 44% identity (E &gt;=
3e<sup>−99</sup>), respectively. <em>Lc</em> also
parasitizes <em>Drosophila</em> spp., but its comparative virulence
and phylogenetic distance are poorly characterized in comparison to
<em>Lh</em> and <em>Lb</em>.</p>
<p id="P69"><strong>(D)</strong> The most similar <em>Lh</em> and
<em>Lc</em> sequences have been extracted from <strong>(C)</strong>

<em>D. melanogaster</em> diedel, a putative homolog, is a
negative regulator of the JAK-STAT pathway.</p>
<p><strong>(B)</strong> A <em>Lh</em> VLP enhancin-like protein is
aligned with similar prokaryotic sequences (BLAST2GO and Delta BLAST nr, 20
– 42% identity; 5.33e<sup>−04</sup>
&lt;= E &lt;= 9e<sup>−03</sup>). Multiple
sequences from <em>Yersinia, Listeria</em>, and other pathogenic
bacterial species were found in our BLASTs and, in a few cases, these
sequences were annotated as M60 peptidases. The VLP sequence contains a
putative secretion signal motif, but no known domains were identified. It is
notable that enhancin homologs encoded in viral genomes were not uncovered
in our searches.</p>
<p><strong>(C)</strong> A <em>Lh</em> VLP GH18 chitinase-like
superfamily (CDD cd02873 domain, E = 0) protein is aligned with five
similar sequences (BLASTp nr, 50 – 75% identity; 0
&lt;= E &lt;= 2 e<sup>−143</sup>) from
other insects, including the yellow-fever mosquito, the Tobacco horn worm,
and three parasitoid wasps (the Jewel and two Braconid wasps). The VLP
protein sequence encodes a predicted secretion signal and is predicted to be
an Imaginal disc growth factor (Idgf)-like protein, a superfamily that
diverged from chitinase-like proteins.</p>
<p><strong>(D)</strong> The <em>Lh</em> VLP venom allergen-like
(CDD domain cd05380, E = 1.95 e<sup>−42</sup>) protein is
aligned with five similar sequences (BLASTp nr, 37 – 42%
identity; 6 e<sup>−41</sup>&lt;= E &lt;= 6
e<sup>−34</sup>) from other insects, including the red imported
fire ant, the Panamanian leafcutter ant, and three parasitoid wasps (Jewel,
a Braconid wasp, and a Chalcidoid egg parasite). A eukaryotic-specific SCP
domain, best characterized in plant pathogenesis defense proteins, has been
identified, as well as a putative secretion signal.</p>
<p><strong>(E)</strong> The <em>Lh</em> VLP Bap31-like (CDD domain
cd05380, E = 1.95 e<sup>−42</sup>) protein is aligned with
five similar sequences (BLASTp nr, 59 – 76% identity; 1
e<sup>−126</sup> &lt;= E &lt;= 4
e<sup>−83</sup>) from other insects, including two species of
ant (Florida carpenter and red imported fire ant), wasp (a Braconid and the
Jewel parasitoid), and <em>Drosophila mojavensis</em>. Canonical
Bap31 proteins regulate ER-stress-mediated apoptosis. Similar to these
proteins, the <em>Lh</em> VLP protein is predicted to encode three
transmembrane helices.</p>
<p><strong>(F)</strong> The <em>Lh</em> VLP knottin-like (pfam11410
domain, E = 5.19 e<sup>−03</sup>) protein is aligned with
five similar sequences (BLASTp nr, 33 – 52% identity; 3
e<sup>−08</sup> &lt;= E &lt;= 2
e<sup>−02</sup>), four from insect species and one from the
eudicotyledon, <em>Mesembryanthemum crystallinum</em>. Among the
most similar putative homologs are sequences from diverse insects:
Hymenoptera (sawfly and wasp), Diptera, and Hemipteran species. The sequence
contains a putative secretion signal motif and three predicted disulfide
bridges. 40% of the VLP sequence shows notable conservation
(54% identity) when compared to a secreted ion channel toxin from
the spider <em>Chilobrachys guangxiensis</em> (Arachnida:
Theraphosidae). Knottins are classified as a cystine-rich plant
antimicrobial peptide family.</p>
<p><strong>(G)</strong> The <em>Lh</em> VLP hemolymph juvenile
hormone binding protein (JHBP)-like (pfam06585 domain, E = 6.10
e<sup>−17</sup>) protein is aligned with four similar sequences
(BLASTp nr, 24 – 30% identity; 1 e<sup>−11</sup>
&lt;= E &lt;= 6 e<sup>−03</sup>) from a
wheat stem sawfly, the Panamanian leafcutter ant, the diamondback moth, and
<em>Drosophila melanogaster</em>. No high identity homologs were
found, but moderately similar homologs were found encoded by wasps, ants,
and arthropods, in general. Homologs in <em>Drosophila</em> spp.
were found, but are not functionally well characterized. The regions of
similarities are within 80 to 90% of the length of the VLP protein
sequence, but largely only in comparisons to putative homologs from other
hymenoptera.</p>
</div>

<span>5</span><p><strong>Data S2: <em>Lh</em> VLP GTPase alignments (Related
to</strong>
<a href="#F2" class="usa-link">Figs 2</a>, <a href="#F3" class="usa-link">3A</a>, &amp; <a href="#F4" class="usa-link">4A –
C</a><strong>)</strong></p>
<p>This document provides alignments of select small and large VLP
GTPases (including SmGTPases01/LgGTPases01) with putative homologs/paralogs
from <strong>(A)</strong>
<em>Lh</em>; <strong>(B)</strong> prokaryotic and eukaryotic species;
<strong>(C)</strong>
<em>Ganaspis sp.1</em> (G1); and <strong>(D, E)</strong>
<em>Leptopilina clavipes</em> (<em>Lc</em>). The far N-and
C-termini show the most variation among the sequences and were trimmed. The
residue range displayed is indicated after the species of origin. The
coloring scheme is according to physiochemical properties. ORF =
open reading frame.</p>
<p><strong>(A)</strong> Eight <em>Lh</em> VLP GTPases are presented
here. Additional putative GTPase family members exist, but are not presented
here. The first three sequences of ~ 300 residues, are SmGTPases,
while the following five sequences of ~ 500 residues are LgGTPases.
Among the eight family members shown here, the percent similarities within
the N-terminal regions range from ~ 25 to 60% and average
pairwise similarity over their full lengths is 45%. Only seven
insertion/deletion sites exist in this 222 amino acid alignment and large
blocks of identity (&gt; 80% of the alignment length) are
present. Highly similar secretion signal peptides are predicted at the
N-termini of all GTPases (not shown).</p>
<p><strong>(B)</strong> The five most-similar ORFs found in the NCBI TSA
transcriptome for G1 (<a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/GAIW00000000.1" class="usa-link" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">GAIW00000000.1</a>) are shown aligned with
<em>Lh</em> VLP Sm/LgGTPase01s. Like the <em>Lh</em> VLP
GTPases, several putative G1 GTPases have predicted secretion signal
peptides (not shown). Potential G1 homologs demonstrate &gt;=
31% and 35% identity (E &lt;=
3e<sup>−03</sup> and 4e<sup>−59</sup>) to
<em>Lh</em> VLP SmGTPase01 and LgGTPase01, respectively (given
75% alignment coverage).</p>
<p><strong>(C)</strong> The five most-similar ORFs found in the NCBI TSA
transcriptome for <em>Lc</em> (<a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/GAXY00000000.2" class="usa-link" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">GAXY00000000.2</a>) are shown aligned
with <em>Lh</em> VLP Sm/LgGTPase01s. Similar to <em>Lh</em>
and G1 GTPases, multiple <em>Lc</em> GTPases have predicted signal
sequence peptides. The <em>Lc</em> transcripts identified show
similarity in the N-terminal G domain, but are generally much shorter than
the <em>Lh</em> VLP GTPases. <em>Lh</em> VLP SmGTPase01
shows &lt;= 34% identity to <em>Lc</em>
transcripts (&gt;= 75% alignment criterion, E
&gt;= 2e<sup>−34</sup>) and LgGTPase01 shows
&lt;= 44% identity (E &gt;=
3e<sup>−99</sup>), respectively. <em>Lc</em> also
parasitizes <em>Drosophila</em> spp., but its comparative virulence
and phylogenetic distance are poorly characterized in comparison to
<em>Lh</em> and <em>Lb</em>.</p>
<p><strong>(D)</strong> The most similar <em>Lh</em> and
<em>Lc</em> sequences have been extracted from <strong>(C)</strong>
and are shown here.</p>
