<p>ANN1 and ANN2 regulate Arabidopsis primary root growth by facilitating post-phloem transport of sugar in root tips.</p></section><section class="abstract" id="abstract2"><h2>Abstract</h2>
<p>Annexins are a multigene family of calcium-dependent membrane-binding proteins that play important roles in plant cell signaling. Annexins are multifunctional proteins, and their function in plants is not comprehensively understood. Arabidopsis (<em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em>) annexins ANN1 and ANN2 are 64% identical in their primary structure, and both are highly expressed in seedlings. Here, we showed that <em>ann-mutant</em> seedlings grown in the absence of sugar show decreased primary root growth and altered columella cells in root caps; however, these mutant defects are rescued by Suc, Glc, or Fru. In seedlings grown without sugar, significant up-regulation of photosynthetic gene expression and chlorophyll accumulation was found in <em>ann-mutant</em> cotyledons compared to that in wild type, which indicates potential sugar starvation in the roots of <em>ann-mutant</em> seedlings. Unexpectedly, the overall sugar content of <em>ann-mutant</em> primary roots was significantly higher than that of wild-type roots when grown without sugar. To examine the diffusion of sugar along the entire root to the root tip, we examined the unloading pattern of carboxyfluorescein dye and found that post-phloem sugar transport was impaired in <em>ann-mutant</em> root tips compared to that in wild type. Increased levels of ROS and callose were detected in the root tips of <em>ann-mutant</em> seedlings grown without Suc, the latter of which would restrict plasmodesmal sugar transport to root tips. Our results indicate that ANN1 and ANN2 play an important role in post-phloem sugar transport to the root tip, which in turn indirectly influences photosynthetic rates in cotyledons. This study expands our understanding of the function of annexins in plants.</p></section><hr class="headless">
<p>Annexins are multifunctional proteins that have been found in all plants and vertebrate species. Their tertiary structure with calcium-binding domains is evolutionarily conserved in all species (<a href="#bib48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib48">Morgan et al., 2004</a>, <a href="#bib49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib49">2006</a>; <a href="#bib34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib34">Konopka-Postupolska et al., 2011</a>; <a href="#bib13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib13">Clark et al., 2012</a>). In plants, annexins have been localized in many parts of the cell, including the plasma membrane, the Golgi apparatus, secretory vesicles, vacuolar membranes, and the nucleus. Certain annexins are also found in the extracellular matrix as secreted proteins.</p>
<p>On the molecular level, certain plant annexins serve as components of calcium channels or regulate calcium channel activity to help initiate and amplify calcium signals in plants (<a href="#bib37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib37">Laohavisit et al., 2010</a>; <a href="#bib36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib36">Laohavisit and Davies, 2011</a>; <a href="#bib15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib15">Davies, 2014</a>). On the cellular level, one of the earliest functions discovered for annexins in plant cells was meditation of vesicle trafficking and secretion (<a href="#bib32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib32">Konopka-Postupolska and Clark, 2017</a>). On the whole-plant level, key discoveries of annexin function have been carried out using mutants of the model plant, Arabidopsis (<em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em>), where there are eight different annexins that show differential expression patterns (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Cantero et al., 2006</a>). Several of these mutant studies show that certain Arabidopsis annexins function to provide stress tolerance in response to a variety of stress conditions (<a href="#bib33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib33">Konopka-Postupolska et al., 2009</a>; <a href="#bib14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib14">Dalal et al., 2014</a>; <a href="#bib52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib52">Richards et al., 2014</a>; <a href="#bib64" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib64">Wang et al., 2015</a>; <a href="#bib41" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib41">Liao et al., 2017</a>). For example, overexpression of ANN1 in Arabidopsis results in increased antioxidant activity and confers drought tolerance (<a href="#bib33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib33">Konopka-Postupolska et al., 2009</a>).</p>
<p>Plant annexins are expressed in all stages of plant growth and development. Recently, <a href="#bib61" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib61">Thieme et al. (2015)</a> found that some Arabidopsis annexin transcripts can be transported across the whole plant by phloem sap. Annexin proteins have also been identified in the phloem sap of several different plant species (<a href="#bib26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib26">Kehr, 2006</a>), and in Arabidopsis, ANN1 was found in phloem exudate of rosette leaves (<a href="#bib24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib24">Guelette et al., 2012</a>). Immunolocalization of a pea annexin protein in young developing sieve elements led to the suggestion that annexins may play a role in phloem cell development (<a href="#bib11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib11">Clark et al., 1992</a>). In situ transcript localization studies found that <em>ANN1</em> is expressed in phloem and phloem parenchyma in Arabidopsis seedlings (<a href="#bib12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib12">Clark et al., 2001</a>). However, the physiological importance of annexins in phloem remains undefined.</p>
<p>Phloem transport links sources to sinks and is a key passageway for communication between leaves and roots. Sugars generated from photosynthetic leaves (source organs) and transported through the phloem to roots (sink organs) serve both as metabolic substrates to fuel plant growth and development and as signals that integrate plant responses to environmental stimuli, hormones, and changes in nutrition status (<a href="#bib45" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib45">Martin et al., 2002</a>; <a href="#bib47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib47">Moore et al., 2003</a>; <a href="#bib57" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib57">Smeekens et al., 2010</a>; <a href="#bib38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib38">Lastdrager et al., 2014</a>; <a href="#bib43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib43">Ljung et al., 2015</a>; <a href="#bib18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib18">Dobrenel et al., 2016</a>; <a href="#bib19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib19">Dodds and Lagudah, 2016</a>; <a href="#bib35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib35">Kühn, 2016</a>; <a href="#bib40" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib40">Li and Sheen, 2016</a>; <a href="#bib2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib2">Baena-González and Hanson, 2017</a>). Plants have developed complex sugar-sensing systems to monitor and respond to continuous fluctuations in sugar status caused by the circadian clock and frequent environmental changes (<a href="#bib59" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib59">Stitt and Zeeman, 2012</a>).</p>
<p>Multiple recent studies have revealed significant roles of sugar in plant growth (<a href="#bib38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib38">Lastdrager et al., 2014</a>) and development (<a href="#bib65" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib65">Wingler, 2018</a>). For example, sugar availability to axillary buds is a key determinant for shoot branching. Removal of pea shoot tips induces a sufficient flow of sugar into axillary buds to repress the expression of the branching inhibitor gene <em>Branched1</em>, which highlights the importance of sugars in mediating apical dominance (<a href="#bib46" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib46">Mason et al., 2014</a>). Sugars in photosynthetic leaves function as mobile signals to repress the expression of microRNA156 and thus promote the maturation of juvenile plants (<a href="#bib66" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib66">Yu et al., 2013</a>).</p>
<p>Important sugar responses are also found in roots, whose growth depends on Suc transported from photosynthetic leaves. This dependence underscores the importance of understanding the effects of sugars on root growth and development. Although sugar controls primary and lateral root growth in a dose-dependent manner (<a href="#bib22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib22">Freixes et al., 2002</a>), sugar demand in roots continuously changes due to environmental signals (<a href="#bib39" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib39">Lemoine et al., 2013</a>) and developmental stages. Having a sugar-sensing system in roots that can efficiently monitor sugar levels would be important for root and whole-plant growth. However, studies of sugar signaling in roots are generally scant.</p>
<p>Root growth is closely associated with sugar concentrations (<a href="#bib62" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib62">Thompson et al., 2017</a>). Greater soluble sugar content has been found in fast-growing roots of pruned barley plants, whereas slow-growing barley roots contain less soluble sugar (<a href="#bib21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib21">Farrar and Jones, 1986</a>). Suc generated by photosynthesis in cotyledons is required to regulate primary root growth (<a href="#bib27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib27">Kircher and Schopfer, 2012</a>). Suc promotes primary root elongation in a dose-dependent manner. The higher the exogenous Suc concentration, the faster primary roots elongate (<a href="#bib22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib22">Freixes et al., 2002</a>). Furthermore, the finding that neighboring secondary roots in the same root system have different growth rates in their elongation zone as a function of local sugar concentration also favors the conclusion that sugar is a major determinant of root growth (<a href="#bib22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib22">Freixes et al., 2002</a>).</p>
<p>Because local sugar concentrations play critical roles in regulating root growth, it is important to learn more about the mechanisms that control sugar transport into the growing zones of the root. Sugars generated by photosynthesis in leaves are loaded into sieve elements of the phloem and then delivered by phloem into roots (<a href="#bib16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib16">De Schepper et al., 2013</a>). According to pressure flow theory, it is the osmotic differential in phloem sieve elements that drives the bulk flow from source photosynthetic organs to sink organs such as roots. After sugar reaches its destination, it must be unloaded from phloem sieve elements into surrounding cells. <a href="#bib53" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib53">Ross-Elliott et al. (2017)</a> have proposed that after sugar reaches the connecting tissue between conducting phloem and sink tissues, called protophloem, it is unloaded into surrounding cells through funnel plasmodesmata and then diffuses freely along a concentration gradient into cells of the root tip in a symplastic way. The unloading of sugar into the elongation zone, mitotic zone, and root cap accommodates the high-energy demands of fast-growing regions in roots. Here, we describe the effect of knocking out two of the eight members of the annexin gene family in Arabidopsis, namely <em>ANN1</em> and <em>ANN2</em>, on the diffusion of sugars from the unloading zone to root tips, and we discuss how these two annexins play an important mechanistic role in post-phloem transport of sugars into root tips.</p>
<section id="s01"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">RESULTS</h2>
<section id="s02"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Inhibition of Primary Root Growth in <em>ANN1</em> and <em>ANN2</em> Knockout Mutants Is Rescued by Exogenous Suc Independent of Its Osmotic Effects</h3>
<p>In 3-d-old seedlings, all <em>ANN1</em> and <em>ANN2</em> knockout mutants (<em>ann1-2</em>, <em>ann1-3</em>, <em>ann2-1</em>, and <em>ann1-2/ann2-1</em>) had primary root lengths that were significantly shorter compared to wild type when grown on agar plates containing no Suc (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1, A and B</a>). However, these knockout mutants had the same hypocotyl lengths compared to that in wild type (<a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.18.00713/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Fig. S1</a>). To rule out that the differences in primary root lengths were due to differences in germination, we also measured growth rates in both mutants and wild type under the same condition (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1B</a>). Consistent with the root length data, the primary roots of <em>ann1-2</em>, <em>ann1-3</em>, <em>ann2-1</em>, and <em>ann1-2/ann2-1</em> grew significantly slower compared to that in wild type.</p>

<p>An RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) analysis comparing the transcript abundance in total mRNA extracted from primary roots of 2-week-old wild type, <em>ann1-2</em>, and <em>ann2-1</em> grown without Suc in light revealed many differentially expressed genes (DEG) in the primary roots of the mutants. <p>Although Arabidopsis roots partially turn green when exposed to light, the main energy source that facilitates root growth originates in the leaves (<a href="#bib30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib30">Kobayashi et al., 2012</a>). Results showed that four out of five photosynthetic genes were significantly up-regulated in cotyledons of <em>ann1-2</em> seedlings grown without Suc (<a href="#fig4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4A</a>). <section id="s09"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">
<em>ANN1</em> and <em>ANN2</em> Knockout Seedlings Show Suc-Dependent Phenotypes</h3>
<p>The observation that significantly higher contents of soluble sugar were detected by GC/MS analysis in primary roots of <em>ann1-2</em> and <em>ann2-1</em> compared to those of wild type when grown together without Suc indicated there was successful phloem transport of sugar from shoots to roots in the knockout mutants, yet the mutants had obvious growth and columella cell defects. The fact that these defects were rescued by exogenous Suc independent of its osmotic effects suggested the possibility that <em>ANN1</em> and <em>ANN2</em> helped to regulate the post-phloem transport of Suc into root tips. Although successful unloading was detected in both the <em>ANN1</em> knockout mutant <em>ann1-2</em> and the <em>ANN2</em> knockout mutant <em>ann2-1</em>, the diffusion of CFDA into root tips was impaired in both <em>ann1-2</em> and <em>ann2-1</em>, indicating these mutants had restricted sugar diffusion in fast-growing regions from the unloading zone to root tips. These results are consistent with the conclusion that compromised expression of <em>ANN1</em> and <em>ANN2</em> leads to impaired diffusion of sugars in the fast-growing root apex, and this, in turn, inhibits primary root growth. However, further studies quantifying carbohydrate content only in the cap region after laser microdissection are required to confirm this conclusion.</p></section><section id="s10"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Sugar in Regulation of Photosynthesis</h3>
<p>The regulatory roles of sugar in photosynthetic gene transcripts and photosynthesis capacity have been widely documented (<a href="#bib31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib31">Koch, 1996</a>; <a href="#bib51" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib51">Pego et al., 2000</a>). Sugar starvation in plants activates photosynthesis, whereas high sugar levels inhibit photosynthesis (<a href="#bib67" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib67">Yu et al., 2015</a>; <a href="#bib55" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib55">Sami et al., 2016</a>). Sugar represses the transcriptional activity of photosynthetic gene promoters transiently expressed in maize mesophyll protoplasts (<a href="#bib56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib56">Sheen, 1990</a>). Sugar accumulation in leaves represses photosynthetic gene expression and photosynthesis (<a href="#bib51" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib51">Pego et al., 2000</a>). Roots are typical sink organs. Sugar demands in young roots promote photosynthesis in leaves. For example, overexpression of the <em>HIGHER YIELD RICE</em> gene encoding an APETALA2/ETHYLENE RESPONSIVE FACTOR transcription factor leads to increased root length, branching, and strength, which in turn promotes photosynthesis in leaves (<a href="#bib1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib1">Ambavaram et al., 2014</a>). However, the mechanism of how plants monitor sugar levels in roots remains an active area of investigation. Here, we hypothesize that root tips are sugar-sensing tissues, and their sugar status can affect photosynthesis capacity in leaves.</p>
<p>As noted above, our results revealed sugar starvation in the actively growing root apex of the <em>ann1-2</em> and <em>ann2-1</em> knockout mutants by using CF as a phloem mobile probe. Evidence that this apical sugar starvation led to an up-regulation of photosynthesis was indicated by the up-regulated expression of genes encoding CAB2 (LHCB1.1), CAB3 (LHCB 1.2), and PSII subunits (LHB1B1 and LHB1B2), along with increased chlorophyll contents in <em>ann1-2</em>. The enhancement of photosynthesis in <em>ann1-2</em> was most likely the change that resulted in the elevated sugar contents observed in primary roots of <em>ann1-2</em>. However, because these accumulated sugars could not reach the root apex due to the defective post-phloem transport of sugars in <em>ann1-2</em>, they failed to rescue primary root growth defects and to reverse the enhancement of photosynthesis. Assuming that sugars accumulate in the phloem and unloading zone without sufficient diffusion into the post-phloem region, this transport defect would lead to sugar starvation in root tips. Because roots can communicate their nutrient status to shoots and induce responses in them (<a href="#bib29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib29">Ko and Helariutta, 2017</a>), the stress of sugar starvation in root tips could initiate a root-to-shoot signal, just as salt stress in roots promote the propagation of Ca<sup>2+</sup> waves from roots to shoots (<a href="#bib10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib10">Choi et al., 2014</a>; <a href="#bib20" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib20">Evans et al., 2016</a>). Plausibly, such a signal could help explain how sugar-deprived root tips could induce the up-regulation of photosynthetic genes in leaves.</p>
<p>Roots efficiently take up Suc and Glc from the media (<a href="#bib9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib9">Chaudhuri et al., 2008</a>), and consistently, exogenously supplied sugars in the medium successfully rescued primary root growth defects and reversed photosynthesis enhancement in <em>ann1-2</em>. These results favor the conclusion that ANN1 helps control root sugar status, which would result in feedback regulation of photosynthesis. Knocking out <em>ANN1</em> may result in sugar starvation in roots, which could serve as a signal for plants to up-regulate photosynthesis.</p></section><section id="s11"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Post-Phloem Transport of Sugar to Root Tips</h3>
<p>Apical sugar concentration in root tips has been considered to play the determinant role in regulating root growth and development (<a href="#bib22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib22">Freixes et al., 2002</a>). The diffusion of CF in root tips of <em>ann1-2</em> and <em>ann2-1</em> was restricted, whereas the <em>ANN1</em>-ovexpressing line <em>35S:ANN1</em> showed promoted diffusion of CF. We interpreted these results to indicate that knocking out <em>ANN1</em> and <em>ANN2</em> restricted symplastic diffusion in post-phloem transport from the unloading zone to root tips and that this restriction impaired primary root growth along with root cap development.</p>
<p>Sugar diffuses freely along the concentration gradient through plasmodesmata in the root apex. It is now widely accepted that plasmodesmata are dynamic channels whose permeability is highly regulated during plant growth and development (<a href="#bib54" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib54">Sager and Lee, 2014</a>). Callose has been found deposited in the neck region of plasmodesmata and can restrict the size exclusion limit of plasmodesmata (<a href="#bib44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib44">Luna et al., 2011</a>; <a href="#bib68" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib68">Zavaliev et al., 2011</a>). ROS accumulation can increase callose accumulation, which may result in reduced plasmodesmatal transport (<a href="#bib4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib4">Benitez-Alfonso et al., 2009</a>, <a href="#bib5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib5">2011</a>; <a href="#bib60" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib60">Stonebloom et al., 2012</a>).</p>
<p>ANNt1 has antioxidant activity (<a href="#bib23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib23">Gidrol et al., 1996</a>), and this activity may help explain the results of prior studies that documented a link between <em>ANN1</em> suppression and an increase in ROS levels. For example, the knockout of <em>ANN1</em> results in the accumulation of higher levels of ROS in leaves compared to that in wild type (<a href="#bib33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib33">Konopka-Postupolska et al., 2009</a>). Similarly, our results show that knocking out <em>ANN1</em> and <em>ANN2</em> leads to hyperaccumulation of ROS in root tips, and, like earlier studies, associates this change with an elevated accumulation of callose. Assuming that some of this accumulated callose is deposited in plasmodesmata, it would restrict plasmodesmatal sugar transport and thus help explain why post-phloem transport of sugar to root tips is inhibited in mutants.</p>
<p>In conclusion, our results provide data linking ANN1 and ANN2 to the regulation of primary root growth and development. This study expands our understanding of the function of annexins in plants to include an important role in the post-phloem transport of sugars to the root tip, which, in turn, indirectly impacts photosynthetic rates in cotyledons.</p></section></section><section id="s12"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">MATERIALS AND METHODS</h2>
<section id="s13"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Plant Materials and Growth Conditions</h3>
<p>Arabidopsis (<em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em>) Colombia (Col-0) ecotype was used as the wild type in all experiments. <em>ANN1</em> (At1g35720) and <em>ANN2</em> (At5g65020) are studied. Annexin T-DNA insertion mutants including <em>ann1-2</em>, <em>ann1-3</em>, <em>ann2-1</em>, <em>ann1-2/ann2-1</em>, and <em>35S:ANN1</em> used in this study were all obtained from <a href="#bib64" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib64">Wang et al. (2015)</a>. All seeds were surface sterilized by 75% (v/v) ethanol for 1 min and 20% bleach (v/v) for 10 min. After washing five times with sterile, deionized water, seeds were sowed on agar plates containing either no Suc, 2% (w/v) Suc, or 1% (w/v) mannitol. Seeds on agar plates were stratified in darkness at 4°C for 3 d. Stratified plates were placed vertically in a growth chamber (Percival AR-66 L; light intensity of 275/µmol m<sup>−2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>, humidity of approximately 80%, 20°C) in continuous light or in darkness. Unless otherwise noted, chemicals were reagent grade from Sigma-Aldrich.</p></section><section id="s14"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Lugol Staining of Columella Cells</h3>
<p>Seedlings grown with 1% Suc or without Suc were submerged in a root cap fixative solution (5% [w/v] formaldehyde, 5% [v/v] acetic acid, and 25% [v/v] ethanol) for 24 h. Lugol staining of root caps was done as described by <a href="#bib25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib25">Hong et al. (2015)</a>. Ten to thirteen seedlings were imaged for each genotype with a DIC optics on a Nikon 90i Stereology microscope, using a 60× objective.</p></section><section id="s15"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Starch Quantification</h3>
<p>Starch levels in 2 mm root tips of 10-d-old wild-type and <em>ann1-2</em> seedlings grown without Suc were assayed in ethanol extracts using methods described by <a href="#bib7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib7">Bergmeyer (1984)</a>, <a href="#bib3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib3">Barratt et al. (2009)</a>, and <a href="#bib17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib17">Divya et al. (2010)</a>.</p></section><section id="s16"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">RNA Extraction</h3>
<p>Trizol reagent was used in all RNA extraction experiments following the manufacturer’s protocol.</p></section><section id="s17"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">RNA-Seq Analysis</h3>
<p>To elucidate the mechanisms of ANN1 and ANN2 in regulation of primary root growth, we performed a comparative RNA-seq using total RNA from primary roots excised from 2-week-old wild-type, <em>ann1-2</em>, and <em>ann2-1</em> seedlings grown without Suc in light. Total RNA extracted from primary roots of 2-week-old wild-type, <em>ann1-2</em>, and <em>ann2-1</em> seedlings grown without Suc was submitted to the Genome Sequencing and Analysis Facility (GSAF) at the University of Texas at Austin. Both library construction and sequencing were performed by GSAF. After filtering low-quality reads and removing adaptor contamination from the raw data of Illumina reads, the following steps were as described by <a href="#bib63" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib63">Van Verk et al. (2013)</a> to detect differential expression in <em>ann1-2</em> and <em>ann2-1</em> compared to Col-0. A cutoff value of a greater than 2-fold change in expression was used to select differentially expressed genes in <em>ann1-2</em> and <em>ann2-1</em>.</p>
<p>To further investigate the biological functions of the DEG in both <em>ann1-2</em> and <em>ann2-1</em>, we subsequently assigned the DEGs to GO terms. TAIR10 was downloaded for gene function descriptions and GO annotations. Ture-path rule was applied in functional enrichment analysis. A gene annotated with a particular GO term was also annotated with all its parents. To avoid very generic, noninformative terms for analysis, only terms annotating 500 or fewer genes were retained. Genes annotated with a given specific GO term were considered as a gene set. All the gene sets were tested for the statistical significance of enrichment among themselves using the cumulative hypergeometric test. Then a Benjamini-Hochberg false discovery rate (<a href="#bib6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib6">Benjamini and Hochberg, 1995</a>) was also calculated. A false discovery rate threshold of 0.05 was used for significance.</p></section><section id="s18"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">RT-qPCR Analysis</h3>
<p>cDNA was transcribed from RNA by the High-Capacity cDNA Reverse Transcription Kit (Thermo Fisher) after DNase I digestion. Ten micrograms cDNA was used for each sample. RT-qPCR was performed on QuantStudio platform (Applied Biosystems) with PowerUp Sybr Green Master Mix (Thermo Fisher). Transcripts of <em>cab2</em> (AT1G29920), <em>cab3</em> (AT1G29910), <em>LHB1B1</em> (AT2G34430), <em>LHB1B2</em> (AT2G34420), <em>ANN1</em> (AT1G35720), and <em>ANN2</em> (AT5G65020) under different conditions were studied. Primer sequences used in all RT-qPCR experiments to detect gene expression are listed in <a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.18.00713/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Table S2</a>. Specificity of gene amplifications were confirmed by melting curves. For each condition tested, three biological replicates for both wild type and <em>ann1-2</em> with three technical replicates for each biological replicate were used. 2<sup>−ΔΔCT</sup> method (<a href="#bib42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib42">Livak and Schmittgen, 2001</a>) was used to calculate fold changes. For significant difference between each condition, either one-way ANOVA for three conditions or Student’s <em>t</em> test for two conditions was used.</p></section><section id="s19"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Detection of Chlorophyll Autofluorescence</h3>
<p>Cotyledons of 4-d-old intact <em>ann1-2</em> and wild-type seedlings grown either with 1% Suc or without Suc were examined. Chlorophyll autofluorescence 660 to 701 nm was detected with an excitation wavelength of 488 nm. Fifteen seedlings were examined for <em>ann1-2</em> and the wild type.</p></section><section id="s20"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">GC/MS Analysis</h3>
<p>Wild-type, <em>ann1-2</em>, and <em>ann2-1</em> seedlings were grown in continuous light on agar plates containing no Suc for a week. Primary roots were collected and washed in sterile water twice. After freeze drying for 2 d, samples were submitted to complex carbohydrate research center at the University of Georgia for GC/MS analysis. Triplicate experiments were carried out.</p></section><section id="s21"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">CFDA Application</h3>
<p>CFDA stock was prepared as 6 mg/mL in acetone and kept at −80°C. A dilution of 1:20 in sterile water was used for application. Wild-type, <em>ann1-2</em>, and <em>ann2-1</em> seedlings were grown on agar plates containing no Suc for 4 d. A cotyledon was grazed by fine tweezers to allow 1 µL CFDA to penetrate. All samples were excited by 488-nm laser. Fluorescence 500 to 566 nm was monitored.</p></section><section id="s22"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">ROS Detection</h3>
<p>Three-day-old wild-type, <em>ann1-2</em>, and <em>ann2-1</em> seedlings grown without Suc were used. Whole seedlings were incubated in the buffer (30 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> KCl and 10 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> MES-KOH, pH 6.15) with 50 µ<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> H<sub>2</sub>DCFDA for 1 h in darkness at room temperature. Extra dye was washed three times by the buffer before confocal microscopy. All samples were excited by 488-nm laser. Fluorescence between 517 and 527 nm was collected.</p></section><section id="s23"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Aniline Blue Staining</h3>
<p>Three-day-old wild-type, <em>ann1-2</em>, and <em>ann2-1</em> seedlings grown without Suc were stained by aniline blue (Biosupplies) as described by <a href="#bib53" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib53">Ross-Elliott et al. (2017)</a>.</p></section><section id="s24"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Confocal Microscopy</h3>
<p>Confocal imaging was done by a confocal laser scanning microscope (Zesis LSM 710) in a set manner, starting with the same image acquisition settings and data processing for all experiments. Fluorescent signals in all samples were recorded in Z stacks. Z-project in Fiji was used to flatten Z stacks either with average intensity for better quantification accuracy or with maximum intensity for better focus in representative images. Relative fold change of fluorescent intensity was calculated by division of the mean fluorescent intensity in wild type. Student’s <em>t</em> test was used to calculate significant difference between samples. Fluorescence was quantified in a way to avoid size difference.</p></section><section id="s25"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Accession Numbers</h3>
