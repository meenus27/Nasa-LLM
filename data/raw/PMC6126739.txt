</div></section></section><section aria-label="Article content"><section class="body main-article-body"><section class="abstract" id="abstract1"><h2>Significance</h2>
<p>Plant vacuoles are essential organelles and occupy up to 90% of the cell volume. Their roles include regulation of stomata movements, protein storage in seeds, gravity sensing, and ion homeostasis. Vacuole or lysosome fusion in eukaryotes is mediated by two multisubunit complexes, SNARE and homotypic fusion and vacuolar protein sorting (HOPS), but only the SNARE complex is well characterized in plants. Here, we show that, similar to other eukaryotes, HOPS mediates vacuole fusion in plants by interaction with SNAREs and that the HOPS subunit VPS33 and the SNARE protein SYP22 display the sites for interaction between these complexes. In contrast to other eukaryotes, however, plant HOPS recruitment to liposomes is inhibited by phosphoinositides, which appear to define strict rules for regulating fusion and fragmentation of dynamic vacuoles.</p>
<section id="kwd-group1" class="kwd-group"><p><strong>Keywords:</strong> <em>Arabidopsis</em>, vacuole fusion, HOPS, SNARE, phosphoinositides</p></section></section><section class="abstract" id="abstract2"><h2>Abstract</h2>
<p>The vacuole is an essential organelle in plant cells, and its dynamic nature is important for plant growth and development. Homotypic membrane fusion is required for vacuole biogenesis, pollen germination, stomata opening, and gravity perception. Known components of the vacuole fusion machinery in eukaryotes include SNARE proteins, Rab GTPases, phosphoinositides, and the homotypic fusion and vacuolar protein sorting (HOPS) tethering complex. HOPS function is not well characterized in plants, but roles in embryogenesis and pollen tube elongation have been reported. Here, we show that <em>Arabidopsis</em> HOPS subunits VPS33 and VPS41 accumulate in late endosomes and that VPS41, but not VPS33, accumulates in the tonoplast via a wortmannin-sensitive process. VPS41 and VPS33 proteins bind to liposomes, but this binding is inhibited by phosphatidylinosiltol-3-phosphate [PtdIns(3)P] and PtdIns(3,5)P<sub>2</sub>, which implicates a nonconserved mechanism for HOPS recruitment in plants. Inducible knockdown of VPS41 resulted in dramatic vacuole fragmentation phenotypes and demonstrated a critical role for HOPS in vacuole fusion. Furthermore, we provide evidence for genetic interactions between VPS41 and VTI11 SNARE that regulate vacuole fusion, and the requirement of a functional SNARE complex for normal VPS41 and VPS33 localization. Finally, we provide evidence to support VPS33 and SYP22 at the initial stage for HOPSâSNARE interactions, which is similar to other eukaryotes. These results highlight both conserved and specific mechanisms for HOPS recruitment and function during vacuole fusion in plants.</p></section><hr class="headless">
<p>The plant vacuole is an essential organelle with multiple roles including storage, pH homeostasis, and recycling (<a href="#r1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r1">1</a>). Vacuole fusion is required for stomata opening, pollen fertility, and embryo viability (<a href="#r2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r2">2</a>â<a href="#r4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r4">4</a>). Two types of vacuoles exist in plant cells. Embryos in developing seeds contain multiple protein storage vacuoles (PSVs), which have a neutral pH and accumulate storage proteins such as the 7S and 11S globulins. Cells in most mature tissues, instead, contain one large lytic vacuole, which is acidic, abundant in lytic proteases, and critical for ion homeostasis and cell turgor (<a href="#r5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r5">5</a>). Transition from PSVs to lytic vacuoles occurs during germination (<a href="#r6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r6">6</a>) and involves PSV fusion and maturation (<a href="#r7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r7">7</a>). Vacuoles in root meristematic cells are formed by interconnected tubular structures (<a href="#r8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r8">8</a>), and membrane fusion is necessary for the establishment of the central lytic vacuole (<a href="#r2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r2">2</a>). Transient and mobile structures such as transvacuolar strands, sheets, and bulbs contribute to the dynamic nature of plant vacuoles (<a href="#r9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r9">9</a>â<a href="#r12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r12">12</a>). In vegetative cells, however, very little vacuole fragmentation occurs and most cells contain a single large vacuole. Two exceptions are the changes in guard cell vacuoles during stomata movements (<a href="#r2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r2">2</a>, <a href="#r13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r13">13</a>, <a href="#r14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r14">14</a>) and in the leaf motor cells of the mimosa tree (<em>Albizia julibrissin</em>) (<a href="#r15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r15">15</a>). In both cases, vacuole remodeling occurs during dramatic changes in cellular turgor that drive organ movements. The changes in guard cell vacuoles that occur during stomata opening require homotypic membrane fusion mediated by the vacuolar soluble NSF attachment protein receptor (SNARE) complex containing VTI11 (<a href="#r2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r2">2</a>).</p>
<p>Vacuole fusion is not well characterized in plants, but in eukaryotic cells it requires SNAREs, RAB GTPases, membrane lipids, and a tethering complex. SNARE proteins mediate membrane fusion by assembly of a four-helix bundle, or <em>trans</em>-SNARE complex, from one SNARE located in the incoming vesicle and three SNAREs located in the target membrane. The SNARE complex brings the two membranes together via a zippering mechanism that is thought to result in membrane fusion (<a href="#r16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r16">16</a>). A SNARE complex for plant prevacuolar compartments (PVCs) and the vacuole is composed of SYP22/VAM3 (Qa-SNARE), VTI11 (Qb-SNARE, called Vti1p in yeast), SYP51 (Qc-SNARE), and VAMP727 (R-SNARE) (<a href="#r17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r17">17</a>, <a href="#r18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r18">18</a>). The Qabc complex can also associate with the R-SNAREs VAMP711 or VAMP713 (<a href="#r19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r19">19</a>, <a href="#r20" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r20">20</a>). A role of these SNAREs in vacuole fusion is evidenced by altered vacuole phenotypes of <em>vti11</em>, s<em>yp22</em>, and <em>syp22 vamp727</em> double mutants (<a href="#r2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r2">2</a>, <a href="#r18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r18">18</a>, <a href="#r21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r21">21</a>).</p>
<p>The homotypic fusion and vacuole protein sorting (HOPS) tethering complex was recently shown to mediate homotypic vacuole fusion in plants (<a href="#r3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r3">3</a>). HOPS is important for the fusion of vacuoles in yeast (<a href="#r16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r16">16</a>, <a href="#r22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r22">22</a>), and fusion of lysosomes in <em>Caenorhabditis elegans</em> (<a href="#r23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r23">23</a>) and humans (<a href="#r24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r24">24</a>). The yeast HOPS complex is the best characterized and is formed by six subunits. Vps39p and Vps41p are HOPS specific, while Vps11p, Vps16p, Vps18p, and Vps33p form the âCOREâ and are shared with CORVET, a tethering complex that functions at the early endosome (<a href="#r25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r25">25</a>, <a href="#r26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r26">26</a>). HOPS recruitment to yeast vacuole membranes requires phosphatidylinositol 3-phosphate [PtdIns(3)P] and PtdIns(4,5)P<sub>2</sub>, two important regulatory lipids in vacuole fusion (<a href="#r27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r27">27</a>). Vps33 belongs to the family of Sec1/Munc18 (SM) proteins, which are implicated in assembly and regulation of SNARE complexes. SM proteins have a three-domain architecture, in which domains 1 and 3 form a cleft that accommodates the SNAREs (<a href="#r28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r28">28</a>). The X-ray structure of a fungal Vps33 bound to a single SNARE, together with in vitro fusion assays, suggests that VPS33 catalyzes SNARE complex assembly through a specific SNARE motif recognition (<a href="#r29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r29">29</a>). All other HOPS subunits are predicted to form a Î²-propeller at the N terminus and an Î±-solenoid at the C terminus (<a href="#r30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r30">30</a>). The complete HOPS complex has been detected in <em>Arabidopsis</em> by mass spectrometry (<a href="#r3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r3">3</a>), and all subunits are encoded in the <em>Arabidopsis</em> genome as single genes (<a href="#r31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r31">31</a>). <em>VACUOLELESS1</em> (<em>VCL1</em>), the homolog of yeast Vps16, <em>VPS11</em>, <em>VPS39</em>, and <em>VPS41</em> are required for vacuole biogenesis and pollen development (<a href="#r3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r3">3</a>, <a href="#r4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r4">4</a>, <a href="#r32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r32">32</a>â<a href="#r35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r35">35</a>). VCL1 interacts with VPS33 and VPS11 and localizes to PVCs and the tonoplast (<a href="#r35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r35">35</a>). VPS18 and VPS39 were also localized to a subdomain of the tonoplast and to cytosolic organelles (<a href="#r3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r3">3</a>). The molecular mechanisms for HOPS function and regulation during vacuole fusion in plants are not well characterized.</p>
<p>The RAB5 and RAB7 GTPase families have been implicated in regulation of endosomes maturation and membrane fusion at the vacuole (<a href="#r36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r36">36</a>). RAB5 and RAB7 localize to partially overlapping endosome populations, with only RAB7 detected at the tonoplast (<a href="#r19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r19">19</a>, <a href="#r37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r37">37</a>â<a href="#r41" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r41">41</a>). The current model is that the SAND-CCZ complex is a RAB5 effector, exerts GEF function on RAB7, and is necessary for the conversion of RAB5 to RAB7 in PVCs (<a href="#r42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r42">42</a>â<a href="#r44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r44">44</a>). Active RAB7 is required for vacuolar localization of HOPS subunits VPS18 and VPS39 (<a href="#r3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r3">3</a>).</p>
<p>Plant homotypic vacuole fusion is also regulated by phosphoinositides. Pollen grains defective in phosphatidylinositol 4-phophate 5-kinase 1 (PI5K1) and PI5K2 have larger vacuoles than the WT, which implicates PtdIns(4,5)P<sub>2</sub> in vacuole biogenesis (<a href="#r45" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r45">45</a>). Depletion of PtdIns(3)P after wortmannin (Wm) treatment induces rapid vacuole membrane fusion in root and hypocotyl cells of <em>vti11/zig1</em> mutants and in guard cells of WT (<a href="#r2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r2">2</a>). Wm treatment also induces the swelling of plant PVCs, which results in doughnut-shaped structures known as Wm bodies (<a href="#r46" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r46">46</a>). These effects of Wm are due to its inhibitory action against phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase (<a href="#r47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r47">47</a>) and implicate PtdIns(3)P and other phosphoinositides in vacuole fusion in plants. Interestingly, these effects of Wm have not been observed in yeast or animal cells (<a href="#r48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r48">48</a>, <a href="#r49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r49">49</a>), suggesting the possibility of specific mechanisms for plant vacuole fusion.</p>
<p>Here, we characterized VPS41 and VPS33 using reverse genetics, fluorescently tagged proteins, and biochemical analyses. We show that VPS41 and VPS33 are important for pollen fertility and PSV maturation in embryos, and that VPS41 is required for homotypic vacuole fusion in developing seedlings. We demonstrate that both proteins localize to late endosomes, that only VPS41 is detected in the tonoplast, and that they interact with each other and with the vacuolar SNARE SYP22. The VPS41-GFP localization at the tonoplast, but not that at late endosomes, is Wm sensitive. In vitro lipidâprotein binding assays show that while VPS33 and VPS41 bind to phosphoinositides on lipid strips, neither protein binds to PtdIns(3)P or PtdIns(3,5)P<sub>2</sub>-containing liposomes, even though they do bind to control liposomes. We speculate that specific affinities for membrane lipids together with membrane curvature are key regulatory mechanisms behind vacuole fusion events mediated by HOPS and SNAREs.</p>
<section id="s1"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Results</h2>
<section id="s2"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">VPS41 and VPS33 Partially Colocalize with Late Endosomes, but only VPS41 Is Detected at the Tonoplast.</h3>
<p>VPS41 (AT1G08190) and VPS33 (AT3G54860) share 23.29% and 19.17% sequence identity with yeast Vps41p and Vps33p, respectively. Therefore, VPS33 and VPS41 are likely homologs of these yeast HOPS proteins (<a href="#r31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r31">31</a>, <a href="#r50" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r50">50</a>). We first investigated whether VPS41 and VPS33 colocalized in vacuolar or prevacuolar membranes, as expected for HOPS. Transient expression of mRFP-VPS41 and VPS33-GFP under control of UBQ10 promoters in <em>Nicotiana benthamiana</em> leaves indicated that these proteins accumulate in the cytosol and nucleus, and associate with membranes in punctate structures (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S1 <em>A</em> and <em>B</em></a>). RFP-VPS41 and VPS33-GFP colocalized in the majority of the punctate structures and the nucleus, and partially at the cell periphery (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S1<em>C</em></a>), which supports their participation in a complex. These experiments, however, do not provide enough resolution to identify the compartments where these proteins accumulate and they may be influenced by overexpression. To better characterize protein localizations, VPS41-GFP (<a href="#r4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r4">4</a>) and VPS33-GFP were analyzed in stable <em>Arabidopsis</em> transgenic plants using full-length genomic fusions to GFP under native promoters. Both proteins were again detected in the cytosol, but membrane association was more discernible. Similar to its localization in pollen (<a href="#r4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r4">4</a>), VPS41-GFP was detected at the tonoplast and punctate organelles in roots (<a href="#fig01" class="usa-link">Fig. 1<em>A</em></a>), and this localization was not affected by the location of the fluorescence tag as the localization of YFP-VPS41 was very similar (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S1<em>D</em></a>). Colocalization with known organelle markers was used to identify the structures labeled with VPS41-GFP and VPS33-GFP. VPS41-GFP colocalized with the tonoplast marker RFP-SYP22 (<a href="#r18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r18">18</a>) and confirmed its tonoplast association (<a href="#fig01" class="usa-link">Fig. 1 <em>A</em>â<em>C</em></a> and <a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S1<em>E</em></a>). Furthermore, VPS41-GFP partially colocalized with mCherry-RAB7, a marker for the late stages of PVC maturation (<a href="#r51" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r51">51</a>) (<a href="#fig01" class="usa-link">Fig. 1 <em>D</em>â<em>F</em></a>), consistent with results from pollen (<a href="#r4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r4">4</a>). Colocalization with the early PVC marker mCherry-RAB5 (<a href="#r51" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r51">51</a>) was detected in only a few organelles (<a href="#fig01" class="usa-link">Fig. 1 <em>G</em>â<em>I</em></a>). These results indicate that VPS41-GFP localizes to the vacuole and late endosomes. Similar experiments with VPS33-GFP indicate that VPS41 and VPS33 have overlapping but not identical localizations. VPS33-GFP rarely colocalized with RFP-SYP22 at the tonoplast, except for a few puncta (<a href="#fig01" class="usa-link">Fig. 1 <em>J</em>â<em>L</em></a> and <a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S1<em>F</em></a>), indicating that this protein is absent from the vacuole. VPS33-GFP is present in the cytosol and in cytosolic punctate structures, where it partially colocalized with mCherry-RAB7 (<a href="#fig01" class="usa-link">Fig. 1 <em>M</em>â<em>O</em></a>) and shows strong colocalization with mCherry-RAB5 (<a href="#fig01" class="usa-link">Fig. 1 <em>P</em>â<em>R</em></a>). Quantification of these colocalization experiments further validates the overlapping but distinct localization of VPS33 and VPS41 (<a href="#fig01" class="usa-link">Fig. 1 <em>S</em> and <em>T</em></a>). Both proteins show significant colocalization with RAB7 endosomes. While VPS33 has the strongest colocalization with RAB5 endosomes, it is weakly colocalized with the tonoplast marker SYP22. In contrast, VPS41 shows significant accumulation at the tonoplast but is not associated with RAB5 endosomes (<a href="#fig01" class="usa-link">Fig. 1 <em>S</em> and <em>T</em></a>). Interestingly, both VPS41-GFP and VPS33-GFP seem to accumulate in more endosomal compartments when coexpressed with mCherry-RAB5 (compare <a href="#fig01" class="usa-link">Fig. 1<em>A</em></a> with <a href="#fig01" class="usa-link">Fig. 1<em>G</em></a> and <a href="#fig01" class="usa-link">Fig. 1<em>J</em></a> with <a href="#fig01" class="usa-link">Fig. 1<em>P</em></a>). The effect of the mCherry-RAB5 fusion on the recruitment or stability of these proteins at the endosome or the endosomes themselves will require further analysis beyond the scope of this work. Overall, these results suggest that VPS33 and VPS41 are cytosolic proteins that preferentially associate with late endosomal compartments, and this is consistent with a role for HOPS in the tethering of vacuoles or prevacuoles. Interestingly, only VPS41 is detected at the tonoplast, and only VPS33 has significant accumulation in RAB5 endosomes.</p>

<p>The distinct localizations of VPS33-GFP and VPS41-GFP prompted us to test whether the two proteins were characterized by different phospholipid binding affinities. This was tested using an in vitro lipid-binding assay with recombinant proteins. GST-VPS41 and GST-VPS33 fusion proteins were expressed in <em>Escherichia coli</em> and purified by affinity chromatography. Purified proteins were incubated on lipid strips, and protein binding was detected with anti-GST. GST alone was used as negative control (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S2<em>A</em></a>). Both GST-VPS41 and GST-VPS33 show affinity toward all phosphoinositides in this assay, including the two that are most abundant at the late endosomes and vacuole, PtdIns(3)P and PtdIns(3,5)P<sub>2</sub> (<a href="#fig02" class="usa-link">Fig. 2<em>A</em></a>). These results are consistent with the hypothesis that VPS41 and VPS33 association with membranes may be regulated in part by phosphoinositide binding.</p>

<p>PtdIns(3)P is a negative regulator for homotypic vacuole fusion in plants (<a href="#r2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r2">2</a>) and recruits HOPS to the vortex ring in yeast (<a href="#r52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r52">52</a>). Hence, PtdIns(3)P and other phosphoinositides are good candidates to regulate HOPS subunit association with the vacuole and PVC. We therefore asked whether the localizations of GFP-VPS41 was affected by phosphoinositide availability using Wm treatment, which results in PtdIns(3)P depletion from cellular membranes (<a href="#r53" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r53">53</a>). GFP-VPS41 was found at the tonoplast and some mCherry-RAB7-labeled endosomes, in the DMSO control as expected (<a href="#fig02" class="usa-link">Fig. 2 <em>C</em>â<em>E</em></a>). As shown in <a href="#fig02" class="usa-link">Fig. 2 <em>I</em>â<em>K</em></a>, the sites of highest VPS41-GFP fluorescence intensity in the control colocalize with RFP-SYP22 at the tonoplast. This tonoplast bright signal is lost after the Wm treatment (<a href="#fig02" class="usa-link">Fig. 2 <em>L</em>â<em>N</em></a>). Quantification of these results further indicates that Wm treatment results in a 35% decrease in the membrane/cytosolic ratio of VPS41-GFP (<a href="#fig02" class="usa-link">Fig. 2<em>O</em></a>). These results indicate that tonoplast association of VPS41 is mediated by phosphoinositide binding, but its association with late endosome membranes is independent of phosphoinositides. Major changes in VPS33-GFP localization were not detected after Wm treatment as a few RAB7 endosomes were still labeled with VPS33-GFP (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S2 <em>B</em>â<em>G</em></a>).</p></section><section id="s4"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">HOPS Is Essential for Male Fertility and Important for Plant Development.</h3>
<p>To define the role of VPS41 and VPS33 in vacuole fusion and dynamics, we identified loss-of-function alleles for <em>VPS41</em> and <em>VPS33</em> from the SALK T-DNA collection (<a href="#r54" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r54">54</a>). T-DNA insertions were confirmed for <em>vps41-1</em> (SALK_076372) and <em>vps41-3</em> (SALK_036406), which carry the T-DNA insertions in the 13th exon and the promoter region of <em>VPS41</em>, respectively. Three T-DNA insertion alleles were confirmed for <em>VPS33</em>, <em>vps33-3</em> (SALK_016684C), <em>vps33-4</em> (SALK_151719), and <em>vps33-5</em> (SALK_143407). RT-PCR from homozygous lines showed no altered <em>VPS41</em> transcript accumulation for <em>vps41-3</em>, and this line was not used further. Similar to VPS41-GFP (<a href="#r4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r4">4</a>), the full-length genomic fusion construct for VPS33-GFP complements the pollen fertility defects of loss-of-function mutants as homozygous <em>vps33-3</em> can be recovered (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S3 <em>B</em>â<em>D</em></a>).</p></section><section id="s5"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">HOPS Is Important for Vacuole Biogenesis and Fusion.</h3>
<p>We identified viable weaker alleles to characterize VPS41 and VPS33 (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S3<em>A</em></a>). One EMS allele for <em>VPS41</em>, <em>zip2/vps41</em> (<a href="#r56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r56">56</a>), and two viable TILLING (<a href="#r57" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r57">57</a>) alleles of <em>VPS33</em>, <em>vps33-1</em> and <em>vps33-2</em>, are available. The <em>zip2/vps41</em> allele was identified from a screen of suppressors of <em>zig-1</em>/<em>vti11</em> in gravitropism (<a href="#r56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r56">56</a>). The mutation results in a G721R substitution within an annotated clathrin heavy chain repeat (CHCR) of the VPS41 protein that is predicted to mediate proteinâprotein interactions (<a href="#r58" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r58">58</a>). The mutations in <em>vps33-1</em> and <em>vps33-2</em> result in S95F and V136M substitutions in the VPS33 protein, respectively. Both are predicted to occur in domain 1 of the VPS33 protein, which, together with domain 3, has been shown in fungi and yeast to form a cleft that is thought to accommodate SNARE bundles (<a href="#r28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r28">28</a>). None of the single mutants shows developmental phenotypes in rosettes or inflorescences under normal growth conditions (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S3<em>E</em></a>). No vacuole phenotypes were observed in <em>zip2/vps41</em>, <em>vps33-1</em>, or <em>vps33-2</em> when seedling roots were analyzed using LysoTracker Red staining (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S3<em>F</em></a>). However, we observed an increased number of PSVs in mature <em>zip2/vps41</em>, <em>vps33-1</em>, and <em>vps33-2</em> embryos compared with the WT (<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">Fig. 3 <em>A</em>â<em>D</em></a>). The number of vacuoles per cell was counted in each genotype to quantify this defect (<a href="#fig03" class="usa-link">Fig. 3<em>E</em></a>). Only cells with a cross-section area between 11 and 15.9 Âµm<sup>2</sup> were included in the analysis. Fifty-three percent of the cells from the WT control have five to six vacuoles per cell, and a smaller fraction (26%) have seven to eight vacuoles. In contrast, only 16.8% of the cells from the <em>zip2/vps41</em> embryos have five to six vacuoles while the rest (88%) have seven or more. Finally, almost all of the <em>vps33-2</em> mutant embryos showed seven to eight vacuoles per cell. These results support the hypothesis that VPS41 and VPS33 are involved in vacuole homotypic fusion and are important for PSV maturation.</p>

<p>If HOPS is involved in membrane fusion at the vacuole, it is likely to interact with the vacuolar SNARE complex (<a href="#r17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r17">17</a>, <a href="#r18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r18">18</a>), including SYP22 and VTI11. To determine whether VPS41 and VPS33 interact with each other and with the vacuolar SNAREs, split YFP fusion proteins were constructed for bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC) experiments in <em>Nicotiana</em> leaf epidermis (<a href="#r59" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r59">59</a>, <a href="#r60" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r60">60</a>). Only N-terminal n/cYFP fusions of the SNARE proteins were used because the C termini of vacuolar SNAREs reside in the vacuole lumen. For VPS33 and VPS41, both N- and C-terminal constructs were generated, but none of the C-terminal n/cYFP fusions restored YFP signal in any combination. Coexpression of cYFP-VPS41 with nYFP-VPS33 resulted in a punctate pattern at the vacuole periphery (<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4<em>A</em></a>), indicating that VPS41 and VPS33 are likely to participate in the HOPS complex. Coexpression of the same cYFP-VPS41 construct with the SNARE fusion nYFP-SYP22 resulted in a punctate pattern as well, indicating that these two proteins interact in vivo (<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4<em>B</em></a>). In contrast, no YFP fluorescence was detected when cYFP-VPS41 was coexpressed with nYFP-VTI11 (<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4<em>C</em></a>). Note that broad fluorescent structures (asterisks) in <a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4 <em>B</em> and <em>C</em></a> are likely background signal because they were observed in negative controls (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S4 <em>A</em> and <em>B</em></a>). Next, we tested interactions with VPS33. Again, coexpression of cYFP-VPS33 with nYFP-VPS41 resulted in a punctate pattern indicative of proteinâprotein interaction (<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4<em>D</em></a>). When cYFP-VPS33 was coexpressed with nYFP-SYP22, a strong YFP signal was detected in punctate foci (<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4<em>E</em></a>), supporting the hypothesis that HOPS and SNAREs interact to mediate membrane fusion. Similar to the VPS41 interactions, no YFP fluorescence was detected when cYFP-VPS33 and nYFP-VTI11 were coexpressed, suggesting the absence of close interactions between these two proteins in vivo (<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4<em>F</em></a>). To further validate these results, the c/nYFP-VPS33 was coexpressed with a n/cYFP- fusion to the aquaporin TIP2;1 as a negative control. No YFP signal was detected in either combination (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S4 <em>D</em> and <em>E</em></a>), which confirms the lack of interaction for the negative controls. The two vacuolar SNAREs VTI11 and SYP22 were used as positive controls as they have been shown to interact (<a href="#r21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r21">21</a>) and localize at the PVC (<a href="#r18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r18">18</a>, <a href="#r61" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r61">61</a>). Indeed, coexpression of cYFP-VTI11 with nYFP-SYP22 as well as cYFP-SYP22 with nYFP-VTI11 resulted in similar punctate structures at the periphery of the vacuole (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S4 <em>C</em> and <em>F</em></a>). Overall, these data show that VPS33 and VPS41 can assemble in subcellular foci and that both proteins can interact in vivo with SYP22, but not with VTI11. Taken together, these data support the hypothesis for HOPSâSNARE interactions mediating membrane fusion at the vacuole.</p>

<p>VPS33 mediates SNARE complex assembly in yeast (<a href="#r29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r29">29</a>), and we could detect interactions between VPS33, VPS41, and SYP22 by BiFC. Therefore, we tested whether either of the two HOPS subunits could support a direct interaction with SYP22 by in vitro coimmunopreciptation. Proteins were expressed as tagged fusions to either GST (VPS33 and VPS41) or HIS (SYP22 and VTI11), and immunoprecipitation was carried out using an Î±-HIS antibody. GST-VPS41 was not detected in the Î±-HIS immunoprecipitate when incubated with either HIS-SYP22 or HIS-VTI11 (<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4<em>G</em></a>), indicating a lack of direct interaction. In contrast, a direct interaction between GST-VPS33 and HIS-SYP22 was detected using this assay, as GST-VPS33 was detected in the immunoprecipitate (<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4<em>G</em></a>). This interaction was confirmed in a complementary experiment where Î±-GST pulled down both HIS-SYP22 and GST-VPS33 in the coimmunoprecipitate (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S4<em>G</em></a>). Whether or not there were direct interactions with HIS-VTI11 was less definitive. Incubation of GST-VPS33 and HIS-VTI11 with Î±-HIS produced detectable signals of GST-VPS33 on immunoblots (<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4<em>G</em></a>), but no signal was detected for HIS-VTI11 when Î±-GST was used (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S4<em>G</em></a>). Overall, these data provide strong evidence that HOPSâSNARE interactions in plants are mediated by direct interaction of VPS33 and SYP22, most likely due to conserved mechanisms.</p></section><section id="s8"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">The HOPS Subunit VPS41 Genetically Interacts with the VTI11 SNARE.</h3>
<p>The evidence for proteinâprotein interactions between HOPS subunits and the vacuolar SNARE SYP22 prompted us to test for genetic interactions between the two complexes. <em>zip2</em>, a mutant allele of <em>VPS41</em> (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S3<em>A</em></a>), was identified as a suppressor of the agravitropic phenotype of <em>zig-1</em>, a loss-of-function allele of <em>VTI11</em> (<a href="#r56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r56">56</a>). To determine whether <em>zip2</em> suppresses the vacuole phenotype of <em>zig-1</em>, vacuoles from the <em>zig-1 zip2</em> double mutants were visualized with LysoTracker Red (<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4 <em>H</em>â<em>K</em></a>). The <em>zig-1</em> mutant displayed the previously described vacuole phenotype in both the root tip and root maturation zone (<a href="#r2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r2">2</a>) (<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4<em>H</em></a> and <a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S4<em>H</em></a>). The <em>zig-1 zip2</em> still exhibited multiple vacuoles near the root tip (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S4<em>I</em></a>), but this phenotype was not observed in the root maturation zone (<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4<em>I</em></a>). These results indicate that <em>zip2</em> partially suppresses the <em>zig-1</em> phenotype at the subcellular level and confirm a genetic interaction between <em>ZIP2</em>/<em>VPS41</em> and <em>VTI11</em>. Furthermore, this result suggests that the <em>zip2</em> mutation results in changes in the VPS41 protein structure that may alter the interactions between HOPS and SNAREs. A similar approach was used to test for genetic interactions between <em>VPS33</em> and <em>VTI11</em>, but unlike <em>zig-1 zip2</em> doubles, <em>vps33-1 zig-1</em> and <em>vps33-2 zig-1</em> double mutants showed the abnormal <em>zig-1</em> vacuole phenotype in all root cells (<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4 <em>J</em> and <em>K</em></a> and <a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S4 <em>J</em> and <em>K</em></a>) and the zig-zagged phenotype of the inflorescence. These results indicate that the <em>vps33-1</em> and <em>vps33-2</em> mutations failed to complement the vacuole phenotype, perhaps due to their hypomorphic nature, which may have innocuous effects on the VPS33 protein.</p>
<p>The proposed interaction between HOPS and SNAREs led us to predict that loss of the VTI11 SNARE from the vacuole could alter the localization of VPS41 or VPS33. We tested this possibility in <em>zig-1</em> mutants. VPS41-GFP localization in the WT background showed the cytosolic, punctate, and tonoplast localization previously described (<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4<em>L</em></a>). In contrast, VPS41-GFP was not detected at the tonoplast and only accumulated in bright aggregated patterns in the <em>zig-1</em> background (<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4<em>M</em></a>). When visualized using the Airyscan mode of the microscope, these bright patterns were associated with adjacent vacuoles or clusters of small organelles in the cytosol (<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4<em>N</em></a>). A similar result was obtained with VPS33-GFP. In contrast to the dispersed puncta of VPS33-GFP in the WT (<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4<em>O</em></a>), <em>zig-1</em> mutants showed clustered structures labeled with VPS33-GFP (<a href="#fig04" class="usa-link">Fig. 4 <em>P</em> and <em>Q</em></a>). At least some of these clusters represent small vacuoles as similar clusters were detected in <em>zig-1</em> mutants labeled with RFP-SYP22 (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S4 <em>L</em>â<em>N</em></a>). These results indicate that a functional SNARE complex is required for the normal localization of VPS41 and VPS33.</p></section></section><section id="s9"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Discussion</h2>
<p>The plant HOPS tethering complex is evolutionarily conserved with other eukaryotes (<a href="#r3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r3">3</a>, <a href="#r31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r31">31</a>, <a href="#r35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r35">35</a>), is involved in vacuole traffic via a RAB7-mediated pathway, and is critical for homotypic vacuole fusion (<a href="#r3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r3">3</a>). HOPS shares four subunits with CORVET, a similar conserved complex that mediates tethering of RAB5 endosomes and vacuoles in a plant-specific manner (<a href="#r3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r3">3</a>). Our results show that stable VPS33âVPS41 interactions predominantly occur in punctate organelles likely to correspond to endosomes as expected for a tether between endosomes and vacuoles. The possibility remains, however, that the functional HOPS complex also assembles transiently at sites of vacuole homotypic fusions, which are not abundant in the cells used in these experiments.</p>
<p>HOPS is essential for vacuole membrane fusion in plants. VPS33 and VPS41 are essential in pollen fertility, and in fact, loss of VPS41 function interfered with pollen penetration into the stigma (<a href="#r4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r4">4</a>). This and previous results (<a href="#r3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r3">3</a>, <a href="#r4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r4">4</a>, <a href="#r33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r33">33</a>, <a href="#r34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r34">34</a>) indicate that HOPS is essential for plant viability. The increased number of PSVs of hypomorphic mutants <em>zip2</em>, <em>vps33-1</em>, and <em>vps33-2</em> is indicative of HOPS function in PSV fusion or trafficking, as it has been observed in other vacuolar trafficking mutants (<a href="#r2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r2">2</a>, <a href="#r18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r18">18</a>, <a href="#r43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r43">43</a>, <a href="#r62" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r62">62</a>, <a href="#r63" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r63">63</a>). Moreover, the inducible amiRNA silencing of VPS41 and VPS11 underscores HOPS function in lytic vacuole fusion.</p>
<section id="s10"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Phosphoinositides and Membrane Curvature Delimit VPS41 and VPS33 Recruitment.</h3>
<p>VPS41-GFP and VPS33-GFP localize to late endosomes, which is consistent with a role in membrane fusion at the PVC and vacuole as part of HOPS (<a href="#r31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r31">31</a>) and with previous results in pollen tubes (<a href="#r4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r4">4</a>). The exclusive presence of VPS41 at the vacuole, however, was unexpected. One interpretation is that VPS33 recruitment to the vacuole is transient and limited to sites of membrane fusion, while VPS41 binding to the tonoplast is stable and results from specific lipid-binding affinities. Even though both proteins showed affinity for most phosphoinositides in a lipid strip assay, these results do not rule out the possibility of different affinities to specific phosphoinositides or that their recruitment to specific membranes is enhanced by interaction with other proteins. Two other HOPS subunits, VPS18 and VPS39, the latter being HOPS specific, are also absent from the vacuole (<a href="#r3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r3">3</a>). Given the competing needs of the core subunits VPS33, VPS18, VPS11, and VPS16 for either HOPS or CORVET assembly, the stable recruitment of VPS41 to the tonoplast could serve as a reservoir for this subunit in a way that does not compete for complex assembly.</p>
<p>Phosphoinositides recruit HOPS to the vertex ring between two juxtaposed vacuoles undergoing fusion in yeast (<a href="#r27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r27">27</a>, <a href="#r52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r52">52</a>). We hypothesized that these lipids could fulfill a similar role in plants, and this was first supported by VPS41 and VPS33 binding to immobilized phosphoinositides on a membrane. Surprisingly, both VPS33 and VPS41 also bound to membrane liposomes but only in the absence of PtdIns(3)P or PtdIns(3,5)P<sub>2</sub>. This result implicates membrane curvature as an additional factor in the recruitment of HOPS to cellular membranes in plants and highlights the sensitivity of these proteins to both membrane topology and phosphoinositide lipid content. The plant vacuole membrane is a flat surface compared with the late endosomes, and membrane curvature could indeed represent a proxy for organelle identity in plant vacuole traffic. We propose that VPS41 binding to the vacuole membrane entails its recruitment by PtdIns(3)P or PtdIns(3,5)P<sub>2</sub>, since PtdIns(3)P has been detected at the tonoplast, and both are important for vacuolar traffic (<a href="#r64" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r64">64</a>, <a href="#r65" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r65">65</a>). In contrast, VPS41 binding to curved membranes such as those of late endosomes is independent of and inhibited by phosphoinositides. This model is further supported by the loss of VPS41-GFP from the vacuole membrane but not from late endosomes in Wm-treated cells.</p>
<p>The inhibitory effect of phosphoinositides on VPS33 and VPS41 binding to liposomes suggests that plants have evolved different mechanisms of regulating vacuole fusion compared with yeast. In contrast to plants, the complete yeast HOPS complex and VPS41 alone bind to liposomes containing vacuolar phosphoinositides, but they do not bind strongly to bare liposomes (<a href="#r27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r27">27</a>, <a href="#r66" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r66">66</a>, <a href="#r67" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r67">67</a>). Therefore, while phosphoinositides promote recruitment of HOPS to curved membranes in yeast, they have the opposite effect in plants. Current models for vacuole fusion propose that the site of HOPS function, the vertex of two juxtaposed vacuoles, represents a highly curved membrane (<a href="#r68" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r68">68</a>, <a href="#r69" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r69">69</a>). Moreover, small liposomes may be used as a proxy for HOPS interactions with the membrane at the vertex ring (<a href="#r68" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r68">68</a>). The liposomes used here are not small enough to represent membrane curvature at sites of vacuole fusion, but if phosphoinositides also abolish HOPS binding at these sites, it would be consistent with yet another phenomenon observed only in plants. Our group has shown that Wm induces vacuole fusion in plants, both in developing roots and in guard cells, and we proposed that phosphoinositides function as negative regulators of vacuole fusion (<a href="#r2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r2">2</a>). This effect of Wm, however, is not observed in yeast vacuoles (<a href="#r48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r48">48</a>) or animal lysosomes (<a href="#r49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r49">49</a>), and therefore, plants seemed to have coerced the regulatory role of phosphoinositides into inhibitors rather than promoters of vacuole fusion. This difference may be due to evolutionary changes in the mechanisms by which VPS41 binds to membranes. VPS41 binding to highly curved membranes in yeast is mediated by an amphipathic lipid packing sensor (ALPS) motif, which also contains two important regulatory phosphorylation sites (<a href="#r67" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r67">67</a>). The ALPS motif is not conserved in plants including <em>Arabidopsis</em> (<a href="http://www.pnas.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1073/pnas.1807763115/-/DCSupplemental" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"><em>SI Appendix</em>, Fig. S5</a>), which further supports the existence of plant-specific mechanisms for recruitment of HOPS to highly curved membranes.</p>
<p>PtdIns(3)P and PtdIns(3,5)P<sub>2</sub> are important phosphoinositides for vacuole traffic and dynamics (<a href="#r64" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r64">64</a>). PtdIns(3,5)P<sub>2</sub> is produced by phosphorylation of the PtdIns(3)P precursor by the PI5 kinases FAB1A and FAB1B (<a href="#r70" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r70">70</a>), and FAB1A has been localized to late endosomes (<a href="#r71" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r71">71</a>). A new sensor of PtdIns(3,5)P<sub>2</sub> indicates that this lipid accumulates in late endosomes (<a href="#r72" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r72">72</a>), but it is not clear how much can persist after late endosome fusion with the vacuole. The possibility that PtdIns(3,5)P<sub>2</sub> accumulates in the tonoplast is supported by the localization of the PtdIns(3,5)P<sub>2</sub> phosphatases SUPPRESSOR OF ACTIN 2â5 (SAC2â5) exclusively to the vacuole membrane (<a href="#r73" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r73">73</a>). Interestingly, Wm treatment resulted in significant dissociation of VPS41-GFP from the tonoplast, which could result from rapid depletion of PtdIns(3,5)P<sub>2</sub> specifically in the tonoplast if both synthesis is stalled due to the unavailability of its precursor and active dephosphorylation by SAC phosphatases (<a href="#r73" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r73">73</a>). PtdIns(3,5)P<sub>2</sub> has recently emerged as an important regulator of plant growth, development, and stress responses. PtdIns(3,5)P<sub>2</sub> is critical for polar growth (<a href="#r74" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r74">74</a>) and is induced under osmotic stress in plants (<a href="#r75" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r75">75</a>) and yeast (<a href="#r76" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r76">76</a>). Loss of FAB1A and FAB1B results in pollen lethality due to the formation of aberrantly large vacuoles during pollen development (<a href="#r77" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r77">77</a>), which implicates PtdIns(3,5)P<sub>2</sub> in vacuole biogenesis. Moreover, PtdIns(3,5)P<sub>2</sub> was found to be important for vacuole acidification and fragmentation during stomata closing (<a href="#r78" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r78">78</a>), and loss of the PtdIns(3,5)P<sub>2</sub> phosphatases SAC2â5, which is expected to increase its accumulation in membranes, induces vacuole fragmentation in <em>Arabidopsis</em> roots (<a href="#r73" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r73">73</a>). It is tempting to speculate that these roles of PtdIns(3,5)P<sub>2</sub> in promoting vacuole fragmentation in guard cells during stomata closure result from its inhibitory effect on VPS33 and VPS41 recruitment to sites of apposing vacuole membranes, which would be otherwise available for fusion. Our results suggest that vacuolar phosphoinositides are likely to play an important role in vacuole fusionâfragmentation dynamics by controlling the recruitment of HOPS to apposing vacuole membranes. Further research is necessary to test this hypothesis.</p></section><section id="s11"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">HOPSâSNARE Interactions Are Mediated by the SYP22 Qa SNARE.</h3>
<p>The HOPS complex is expected to interact with SNARE proteins to regulate membrane fusion at the vacuole. That <em>zip2/vps41</em> partially suppressed the fragmented vacuole phenotype of <em>zig-1</em>/<em>vti11</em> is consistent with functional interactions between HOPS and the vacuole SNARE complexes containing VTI11 and SYP22. The mutation in <em>zip2/vps41</em> may result in altered HOPSâSNARE interactions or the ability of HOPS to promote or proofread the SNARE complex assembly. Furthermore, the altered localization of VPS41-GFP and VPS33-GFP in the <em>zig-1</em> mutant is consistent with a functional interaction between HOPS and SNARE that mediates vacuole fusion. We speculate that the bright punctate localizations of the tagged VPS41 and VPS33 in <em>zig-1</em> may represent nonfunctional fusogenic complexes where SNAREs and HOPS are retained, with delays in their tonoplast accumulation and recycling. This was also observed when the SYP22 SNARE was localized to the same clusters of organelles. Furthermore, we previously hypothesized that the fragmented vacuole phenotype of <em>zig-1</em> mutants could result from a putative proofreading activity of HOPS upon the mismatched vacuolar SNARE complex missing VTI11 (<a href="#r2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r2">2</a>), as it has been shown for yeast (<a href="#r79" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r79">79</a>). The aggregation of VPS41 molecules in <em>zig-1</em> is consistent with such activity, as HOPS could be loaded onto the abnormal SNARE complex while preventing fusion to occur. Further experiments are necessary to confirm this hypothesis. BiFC experiments showed that both HOPS subunits, VPS33 and VPS41, interacted with SYP22 in vivo, but only the VPS33âSYP22 interaction was detected in vitro. This is consistent with a role of HOPS as a chaperone for SNARE complex assembly (see below) and with coimmunoprecipitation between VCL1 and SYP22 in <em>Arabidopsis</em> seedlings (<a href="#r35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r35">35</a>). These interactions were detected in punctate structures similar to those of VPS33-VPS41 and VTI11-SYP22, suggesting that HOPSâSNARE interactions probably occur in late endosomes. Neither VPS33 or VPS41 interacted with VTI11 in the BiFC assay, which suggests that SNAREâHOPS interactions are not mediated by VTI11 protein surfaces. Overall, our results are consistent with the current model for HOPSâSNARE interactions that mediate membrane fusion in yeast. In this model, VPS33 binding to the Qa-SNARE Vam3 is the first step for SNARE complex assembly. The VPS33âQa-SNARE interaction is thought to promote a conformational change, which exposes binding surfaces for other SNAREs on the VPS33 subunit (<a href="#r80" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="r80">80</a>). The interactions between VPS33 and SYP22, both in BiFC and in vitro, suggest that this mechanism is conserved in plants. Therefore, initial binding of VPS33 and SYP22 may be the first step in the HOPSâSNARE interactions that mediate vacuole fusion in plants.</p></section></section><section id="s12"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Methods</h2>
