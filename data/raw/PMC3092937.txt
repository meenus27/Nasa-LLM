</div></section></section><section aria-label="Article content"><section class="body main-article-body"><section class="abstract" id="Abs1"><h2>Abstract</h2>
<p>A major category of mutant hematopoietic phenotypes in <em>Drosophila</em> is melanotic tumors or nodules, which consist of abnormal and overproliferated blood cells, similar to granulomas. Our analyses of the melanotic mutant <em>dappled</em> have revealed a novel type of gene involved in blood cell regulation. The <em>dappled</em> gene is an essential gene that encodes cytochrome b5, a conserved hemoprotein that participates in electron transfer in multiple biochemical reactions and pathways. Viable mutations of <em>dappled</em> cause melanotic nodules and hemocyte misregulation during both hematopoietic waves of development. The sexes are similarly affected, but hemocyte number is different in females and males of both mutants and wild type. Additionally, initial tests show that curcumin enhances the <em>dappled</em> melanotic phenotype and establish screening of endogenous and xenobiotic compounds as a route for analysis of cytochrome b5 function. Overall, <em>dappled</em> provides a tractable genetic model for cytochrome b5, which has been difficult to study in higher organisms.</p>
<section id="kwd-group1" lang="en" class="kwd-group"><p><strong>Keywords:</strong> Hemocytes, <em>dappled</em>, Cytochrome b5, Melanotic tumors, Granulomas</p></section></section><section id="Sec1"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Introduction</h2>
<p>
<em>Drosophila</em> is well studied as a model of innate immunity in humans and has been instrumental in establishing principles of defense and gene signaling pathways that involve both immune induction and hematopoiesis (Lemaitre and Hoffmann <a href="#CR36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR36">2007</a>). The <em>Drosophila</em> Toll pathway mediates the response to microbial infections, and humans were subsequently found to have a family of Toll receptors that also respond to infections. Other shared immune signaling pathways are the Imd (immune deficiency)/TNF1 (tumor necrosis factor 1), JNK (Jun kinase), and JAK-STAT (Janus kinase-STAT transcription factor) pathways. Activation of NFkB transcription factors, which regulate target genes such as cytokines and antimicrobial proteins, is a key shared feature (Wang and Ligoxygakis <a href="#CR72" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR72">2006</a>). Like the signaling pathways, the cell and tissue components of the <em>Drosophila</em> and human immune systems are analogous (Kimbrell and Beutler <a href="#CR31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR31">2001</a>). In <em>Drosophila</em>, the hemocytes and the fat body mediate, respectively, the major cellular and humoral arms of the response. Hemocytes participate in immune signaling to the fat body, phagocytosis, damaged tissue surveillance, encapsulation of foreign intruders, and remodeling of cells during development and also respond to aberrant self-cells to form melanotic tumors/pseudotumors (Babcock et al. <a href="#CR5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR5">2008</a>; Brennan et al. <a href="#CR9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR9">2007</a>; Gateff <a href="#CR23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR23">1994</a>; Rizki and Rizki <a href="#CR55" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR55">1983</a>; Tepass et al. <a href="#CR67" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR67">1994</a>; Vass and Nappi <a href="#CR71" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR71">2000</a>). The fat body, like the mammalian liver, produces abundant humoral response molecules, including antimicrobial peptides released into the hemolymph (Aggrawal and Silverman <a href="#CR1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR1">2007</a>).</p>
<p>
<em>Drosophila</em> produces three types of hemocytes: plasmatocytes, lamellocytes, and crystal cells (Crozatier and Meister <a href="#CR14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR14">2007</a>). Plasmatocytes account for ~95% of total blood cells and most functions. In particular, plasmatocytes are responsible for the phagocytosis of microorganisms during an infection and apoptotic cells during development. Lamellocytes are produced only in response to parasites or by mutations that produce melanotic tumors. Crystal cells constitute ~5% of total blood cells and are involved in melanization. As in vertebrates, <em>Drosophila</em> blood cells also have two developmental origins: one early in the embryonic head mesoderm and another during larval development in the lymph gland (Evans et al. <a href="#CR21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR21">2003</a>). Molecular cascades and factors regulating hematopoiesis are also shared with humans and involve many pathways, for example, Toll, JAK/STAT, Hedgehog, Notch, GATA, and Runx (Evans and Banerjee <a href="#CR20" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR20">2003</a>; Mandal et al. <a href="#CR41" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR41">2007</a>).</p>
<p>A major category of mutant hematopoietic phenotypes in <em>Drosophila</em> is that of melanotic tumors or nodules. Melanotic nodules may be in the hemocoel or associated with the lymph glands and consist of abnormal and overproliferated blood cells that are covered by lamellocytes and melanized, similar to the immune response to a parasitic wasp egg (Luo et al. <a href="#CR40" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR40">1995</a>; Meister and Lagueux <a href="#CR45" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR45">2003</a>; Minakhina and Steward <a href="#CR48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR48">2006b</a>; Stofanko et al. <a href="#CR65" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR65">2008</a>). Mutations in a variety of genes and genetic pathways have been found to produce this phenotype, such as JAK–STAT, which is also associated with blood cell overproliferation in humans, the Toll pathway, and Ras/MAPK (Asha et al. <a href="#CR4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR4">2003</a>; Li <a href="#CR37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR37">2008</a>; Minakhina and Steward <a href="#CR47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR47">2006a</a>; Zettervall et al. <a href="#CR84" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR84">2004</a>). Some other genes with this mutant phenotype are <em>air</em>
<sup><em>8</em></sup>, encoding ribosomal protein s6 (Watson et al. <a href="#CR75" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR75">1992</a>); <em>black pearl</em>, encoding a DnaJ homolog (Becker et al. <a href="#CR6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR6">2001</a>); <em>l(3)malignant blood</em> neoplasm, encoding a protein with human cytokeratin homology (Gateff <a href="#CR23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR23">1994</a>); <em>yantar</em>, encoding a conserved arginine-rich protein (Sinenko et al. <a href="#CR63" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR63">2004</a>); and the Polycomb group gene <em>multi sex combs</em> (Remillieux-Leschelle et al. <a href="#CR54" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR54">2002</a>). The melanotic tumor genes are an important category to identify and define in order to understand blood cell function and regulation.</p>
<p>In a P-element enhancer detector screen concentrating on immune-related tissues and phenotypes, we identified the melanotic tumor gene <em>dappled</em> (<em>dpld</em>) (Rodriguez et al. <a href="#CR56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR56">1996</a>). Enhancer detector expression of <em>dappled</em> was high in the fat body, oenocytes, ring gland, and gut. Lethal and viable mutations of <em>dappled</em> produced melanotic tumors, and lethal mutation was also associated with aberrant tissue morphology, for example, of the fat body and gut (Rodriguez et al. <a href="#CR56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR56">1996</a>). A distinguishing feature of <em>dappled</em> mutants is consistent melanotic tumor formation, in contrast to other melanotic tumor mutants, which typically have low and variable rates. We now report identification of <em>dappled</em> as a member of the cytochrome b5 family. Cytochrome b5 is a conserved, ubiquitous, small hemoprotein that participates in electron transfer in multiple biochemical reactions and pathways, such as fatty acid desaturation, cytochrome P450-catalyzed reactions, sterol metabolism, and conversion of methemoglobin (Schenkman and Jansson <a href="#CR59" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR59">2003</a>). Cytochrome b5 is thus involved in processes such as metabolism of endogenous and xenobiotic compounds, maintenance of oxygen levels, and production of steroid hormones (Locuson et al. <a href="#CR39" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR39">2007</a>; Miller <a href="#CR46" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR46">2005</a>; Yamazaki and Shimada <a href="#CR80" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR80">2006</a>). Cytochrome b5 genetics in higher organisms has not been very amenable to study, and <em>dappled</em> provides a new avenue of approach for study of cytochrome b5 functions. In this report, we characterize the <em>dappled</em> mutant hemocyte phenotype and present the potential to study cytochrome b5 function by screening for compounds that affect the <em>dappled</em> hemocyte phenotype.</p></section><section id="Sec2"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Materials and Methods</h2>
<section id="Sec3"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">
<em>Drosophila</em> Stocks and Rearing</h3>
<p>Oregon R flies were used as the standard wild-type strain. The <em>dappled</em> mutants are the <em>dpld</em>
<sup><em>MLB</em></sup> (97/16) and <em>dpld</em>
<sup><em>EJL</em></sup> (142/2) strains of Rodriguez et al. (<a href="#CR56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR56">1996</a>). Experiments were performed using <em>dpld</em>
<sup><em>MLB</em></sup>/<em>dpld</em>
<sup><em>EJL</em></sup> transheterozygotes or <em>dpld</em> mutants hemizygous with <em>Df(2R)ST1</em> (Bloomington Stock Center). The <em>hemese</em>-<em>Gal4</em> strain is that of Zettervall et al. (<a href="#CR84" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR84">2004</a>). The <em>UAS</em>-<em>dappled</em> transgenic strain was made using the cDNA clone RE66521 from the Bloomington Drosophila Genome Project, which matched our splice product for <em>dappled</em> mRNA (see below). The insert of plasmid RE66521 was isolated using the <em>Eco</em>RI and <em>Bam</em>HI restriction enzyme sites, ligated into the <em>Eco</em>RI and <em>Xba</em>I sites of the pUAST vector using <em>Bam</em>HI/<em>Xba</em>I linkers, and transgenic strains made by Genetic Services, Inc. Insertion strains of <em>UAS</em>-<em>dappled</em> into chromosomes X, 2, and 3 were recovered. All stocks were raised on the Bloomington Stock Center food recipe in a 25°C incubator. For the curcumin-raised flies, the food, before cooling, was supplemented with 20 mg/ml curcumin (Sigma), and parents were placed onto the food for mating and egg laying. Compare with Gupta et al. The PCR primers used to compare the 88/15 (k08815) strain P-element insertion sequences <a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/AQ034113" class="usa-link" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">AQ034113</a> and <a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/AQ025871" class="usa-link" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">AQ025871</a> (Tweedie et al. <a href="#CR69" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR69">2009</a>) to wild-type sequences were TATCGTCGCTGTGCTTACAGAT (60, forward) and CTAAACCTCCAATTCGGCTATG (61, reverse) at the <a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/AQ034113" class="usa-link" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">AQ034113</a> end and CTCCACGCGTAATATACCCATT (58, forward) and AGTCATGGATGCGTAGTCACAC (59, reverse) at the <a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/AQ025871" class="usa-link" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">AQ025871</a> end. In order to confirm the 88/15 insertion site, PCR fragments that resulted from the P-element end primer <em>Rsy</em>II (Tweedie et al. <a href="#CR69" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR69">2009</a>) in combination with primers 59 and 60 were sequenced. The PCR primers used to test for possible alternative splicing of CG2140/<em>dappled</em> were sequences 55, 56, and 57 (Fig. <a href="#Fig1" class="usa-link">1</a>). Clustal W was used to align the <em>dappled</em> cytochrome b5 sequence to selected other cytochrome b5 proteins.

<p>Total RNA was isolated using the Totally RNA kit (Ambion), and poly-A RNA was isolated using the Oligotex Direct mRNA Purification kit (Qiagen). <p>Third-instar larvae were developmentally staged as early versus late wandering phase based on retention in the gut of food dye (Zettervall et al. <a href="#CR84" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR84">2004</a>). The Bright-Line Counting Chamber (Fisher) hemocytometer was used to count blood cells stained with monochlorobimane dye (Tirouvanziam et al. <a href="#CR68" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR68">2004</a>) or GFP-expressing blood cells as in Zettervall et al. (<a href="#CR84" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR84">2004</a>), except that instead of red food color we used blue (Deep Blue Shade, Esco Foods, Inc.). Siblings that were heterozygous for <em>dappled</em> were distinguished based on either a CyO balancer with the marker y+ (i.e., <em>y</em>; +/<em>dpld</em> vs. <em>y</em>; +/<em>CyO, y</em>+) or B-galactosidase staining to indicate presence or absence of a <em>dpld</em> enhancer trap (Rodriguez et al. <a href="#CR56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR56">1996</a>). Separate counts of blood cells in <em>y</em> versus <em>y</em>+ larvae established that <em>y</em> did not affect the blood cell counts. Determination of sex was by the size of the gonads or by marking the sexes with <em>y</em> versus <em>y</em>+. Hemocyte counts from different samples were compared using the <em>t</em> test, and <em>P</em> values were calculated using VassarStats (<a href="http://faculty.vassar.edu/lowry/VassarStats.html" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">http://faculty.vassar.edu/lowry/VassarStats.html</a>).</p></section></section><section id="Sec7"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Results</h2>
<section id="Sec8"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Cytochrome b5, the Encoded Product of <em>dappled</em>
</h3>
<p>The original <em>dappled</em> alleles, <em>dpld</em>
<sup><em>EJL</em></sup> and <em>dpld</em>
<sup><em>MLB</em></sup>, result from the P-element insertions (P-lacW) in strains 142/2 (k14202) and 97/16 (k09716), respectively (Rodriguez et al. <a href="#CR56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR56">1996</a>). The <em>dpld</em>
<sup><em>EJL</em></sup> allele is homozygous and hemizygous lethal, and <em>dpld</em>
<sup><em>MLB</em></sup> is homozygous and hemizygous viable. Heteroallelic <em>dpld</em>
<sup><em>EJL</em></sup>
<em>/dpld</em>
<sup><em>MLB</em></sup> is also viable and, like <em>dpld</em>
<sup><em>EJL</em></sup>
<em>and dpld</em>
<sup><em>MLB</em></sup> homozygotes and hemizygotes, has melanotic tumors (Rodriguez et al. <a href="#CR56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR56">1996</a>). In order to identify the gene associated with the melanotic tumor phenotype, we molecularly cloned and sequenced the <em>dappled</em> gene. We used the technique of plasmid rescue to isolate the genomic DNA flanking the k14202 and k09716 insertions. Sequencing of the flanking DNA and alignment to the genome placed both insertions into the 5′ UTR of CG2140 (Tweedie et al. <a href="#CR69" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR69">2009</a>), with k09716 46 bp farther upstream than k14202 (Fig. <a href="#Fig1" class="usa-link">1</a>).</p>
<p>In contrast to our results, genome annotation assigned <em>dappled</em> as CG1624 based on the position of the lethal P-element insertion 88/15 (k08815), which did not complement the 142/2 insertion (Spradling et al. <a href="#CR64" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR64">1999</a>). We therefore investigated this discrepancy in the identification of <em>dappled.</em> The 5′ and 3′ flanking sequences for the k08815 insertion are 3′-<a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/AQ034113" class="usa-link" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">AQ034113</a> and 5′-<a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/AQ025871" class="usa-link" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">AQ025871</a>. Alignment of these flanking sequences on the latest <em>Drosophila</em> genome sequence, however, places them 176,059 bp apart. There are several potential causes for this gap in the sequence: multiple insertions, an error in data reporting, or a large deletion. To test for the accuracy of the k08815 flanking sequence, primers were designed to (1) flank the 3′ and 5′ insertion sites of the k08815 transposon and (2) extend from a P-element end primer (p<em>Rsy</em>II) into a flanking sequence. The resultant PCR fragments supported the published flanking sequences, as wild-type products were not observed in the mutant strain and both insertion sites were verified with the P-element end primer (Fig. <a href="#Fig1" class="usa-link">1</a>). Finally, these PCR products were sequenced to provide confirmation of the flanking sequences. These results indicate that k08815 is not a simple insertion but is associated with additional disruption at its genomic position. We set out to differentiate between either multiple insertions or a large deletion by performing complementation tests with other lethal insertion strains in the region. Complementation tests confirmed that a large deletion accompanies the k08815 insertion (Fig. <a href="#Fig1" class="usa-link">1</a>). Complementation and mapping also placed two more insertions, k00107 and 01857, into the 5′ UTR and intron of CG2140/<em>dappled</em>, respectively (Fig. <a href="#Fig1" class="usa-link">1</a>).</p>
<p>We tested these various <em>dappled</em> mutants for alterations in transcription, further confirming that the <em>dappled</em> phenotype results from an effect on CG2140, by performing Northern blot analysis using RNA from <em>dappled</em> mutants. This analysis showed a decrease in CG2140 transcript levels in the <em>dappled</em> mutants compared with the Oregon R controls (Fig. <a href="#Fig2" class="usa-link">2</a>). The <em>dappled</em> strains are hypomorphic, with the lethal alleles stronger. The lethal allele <em>dpld</em>
<sup><em>EJL</em></sup> expresses approximately 10% of wild-type embryonic RNA levels, whereas the viable allele <em>dpld</em>
<sup><em>MLB</em></sup> has ~80% of wild-type adult RNA levels. Analysis of the transcripts by RT-PCR and sequencing showed one splice variant, with a single intron (Fig. <a href="#Fig1" class="usa-link">1</a>).

<p>The <em>dpld</em>
<sup><em>MLB</em></sup> insertion has made analysis possible, as in addition to producing consistent melanotic tumor formation, it is the only viable allele. Maintenance of <em>dpld</em>
<sup><em>MLB</em></sup> homozygotes as a stock, however, was not feasible because of effects on fertility. Homozygous <em>dpld</em>
<sup><em>MLB</em></sup> or transheterozygous <em>dpld</em>
<sup><em>MLB</em></sup>/<em>dpld</em>
<sup><em>EJL</em></sup> females and males produced few progeny, even when mated with wild-type flies (&lt;5 progeny/<em>dappled</em> parent), and mutant females laid few eggs (~50/female). It is unlikely that this fertility problem is due simply to physical obstruction in the abdomen, because flies with less obtrusive tumors (i.e., small tumors near the thorax) also show low fertility. To avoid the effects of accumulation of second site mutations by maintaining the <em>dpld</em>
<sup><em>MLB</em></sup> chromosome over a balancer, all experiments were done on progeny from crosses that produced transheterozygotes or hemizygotes.</p>
<p>The development of the Gal4–UAS system (Brand and Perrimon <a href="#CR8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR8">1993</a>) to include drivers for the hematopoietic system makes it more feasible to be able to investigate the blood cell phenotypes of mutant genes, for example, Kimbrell et al. (<a href="#CR32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR32">2002</a>) and Zettervall et al. (<a href="#CR84" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR84">2004</a>). The <em>hemese</em>-<em>Gal4</em> construct drives expression in blood cells of the plasmatocyte and lamellocyte classes (Zettervall et al. <a href="#CR84" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR84">2004</a>). Strains with <em>hemese</em>-<em>Gal4 UAS</em>-<em>GFP</em> thus have the blood cells marked with GFP, enabling hemocyte counting and phenotype analysis in various genetic strains (Zettervall et al. <a href="#CR84" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR84">2004</a>). We used <em>hemese</em>-<em>Gal4</em>
<em>UAS</em>-<em>GFP</em> to investigate the blood cells of <em>dappled</em> melanotic tumor mutants. In melanotic tumors of <em>dpld</em>
<sup><em>EJL</em></sup>
<em>/dpld</em>
<sup><em>MLB</em></sup>; <em>hemese</em>-<em>Gal4 UAS</em>-<em>GFP</em> larvae, all of the cells in the masses were found to fluoresce strongly for GFP (Fig. <a href="#Fig4" class="usa-link">4</a>). Cell counts of circulating plasmatocytes and lamellocytes showed large numbers of circulating lamellocytes (Fig. <a href="#Fig4" class="usa-link">4</a>). Lamellocytes are not found in normal, healthy larvae, whereas in melanotic nodule mutants lamellocytes are abundant (Crozatier and Meister <a href="#CR14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR14">2007</a>). The <em>dappled</em> melanotic tumors are thus specifically designated as melanotic nodules, which consist of hemocytes, as opposed to melanizations, which are non-blood cell tissues that have melanized (Minakhina and Steward <a href="#CR48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR48">2006b</a>).
</p>

Larvae with small versus large melanotic nodules showed different numbers of circulating plasmatocytes and lamellocytes (Fig. <a href="#Fig4" class="usa-link">4</a>). Notably, lamellocytes are more prevalent in small tumors, whereas plasmatocytes are more abundant in large nodules. One possible interpretation of the higher number of lamellocytes in smaller nodules is that these nodules are still actively forming, and lamellocytes are still being recruited to the nodules. During normal larval development, the number of plasmatocytes typically increases two- to threefold from the early wandering (early) to the late wandering (late) stage of the third instar (Crozatier and Meister <a href="#CR14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR14">2007</a>; Zettervall et al. <a href="#CR84" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR84">2004</a>). Plasmatocyte representation from early to late stage wandering was different for small and large tumors. Larvae with large tumors showed an increase from early to late stage, whereas larvae with small tumors did not (Fig. <a href="#Fig4" class="usa-link">4</a>), and this may also reflect the dynamic nature of <em>dappled</em> melanotic nodule formation.</p>
<p>Heterozygosity of <em>dappled</em> does not produce tumors, but it does have a dominant effect on blood cell number. The <em>dappled</em> heterozygotes (Bal/<em>dpld</em>) have a significantly higher number of plasmatocytes than wild-type larvae (+/+). This difference was confirmed by counting hemocytes of <em>dappled</em> heterozygotes (<em>dpld/</em>+) and their control siblings (Bal/+) (Fig. <a href="#Fig5" class="usa-link">5</a>). Further, counts of siblings showed that the increase in plasmatocytes is present both early and late in the third instar. The relative numbers of lamellocytes in <em>dappled</em> heterozygotes is less clear, as the average was very low, many larvae had no lamellocytes, and the number did not increase from early to late stage (Fig. <a href="#Fig5" class="usa-link">5</a>). Thus, although the lamellocyte data are statistically significant (<em>P</em> &lt; 0.05), <em>dappled</em> heterozygosity at most yields a very weak induction of lamellocyte differentiation.
</p>

<p>We next asked if the <em>dappled</em> phenotype could be rescued by expressing wild-type <em>dappled</em> only in blood cells. We constructed a plasmid containing <em>UAS</em>-<em>dpld</em>, which was used to make transgenic strains with <em>UAS</em>-<em>dpld</em> in different chromosomal locations. The <em>hemese</em>-<em>Gal4</em> was then used to drive <em>UAS</em>-<em>dpld,</em> and the rates of melanotic nodule formation were compared in <em>dappled</em> mutants and controls. In addition to hemocytes, <em>hemese</em>-<em>Gal4</em> drives strong expression in the salivary glands, so a control salivary gland driver, <em>sgs3</em>-<em>Gal4</em>, was also used to drive <em>UAS</em>-<em>dpld</em>. To test for rescue of lethality by ubiquitous expression, the <em>da</em>-<em>Gal4</em> driver (Pili-Floury et al. <a href="#CR51" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR51">2004</a>) was used.</p>
<p>The <em>dappled</em> mutants with <em>hemese</em>-<em>Gal4</em>, but not <em>sgs3</em>-<em>Gal4</em>, driving <em>UAS</em>-<em>dpld</em> are rescued from the melanotic phenotype. Three different <em>UAS</em>-<em>dpld</em> insertions were tested with <em>hemese</em>-<em>Gal4</em>, and all showed similar rates of rescue (Table <a href="#Tab1" class="usa-link">1</a>). Dissections were performed to determine if blood cell nodules might be forming but not melanizing. The dissections confirmed that blood cell nodule formation without accompanying melanization did not occur.
</p>

<p>The <em>dappled</em> nodules form early in larval development, which is more consistent with an increase of plasmatocytes from the first hematopoietic wave (embryonic origin) rather than the second hematopoietic wave (lymph gland origin at the end of the third instar). As noted above, plasmatocytes in <em>dappled</em> tumors express the <em>hemese</em>-<em>Gal4</em> driver, which does not express in the lymph glands, further indicating an early developmental origin for these cells. Additionally, the lymph glands appear normal, at least at a gross level, and only one <em>dappled</em> enhancer trap expresses in the lymph glands (Rodriguez et al. <a href="#CR56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR56">1996</a>). We analyzed 1 kb of the <em>dappled</em> upstream region for potential binding by blood cell, lymph gland, and other transcription factors using the Transfac database (Wingender <a href="#CR77" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR77">2008</a>) provided by Biobase Biological Databases (<a href="http://www.biobase-international.com/index.php?id=home" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">http://www.biobase-international.com/index.php?id=home</a>). Among the transcription factor binding sites are many that are predicted to be T cell factor/pan binding sites (Brunner et al. <a href="#CR11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR11">1997</a>), consistent with blood cell expression, and tinman/Nkx2.5 binding sites (Kimbrell et al. <a href="#CR32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR32">2002</a>), suggesting lymph gland expression (Table <a href="#Tab3" class="usa-link">3</a>).

<p>Cytochrome b5 is widely studied for its effects on drug metabolism, and conversely, various drugs are investigated for alteration of cytochrome b5 levels (El-Lakkany et al. <a href="#CR19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR19">2004</a>; Juvonen et al. <a href="#CR30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR30">1992</a>; Portoles et al. <a href="#CR52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR52">1989</a>; Schenkman and Jansson <a href="#CR58" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR58">1999</a>; Sheweita <a href="#CR61" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR61">2004</a>; Yamaori et al. <a href="#CR79" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR79">2003</a>; Yamazaki et al. <a href="#CR81" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR81">2002</a>). We hypothesized that modulation of the <em>dappled</em> phenotype could be used as an in vivo assay for cytochrome b5 related effects of endogenous and xenobiotic compounds. We are therefore screening compounds with a known connection to cytochrome P450s and/or anti-inflammatory and anticarcinogenic properties for enhancement or suppression of the <em>dappled</em> melanotic phenotype. Initial studies with aspirin and the corticosteroid dexamethasone are negative (data not shown), whereas curcumin, the active ingredient in the spice turmeric, has consistently shown an enhancement of the melanotic phenotype (Table <a href="#Tab5" class="usa-link">5</a>; Fig. <a href="#Fig7" class="usa-link">7</a>). Adult <em>dappled</em> mutants that have been raised (egg to adult) on curcumin-supplemented food have an enhanced melanotic phenotype, showing more and/or larger melanotic tumors (Table <a href="#Tab5" class="usa-link">5</a>). The enhanced phenotype of the <em>dappled</em> mutants is already evident in the larval stages (Fig. <a href="#Fig7" class="usa-link">7</a>). Adult <em>dappled</em> heterozygotes, however, do not develop a melanotic phenotype when raised on curcumin (Table <a href="#Tab5" class="usa-link">5</a>). Since both the heterozygotes and heteroallelic mutants have some reduction in <em>dappled</em> transcript levels, the effects of curcumin may depend on the exact amount of cytochrome b5 protein that is produced.

<p>We have identified <em>dappled</em> as a member of the conserved, ubiquitous cytochrome b5 family. The complexities of cytochrome b5 function, through its participation in electron transfer in multiple biochemical reactions, are reflected in the difficulties and limited study of its genetics in higher organisms. Characterizations in humans have shown that cytochrome b5 mutation causes methemoglobinemia, which interferes with the balance between hemoglobin and methemoglobin, thus affecting oxygenation and inflammation (Hegesh et al. <a href="#CR27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR27">1986</a>; Umbreit <a href="#CR70" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR70">2007</a>). Mutation of cytochrome b5 reductase also causes methemoglobinemia and is more common (Davis et al. <a href="#CR16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR16">2004</a>). In addition, one case of cytochrome b5 methemoglobinemia may also have been associated with hormonal defects (Giordano et al. <a href="#CR25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR25">1994</a>). With the exception of a porcine mutation associated with an altered androstenone level (Lin et al. <a href="#CR38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR38">2005</a>), cytochrome b5 mutations in other organisms have not been identified. In mice, cytochrome b5 deletion is also lethal, but experiments with a tissue-specific knockout have allowed liver function to be addressed (Finn et al. <a href="#CR22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR22">2008</a>). With our studies of <em>dappled</em>, <em>Drosophila</em> now provides a tractable genetic model for the study of cytochrome b5 in blood cells and other tissues.</p>
<p>Hemocytes are particularly sensitive to mutation of <em>dappled</em> and show that cytochrome b5 functions to produce normal differentiation and numbers of hemocytes. Mutation of one copy of <em>dappled</em> results in a significant increase in the number of circulating hemocytes, and mutation of both copies results in the formation of melanotic nodules. The formation of <em>dappled</em> melanotic nodules is a Class 2 reaction in the classification of Watson et al. (<a href="#CR74" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR74">1991</a>), in which Class 2 represents alteration of immune cells and Class 1 represents normal immune cells responding to other cell types that are aberrant. The dominant effect on blood cell number emphasizes that wild-type levels of cytochrome b5 are needed for full normal functions. In humans, levels of cytochrome b5 have been implicated as important with respect to xenobiotic metabolism and health. Kurian et al. (<a href="#CR34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR34">2007</a>) hypothesized that functionally significant genetic variability in cytochrome b5 occurs in individuals without clinical symptoms. They investigated processing of hydroxylamines, which are oxidative products of arylamine drugs and environmental carcinogens, by the mutant cytochrome b5 of one heterozygous individual and found, in vitro, altered affinity for hydroxylamine substrates and dramatically reduced cytochrome b5 expression (Kurian et al. <a href="#CR34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR34">2007</a>).</p>
<p>Expression of wild-type <em>dappled</em> in hemocytes rescued both the increase in blood cell number and the formation of melanotic nodules. The driver used was <em>hemese</em>-<em>Gal4</em>, which is active in circulating plasmatocytes and sessile hemocytes, but not the lymph glands (Markus et al. <a href="#CR42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR42">2009</a>; Zettervall et al. <a href="#CR84" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR84">2004</a>). Interestingly, the sessile hemocytes are a newly discovered hematopoietic site and appear to be the main developmental origin of lamellocytes (Markus et al. <a href="#CR42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR42">2009</a>). This site of expression may be the key factor in rescuing the <em>dappled</em> melanotic nodule phenotype. Rescue of the plasmatocyte increase in <em>dappled</em> heterozygotes is expected to result from the <em>hemese</em>-<em>Gal4</em> expression in circulating blood cells of the first hematopoietic wave, both because of the age of the larvae (prerelease of lymph gland cells) and because <em>hemese</em>-<em>Gal4</em> does not express in lymph glands. However, <em>dappled</em> also increased the number of hemocytes from the second hematopoietic wave. Hemocytes from both developmental origins, embryonic and lymph gland, require <em>dappled</em> in order to be produced in normal amounts. It is an interesting question as to whether the plasmatocyte increase in <em>dappled</em> mutants can be independently controlled in the first and second hematopoietic waves.</p>
<p>Does the increase in plasmatocytes indicate overproliferation or less apoptosis? Since <em>dappled</em> encodes a cytochrome b5, the obvious choice would be apoptosis rather than overproliferation. There are two problems with this choice: it is mitochondrial cytochrome b5 that is established in the canonical cytochrome C signaling apoptotic pathway, and the use of that pathway by <em>Drosophila</em> is controversial (Kornbluth and White <a href="#CR33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR33">2005</a>; Means and Hays <a href="#CR44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR44">2007</a>; Oberst et al. <a href="#CR49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR49">2008</a>; Ow et al. <a href="#CR50" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR50">2008</a>). Microsomal cytochrome b5, however, has also been suggested to be involved in cytochrome C mediated apoptosis (Davydov <a href="#CR17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR17">2001</a>), and <em>Drosophila</em> may have its own version of apoptotic signaling that uses the <em>dappled</em> cytochrome b5. Further, blood cells are well described for their role in response to apoptotic cells, but the role of their own apoptosis in development and infection is not clear (Bruckner et al. <a href="#CR10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR10">2004</a>; Crozatier and Meister <a href="#CR14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR14">2007</a>; Lemaitre and Hoffmann <a href="#CR36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR36">2007</a>).</p>
<p>The sensitivity of hemocytes (compared with other cell types) to <em>dappled</em> mutation may result from blood cell mitotic cell division continuing during development (Crozatier and Meister <a href="#CR14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR14">2007</a>). There are many potential mechanisms for <em>dappled</em> to affect overproliferation, especially given the increasingly complex functions of plasmatocytes (Charroux and Royet <a href="#CR13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR13">2009</a>). One option consistent with cytochrome b5 function, and potentially the <em>dappled</em> effects on fertility, is that hormonal signaling of hematopoiesis is affected. In that case, however, rescue of the <em>dappled</em> blood cell phenotype would be predicted by adding back wild-type <em>dappled</em> expression in the steroid-producing ring gland (Gilbert <a href="#CR24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR24">2004</a>) rather than hemocytes. Alternatively, <em>dappled</em> expression could rescue a steroid-processing step in the hemocytes.</p>
<p>An intriguing option is that cytochrome b5 has a new, previously unidentified role in blood cell signaling pathways, for example, through JAK/Stat or JNK. More specifically, a candidate to mediate the effects of <em>dappled</em> is TNF-receptor associated factor 6 (Traf6), which is predicted from protein studies to interact with cytochrome b5 (Drosophila Interactions Database, <a href="http://www.droidb.org" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">http://www.droidb.org</a>). Among its activities, Traf6 is an upstream activator of nuclear factor-kappaB, and mutants are immune response defective (Cha et al. <a href="#CR12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR12">2003</a>; Wu and Arron <a href="#CR78" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR78">2003</a>). Cytochrome b5 involvement with Traf6 is a testable hypothesis through analysis of genetic interaction between <em>dappled</em> and <em>Traf6</em> mutants.</p>
<p>Our results with curcumin demonstrate that screening of endogenous and xenobiotic compounds for effects on the <em>dappled</em> phenotypes can be done as an in vivo approach for analysis of cytochrome b5. Curcumin is widely studied for its potential as a nutritional pharmaceutical (Duvoix et al. <a href="#CR18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR18">2005</a>; Jagetia and Aggarwal <a href="#CR29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR29">2007</a>). Among its many activities, which are mainly anti-inflammatory and anticancer, curcumin inhibits cytochrome P450s, down-regulates NFkB, and affects apoptosis and the cell cycle (Alpers <a href="#CR2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR2">2008</a>; Appiah-Opong et al. <a href="#CR3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR3">2007</a>; Kwon et al. <a href="#CR35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR35">2007</a>; Sa and Das <a href="#CR57" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR57">2008</a>). Curcumin enhanced the melanotic phenotype of <em>dappled</em> mutants. This effect of curcumin is consistent with NFkB involvement, but there are other alternatives, in particular, cytochrome P450 processing in the fat body.</p>
<p>Mutation of <em>dappled</em> showed similar effects in females and males, but an unexpected consequence of our studies is the finding that females and males differ significantly in the number of circulating blood cells. In humans, a difference between the sexes in the numbers of blood cell types is well established, but the effects are unclear (Blum and Pabst <a href="#CR7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR7">2007</a>; Huppert et al. <a href="#CR28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR28">1998</a>; Reichert et al. <a href="#CR53" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR53">1991</a>). In both humans and <em>Drosophila</em>, there are differences in the immune responses between the sexes that affect health (Dale et al. <a href="#CR15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR15">2006</a>; Marriott and Huet-Hudson <a href="#CR43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR43">2006</a>; Shames <a href="#CR60" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR60">2002</a>; Taylor and Kimbrell <a href="#CR66" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR66">2007</a>; Ye et al. <a href="#CR82" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR82">2009</a>; Zandman-Goddard et al. <a href="#CR83" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR83">2007</a>), and the blood cell number differential may be part of mediating the immune response differences. Recently, <em>Drosophila</em> hemocytes have been shown to directly signal immune induction of the fat body, and a reduced number of hemocytes resulted in less immune induction (Shia et al. <a href="#CR62" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR62">2009</a>). In <em>Drosophila</em>, the increase in hemocytes of <em>dappled</em> and females may result in higher immune induction, which may or may not provide a healthier condition. For example, human females typically have a more robust immune response but also a higher incidence of autoimmune disorders (Dale et al. <a href="#CR15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR15">2006</a>; Zandman-Goddard et al. <a href="#CR83" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR83">2007</a>).</p>
<p>Cytochrome b5 expression is critical for survival, and its importance to hemocytes and additional tissues is emphasized by the finding that ubiquitous, but not hemocyte, expression of wild-type <em>dappled</em> rescued lethality. The functions of cytochrome b5 are open for testing in a wide range of tissue types using <em>dappled</em>. In all respects, genetic interaction and drug screens for suppressors and enhancers of <em>dappled</em> will be useful ways to discern the many roles of cytochrome b5 function.</p></section><section id="ack1" class="ack"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Acknowledgments</h2>
