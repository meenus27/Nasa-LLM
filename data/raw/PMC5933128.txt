<p>Modulation of arogenate dehydratase in Arabidopsis causes changes in secondary metabolism, transient overaccumulation of starch, and lower photosynthetic electron transport rates.</p></section><section class="abstract" id="abstract2"><h2>Abstract</h2>
<p>Arogenate dehydratase (ADT) catalyzes the final step of phenylalanine (Phe) biosynthesis. Previous work showed that ADT-deficient Arabidopsis (<em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em>) mutants had significantly reduced lignin contents, with stronger reductions in lines that had deficiencies in more ADT isoforms. Here, by analyzing Arabidopsis <em>ADT</em> mutants using our phenomics facility and ultra-performance liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry-based metabolomics, we describe the effects of the modulation of ADT on photosynthetic parameters and secondary metabolism. Our data indicate that a reduced carbon flux into Phe biosynthesis in <em>ADT</em> mutants impairs the consumption of photosynthetically produced ATP, leading to an increased ATP/ADP ratio, the overaccumulation of transitory starch, and lower electron transport rates. The effect on electron transport rates is caused by an increase in proton motive force across the thylakoid membrane that down-regulates photosystem II activity by the high-energy quenching mechanism. Furthermore, quantitation of secondary metabolites in <em>ADT</em> mutants revealed reduced flavonoid, phenylpropanoid, lignan, and glucosinolate contents, including glucosinolates that are not derived from aromatic amino acids, and significantly increased contents of putative galactolipids and apocarotenoids. Additionally, we used real-time atmospheric monitoring mass spectrometry to compare respiration and carbon fixation rates between the wild type and <em>adt3/4/5/6</em>, our most extreme <em>ADT</em> knockout mutant, which revealed no significant difference in both night- and day-adapted plants. Overall, these data reveal the profound effects of altered ADT activity and Phe metabolism on secondary metabolites and photosynthesis with implications for plant improvement.</p></section><hr class="headless">
<p>Arogenate dehydratases (ADTs) catalyze the dehydrative decarboxylation of arogenate to Phe (<a href="#bib19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib19">Jung et al., 1986</a>; <a href="#bib40" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib40">Siehl and Conn, 1988</a>; <a href="#bib7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib7">Cho et al., 2007</a>) in the stroma of chloroplasts and plastids in vascular plants (<a href="#bib34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib34">Rippert et al., 2009</a>). In terms of carbon allocation from photosynthesis, only a very small fraction of this Phe resides in proteins. Phe downstream metabolism is instead targeted largely toward generating substrates/precursors of lignin biopolymers and an enormous array of compounds that are predominantly phenylpropanoid/phenylpropanoid acetate metabolites, such as flavonoids (<a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.17.01766/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Fig. S1</a>; <a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Corea et al., 2012</a>).</p>
<p>Arabidopsis (<em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em>) has a six-member <em>ADT</em> gene family. However, it is not well understood (1) to what extent specific <em>ADT</em> genes are differentially regulated as to carbon allocation into distinct downstream metabolic branches and/or (2) what effect differential gene expression might have on metabolism overall, including upstream effects on photosynthesis capacity and source/sink relationships.</p>
<p>As shown previously, knockout mutants of the Arabidopsis <em>ADT</em> gene family were associated with different degrees of reduction of lignin content (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Corea et al., 2012</a>). These plant lines were grown under long-day conditions (16 h of light) from germination to maturation (8–10 weeks), which induced stem elongation (bolting) and lignification. From the eight knockout lines generated, single knockouts of <em>ADT1</em> and <em>ADT3</em> had no apparent impact on bolting stem lignin content, whereas the <em>ADT4</em> knockout resulted in a slightly higher lignin stem content and the <em>ADT5</em> knockout had decreased lignin stem content by ∼18%. The <em>adt4/5</em> line displayed a further reduction in lignin content of ∼38%, as did the triple knockouts <em>adt1/4/5</em> and <em>adt3/4/5</em>, which displayed ∼50% reductions. The quadruple knockout <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> possessed the greatest magnitude of lignin reduction of ∼70% and was unable to maintain bolting stem structural integrity. The double mutant <em>adt4/5</em> and the triple mutants <em>adt1/4/5</em> and <em>adt3/4/5</em> also displayed some visual evidence of bolting stem structural weakening.</p>
<p>Due to this important role in cell wall lignin biosynthesis, ADT is a potentially attractive target for metabolic engineering of lignin contents in plants (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Corea et al., 2012</a>). However, remodeling of plant metabolism by genetic tools has to take into account the fact that biochemical reactions in the cell are interconnected, meaning that the manipulation of one specific pathway can potentially have several systemic consequences. Understanding such interdependencies is essential for metabolic engineering approaches to, for example, improve traits of interests in crop plants and forestry species. To explore the consequences of ADT modulation, the effects on the reactions biochemically upstream of photosynthetic energy conversion and overall metabolism were examined in <em>ADT</em> mutants.</p>
<p>Here, we describe a high-throughput phenotyping system (phenomics; <a href="#bib33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib33">Puthiyaveetil et al., 2014</a>; <a href="#bib45" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib45">Tietz et al., 2015</a>) that allowed us to monitor changes in photosynthetic performance and growth over almost the entire vegetative growth period of Arabidopsis wild-type and <em>ADT</em> knockout mutant plants. For photosynthesis, this high-throughput approach was based on non-invasive optical determination of key photosynthetic parameters derived from chlorophyll fluorescence measurements (<a href="#bib23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib23">Kramer et al., 2004</a>; <a href="#bib3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib3">Baker, 2008</a>; <a href="#bib5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib5">Brooks and Niyogi, 2011</a>). Metabolomics analyses performed following photosynthetic parameter assessments (i.e. plants analyzed 44 days after sowing [DAS]), as well as later (70 DAS), focused on both targeted and non-targeted (polar) secondary metabolites using hyphenated liquid chromatography-high resolution mass spectrometry approaches. Real-time atmospheric monitoring-mass spectrometry (RTAM-MS) also was employed to evaluate net CO<sub>2</sub> uptake rates. The significance of our findings is described below.</p>
<section id="s1"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">RESULTS</h2>
<section id="s2"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Selection of Growth Conditions</h3>
<p>Arabidopsis is a facultative long-day plant; its flowering is delayed under short-day conditions (<a href="#bib17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib17">Haughn et al., 1995</a>; <a href="#bib22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib22">Koornneef et al., 1998</a>). In our previous studies, <em>ADT</em> knockout mutants (<em>adt1</em>, <em>adt3</em>, <em>adt4</em>, <em>adt5</em>, <em>adt4/5</em>, <em>adt1/4/5</em>, <em>adt3/4/5</em>, and <em>adt3/4/5/6</em>) and wild-type plants were grown under long-day conditions (16 h of light and 8 h of dark), with lignifying stems beginning to bolt by about 3 weeks (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Corea et al., 2012</a>).</p>
<p>For the photosynthetic studies described herein, plants were grown under short-day conditions (9 h of light), which are typically employed in Arabidopsis photosynthetic studies. That is, plants did not form inflorescence stems (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1A</a>) within the study time frame, as this developmental stage is delayed under short-day conditions. The overall purpose here was to simultaneously study, in our phenomics facility, the effect of altered ADT activity on photosynthetic efficiency, plant growth parameters, and secondary metabolism. Photosynthetic and growth parameters were thus measured every 2 d. These measurements were complemented by the determination of starch content and other metabolomics analyses as well as the analysis of net CO<sub>2</sub> uptake of plants at the completion of the phenomics analyses that were still in the vegetative growth phase.</p>

<p>Large-scale phenotyping of plants is an important emerging field in plant biology and agricultural research (<a href="#bib44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib44">Tester and Langridge, 2010</a>; <a href="#bib1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib1">Araus and Cairns, 2014</a>; <a href="#bib10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib10">Fahlgren et al., 2015</a>). However, it is a challenge to ensure uniform environmental conditions over the entire growth facility, as small heterogeneities in microclimates often occur that lead to artificial differences in plant physiological responses. To minimize such potential differences, a block approach was applied that consisted of one wild-type and eight <em>ADT</em> mutant plants per block (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1A</a>). In this way, data gained from the mutants can be normalized to the corresponding wild-type plant in the same block. Since the block size is small (26 × 26 cm), microclimate differences can be considered to have a negligible effect. Starting with the block arrangement, the entire phenomics facility was filled as shown in <a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Figure 1B</a>, thereby permitting each genotype to be measured in 12-fold replicates. To further enhance the statistical significance of comparisons between <em>ADT</em> mutants and the wild type, four independent sets of measurements were performed (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1C</a>), creating 40 biological replicates per genotype. Furthermore, for the automated determination of physiological parameters from chlorophyll fluorescence data, areas belonging to the plant were separated from non-plant areas. This was readily achieved by thresholding chlorophyll fluorescence values (i.e. when the fluorescence intensity of a pixel is above a certain level, it counts as part of the plant). Accordingly, plant and nonplant areas can be separated seamlessly (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1D</a>).</p>
<p>An advantage of noninvasive optical screening is that plants can be observed over long time periods without perturbing growth/development. Wild-type and <em>ADT</em> mutant plants were thus screened for over 4 weeks in the phenomics facility starting from 20 DAS (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1F</a>), with plant size estimated as total pixel size that represents the projection of all plant leaves during the vegetative growth period studied (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1D</a>). The growth curves of the four individual sets of plants (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1E</a>) displayed quite high congruence that justified averaging the four sets (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1F</a>). Additionally, the 4-week growth measurement in the phenomics facility (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1, E and F</a>) covers almost the entire vegetative life span from young seedlings to mature plants, but prior to the onset of inflorescence stem growth. Since the photosynthetic phenotypes were most pronounced in the <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> quadruple knockout relative to the wild type (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Fig. 2A</a>), we concentrated on functional analysis of this mutant for additional insights into effects on the photosynthetic apparatus. This is detailed in the next section.</p>

<p>The consequences of impaired carbon flux into lignin and potentially other phenylpropanoid metabolites with regard to photosynthetic primary reactions are unknown. To address this, a comparison between <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> and wild-type plants was performed, with total leaf areas plotted in <a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Figure 2A</a>. For young seedlings (20–30 DAS) and older plants (40–50 DAS) prior to inflorescence stem development, no significant differences in leaf growth were apparent between both lines. However, at midgrowth phase (30–40 DAS), where plants show the most rapid increase in biomass, <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> plants displayed a somewhat diminished growth relative to wild-type plants (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Fig. 2A</a>, with statistically significant differences marked by red stars).</p>
<p>To further study the impact of ADT modulation on photosynthesis, a chlorophyll fluorescence analysis was conducted in parallel to the growth measurements. In this regard, the ΦII parameter measures the photochemical energy conversion efficiency of PSII and is a measure of linear photosynthetic electron flux (<a href="#bib12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib12">Genty et al., 1989</a>; <a href="#bib23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib23">Kramer et al., 2004</a>; <a href="#bib3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib3">Baker, 2008</a>). ΦII is determined by two parameters: photoprotective high-energy quenching (qE) and the fraction of PSII reaction centers that are open (qL; <a href="#bib23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib23">Kramer et al., 2004</a>; <a href="#bib3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib3">Baker, 2008</a>). The latter is a measure of electron backpressure in the photosynthetic electron transport (PET) chain (expressed as the fraction of closed centers: 1-qL) and depends on the efficiency of NADPH + H<sup>+</sup> consumption by downstream metabolic reactions (mainly the Calvin-Benson cycle). Over the measurement period, ΦII fluctuated randomly by about ±10% without a time-dependent trend (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Fig. 2B</a>), although ΦII tended to be slightly smaller for the <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> line. This became statistically significant when ΦII was averaged over the entire measuring period (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Fig. 2B</a>, right). When these measurements were performed with plants at the end of the night period (night-adapted [NA] plants), essentially the same effect for ΦII was obtained for day-adapted (DA) plants (measured around noon), as shown in the histogram at right in <a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Figure 2B</a>.</p>
<p>The qE mechanism is induced by acidification of the thylakoid lumen that is caused by light-induced proton pumping of PSII and the cytochrome <em>b</em><sub>6</sub><em>f</em> complex (<a href="#bib35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib35">Ruban et al., 2012</a>). As for ΦII, both qE and 1-qL fluctuated randomly over the measurement period (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Fig. 2, C and D</a>). Therefore, they were averaged over the entire time frame to identify significant changes (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Fig. 2, C and D</a>, right). Comparison of both parameters revealed that the reason for the lower ΦII in <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> was an increased qE but not a change in redox status of the PET chain (no change in 1-qL). The higher qE in the mutant line is indicative of a higher ΔpH across thylakoid membranes. These data provisionally suggested that ATP, generated from ADP and inorganic phosphate (Pi) during photosynthesis, was less efficiently consumed by energy-dependent metabolic processes in <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> (i.e. ΔpH increased because substrates for the ΔpH-consuming ATPase [ADP + Pi] became limiting due to inefficient consumption of ATP). Impairment in metabolizing ATP in <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> would be in accordance with the observed overaccumulation of starch in the DA samples acting as an ATP sink in <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> plants (<a href="#fig3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3A</a>). This was supported further by measurements of ATP/ADP ratios (see “Materials and Methods”). For both the DA and NA light conditions, the ATP/ADP ratios for <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> were higher compared with those in the wild type (0.77 and 0.6 for <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> versus 0.62 and 0.52 for the wild type, respectively), which suggested restricted ATP consumption in <em>adt3/4/5/6</em>.</p>

<p>To characterize photosynthetic energy conversion in more detail, light saturation curves were recorded under laboratory conditions (<a href="#fig4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4</a>). The measurements utilized NA leaves adapted to different light intensities with the dark-interval-relaxation kinetics (DIRK) method (for details, see “Materials and Methods”). The determination of qE requires a dark period after illumination that is impractical for measuring a light saturation curve (constant changes of light intensities). Therefore, the nonphotochemical quenching (NPQ) parameter was measured instead, this being dominated by the qE component (<a href="#bib3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib3">Baker, 2008</a>). NPQ can be used as a proxy for qE (herein, for the sake of simplicity, the term NPQ will be used also if qE is measured). As expected, increasing light intensity led to reduction in ΦII (<a href="#fig4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4A</a>) because of electron backpressure from the Calvin-Benson cycle (saturation of biochemical pathways) and down-regulation of PSII activity by NPQ. The ΦII values for the wild type and <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> at 200 μmol quanta m<sup>–2</sup> s<sup>–1</sup> were between 0.35 and 0.4, which is in good agreement with the data collected for NA plants in the phenomics facility (∼0.32) for the same light intensity (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Fig. 2B</a>). Activation of NPQ with increasing light intensity was clearly observed (<a href="#fig4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4B</a>). Comparing the wild type with <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> showed a small decrease in ΦII (5%–10%) and a small increase in NPQ (5%–15%), mainly at moderate light intensities (120–300 μmol quanta m<sup>–2</sup> s<sup>–1</sup>; i.e. approximately the light intensity of the 200 μmol quanta m<sup>–2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> used in the phenomics facility). Plotting ΦII as a function of NPQ showed an almost linear correlation with no significant differences observed between the wild type and <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> (<a href="#fig4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4C</a>). This high congruence gave strong evidence that the sensitivity of linear electron transport flux on the NPQ mechanism was unchanged in <em>adt3/4/5/6</em>.</p>

The ECS measurement is a noninvasive optical absorption method that quantifies the magnitude of the light-generated PMF in thylakoid membranes (<a href="#bib2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib2">Bailleul et al., 2010</a>), with an apparent peak absorption at 520 nm (<a href="#bib48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib48">Witt, 1979</a>; <a href="#bib2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib2">Bailleul et al., 2010</a>). For relative fast absorption measurements (faster than 1 s), the <em>A</em><sub>520</sub> is almost free of other signal components that could interfere. It was next measured in the DIRK measuring mode on NA plants. Typical measurements for different light intensities (ranging from 70 to 850 μmol quanta m<sup>–2</sup> s<sup>–1</sup>) are shown in <a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">Figure 5A</a>. The actual ECS signal for a given light intensity (ECS total) is given as the difference of absorption level in the light minus the level in the subsequent dark pulse (see the arrow in <a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">Fig. 5A</a>, for 850 µmol quanta m<sup>–2</sup> s<sup>–1</sup>). <a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">Figure 5B</a> shows a statistical analysis of ECS amplitude as a function of light intensity. Increasing the latter led to higher ECS, because the rate of light-induced proton pumping by PSII and the cytochrome <em>b</em><sub>6</sub><em>f</em> complex increased. The ECS was saturated at around 400 μmol quanta m<sup>–2</sup> s<sup>–1</sup>. Importantly, the ECS was higher in <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> compared with wild-type leaves. Thus, the higher light-generated PMF in <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> was most likely the reason for its elevated NPQ (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Figs. 2C</a> and <a href="#fig4" class="usa-link">4B</a>). The higher PMF in <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> agrees with data described above that indicate restricted ATP consumption.</p>

<p>Two sets of metabolomics analyses of the rosette leaves were carried out. The first set corresponded with the completion of the photosynthetic (phenomics) study at 44 DAS, whereas the second was at 70 DAS, when all plant lines were still in vegetative growth (i.e. they were not senescing).</p></section><section id="s7"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Metabolic Analysis of Rosette Leaves (70 DAS)</h3>
<p>Nine plants for all of the different lines were harvested for both DA and NA treatments 70 DAS. This was partly in order to establish whether, under short-day conditions, the different <em>ADT</em> knockout mutants displayed similar trends in terms of modulating (reducing) levels of soluble low-<em>M</em><sub>r</sub> phenylpropanoid/phenylpropanoid acetate metabolites, as noted previously for lignins when grown under long-day conditions (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Corea et al., 2012</a>). In addition, analyses of both DA and NA plant lines were aimed at determining specific changes that could potentially be affected by the circadian rhythm.</p>
<p>Non-targeted metabolomics analyses were first conducted. Using freely available computational tools (XCMS, CAMERA, DeviumWeb, and MeV; see “Materials and Methods”), ultra-performance liquid chromatography-quadrupole time of flight (UPLC-qTOF)-electrospray ionization chromatograms were processed for retention time correction and integration, resulting in ∼540 detected features (i.e. unique <em>m/z</em> versus retention time signals). Initially, we focused on comparing wild-type plants with <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> plants, the latter being hypothesized to display the most dramatic effects on metabolism given the phenotype observed. In this comparison, we identified 304 and 250 features that were altered significantly (<em>P</em> ≤ 0.05, after normalization to the internal standard naringenin recoveries) following comparison of DA and NA plants, respectively. <a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.17.01766/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Table S1</a> summarizes the features detected with corresponding statistical analysis for all individual features.</p>
<p>An initial global assessment of the metabolomics data also was performed using hierarchical clustering analysis (HCA) and partial least square discriminant analysis (PLSDA), after peak intensities were normalized to internal standard naringenin recoveries, centered, and standardized. This initial analysis was used for rapid visualization of overall metabolic effects and trends across the different <em>ADT</em> mutants and time of harvest (i.e. DA and NA plants) as well as for the selection of those metabolites most significantly affected by ADT modulation, thereby helping guide subsequent annotation efforts.</p>
<p>It was next instructive to establish (to the extent possible) the identities of significantly altered features. Accordingly, our experimental approach focused on the known polar secondary metabolites found in Arabidopsis. These included flavonoids, phenylpropanoids (i.e. sinapoyl esters and lignans), benzenoids, and glucosinolates (<a href="#bib9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib9">D’Auria and Gershenzon, 2005</a>; <a href="#bib32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib32">Nakabayashi et al., 2009</a>), as detected in chromatograms of leaf extracts (<a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.17.01766/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Fig. S2</a>). In this regard, compounds were identified either through the use of authentic standards or were annotated based on comparisons with the literature and/or public spectral libraries of known Arabidopsis metabolites (<a href="#bib9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib9">D’Auria and Gershenzon, 2005</a>; <a href="#bib13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib13">Glauser et al., 2008</a>; <a href="#bib32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib32">Nakabayashi et al., 2009</a>; <a href="#bib29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib29">Matsuda et al., 2010</a>). In addition, a third group of features was provisionally annotated in terms of putative chemical class (<a href="#bib42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib42">Sumner et al., 2007</a>), but their structures were not unambiguously determined (<a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.17.01766/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Table S1</a>).</p>
<p>In the wild type, six flavonoids, some of the most characteristic and abundant metabolites in Arabidopsis (<a href="#bib47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib47">Veit and Pauli, 1999</a>; <a href="#bib38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib38">Saito et al., 2013</a>), were detected. These consisted of four kaempferol glycosides (kaempferol-3-glucoside-7-rhamnoside, kaempferol-3-glucoside-2ʹʹ-rhamnoside-7-rhamnoside, kaempferol-3-glucoside-2ʹʹ-glucoside-7-rhamnoside, and kaempferol-3,7-dirhamnoside) and two quercetin derivatives (quercetin-3-glucoside-7-rhamnoside and quercetin-3-glucoside-2ʹʹ-rhamnoside-7-rhamnoside; <a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.17.01766/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Fig. S3A</a>; <a href="#tblI" class="usa-link">Table I</a>).</p>

These included the known Phe-derived gluconasturtiin (2-phenethylglucosinolate), three indole-derived glucosinolates (4-hydroxyglucobrassicin, 1-methoxyglucobrassicin and glucobrassicin), and seven aliphatic glucosinolates (glucoiberin, glucoraphanin, glucoalyssin, glucohesperalin, glucoibarin, glucohirsutin, and hexyl glucosinolate or 4-methylpentyl glucosinolate; <a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.17.01766/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Fig. S3B</a>; <a href="#tblI" class="usa-link">Table I</a>).</p>
<p>The extracts also contained several other known phenylpropanoid-derived metabolites, corresponding to monomeric or dimeric (lignan) phenylpropanoid metabolites (<a href="#bib9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib9">D’Auria and Gershenzon, 2005</a>; <a href="#bib39" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib39">Seneviratne et al., 2015</a>). The former included three known malate-containing compounds (5-hydroxyferuloyl malate, sinapic acid malate ester, and sinapoyl malate) as well as three glycosides (coniferin, 1,2-di-<em>O</em>-sinapoyl-β-<span class="font-variant-small-caps">d</span>-Glc, and sinapic acid glucoside; <a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.17.01766/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Fig. S3C</a>). The dimeric phenylpropanoids (lignans) included two known pinoresinol derivatives (pinoresinol-4,4ʹ-di-<em>O</em>-β-<span class="font-variant-small-caps">d</span>-glucoside and pinoresinol-4-<em>O</em>-β-<span class="font-variant-small-caps">d</span>-glucoside; <a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.17.01766/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Fig. S3D</a>; <a href="#bib39" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib39">Seneviratne et al., 2015</a>); however, there were four other putative lignans detected (<a href="#tblI" class="usa-link">Table I</a>; <a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.17.01766/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Tables S1 and S2</a>). These were generically annotated as lignan glucosides (<a href="#tblI" class="usa-link">Table I</a>), but with no precise structure being determined due to the lack of sufficient material for unambiguous structure determination.</p>
<p>Three putative galactolipids also were detected. These were annotated previously in Arabidopsis (<a href="#bib13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib13">Glauser et al., 2008</a>), based on comparison of high-resolution tandem mass spectrometry (MS/MS) spectra, as <em>sn</em>1-<em>O</em>-(12-oxophytodienoyl)-monogalactosyl monoglyceride, <em>sn</em>1-<em>O</em>-(12-oxophytodienoyl)-digalactosyl monoglyceride, and <em>sn</em>2-<em>O</em>-(di-nor-oxophytodienoyl)-monogalactosyl monoglyceride (<a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.17.01766/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Fig. S3E</a>; <a href="#tblI" class="usa-link">Table I</a>).</p>
<p>Finally, three putative phenolic glucosides were detected, including one hydroxybenzoic acid glucopyranoside and two isomers of dihydroxybenzoic acid-<em>O-</em>pentoside (<a href="#tblI" class="usa-link">Table I</a>).</p>

<p>Finally, for putative galactolipid levels, the <em>adt4</em> line potentially had the second highest increase among all lines relative to wild-type levels (∼60%), although this was not statistically significant due to the variability observed (<em>P</em> = 0.115; <a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">Fig. 6G</a>; <a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.17.01766/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Table S1</a>).</p>

<section id="s13"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Plant Growth/Development, Photosynthetic Performance, ATP, and Carbon Sinks</h3>
<p>Our high-throughput phenomics platform (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Figs. 1</a> and <a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">2</a>) allowed the detection of small but significant changes in physiological parameters and photosynthetic performance. The analysis of PET showed significantly decreased rates for linear electron flux, measured as ΦII, in the <em>ADT</em> mutants relative to the wild-type plants (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Figs. 2B</a> and <a href="#fig4" class="usa-link">4A</a>). Detailed analysis of this PET reduction revealed that it resulted from an elevated qE (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Figs. 2C</a> and <a href="#fig4" class="usa-link">4B</a>), which causes heat dissipation of the harvested sunlight no longer available for photosynthesis. Further analyses indicated that the increased qE in <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> resulted from a higher ΔpH across the thylakoid membrane, as measured by ECS (<a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">Fig. 5B</a>).</p>
<p>Our data also indicated that transitory starch in chloroplasts could serve, at least in part, as an alternative carbon sink in ADT-deficient plants. That is, in the <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> knockout, the starch content was increased by more than 60% in DA plants and all other knockouts also had elevated starch contents even after 70 DAS (<a href="#fig7" class="usa-link">Fig. 7A</a>). Furthermore, comparing starch contents (<a href="#fig3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3A</a>) with secondary metabolite data (<a href="#fig3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3, B–E</a>) revealed an inverse relationship that provisionally suggested the redirection of fixed carbon from phenylpropanoid metabolites into starch. Yet, the growth curve for <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Fig. 2A</a>) also indicated that additional carbon used from transitory starch reserves in the night was not utilized to increase plant growth in the mutant. In fact, growth was retarded slightly in the quadruple mutant as compared with wild-type plants. This could be interpreted as an indication that the excess carbon/energy (i.e. by reduced metabolism into phenylpropanoid metabolites/lignins) was not used for enhanced plant growth. Indeed, the carbon stored was transitory, as the starch was fully metabolized when photosynthesis was shut down in the NA period (<a href="#fig3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3A</a>, left). Where this catabolized fixed carbon ends up is currently unknown.</p>
<p>A potential direct connection between secondary metabolism produced by carbon processing through ADT and photosynthesis is ATP. Metabolic pathways leading to lignin and other phenylpropanoids utilize considerable amounts of ATP (<a href="#bib27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib27">Lewis and Yamamoto, 1989</a>). It is not clear, however, whether this ATP comes from respiration, photosynthesis, or both. Yet, with the ADT-catalyzed conversion of arogenate to Phe being localized in the chloroplast, it is possible that ATP produced by photosynthetic light reactions makes a major contribution. Increased starch content is in line with an expected reduction in the putative ATP consumption in <em>ADT</em> knockouts (i.e. since starch synthesis requires ATP; <a href="#bib11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib11">Geigenberger et al., 2005</a>). Thus, it seems that the transient accumulation of starch could be related to the diminished level of ATP required for phenylpropanoid metabolism and lignin biosynthesis in <em>ADT</em> knockouts.</p>
<p>The photosynthetic performance measurements of <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> also indicated that starch synthesis only partly compensated for the reduced demand for ATP consumption. This became apparent by analyzing the correlation between starch contents and linear electron transport (measured as ΦII) for the wild type and all <em>ADT</em> knockouts (<a href="#fig7" class="usa-link">Fig. 7B</a>). Higher starch accumulation in the different knockouts was accompanied by a lower linear photosynthetic electron flux. This suggests a limited consumption of extra ATP produced in the mutants by the formation of transitory starch with feedback to electron transport by an increased PMF (<a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">Fig. 5</a>). The quadruple mutant, though, still has higher ATP levels, as indicated by elevated ΔpH that leads to higher qE and lower electron flux and the elevated ATP/ADP ratio. In this way, reduced phenylpropanoid biosynthesis can be envisaged to have two interconnected consequences for photosynthesis: (1) reduced consumption of fixed carbon by the Calvin-Benson cycle, leading to an increase in transitory starch; and (2) lowered consumption of ATP, leading to slightly lower electron transport rates via an increase in the feedback regulation of qE.</p></section><section id="s14"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Metabolomics</h3>
<p>As anticipated, some of the most marked effects observed at the end of the photosynthetic measurement period were the reductions in levels of metabolites derived directly from Phe (i.e. flavonoids, monomeric phenylpropanoids, and lignans). In contrast, the levels of the phytohormones salicylic acid and jasmonic acid were below detection limits under the conditions employed (data not shown). For those metabolite classes detected, the largest reductions were observed in the multiple <em>ADT</em> knockout mutants, whereas the smallest reductions were associated with single knockouts (i.e. <em>adt1</em>, <em>adt3</em>, <em>adt4</em>, and <em>adt5</em>).</p>
<p>More specifically, in the quadruple knockout <em>adt3/4/5/6</em>, levels of the Phe-derived lignans (<a href="#fig3" class="usa-link">Figs. 3D</a> and <a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">6C</a>) and gluconasturtiin (<a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">Fig. 6F</a>) were most affected (∼67% and ∼77% reductions, respectively), which was comparable to the lignin content reductions of ∼70% in <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> plants under long-day growth/development (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Corea et al., 2012</a>). By contrast, compared with those in the wild type, Phe-derived flavonoid (<a href="#fig3" class="usa-link">Figs. 3B</a> and <a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">6A</a>) and phenolic glucoside (<a href="#fig3" class="usa-link">Figs. 3E</a> and <a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">6D</a>), as well as overall glucosinolate (<a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">Fig. 6E</a>), levels in <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> were reduced by only ∼30% to ∼45%, and those of phenylpropanoids (<a href="#fig3" class="usa-link">Figs. 3C</a> and <a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">6B</a>) were reduced by only by 25%.</p>
<p>Nevertheless, under short-day growth conditions, the knockout of individual <em>ADT</em> genes led to somewhat different outcomes than those observed for lignin contents in long-day-grown plants (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Corea et al., 2012</a>). Specifically, the <em>adt1</em> knockout displayed the greatest reduction in levels of metabolites derived directly from Phe, especially for monomeric phenylpropanoids and flavonoids. Conversely, lignin contents under long-day growth/development were reduced the most in <em>adt5</em>, whereas there was no detectable effect on lignin contents in <em>adt1</em> plants (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Corea et al., 2012</a>). Additionally, glucosinolate levels were most reduced in <em>adt4</em> (<a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">Fig. 6E</a>), whereas they had no reduction in lignin contents (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Corea et al., 2012</a>). These data apparently suggest quite a distinct role in metabolic pathway carbon allocation and could indicate a specific role for ADT4 in, for example, gluconasturtiin biosynthesis.</p>
<p>Although the reduction in Phe-derived gluconasturtiin content described above was expected, the reductions in the levels of other glucosinolates were striking. That is, partial blockage of Phe biosynthesis also led to reduced levels of glucosinolates that are biosynthetically independent of Phe. This pleiotropic effect (i.e. when one gene influences two or more seemingly unrelated phenotypic traits) is nevertheless consistent with previous research suggesting possible cross talk between glucosinolate and phenylpropanoid biosynthetic pathways (<a href="#bib18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib18">Hemm et al., 2003</a>; <a href="#bib20" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib20">Kim et al., 2015</a>; <a href="#bib28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib28">Mach, 2015</a>). Furthermore, this class of Brassicaceae metabolites (<a href="#bib31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib31">Matthäus and Luftmann, 2000</a>), characteristic of Arabidopsis, is used as defense compounds and has long been linked directly to responses to wounding and insect herbivory. Their effectiveness is due to the release of toxic isothiocyanates (<a href="#bib15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib15">Grubb and Abel, 2006</a>; <a href="#bib16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib16">Halkier and Gershenzon, 2006</a>; <a href="#bib4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib4">Beekwilder et al., 2008</a>). Clearly, a reduction in the levels of both phenylpropanoids and glucosinolates could potentially lessen overall chemical defenses of ADT-deficient plants, thus causing negative effects regarding fitness.</p>
<p>Lastly, the global metabolomics analysis of leaf tissue extracts from the various <em>ADT</em> mutants and the wild type, initially used for the selection of metabolites of interest, is revisited by focusing on the annotated compounds among the hundreds of significantly altered features (<a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.17.01766/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Table S1</a>). HCA (<a href="#fig9" class="usa-link">Fig. 9A</a>) and PLSDA (<a href="#fig9" class="usa-link">Fig. 9B</a>) analyses can be presented in a form that provides a comprehensive view of the data, clearly illustrating the trends observed. In this regard, HCA groups features that are based on trends across samples, with above-average features in red and below-average features in green. In <a href="#fig9" class="usa-link">Figure 9A</a>, we can clearly observe two main groups of opposite behavior, namely those that accumulate to lower levels in <em>ADT</em> knockout lines and those that accumulate to higher amounts. The first and largest group contains flavonoids, phenylpropanoids, lignans, phenolic glucosides, and glucosinolates. The second group consists of the putative apocarotenoid compounds that are present in higher amounts in the knockout lines and the putative galactolipids, with the latter subject to a pronounced circadian rhythm effect (<a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">Fig. 6G</a>). The PLSDA analysis simplifies multiple variables (features) into two orthogonal components that clearly distinguish between the wild type and <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> and between DA and NA plants (<a href="#fig9" class="usa-link">Fig. 9B</a>). In this analysis, we can see not only separation of the different groups of samples but, with the overlaid loadings for the different annotated metabolites, we can also rapidly visualize those metabolites that are characteristic of the different lines and harvest times. The strongest effect is in the separation of the wild type and <em>adt3/4/5/6</em>, with the Phe-derived compounds, phenolic glucosides, and glucosinolates being characteristic of the wild type and galactolipids and putative apocarotenoids associated with <em>adt3/4/5/6</em>.</p>

<p>Our data show higher levels of starch accumulation but do not show significantly higher rates of carbon fixation in Arabidopsis plants with compromised ADT activity. This corroborates the additional observation that we do not see overall significant biomass differences between wild-type and <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> plants. Photorespiration is typically thought of as a mechanism for a plant to mitigate the accidental fixation of oxygen instead of CO<sub>2</sub>. Our data (<a href="#fig8" class="usa-link">Fig. 8</a>), however, show no significant difference in the rates of overall CO<sub>2</sub> release between the wild type and <em>adt3/4/5/6</em>. Since we only detected the overall net release of CO<sub>2</sub> from the plant, we cannot determine whether this CO<sub>2</sub> came from respiration or photorespiration. Future RTAM-MS focusing on oxygen uptake flux analysis, such as using <sup>18</sup>O<sub>2</sub>, may be an interesting extension of this study to see if any utilization of starch accumulation results in significant variations in photorespiration rates in the wild type compared with <em>adt3/4/5/6</em>.</p></section></section><section id="s16"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">CONCLUSION</h2>
<p>Our high-throughput phenomics approach allowed us to visualize small changes in photosynthetic and growth performance in <em>ADT</em> knockouts. The data established that reduced metabolism through Phe, leading to reductions in the levels of various phenylpropanoids and other metabolites, has a moderate impact on PET that is most likely caused by impaired recycling of ADP + Pi.</p>
<p>Additionally, the metabolomic analysis significantly increased our understanding of the effects of ADT modulation in Arabidopsis, as partial blockage of this crucial biosynthetic pathway has significant and widespread metabolic effects. We established significant reductions in the contents of several phenylpropanoid-derived metabolites, including flavonoids, lignans, and glucosinolates, at both harvesting points (44 and 70 DAS). Our metabolomic analysis also revealed a number of systemic pleiotropic effects of the genetic manipulations. These effects included reductions in glucosinolate and phenolic glucoside contents, with related increases in the levels of putative galactolipids and apocarotenoids.</p>
<p>The small impairment of photosynthetic energy conversion, however, indicates that the metabolic engineering of lignin biosynthesis and phenylpropanoid metabolism demonstrated here could be a viable strategy to improve, for example, biofuel crops. Examples exist where only small changes in photosynthetic performance visible under controlled greenhouse conditions translate to much more pronounced effects in field-grown plants (<a href="#bib26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib26">Lawson et al., 2012</a>). A good example is the PsbS knockout mutant (impaired in qE) that displays a similar electron transport rate in the laboratory but significantly reduced fitness under field conditions (<a href="#bib25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib25">Külheim et al., 2002</a>). Thus, the small changes measured in this study may have greater effects in field conditions. This highlights the need in future studies to assess changes in physiological parameters under both greenhouse and field conditions and to determine to what extent, if any, plant fitness is compromised. The results of this study show that engineering one pathway alone may not be sufficient to improve certain traits. In particular, further improvements of ATP consumption can be a follow-up approach to make <em>ADT</em> mutants an interesting model for the improvement of, for example, biofuel conversion efficiencies.</p></section><section id="s17"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">MATERIALS AND METHODS</h2>

<p>Wild-type Arabidopsis (<em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em> ecotype Columbia) and mutants (<em>adt1</em>, <em>adt3</em>, <em>adt4</em>, <em>adt5</em>, <em>adt4/5</em>, <em>adt1/4/5</em>, <em>adt3/4/5</em>, and <em>adt3/4/5/6</em>, generated as described [<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Corea et al., 2012</a>]) were sown and grown in a growth chamber for 20 d and then moved to the phenomics facility. Plants were grown in eight to 12 blocks in four independent sets (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1, A–C</a>), with each set having a randomized arrangement of genotypes per block. They were monitored in the phenomics facility for 15 to 30 d after being transferred. Both chambers utilized a 9-h photoperiod at 200 μmol quanta m<sup>–2</sup> s<sup>–1</sup> illumination, with daily temperatures of 23°C/21°C (light/dark). Measurements were performed daily, once in the middle of the photoperiod (day) and once in the middle of the dark period (night). Measurements were taken by utilizing a movable camera unit, the Fluorcam XYZ (PSI). This instrument is a mobile, programmable fluorescence imaging robot capable of moving throughout the growth chamber on a gantry, performing automated measurements utilizing blue (455 nm) and red (618 nm) LEDs to excite fluorescence, with a light intensity of 200 μmol quanta m<sup>2</sup> s<sup>–1</sup>. Fluorescence was captured by a Fluorcam 2701 LU camera equipped with a fluorescence filter. The software then automatically calculates and exports many parameters, such as <em>F</em><sub>v</sub>/<em>F</em><sub>m</sub> (maximum quantum efficiency of PSII photochemistry), ΦII, NPQ, qE, and qL (fraction of open PSII reaction centers; i.e. with reduced primary quinone acceptor Q<sub>A</sub>) according to the following formulae:</p>

After 3 min of illumination, photosynthetic parameters do not change significantly (i.e. a steady state is reached).</p></section><section id="s20"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Measurements in the Flash Spectrometer</h3>
<p>Flash spectrometry measurements were performed in a laboratory setting on wild-type and knockout plants grown in the phenomics facility and harvested 6 weeks after sowing. Plants were adapted to each of the six different light intensities of 70, 120, 200, 300, 500, and 850 μmol quanta m<sup>–2</sup> s<sup>–1</sup> and then measured using a DIRK approach. DIRK is used to quantify steady-state electron fluxes through photosynthesis in light-adapted samples by analyzing the kinetics of the relaxation of spectroscopic signals (<a href="#bib37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib37">Sacksteder and Kramer, 2000</a>). This relaxation of spectroscopic signals occurs during short dark intervals that are achieved by shuttering off the actinic light (<a href="#bib37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib37">Sacksteder and Kramer, 2000</a>).</p>
<p>Minimum fluorescence was determined for 2 s at 2 μmol quanta m<sup>–2</sup> s<sup>–1</sup>, and <em>F</em><sub>m</sub> and <em>F</em><sub>m</sub>′ were determined with a 0.6-s light pulse at 2,000 μmol quanta m<sup>–2</sup> s<sup>–1</sup>. The photosynthetic parameters ΦII and NPQ were calculated as above.</p>
<p>For ECS measurements, plants were preilluminated for 10 min at 100 μmol quanta m<sup>–2</sup> s<sup>–1</sup> and then adapted for 2 min to six light intensities (70, 120, 200, 300, 500, and 850 μmol quanta m<sup>–2</sup> s<sup>–1</sup>). For each light intensity, the ECS was measured as absorption change at 520 nm (<a href="#bib48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib48">Witt, 1979</a>; <a href="#bib2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib2">Bailleul et al., 2010</a>). The maximal ECS amplitude (reflecting the maximal PFM) was determined as the difference of the 520-nm absorption signal in the light minus the 520-nm signal in the subsequent dark pulse (arrow in <a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">Fig. 5A</a>). This was repeated for all light intensities (<a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">Fig. 5</a>). ECS measurements were tested in the direction of increasing light intensity followed directly by decreasing light intensities. As no hysteresis effect was apparent between both directions, measurements were performed with decreasing light intensities for all genotypes.</p></section><section id="s21"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">ATP and ADP Determination</h3>
<p>Wild-type and <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> mutant plants were dark adapted overnight or taken from the phenomics facility after 6 h of illumination. Ten leaf discs (with 45.6 mm<sup>2</sup> surface area) were harvested from DA and NA plants, pooled in one e-cup, and shock frozen immediately in liquid N<sub>2</sub>. Each harvested and frozen plant material was homogenized in the TissueLyserII (Qiagen) before the addition of 200 μL of ATP or ADP buffer of the ATP/ADP calorimetric assay kit (BioVision). Samples were individually sonicated with a Sonicator Microtip (Sonicator S 4000; Branso) for 3 × 30 s (at 30–50 J) to ensure complete extraction of the aqueous phase from the leaf discs. After centrifugation, an aliquot (30 μL) of each supernatant was added to 40 μL of ATP or ADP buffer and mixed with 50 μL of the reaction mix from either the ATP or ADP kit. The reaction mix contained either the ATP or ADP probe, ATP or ADP converter, and ATP or ADP developer (BioVision). After 30 min of incubation at room temperature in the dark, each absorption was measured at 570 and 600 nm to account for the chlorophyll signal. Subtraction of the chlorophyll signal at 600 nm led to the corrected absorption value at 570 nm that was used for the calculation of ADP or ATP amount (nmol) using the respective ADP or ATP calibration curve. The calibration curve for ATP was generated using 0, 0.5, 1, 1.5, and 2 nmol final concentration of the ATP standard. The calibration curve for ADP was generated using 0, 0.5, 1, 1.5, 2, 4, 6, and 8 nmol final concentration of the ADP standard. Data were averaged from six to eight biological replicates per genotype.</p></section><section id="s22"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Plant Harvesting and Metabolite Extraction</h3>
<p>Two sets of Arabidopsis plants were subjected to metabolomics analyses, one at 44 DAS and the second at 70 DAS, where, in each case, leaf tissues from nine individual plants of each genotype were collected, at the end of the light (DA) and dark (NA) periods, respectively, and frozen immediately in liquid nitrogen. Samples were then freeze dried and kept at −80°C until subjected to solvent extraction. Freeze-dried samples were weighed (∼14–23 mg) and individually ground to fine powder using the TissueLyserII (Qiagen) for 30 s at a frequency of 30 Hz. Methanol:water (70:30, v/v) containing 0.05 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> naringenin (Aldrich) as an internal standard was individually added at a ratio of 1 mL to 50 mg of tissue powder. Samples were vortexed for a few seconds, sonicated at room temperature for 10 min, vortexed again, and finally centrifuged at 16,000<em>g</em> (4°C for 15 min). Supernatants were individually transferred to 1.5-mL screw-cap glass vials and stored at −80°C until analysis. The pellets in each case also were stored at −80°C prior to starch analysis.</p></section><section id="s23"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">UPLC-qTOF-MS Analysis</h3>
<p>Metabolite analyses were performed using a Waters Acquity Ultra Performance LC system (Waters) equipped with a photodiode array eλ detector (Waters) and coupled to a Xevo G2 QTOF mass spectrometer (Waters MS Technologies) using MassLynx (version 4.1) software. Separation of metabolites was performed using a BEH C<sub>18</sub> column (Waters; 100 × 2.1 mm i.d., 1.7 µm particle size). The mobile phase consisted of 0.1% (v/v) formic acid in water (phase A) and 0.1% (v/v) formic acid in acetonitrile (phase B). The linear gradient program was as follows: 100% A over 0.5 min, 0% to 45% (v/v) B over 25 min, 45% to 100% (v/v) B over 1 min, held at 100% B for a further 3.5 min, then returned to the initial conditions (100% A) in 0.5 min, followed by reconditioning at 100% A for 10 min. The flow rate was 0.2 mL min<sup>−1</sup>, with column temperature kept at 25°C and samples at 5°C. Spectra were recorded between 200 and 500 nm with a resolution of 1.2 nm. The masses of eluted compounds were detected using an electrospray ionization source in the negative ion mode. The following settings were applied: capillary voltage at 2.2 kV, cone voltage at 20 eV, and collision energy at 6 and 30 eV; argon was used as the collision gas. For LC-MS/MS analyses (<a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.17.01766/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Table S2</a>), 5- to 50-, 30-, and 30- to 60-eV collision energies were used. The <em>m/z</em> range was 50 to 1,000 D. The mass spectrometer was calibrated using sodium formate, and Leu enkephalin was employed as the lock mass. The injection volume was 2 μL for LC-MS analysis and 4 μL for LC-MS/MS.</p></section><section id="s24"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Metabolomics Data Processing</h3>
<p>Chromatograms obtained from UPLC-photodiode array-qTOF-MS analysis were inspected visually in MassLynx software (Waters) with obvious outliers excluded. In order to conduct subsequent data processing, .RAW data files were converted to NetCDF using MassLynx’s Databridge software. NetCDF files were then processed in RStudio (version 0.9.332) using the package XCMS (<a href="#bib41" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib41">Smith et al., 2006</a>; <a href="https://xcmsonline.scripps.edu" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">https://xcmsonline.scripps.edu</a>) for feature (unique mass/retention time signals, MxxxTxxx) detection and retention time correction, followed by the CAMERA package (<a href="#bib24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib24">Kuhl et al., 2012</a>; <a href="https://bioconductor.org/packages/release/bioc/html/CAMERA.html" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">https://bioconductor.org/packages/release/bioc/html/CAMERA.html</a>) for features grouping together and for partial annotation. XCMS-based feature detection and integration were performed using the Centwave method (<a href="#bib43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib43">Tautenhahn et al., 2008</a>) with the following parameters: prefilter = c(0,0), snthr = 2, ppm = 15, peakwidth = c(5,20), and nSlaves = 4. Retention time correction was performed twice via the obiwarp method with the following parameters: bw = 2, minfrac = 0.5, mzwid = 0.015, and plottype = c(‘deviation’). The CAMERA package was used with the following parameters: perfwhm = 0.6 and groupCorr, findIsotopes, and findAdducts with polarity = ‘negative’.</p>
<p>Pairwise comparison in XCMS was conducted after setting the two groups, one including all wild-type samples (both DA and NA) and one including all mutant line samples (both DA and NA). After initial XCMS and CAMERA processing, TSV and CSV files generated were combined and further analyzed manually (<a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.17.01766/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Table S1</a>). Feature lists were normalized to the internal standard naringenin (integrated in XCMS results as feature M271T1196, <em>Rt</em> 19.93, <em>m/z</em> 271.0608 [calculated 271.0606 for C<sub>15</sub>H<sub>11</sub>O<sub>5</sub>]) for statistical analysis. Comparisons between the wild type and <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> (either from DA or NA plants) were performed separately for the selection of statistically significant features subjected to annotation efforts. Box plots were made using Excel with the following results obtained: <em>P</em> value (−log<sub>10</sub> [<em>P</em>]) and fold change for pairwise comparison between the wild type and each <em>ADT</em> mutant (NA and DA) and for NA versus DA in the wild type and each <em>ADT</em> mutant (<a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.17.01766/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Table S1</a>). For HCA and PLSDA, data were transformed to zero mean and unit variance. The annotated features (after the removal of isotopes, adducts, and fragments) were imported to MeV version 4.8 and subsequently clustered using the following settings for hierarchical clustering: tree selection = gene tree, ordering optimization = optimize gene leaf order, distance metric selection = optimize gene leaf order, linkage method selection = average linkage clustering. PLSDA of NA_wild type, DA_wild type, NA_<em>adt3/4/5/6</em>, and DA_<em>adt3/4/5/6</em> was performed using DeviumWeb (version 0.4; <a href="#bib14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib14">Grapov, 2014</a>).</p></section><section id="s25"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Metabolite Identification and Annotation</h3>
<p>Information obtained from computational processing was used as a starting point for manual peak identification. Metabolites were then identified by comparison with standard compounds of their retention time, UV spectra, and MS/MS fragmentation. Other metabolites were putatively annotated using the following two approaches: (1) putative molecular formulae calculated from accurate mass and isotopic pattern recognition were used to screen the Metlin (<a href="https://metlin.scripps.edu/index.php" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">https://metlin.scripps.edu/index.php</a>) and SciFinder Scholar (SciFinder Scholar 20##, <a href="https://scifinder.cas.org/scifinder/login.jsf" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">https://scifinder.cas.org/scifinder/login.jsf</a>) metabolite databases as well as other sources, including KEGG (<a href="http://www.genome.jp/kegg/pathway.html" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">http://www.genome.jp/kegg/pathway.html</a>) and the Plant Metabolic Network (<a href="http://www.plantcyc.org/" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">http://www.plantcyc.org/</a>) databases; and (2) MS/MS fragmentation of metabolites (<a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.17.01766/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Table S2</a>) was compared with candidate molecules found in databases (ReSpect for Phytochemicals [<a href="http://spectra.psc.riken.jp/" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">http://spectra.psc.riken.jp/</a>] and Massbank [<a href="http://www.massbank.jp/" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">http://www.massbank.jp/</a>]) and verified with earlier literature, especially for metabolites reported previously in Arabidopsis.</p></section><section id="s26"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Starch Quantification</h3>
<p>Following the extract preparation for metabolomic analyses above, the corresponding pellets were individually subjected to starch quantification. Each pellet sample was next washed three times with 1 mL of ethanol: water (80:20, v/v) with sonication at room temperature, followed by centrifugation at 16,000<em>g</em>, with solvent discarded. A 0.2 <span class="font-variant-small-caps">n</span> potassium hydroxide solution in water was then added to each sample at a ratio of 1 mL to 40 mg of sample (i.e. the freeze-dried plant material, prior to metabolite extraction), and gelatinization was conducted at 85°C for 45 min. After cooling down to room temperature, acetic acid (1 <span class="font-variant-small-caps">n</span>) was added for neutralization at a ratio of 0.2 mL to 40 mg of sample (as above), and samples were individually vortexed and centrifuged at 16,000<em>g</em> and 25°C for 15 min. Amyloglucosidase solution (A-1602; Sigma) was prepared at 100 units mL<sup>–1</sup>, and digestion proceeded by adding the solution (40 μL) to each supernatant (60 μL) and incubating for 15 min at 60°C in a shaking water bath. Acetonitrile (100 μL) was added immediately to each sample after digestion. Finally, samples were vortexed and centrifuged at 16,000<em>g</em> at 4°C for 15 min. Supernatants (180 μL) and 10 mg mL<sup>–1</sup> Xyl solution (20 μL) were transferred into screw-cap glass vials and analyzed.</p>
<p>Released Glc was quantified using an HPLC device (2695; Waters) coupled to an evaporative light-scattering detector (2424; Waters) equipped with a Supercosil LC-NH column (250 × 4.6 mm i.d., 5 µm; Supelco). The mobile phase consisted of acetonitrile: water (75:25, v/v), the flow rate was 0.5 mL min<sup>−1</sup>, with nebulizer temperature of 70°C and N<sub>2</sub> pressure at 50 p.s.i. The injection volume was 5 μL. Xyl in each sample solution was used as an internal standard. By using isocratic elution, Xyl and Glc were resolved and eluted at 11.2 and 13.6 min, respectively. A control recovery test was performed using starch standard powder (S5296; Sigma), giving 100% ± 10% recovery. Calibration curves for Glc exhibited good polynomial expression, with correlation coefficients of 0.9993 (0.125–0.5 mg mL<sup>–1</sup>) to 0.9985 (0.5–1.5 mg mL<sup>–1</sup>). A standard solution containing 1 mg mL<sup>–1</sup> Xyl and 0 to 2 mg mL<sup>–1</sup> Glc was used (5-μL injection volume) to monitor the evaporative light-scattering detection reproducibility throughout the runs.</p></section><section id="s27"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">RTAM-MS of CO<sub>2</sub>
</h3>
<p>Wild-type and <em>adt3/4/5/6</em> plants (56 DAS) were placed in an in-house-built isolation/exposure chamber (<a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.17.01766/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Fig. S4</a>) and were sealed. The chamber was made of clear polycarbonate for light penetration and was modified with three Swagelock connection ports in the lid to allow air flow in and to control air/gas flow to a vacuum or to a Shimadzu QP2010 single quad mass spectrometer with an electron-impact source for analysis. NA plants were kept in the dark for 12 h to consume all starch reserves, and DA plants were kept in the light for 12 h within an environmental growth chamber to allow for starch accumulation. A flow rate of 200 mL min<sup>–1</sup> was used to initially purge the chamber and to maintain a constant atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> level throughout each plant acclimation period. The mass spectrometer was calibrated by running a series of volumetrically prepared CO<sub>2</sub> standards. Data acquisition began with the analysis of just the room air CO<sub>2</sub> concentration for 15 min, then a valve was switched and the plants that had acclimated for 12 h in either the light (DA) or dark (NA) were analyzed by measuring the air that passes around the plant and that enters the mass spectrometer. The flow rate of this analysis was set at 2 mL min<sup>–1</sup> CO<sub>2</sub> concentration and was monitored over 30 min to allow the CO<sub>2</sub> concentration measurement to stabilize and arrive at a steady state. The average concentration at the last 5 min of the 30-min period was used to calculate the total CO<sub>2</sub> concentration of the air plus what was released or fixed by the plant. Then, the light was either turned on for the NA plants or turned off for the DA plants, and the CO<sub>2</sub> concentration was allowed to stabilize and reach a steady state for another 50 min. The last 5 min of this period was used to determine the CO<sub>2</sub> released or absorbed under light or dark conditions in plants depleted of starch (NA) or in which starch accumulated (DA). After analysis, the aboveground portion of the plant was dried and weighed to normalize the measurements to dry weight. Plantless pots with soil also were evaluated under the same conditions. CO<sub>2</sub> levels attributed to release from the soil and room atmosphere were subtracted from the experimental CO<sub>2</sub> measurements to obtain the levels attributed to just the plant.</p></section><section id="s28"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Accession Numbers</h3>
