<section id="sec1"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Background</h3>
<p id="Par1">Recent studies have provided insights into the persistence and succession of microbes aboard the International Space Station (ISS), notably the dominance of <em>Klebsiella pneumoniae</em>. However, the interactions between the various microbes aboard the ISS and how they shape the microbiome remain to be clearly understood. In this study, we apply a computational approach to predict possible metabolic interactions in the ISS microbiome and shed further light on its organization.</p></section><section id="sec2"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Results</h3>
<p id="Par2">Through a combination of a systems-based graph-theoretical approach, and a constraint-based community metabolic modeling approach, we demonstrated several key interactions in the ISS microbiome. These complementary approaches provided insights into the metabolic interactions and dependencies present amongst various microbes in a community, highlighting key interactions and keystone species. Our results showed that the presence of <em>K. pneumoniae</em> is beneficial to many other microorganisms it coexists with, notably those from the <em>Pantoea</em> genus. Species belonging to the <em>Enterobacteriaceae</em> family were often found to be the most beneficial for the survival of other microorganisms in the ISS microbiome. However, <em>K. pneumoniae</em> was found to exhibit parasitic and amensalistic interactions with <em>Aspergillus</em> and <em>Penicillium</em> species, respectively. To prove this metabolic prediction, <em>K. pneumoniae</em> and <em>Aspergillus fumigatus</em> were co-cultured under normal and simulated microgravity, where <em>K. pneumoniae</em> cells showed parasitic characteristics to the fungus. The electron micrography revealed that the presence of <em>K. pneumoniae</em> compromised the morphology of fungal conidia and degenerated its biofilm-forming structures.</p></section><section id="sec3"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Conclusion</h3>
<p id="Par3">Our study underscores the importance of <em>K. pneumoniae</em> in the ISS, and its potential positive and negative interactions with other microbes, including potential pathogens. This integrated modeling approach, combined with experiments, demonstrates the potential for understanding the organization of other such microbiomes, unravelling key organisms and their interdependencies.</p>

<p>The online version contains supplementary material available at 10.1186/s40168-022-01279-y.</p></section><section id="kwd-group1" lang="en" class="kwd-group"><p><strong>Keywords:</strong> Microbial communities, Space microbiome, Community modeling, Network biology, Metabolic networks</p></section></section><section id="Sec1"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Background</h2>
<p id="Par9">Microorganisms are ubiquitous and exist in diverse communities around us. They form complex dynamic assemblages in every ecosystem, and their interactions shape the biotic and abiotic environments [<a href="#CR1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR1">1</a>–<a href="#CR3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR3">3</a>]. Beyond natural environments such as the soil or the ocean, microorganisms abound in all human habitats, where they directly impact human health [<a href="#CR4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR4">4</a>]. The human-made International Space Station (ISS) is a unique and controlled system to study the interplay between the human microbiome and the microbiome of their habitats. The ISS is a hermetically sealed closed system, yet it harbors many microorganisms that survive extreme environmental conditions such as microgravity, radiation and elevated CO<sub>2</sub> levels [<a href="#CR5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR5">5</a>–<a href="#CR8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR8">8</a>]. Recent studies have demonstrated the unique link between crew member microbiomes and surface microbiomes on the ISS [<a href="#CR9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR9">9</a>]. These microorganisms might have been brought into the ISS via routine payloads and astronauts [<a href="#CR9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR9">9</a>].</p>
<p id="Par10">Several studies have focused on microbial isolation [<a href="#CR10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR10">10</a>] and molecular microbial community analyses of the ISS microbiome, through experiments performed on vacuum filter debris [<a href="#CR11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR11">11</a>], high-efficiency particle arrestance (HEPA) filters [<a href="#CR12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR12">12</a>, <a href="#CR13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR13">13</a>], the ISS environmental surfaces [<a href="#CR8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR8">8</a>, <a href="#CR14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR14">14</a>] and astronauts’ microbiome [<a href="#CR9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR9">9</a>]. A recent report using a shotgun metagenomic sequencing approach on intact cells of the ISS environmental microbiome revealed the succession and persistence of certain microbial populations on the ISS environmental surfaces [<a href="#CR15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR15">15</a>]. The study posited a dominant, viable presence of Biosafety Level – 2 (BSL-2) pathogens such as <em>Klebsiella pneumoniae, Staphylococcus aureus, Enterococcus faecalis</em> and <em>Salmonella enterica</em> (Figure S<a href="#MOESM2" class="usa-link">1</a>).</p>
<p id="Par11"><em>K. pneumoniae</em> is well-known for its ability to cause pneumonia and other nosocomial infections and is largely studied for its known resistance to a wide spectrum of antibiotics, such as carbapenems, and its hypervirulence [<a href="#CR16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR16">16</a>–<a href="#CR20" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR20">20</a>]. The present study is motivated by the evidence presented about the dominance of <em>K. pneumoniae</em> at multiple locations of the ISS, its succession over time [<a href="#CR15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR15">15</a>], and the potential clinical implications it could have on the health of the astronauts inhabiting the ISS.</p>
<p id="Par12">Metabolic interactions are a key driver in shaping microbial communities [<a href="#CR21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR21">21</a>]. Studying these metabolic interactions can be instrumental in understanding the interplay between various microorganisms in diverse communities [<a href="#CR22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR22">22</a>–<a href="#CR24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR24">24</a>]. Genome-scale metabolic modeling is a powerful tool to study and understand microbial metabolism [<a href="#CR25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR25">25</a>]. These modeling approaches capture in detail, the known metabolic reactions happening in a microorganism, along with the enzymes that catalyze them, representing them in a mathematical form amenable to simulations [<a href="#CR26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR26">26</a>]. Beyond single microorganisms, metabolic modeling can also be extended to study microbial communities; many paradigms have been developed, including those based on graph theory, and constraint-based modeling, as reviewed elsewhere [<a href="#CR27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR27">27</a>, <a href="#CR28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR28">28</a>].</p>
<p id="Par13">In this study, we leverage the metagenome datasets that have captured the microbial composition of the ISS surfaces [<a href="#CR15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR15">15</a>] to specifically predict how various species in these communities can influence one another’s metabolism, leading to mutually beneficial interactions and stable microbiomes. In particular, we focus our study on <em>K. pneumoniae</em> and its coexisting species, to elucidate the possible underlying metabolic interactions that drive the structure of these communities. Through a combination of a systems-based graph-theoretical approach, MetQuest [<a href="#CR29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR29">29</a>, <a href="#CR30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR30">30</a>], and a constraint-based community modeling approach, SteadyCom [<a href="#CR31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR31">31</a>], we illustrate the central role of metabolic interactions and dependencies in shaping the ISS microbiome.</p></section><section id="Sec2"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Methodology</h2>
<p id="Par14">In this study, a two-pronged computational approach was adopted to investigate and decipher the prevailing microbial interactions in the ISS. In the first graph-based approach [<a href="#CR29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR29">29</a>], bipartite graphs were created to represent the metabolic networks of microorganisms taken individually, as well as when in pairs or larger communities. The metabolic networks underlying the organisms were represented as bipartite graphs, where two groups of nodes represent metabolites and reactions, and directed edges connect substrates to reactions and reactions to products. These networks were used to identify possible metabolic dependencies between different microorganisms. In the second constraint-based approach [<a href="#CR31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR31">31</a>], microbial growth rates were predicted, individually and in communities, to gauge the nature of their interactions, as we describe in the following sections. Figure <a href="#Fig1" class="usa-link">1</a> shows a broad overview of the approach of this study, outlining the steps beginning with the identification of microorganisms coexisting with <em>K. pneumoniae</em> to various metabolic network analyses.</p>

<p id="Par15">Microbial abundances associated with samples taken across three flights at eight ISS locations were previously measured using metagenomic sequencing [<a href="#CR15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR15">15</a>]. Among eight locations, only seven locations yielded measurable metagenomics data. In the present study, the data were further pruned such that only shotgun metagenome sequences associated with propidium monoazide (PMA)-treated samples that recorded a presence of <em>K. pneumoniae</em> were retained. The PMA-treatment removed naked DNA and dead cells, thus retaining sequences associated with viable and intact cells only [<a href="#CR32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR32">32</a>]. Further, only microorganisms that were found to coexist with <em>K. pneumoniae</em> with &gt;1% relative abundance at the location were used in this study.</p>
<p id="Par16">For this study, the available whole-genome sequences (WGS) of microorganisms from the ISS<em>,</em> which were compatible with KBase [<a href="#CR33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR33">33</a>] along with reference genomes for the relevant microorganisms taken from the NCBI database, were collected. The RefSeq and GenBank accession identification numbers, along with the underlying reasoning for the choice of these sequences, are provided in Supplementary Table S<a href="#MOESM8" class="usa-link">1</a>. Although the relative abundance of <em>Pantoea</em> sp. PSNIH2 was &gt;1% in some of the locations considered in this study, as its reference genome sequence was removed from the NCBI database, at the time of this study, this microorganism therefore had to be excluded. Metabolic models were constructed for the 52 microorganisms using ModelSEED [<a href="#CR34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR34">34</a>]. The detailed process is elaborated in Supplementary Methods M<a href="#MOESM1" class="usa-link">1</a>. The details of the reconstructions, templates and information about reaction counts are specified in Supplementary Table S<a href="#MOESM8" class="usa-link">2</a>.</p></section><section id="Sec4"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Predicting metabolic dependencies in the community</h3>
<p id="Par17">To investigate the microbial interactions underlying the chosen microbial communities of the ISS, MetQuest [<a href="#CR29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR29">29</a>], a graph-theoretic algorithm was first employed. Through a novel dynamic-programming-based enumeration, MetQuest assembles reactions into pathways of a specified size producing a given target from a specified set of source molecules (‘seed'). Through a guided breadth-first search, MetQuest can identify all the metabolites that can be traversed from a set of seed metabolites. This functionality was exploited in this study as previously published [<a href="#CR30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR30">30</a>]. Using this algorithm, the present study presents a top-down view of metabolic dependencies in these communities, beginning with predicting the most supportive families, then the most dependent genera, and finally the beneficial and dependent microorganisms by looking at pairs of potentially interacting microorganisms. In each scenario, the extent of benefit derived was calculated using a measure known as the Metabolic Support Index (MSI) [<a href="#CR30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR30">30</a>]. This was also extended to measure the possible support an organism derives from (or provides to) a community where it is present, denoted Community Support Index (CSI).</p>
<p id="Par18">The composition of the seed metabolites was chosen so as to incorporate the co-factors and coenzymes that would be present in the environment and required by a living cell, and capture the minimal nutrient content offered by the ISS. The minimal medium of each metabolic model was identified by minimizing the components required to achieve a minimum growth rate of 0.1 h<sup>-1</sup>, using built-in functions in COBRApy [<a href="#CR35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR35">35</a>], namely minimal_medium from the cobra.medium module. This approach was adopted on the premise that each microorganism is capable of individual growth. For each location at each flight, the minimal medium sets corresponding to each constituent microorganism were pooled together to form a consolidated medium. Additionally, a base set of co-factors and coenzymes were added to each of these sets. The same medium was used for both the graph-based and constraint-based analyses. Supplementary Table S<a href="#MOESM8" class="usa-link">3</a> contains the list of all such metabolites.</p>

<p id="Par28">Microbial interactions have major relevance in building and shaping the community. Based on the effects a microorganism has on another, the interactions can be categorized into six types, namely amensalism, commensalism, competition, neutralism, mutualism and parasitism [<a href="#CR37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR37">37</a>]. The present study reports a constraint-based community modeling study using the algorithm, SteadyCom [<a href="#CR31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR31">31</a>].</p>
<p id="Par29">SteadyCom [<a href="#CR31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR31">31</a>] was employed to determine the biomass production rates of all microorganisms that are a part of a community under steady state. The seed metabolites described earlier, in the network-based approach were used here as the medium (see Supplementary Table S<a href="#MOESM8" class="usa-link">3</a>). The lower bounds of the exchange reactions for the uptake of these metabolites were constrained to −10 mmol/gDW-h [<a href="#CR38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR38">38</a>]. Joint models were then created for all pairs of microorganisms that can potentially interact by virtue of being at the location at that point in time. These joint models were optimized using SteadyCom [<a href="#CR31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR31">31</a>] to find the individual biomass rates when existing as a community.</p>
<p id="Par30">The growth rate observed for each microorganism in community was compared with that observed for the individual microorganism. A 10% or higher increase or decrease in growth rate, when in a community, was taken to be a significant effect [<a href="#CR39" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR39">39</a>]. The interactions were accordingly classified into the types listed above. The equation defined below was used to determine the effect of microorganism <em>B</em> on <em>A</em>, in a two-membered community constituting <em>A</em> and <em>B</em>.</p>

<em>K. pneumoniae</em> and <em>A. fumigatus</em> interactions</h3>
<p id="Par32">Overnight grown cultures of <em>K. pneumoniae</em> (10<sup>6</sup> cells per 100 μL) and purified conidia of <em>A. fumigatus</em> (10<sup>6</sup> per 100 μL) were either mixed or grown alone in a 10-mL sterile potato extract (0.4%; w/v) dextrose (2%; w/v) liquid medium and grown under normal and simulated microgravity conditions using high aspect ratio vessel (HARV; Synthecon inc., Houston, TX) culturing units [<a href="#CR40" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR40">40</a>]. The autoclavable HARVs provided oxygenation to the culture media via their large-diameter gas permeable membrane. The HARVs with microbes were incubated at 30 °C; 150 rpm, for 48 h. HARVs were run in parallel in both horizontal rotation (normal gravity control) and vertical rotation (simulated microgravity) orientation during growth. After growth, the bacterial and fungal cells were harvested, then fixed by incubating the cells in 2.5% glutaraldehyde in 0.1M Sodium Cacodylate (Sigma) buffer at 4 °C for 1 h for scanning electron microscopy (SEM) study. Samples were then washed in 0.1M sodium cacodylate buffer three times. Fixed cells were then dehydrated in isopropyl alcohol (IPA, Sigma), using a stepped series of increasing concentrations (50%, 70%, 80%, 90%, 95 to 100%). Each step consisted of a 10-min incubation at 4 °C, followed by 3 x replacements with 100% IPA, and finally stored at 4 °C. Samples were critically point dried in an Automegasamdri 915B critical point dryer (Tousimis, Rockville, MD). Samples were attached in SEM stubs with carbon tape (Ted Pella Inc., Redding, CA), followed by carbon coating with a Leica EM ACE600 Carbon Evaporator (Leica, Wetzlar, Germany) to a thickness of ~12nm. SEM analysis was performed with an FEI Quanta 200F (Thermo Fisher, Waltham, MA).</p></section></section><section id="Sec9"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Results</h2>

<p id="Par35">Through a ‘leave-one-out’ approach, microorganisms were knocked out one at a time from the community to estimate the extent of benefit provided by that single microorganism to the remaining coexisting microorganisms. From the analysis, it was evident that some microorganisms do have an influence on the metabolic capabilities on the rest of the community, albeit low in most cases. <p id="Par36">These low values of <span xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" id="IEq18"><math id="M44"><msub><mrow><mi mathvariant="italic">CSI</mi></mrow><mrow><mi>A</mi><mo stretchy="false">→</mo><mover accent="true"><mi>A</mi><mo stretchy="false">~</mo></mover></mrow></msub></math></span> are expected, given the possible high metabolic overlap between members within a community, thereby rendering individual organisms less important to a community. To examine this, we grouped organisms based on their phylogenetic affiliation, and removed these groups, one at a time, from the community, to study the importance of a given family to the community.</p>
<p id="Par37">Overall, with respect to the locations considered in this study, the <em>Enterobacteriaceae</em> family comprising species of the <em>Klebsiella</em>, <em>Escherichia, Salmonella, Shigella,</em> and <em>Enterobacter</em> genera, was often found to be the most beneficial of all clusters, with the highest <span xmlns:mml="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" id="IEq19"><math id="M46"><msub><mrow><mi mathvariant="italic">CSI</mi></mrow><mrow><mi>A</mi><mo stretchy="false">→</mo><mover accent="true"><mi>A</mi><mo stretchy="false">~</mo></mover></mrow></msub></math></span>s in six out of a total eleven communities under study (Fig. <a href="#Fig3" class="usa-link">3</a> and Supplementary Table S<a href="#MOESM8" class="usa-link">6</a>), five of which were in Flight 3. Here, <em>A</em> refers to the family of microorganisms that provides metabolic support.</p>

<em>K. pneumoniae</em> are beneficial <em>to</em> its coexisting microorganisms</h3>
<p id="Par41">In every community, microorganisms exchange metabolites, influencing one another’s growth and survival. We performed a pairwise analysis of a total of 761 flight-location-specific communities to identify how every pair of microorganisms interacts with one another, at a given location, during a given flight, and through various metabolic exchanges support each other.</p>
<p id="Par42">In communities with <em>K. pneumoniae</em> and a member outside of the <em>Enterobacteriaceae,</em> the MSI values in the presence of <em>K. pneumoniae</em> were found to fall in the range of 0–6.46%. Most <em>Pantoea</em> species were found to be highly dependent on <em>K. pneumoniae</em>, with MSIs at the higher end (4.41–6.46%) of the spectrum (Supplementary Table S<a href="#MOESM8" class="usa-link">8</a>). With the exception of <em>E. coli</em> and <em>S. enterica</em>, the MSI values of all other <em>Enterobacteriaceae</em> members were found to be 0% in the presence of <em>K. pneumoniae</em> (Supplementary Figures S<a href="#MOESM4" class="usa-link">3</a> and S<a href="#MOESM5" class="usa-link">4</a>)<em>.</em> Out of 118 pairs, <em>K. pneumoniae</em> was found to have a non-zero MSI value in only nine such pairs. In these nine pairs, the other interacting member belonged to a species of the <em>Penicillium</em> genus.</p>
<p id="Par43">Since <em>K. pneumoniae</em> was present across the three flights only at location #5, the corresponding microbial association networks were constructed to gain insights into microbial interaction patterns over time (Fig. <a href="#Fig5" class="usa-link">5</a>). In Flight 1, fungi such as <em>P. rubens</em> and <em>Rhodotorula</em> sp. JG-1b are prevalent and shown to decline in abundance over time (Flight 2 and Flight 3). On the contrary, members of the <em>Panteoa</em> genus were observed to dominate this location over time, with many more <em>Pantoea</em> species surfacing. In agreement with earlier results discussed, many species of the <em>Enterobacteriaceae</em> family shown here such as <em>K. pneumoniae</em> and <em>E. cloacae,</em> were beneficial to members of the <em>Erwiniacea</em> family to a great extent (Fig. <a href="#Fig5" class="usa-link">5</a>).</p>

<p id="Par44">The graph-theoretic approaches described earlier do not explicitly account for the growth of microorganisms in the presence of one another or their rates, although a higher MSI does point towards a possible higher biomass in the community [<a href="#CR30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR30">30</a>]. Complementary to the above approaches, constraint-based modeling approaches such as SteadyCom [<a href="#CR31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR31">31</a>] can shed light on the ability of microorganisms to grow in a steady-state. Based on these predicted growth rates, it is also possible to classify the nature of the interaction between various pairs of microorganisms [<a href="#CR37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR37">37</a>].</p>
<p id="Par45">A total of 761 flight-location-specific pairs of microorganisms were studied. Among these, 458 pairs exhibited amensalism and 166 were predicted to be parasitism. Of the remaining, 48 were commensal, 36 were competitive, 34 were mutualistic and 19 were neutral.</p>
<p id="Par46">Looking closely into the nature of interactions of <em>K. pneumoniae</em> and its coexisting microorganisms, in 118 such pairs, 72 were predicted to be amensal, 14 commensal, 13 involved in competition, 10 were parasitic interactions, seven were mutual and two were neutral. In communities with the <em>Enterobacter</em> species, <em>K. pneumoniae</em> was involved in amensalistic interactions as observed across all such flight-location pairs. While in the interactions with <em>E. cancerogenus</em> and <em>Enterobacter</em> sp. strain NFIX59, the growth rate of <em>K. pneumoniae</em> was predicted to decrease, in the interactions with all other <em>Enterobacter</em> species, there was no significant effect on the predicted growth rate of <em>K. pneumoniae.</em></p>
<p id="Par47">In eight out of nine flight-location-specific communities of <em>K. pneumoniae</em> with <em>E. coli</em>, <em>E. coli</em> was predicted to have no significant change in growth rate while that of <em>K. pneumoniae</em> was predicted to decrease, suggesting an amensalistic interaction. Further, in Flight 3, at location #3, <em>E. coli</em> was predicted to be parasitic towards <em>K. pneumoniae</em>. In the interactions with <em>S. enterica</em>, in four out of the six locations, the growth rate of <em>K. pneumoniae</em> was decreased in its presence, whereas that of <em>S. enterica</em> was increased, resulting in a parasitic interaction. This is in accordance with the previously observed high MSI values at locations #3, #5, #7, and #8 during Flight 3. In the other cases during Flight 1 at location #2 and Flight 3 at location #1, the interaction was predicted to be amensalistic, wherein the growth rate of <em>K. pneumoniae</em> was reduced.</p>
<p id="Par48">With other <em>Klebsiella</em> species, the interactions were predicted to be predominantly amensalistic, with the growth rate of <em>K. pneumoniae</em> reduced, except in the interaction with <em>Klebsiella</em> sp. strain MS 92-3, where the reverse was observed. <em>K. pneumoniae</em> was predicted to be parasitic towards <em>K. aerogenes</em> strain IIIF7SW-P1.</p>
<p id="Par49">The interactions with <em>Pantoea</em> species, such as <em>P. agglomerans</em>, <em>P. conspicua</em>, <em>Pantoea</em> sp. strain 3.5.1, <em>Pantoea</em> sp. strain A4, <em>Pantoea</em> sp. strain At-9b, <em>Pantoea</em> sp. strain FF5, <em>Pantoea</em> sp. strain NGS-ED-1003, <em>Pantoea</em> sp. strain OXWO6B1 and <em>P. vagans</em>, were amensalistic with a decreased growth rate of the <em>Pantoea</em> species, in all cases except in Flight 3 at location #1, where the interactions were predicted to be competitive. Interactions with <em>P. ananatis</em> however were found to be commensal, with an observed increased growth rate. <em>P. dispersa</em> and <em>Pantoea</em> sp. strain IMH were found to compete with <em>K. pneumoniae</em> at their coexisting locations (Fig. <a href="#Fig6" class="usa-link">6</a> and Supplementary Table S<a href="#MOESM8" class="usa-link">9</a>).</p>

<p id="Par52">The parasitic interactions of <em>K. pneumoniae</em> on <em>A. niger</em> were observed using various microscopic techniques (data not shown). Since <em>A. niger</em> is not a pathogenic bacterium, we extensively characterized an ISS isolated <em>A. fumigatus</em>, a BSL-2 bacterium, to understand the detrimental effect of <em>K. pneumoniae</em>. The scanning electron micrographs confirmed the parasitic effect of <em>K. pneumoniae</em> over <em>A. fumigatus</em> (Fig. <a href="#Fig7" class="usa-link">7</a>). Under normal gravity conditions, <em>A. fumigatus</em> was found to be healthy where conidiophore holding vesicle with healthy metulae and phialides harboring conidia were noticed (Fig. <a href="#Fig7" class="usa-link">7</a>A), whereas simulated microgravity affected the phialides structure (Fig. <a href="#Fig7" class="usa-link">7</a>C) and subsequently conidia-forming cells were not clearly visible. When <em>K. pneumoniae</em> was co-cultured with the fungus, under normal gravity conditions, the bacterial cells (artificially colored in red) were shown to destroy the conidia-forming cells and also partially degraded metulae and phialides (Fig. <a href="#Fig7" class="usa-link">7</a>B).</p>

<p id="Par55">In this study, we used a metabolic perspective to analyze the ISS microbiome with a focus on <em>K. pneumoniae</em>, a BSL-2 human pathogen, and its coexisting microorganisms. In our two-pronged approach, we first used a graph-theoretical approach to unravel the metabolic interdependencies in the microbiome, and we further complemented this with constraint-based analyses, to identify the consequences of microbial interactions on growth. We begin by looking at higher-order interactions in the community and further delve deeper and look at pairwise interactions that have been previously shown to be major drivers of community dynamics [<a href="#CR41" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR41">41</a>]. We traverse three taxonomic levels—family, genus and species and provide insights into the keystone species of the community through metabolic network analyses.</p>
<p id="Par56">The key results of this study are four-fold. First, among the families considered, species belonging to the <em>Enterobacteriaceae</em> family were often found to be the most beneficial, among the ISS microbiome. Secondly, <em>Pantoea</em> species were predicted to be extensively dependent on their coexisting microorganisms. Third, through metabolic network analysis of microorganisms taken pairwise, <em>K. pneumoniae</em> was found to be beneficial to many of its coexisting microorganisms, especially to those species belonging to the <em>Pantoea</em>. Fourth, metabolic interactions in the community broadly fell under the categories of amensalism and parasitism. The parasitic interaction under normal and simulated microgravity between <em>K. pneumoniae</em> and <em>A. fumigatus</em> was experimentally checked.</p>
<p id="Par57">Even though &lt;7% of the metagenomic reads of ISS environmental surfaces constituted BSL-2 microorganisms, the dominant and persistent human microbial pathogens were <em>Pantoea, Klebsiella, Staphylococcus, Erwinia</em> and <em>Penicillium</em> [<a href="#CR15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR15">15</a>]<strong>.</strong> Among the ISS surfaces, Zero-G Stowage Rack (Location #5) had more <em>Pantoea</em> reads compared to other locations of all flights. When metagenomic reads of all BSL-2 microorganisms were compiled, <em>K. pneumoniae</em> was found to be persistent and dominant in Zero-G Stowage Rack of all three flights (Fig. <a href="#Fig2" class="usa-link">2</a>). In general, <em>K. pneumoniae</em> reads were more and dominant in all seven locations sampled in Flight 3. Unlike in Flight 3, <em>K. pneumoniae</em> reads were retrieved sparingly in Flight 1 (Cupola and WHC) and Flight 2 (Zero-G Stowage Rack). The cleaning reagent used in ISS consists of benzalkonium chloride, which might be able to eradicate the fungal population; thus fungal succession was not observed, unlike bacterial members which were persistent in the ISS locations examined [<a href="#CR42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR42">42</a>]. It is also documented that <em>K. pneumoniae</em> cells were resistant to the quaternary ammonium compound concentration used as cleaning agents in ISS [<a href="#CR43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR43">43</a>].</p>
<p id="Par58">On applying graph-theoretical algorithms and surveying the microbiome at multiple levels, we predicted that among the families in the dataset, members of the <em>Enterobacteriaceae</em> family were perhaps the most beneficial to other microorganisms in the community, whereas in most cases, members of the families such as <em>Erwiniaceae, Staphylococcaceae</em> and <em>Sporidiobolaceae</em> (yeast) offered little or no additional metabolic benefit to the community. It has been documented that some <em>Rhodotorula</em> species produced silver nanoparticles containing antimicrobial properties against a wide variety of Gram-positive and negative microorganisms [<a href="#CR44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR44">44</a>]. The members of the family <em>Sporidiobolaceae</em> (<em>Rhodotorula,</em> pink yeast) were isolated from the ISS locations [<a href="#CR8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR8">8</a>], their antagonistic and parasitic behavior predicted during this study should be tested against opportunistic microbial pathogens to aid in the development of appropriate countermeasures. Similarly, the amensalism predicted towards <em>Erwiniaceae</em> and mutualism of <em>Staphylococcaceae</em> members with <em>K. pneumoniae</em> need further study utilizing the ISS strains.</p>
<p id="Par59">At the genus level, we observed that <em>Pantoea</em> genus typically derives the most benefit in the community. On looking at the pairwise interactions, we observed that amongst other <em>Enterobacteriaceae</em>, <em>K. pneumoniae</em> provides metabolic support to many of its coexisting species, notably those from the <em>Pantoea</em> genus. The members of the genus <em>Pantoea</em> were primarily considered as plant pathogens, but subsequently, they have been isolated from many aquatic and terrestrial environments, including ISS [<a href="#CR8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR8">8</a>] as well as in association with insects, animals and humans [<a href="#CR45" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR45">45</a>–<a href="#CR49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR49">49</a>]. During this study [<a href="#CR8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR8">8</a>], the metagenomic sequences of the opportunistic human pathogens associated with <em>Pantoea</em> genus were <em>P. agglomerans, P. conspicua, P. brenneri, P. ananatis</em> and <em>P. dispersa,</em> whereas the plant pathogenic <em>Pantoea</em> species were not observed. The competitive metabolomic properties predicted during this study by <em>K. pneumoniae</em> with <em>Pantoea</em> members (Fig. <a href="#Fig6" class="usa-link">6</a>A) might be due to the assimilation of similar compounds for sustaining their growth. Detailed phenotypic metabolic profiles of these microbes are needed to confirm the competition and amensalism predicted in this study.</p>
<p id="Par60">Finally, from the constraint-based analyses, many amensal and parasitic interactions were noticed. This is corroborated by earlier findings that <em>K. pneumoniae</em> can inhibit fungal conidia germination and hyphal growth, as well as biofilm formation of <em>Aspergillus</em> species [<a href="#CR50" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR50">50</a>]. Recent reports documented that <em>K. pneumoniae</em> shifted to a pathogenic state, potentially leading to septic infections when cooccurred with <em>A. fumigatus</em> in immunocompromised individuals [<a href="#CR50" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR50">50</a>]. In addition, this study shows that <em>K. pneumoniae</em> cells prevented germination of conidia and hyphal development of the fungi. It has been further shown that <em>K. pneumoniae</em> bacterial cells induced the fungal cell wall stress response mechanisms and suppressed the filamentous growth of fungi [<a href="#CR50" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR50">50</a>]. The simple in silico metabolic model of this study predicted the antagonistic (parasitic) metabolic interaction between <em>K. pneumoniae</em> and <em>A. niger</em> (Fig. <a href="#Fig6" class="usa-link">6</a>B), which further enabled to validate parasitism in vivo, using the strains isolated from the ISS. However, this experimental evidence might not be enough to fully prove the metabolic prediction. Additional positive and negative controls are necessary, which would further improve the evidence of antagonistic metabolic interaction predicted during this study. Furthermore, quantitative measurements of <em>K. pneumoniae</em> and <em>Aspergillus</em> growth are needed to validate the computational prediction of this study and warrants further study.</p>
<p id="Par61">Our study does have some limitations. First, the metabolic models used in the study are automated reconstructions, which despite gap-filling, could potentially contain gaps and blocked reactions and need to be curated [<a href="#CR51" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR51">51</a>]. Nevertheless, such automated reconstructions are being widely used in many studies as they serve as useful predictive tools and representations of the metabolism of microorganisms [<a href="#CR52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR52">52</a>]. Secondly, wherever it is applicable, genomes generated from the cultured ISS microorganisms were utilized. Conversely, when strains were not isolated from the ISS environment, and thus, genomes were not available, the reference genomes of the corresponding type strains were used. However, reference genomes provide a useful perspective of an organism’s metabolic capabilities and have also been used in many other studies that incorporate metabolic models [<a href="#CR53" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR53">53</a>, <a href="#CR54" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR54">54</a>]. In the absence of exact estimates of environmental metabolites, we posited that the environment had at least the minimal set of metabolites necessary for a given organism to survive, and also included additional cofactors and coenzymes. We assumed that each microorganism is capable of minimal independent growth and thus calculated the minimal medium required to support survival. It is possible that some of the interactions remain conserved, in richer media, although others may disappear, depending on the inherent metabolic capabilities of the organisms.</p>
<p id="Par62">The graph-based approach provides a somewhat static snapshot of the metabolic interactions happening between the organisms. Indeed, the graph-theoretic approach does not account for differential importance of reactions, or for example, the increase in growth rate, that may result from potential interactions. This is captured by the complementary constraint-based modeling approaches that were employed, and together, we believe, they provide a more complete picture of the possible interactions between the microbes. As previously highlighted in the ‘Methods’ section as well, there have been previous reports wherein a higher MSI does point towards a possible higher biomass in the community. As reported for <em>Candida albicans,</em> in addition to the genome-scale metabolic model, refining the model using phenotypic microarray and other wet-lab confirmation are needed [<a href="#CR55" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR55">55</a>]. Yet, it is highly useful and offers valuable insights into the potential microbial interactions in the community [<a href="#CR30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR30">30</a>, <a href="#CR56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR56">56</a>] and well complements the constraint-based methods. Of course, it is important to emphasize that our computational methodology studies microbes through a metabolic lens only, and in reality, other interactions may be plausible. Nevertheless, metabolic interactions have been previously shown [<a href="#CR21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CR21">21</a>] to be important drivers of community structure and microbial interactions. Importantly, this study offers a first glimpse into the metabolic interactions of the ISS microbiome, upon which several hypotheses can be formulated for future experimental design.</p></section><section id="Sec17"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Conclusion</h2>
<p id="Par63">In summary, our analyses show the key role played by <em>Klebsiella</em> and other <em>Enterobacteriaceae</em> in mediating the metabolic interactions taking place between microorganisms in the ISS. Metabolic modeling, through a combination of graph-theoretic approaches and steady-state constraint-based modeling, paints a more comprehensive picture of possible microbial interactions, which are as yet inscrutable to this extent by experimental approaches. Our results point towards key dependencies of microorganisms in various locations on the ISS and can pave the way for possible interventions that may rely on targeted disinfection of surfaces aboard the ISS. Our approach also underscores the importance of complementary modeling approaches in dissecting a fairly complex microbiome and understanding various possible interactions. Our methodology is fairly generic and can be readily extended to predict microbial interactions in other interesting milieu and generate testable hypotheses for wet lab experiments, as we have demonstrated in this study with <em>Klebsiella and Aspergillus</em> species.</p></section><section id="sec22"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Supplementary Information</h2>
