</div></section></section><section aria-label="Article content"><section class="body main-article-body"><section class="abstract" id="abstract1"><h2>Abstract</h2>
<p><em>Ehrlichia chaffeensis</em> is an obligate intracellular bacterium that causes human monocytic ehrlichiosis (HME). To determine what host components are important for bacterial replication, we performed microarray analysis on <em>Drosophila melanogaster</em> S2 cells by comparing host gene transcript levels between permissive and nonpermissive conditions for <em>E. chaffeensis</em> growth. Five-hundred twenty-seven genes had increased transcript levels unique to permissive growth conditions 24 h postinfection. We screened adult flies that were mutants for several of the “permissive” genes for the ability to support <em>Ehrlichia</em> replication. Three additional <em>D. melanogaster</em> fly lines with putative mutations in pyrimidine metabolism were also tested. Ten fly lines carrying mutations in the genes <em>CG6479</em>, <em>separation anxiety</em>, <em>chitinase 11</em>, <em>CG6364</em> (<em>Uck2</em>), <em>CG6543</em> (<em>Echs1</em>), <em>withered</em> (<em>whd</em>), <em>CG15881</em> (<em>Ccdc58</em>), <em>CG14806</em> (<em>Apop1</em>), <em>CG11875</em> (<em>Nup37</em>), and <em>dumpy</em> (<em>dp</em>) had increased resistance to infection with <em>Ehrlichia</em>. Analysis of RNA by quantitative real-time reverse transcription-PCR (qRT-PCR) confirmed that the bacterial load was decreased in these mutant flies compared to wild-type infected control flies. Seven of these genes (<em>san</em>, <em>Cht11</em>, <em>Uck2</em>, <em>Echs1</em>, <em>whd</em>, C<em>cdc58</em>, and <em>Apop1</em>) encoded proteins that had mitochondrial functions or could be associated with proteins with mitochondrial functions. Treatment of THP-1 cells with double-stranded RNA to silence the human <em>UCK2</em> gene indicates that the disruption of the uridine-cytidine kinase affects <em>E. chaffeensis</em> replication in human macrophages. Experiments with cyclopentenyl cytosine (CPEC), a CTP synthetase inhibitor and cytosine, suggest that the nucleotide salvage pathway is essential for <em>E. chaffeensis</em> replication and that it may be important for the provision of CTP, uridine, and cytidine nucleotides.</p></section><section id="sec1"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">INTRODUCTION</h2>
<p><em>Ehrlichia chaffeensis</em> is the causative agent of human monocytic ehrlichiosis (HME). There were 1,429 cases of HME in 2010 and 2011 (<a href="#B14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B14">14</a>). This represents a significant increase in the incidence of the disease since 2003 and qualifies HME as an emerging infectious disease (<a href="#B25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B25">25</a>). In addition to being reported in the United States, HME has also been documented in Africa, Europe, China, and Brazil (<a href="#B9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B9">9</a>, <a href="#B16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B16">16</a>, <a href="#B42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B42">42</a>, <a href="#B67" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B67">67</a>). <em>E. chaffeensis</em> is an obligate intracellular bacterium. However, little is known about the parasitized-host requirements for bacterial replication.</p>
<p><em>Drosophila melanogaster</em> has been used to study a variety of intracellular pathogens. In particular, it has been successfully manipulated for the identification of genes involved in host-pathogen interactions. These pathogens include <em>Listeria monocytogenes</em> (<a href="#B1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B1">1</a>, <a href="#B2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B2">2</a>), <em>Chlamydia trachomatis</em> (<a href="#B20" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B20">20</a>), <em>Mycobacterium marinum</em> (<a href="#B1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B1">1</a>, <a href="#B18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B18">18</a>, <a href="#B30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B30">30</a>, <a href="#B48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B48">48</a>), <em>Francisella tularensis</em> (<a href="#B51" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B51">51</a>, <a href="#B64" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B64">64</a>), and the protozoan parasite <em>Plasmodium gallinaceum</em> (<a href="#B8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B8">8</a>, <a href="#B53" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B53">53</a>).</p>
<p>We previously demonstrated that <em>E. chaffeensis</em> is capable of infecting, completing its life cycle, and maintaining its pathogenicity in both <em>Drosophila</em> S2 cells (<a href="#B39" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B39">39</a>) and adult flies (<a href="#B40" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B40">40</a>). We have also identified growth conditions that were nonpermissive for the growth of <em>E. chaffeensis</em> infection in <em>Drosophila</em> S2 cells (<a href="#B39" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B39">39</a>). Therefore, we hypothesized that a transcriptional microarray analysis of permissively infected and nonpermissively infected S2 cells would reveal host genes that contribute to the replication of <em>Ehrlichia</em>. We used the Affymetrix <em>Drosophila</em> 2.0 array to identify a subset of genes that were exclusively expressed during <em>E. chaffeensis</em> infection in infected S2 cells under permissive growth conditions. We screened adult flies carrying mutations in several of the candidate genes, 10 of which change the fly response to <em>E. chaffeensis</em> compared to wild-type flies. Gene products from 7 of the 10 genes identified are associated with mitochondrial function and/or location. We also describe follow-up experiments to investigate how <em>Uck2</em> might function and its relevance to mammalian infections.</p></section><section id="sec2"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">MATERIALS AND METHODS</h2>
<section id="sec3"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Maintenance of cell lines and <em>E. chaffeensis</em> infections.</h3>
<p>The canine macrophage cell line DH82 (ATCC CRL-10389) was cultured in Eagle's minimal essential medium supplemented with 7% fetal bovine serum (FBS) (Atlanta Biologicals, Atlanta, GA) (EMEM<sub>7</sub>). THP-1 (ATCC TIB-202) cells were cultured in RPMI 1640 medium supplemented with 10% FBS. <em>Drosophila</em> S2 cells, obtained from the <em>Drosophila</em> RNAi Screening Center (Harvard Medical School, Boston, MA), were cultivated at 25°C in Schneider's <em>Drosophila</em> medium (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum. For experiments, S2 cells were seeded at a concentration of 1 × 10<sup>6</sup> cells/well in 6-well plates.</p>
<p>The <em>E. chaffeensis</em> Arkansas isolate was continuously cultivated in DH82 cells, as described previously, at 37°C, 8% CO<sub>2</sub> in EMEM<sub>7</sub> (<a href="#B40" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B40">40</a>). Purified bacteria were used to reinfect DH82, THP-1, and S2 cells.</p></section><section id="sec4"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Infections in permissive and nonpermissive <em>Drosophila</em> S2 cells.</h3>
<p>To make S2 cells nonpermissive to <em>E. chaffeensis</em> infection, the cells were allowed to adhere for 30 min prior to adding sonicated lipopolysaccharide (LPS) from <em>Salmonella enterica</em> serovar Minnesota (Sigma, St. Louis, MO) at a concentration of 10 μg per ml. The S2 cells plus LPS were incubated for 5 h prior to infection with <em>E. chaffeensis</em>. The <em>Ehrlichia</em>-infected S2 cells were monitored at 24 and 96 h postinfection (p.i.). Additionally, uninfected S2 cells, inactivated and activated with LPS, were used as controls. S2 cells were centrifuged at 300 × <em>g</em> for 5 min prior to RNA isolation from cell pellets using 1 ml of TriReagent (Molecular Research Center, Inc., Cincinnati, OH). The TriReagent-cell mixture was transferred to 2.0-ml Heavy Phase Lock Gel tubes (5 Prime, Westbury, NY). Chloroform (200 μl) was added, and the mixture was gently mixed for 15 s. After centrifugation at 12,000 × <em>g</em> for 10 min at 4°C, the aqueous phase was poured into 1.5-ml tubes. The RNA was precipitated and washed with isopropanol and ethanol according to the manufacturer's instructions. The RNA pellet was suspended in 50 μl of nuclease-free water, and the RNA concentration was determined spectrophotometrically (NanoDrop Technologies, Wilmington, DE) before and after DNA digestion using a Turbo DNA-free kit (Ambion Inc., Austin, TX).</p></section><section id="sec5"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Determination of infection by RT-PCR and quantitative real-time RT-PCR (qRT-PCR).</h3>
<p>For the microarray, infections were confirmed by reverse transcriptase (RT) PCR using the Promega Access One-Step RT-PCR kit (Madison, WI). Total RNA (750 ng) was used for each 25-μl reaction mixture, which contained the following reagents: 1× buffer, 0.2 mM deoxynucleoside triphosphates (dNTPs), 2 μM forward primer, 2 μM reverse primer, 1.5 mM MgSO<sub>4</sub>, 1 U/μl DNA polymerase, 1 U/μl reverse transcriptase, and nuclease-free water. RT-PCRs were performed in a thermocycler (Eppendorf Mastercycler Gradient, Hauppauge, NY). Primers and probes were designed with Primer Quest software (Integrated DNA Technologies, Coralville, IA) and data from NCBI reference sequences (see Table S1 in the supplemental material). RT-PCR conditions for the 16S rRNA of <em>E. chaffeensis</em> were 48°C for 45 min, 94°C for 4 min, and then 35 cycles of 94°C for 30 s, 52°C for 30 s, and 72°C for 1 min (<a href="#B21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B21">21</a>). The ribosomal protein 49 gene (<em>rp49</em>) was used as a housekeeping gene in RT-PCR experiments with <em>Drosophila</em> S2 cells. No-template RT-PCR controls were also included. RT-PCR product bands were identified on a ChemiImager (Protein Simple, Santa Clara, CA) after electrophoresis in 2% agarose gels and staining with ethidium bromide.</p>
<p>Flies were anesthetized using CO<sub>2</sub> prior to homogenization with disposable pestles (Kimble Chase, Vineland, NJ) in 1 ml of TriReagent. The homogenates were transferred to 2.0 ml Heavy Phase Lock Gel tubes and processed as described above for RNA isolation.</p>
<p><em>Ehrlichia</em> quantification in S2 cells and fly experiments was estimated using TaqMan-based qRT-PCR, as previously described (<a href="#B40" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B40">40</a>, <a href="#B55" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B55">55</a>), using <em>Drosophila ribosomal protein 15a</em> as the housekeeping gene with primers described in Table S1 in the supplemental material. <em>E. chaffeensis</em> was detected as described previously (<a href="#B40" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B40">40</a>, <a href="#B55" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B55">55</a>). The qRT-PCRs were performed in the Cepheid SmartCycler System (Sunnyvale, CA).</p>
<p>Analysis of gene expression based on qRT-PCR results was performed using the method described by Pfaffl (<a href="#B47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B47">47</a>). In short, primer efficiencies were calculated from standard dilution curves plotting threshold cycle (<em>C<sub>T</sub></em>) values versus log RNA using the following equation: efficiency = 10<sup>(−1/slope of standard curve)</sup>.</p>
<p>The change in <em>C<sub>T</sub></em> values for both genes of interest and housekeeping controls was determined by calculating the ΔΔ<em>C<sub>T</sub></em> using the following equation: (efficiency of gene of interest<sup>Gene of interest:Δ<em>CT</em> control − treated</sup>)/(efficiency of housekeeping gene<sup>Housekeeping gene:Δ<em>CT</em> control − treated</sup>).</p></section><section id="sec6"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Microarray analysis.</h3>
<p>Microarray analysis was performed at the University of Kansas Medical Center Microarray Facility (Kansas City, KS) using Affymetrix (Santa Clara, CA) <em>Drosophila</em> 2.0 Gene chips according to the manufacturer's specifications. The analysis was performed on four treatment groups (each submitted in triplicate at 24 h p.i.): (i) S2 cells infected with <em>E. chaffeensis</em>, (ii) S2 cells incubated with LPS and then infected with <em>E. chaffeensis</em>, (iii) S2 cells incubated with LPS, and (iv) untreated/uninfected S2 cells. Genes upregulated 1.5-fold or more above basal expression levels were identified under both permissive and nonpermissive conditions compared to uninfected controls at 24 h p.i. These gene sets were then compared, and those upregulated exclusively under either permissive or nonpermissive conditions at 24 h p.i. were identified. <section id="sec16"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Microarray “permissive-exclusive” genes after infection with <em>E. chaffeensis</em>.</h3>
<p>To identify host genes that are necessary for <em>E. chaffeensis</em> infection, we examined transcript levels of <em>Drosophila</em> S2 cells under four different conditions at 24 h p.i. to assess the host factors that might be needed to initiate the infection. They included (i) uninfected S2 cells, (ii) uninfected S2 cells activated with LPS, (iii) S2 cells activated with LPS and then infected with <em>E. chaffeensis</em> (nonpermissive conditions; this experiment, performed as in previous studies, demonstrated the inhibition of <em>E. chaffeensis</em> replication in LPS-activated S2 cells [<a href="#B43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B43">43</a>]), and (iv) S2 cells infected with <em>E. chaffeensis</em> (permissive conditions). <em>E. chaffeensis</em> replicated only under permissive conditions and not under nonpermissive conditions for as long as 96 h p.i. (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1</a>). Uninfected cells were used to discern the basal transcript levels in the S2 cells, and comparisons across the different conditions were based on this basal level of expression. In order to understand which gene transcripts were specific to nonpermissive S2 cells, we used the S2 cells that were treated only with LPS as a comparison. Cells that received only LPS treatment revealed activation-specific gene transcripts. We compared those genes to the genes that had increased transcript levels in cells infected under nonpermissive conditions for bacterial growth to deduce transcripts that were upregulated exclusively under nonpermissive conditions. We also determined the gene transcripts that were upregulated 1.5-fold or more exclusively under permissive infection conditions for <em>E. chaffeensis</em> growth, and 2,128 genes were upregulated 1.5-fold more than in uninfected controls. Under nonpermissive growth conditions, 1,742 of the genes were upregulated 1.5-fold more than in uninfected S2 cells. When we compared transcripts that were upregulated under permissive growth conditions to transcripts that were upregulated under nonpermissive growth conditions, we identified 527 genes unique to permissive conditions (see Table S3 in the supplemental material). Of these, 210 had previously been ascribed some function and had some characterization. The functions of the remaining 307 genes had yet to be defined and had “<em>CG</em>” (computed gene) gene designations. However, a number of these genes had orthologs with some described properties. These ortholog gene descriptions were used to ascribe functions for our analyses. Viable and fertile adult fly mutant stocks were available for 118 of the 527 genes (37 stocks for defined genes and 81 stocks for undefined genes). These flies had appropriate mutations in coding exons in the genes of interest and allowed testing of how the absence of a functional gene would affect fly survival and/or bacterial replication <em>in vivo</em>. Our screen of mutant fly lines was loosely based on descriptions of genes that controlled functions that we guessed might be important for bacterial growth or were random choices of <em>CG</em>-designated genes.</p>

<p>To understand the physiological relevance of disruption of gene function to <em>E. chaffeensis</em> growth in the host, we focused on <em>Uck2</em>, as an ortholog for this gene has been identified in mammalian species (<a href="#B43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B43">43</a>). Stroman (<a href="#B56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B56">56</a>) previously reported that the <em>Drosophila dumpy</em>, <em>tilt</em>, and <em>withered</em> stocks carried a mutation(s) that affects uridine/cytidine kinase function. To confirm the impact of <em>Uck2</em> on <em>E. chaffeensis</em> growth, the three fly lines with putative defects in uridine/cytidine kinase function were infected with <em>E. chaffeensis</em> and assessed for bacterial replication. Two of these fly lines were significantly more resistant to <em>E. chaffeensis</em> challenge than WT flies (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4</a>). For example, at 96 h p.i., 61% of the <em>dumpy</em> flies were alive compared to 42% of WT flies; similarly, 66% of <em>withered</em> flies survived compared to 38% of WT flies (<em>P</em> &lt; 0.05). Although there was a tendency for the <em>tilt</em> flies (60% ± 16% survival) to be more resistant than the WT flies (46% ± 7% survival), the differences were not statistically significant (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4</a>). The increased survival of the <em>withered</em> and <em>dumpy</em> mutants was accompanied by a significant decrease in the replication of the <em>Ehrlichia</em> organisms, as measured by qRT-PCR assay (<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3</a>). At 96 h p.i., WT flies contained significantly more bacteria than <em>withered</em> and <em>dumpy</em> mutants (<em>P</em> = 0.02) (<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3</a>, bottom). The <em>tilt</em> mutants averaged fewer bacteria, but there was more variation in the samples.</p>

<p>The uridine/cytidine kinase enzyme functions in pyrimidine synthesis pathways and is specifically involved in the conversion of uridine to UMP and cytidine to CMP (<a href="#B52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B52">52</a>, <a href="#B62" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B62">62</a>, <a href="#B63" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B63">63</a>) (<a href="#F5" class="usa-link">Fig. 5</a>). dCTP can also be synthesized from glutamine through the <em>de novo</em> synthesis pathway, as well (<a href="#B52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B52">52</a>, <a href="#B62" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B62">62</a>) (<a href="#F5" class="usa-link">Fig. 5</a>). To determine the importance of the <em>de novo</em> synthesis pathway to <em>E. chaffeensis</em> infections, we obtained the drug cyclopentenyl cytosine, which inhibits the conversion of [<sup>3</sup>H]UTP to [<sup>3</sup>H]CTP (<a href="#B28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B28">28</a>), as an inhibitor of CTP synthetase (<a href="#B45" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B45">45</a>, <a href="#B52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B52">52</a>, <a href="#B62" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B62">62</a>). The drug effectively inhibits <em>de novo</em> synthesis of pyrimidines, leaving only the salvage pathway, with cytidine as the substrate for pyrimidine synthesis. To explore the impact of CPEC on <em>E. chaffeensis</em> growth, S2 cells were treated with CPEC for 48 h at a final concentration of 0.1, 1, 10, or 100 μM. Subsequently, we infected those cells with <em>E. chaffeensis</em> for an additional 48 h. We found a significant increase in bacterial growth in all of the treated cells compared to infected cells treated with carrier only (<a href="#F6" class="usa-link">Fig. 6</a>, solid bars; <em>P</em> &lt; 0.01). Similarly, when S2 cells were first infected with <em>E. chaffeensis</em> for 48 h and then treated with different concentrations of CPEC for 48 h, we observed significant increases in bacterial growth in cells treated with 10 and 100 μM CPEC compared to infected cells treated with carrier only (<a href="#F6" class="usa-link">Fig. 6</a>, cross-hatched bars; <em>P</em> &lt; 0.01). Therefore, the <em>de novo</em> synthesis pathway was not needed for bacterial growth in the S2 cells and usually enhanced infection.</p>

<p>Cytosine combines with ribose to form cytidine in the nucleotide salvage pathway. Therefore, to determine if it is an important substrate for bacterial growth, we supplemented S2 cell culture medium for 24 h with 25 mM cytosine prior to infection with <em>E. chaffeensis</em>, and bacterial replication was assessed at up to 96 h p.i. (<a href="#F7" class="usa-link">Fig. 7</a>). The cytosine-treated infected cells contained averages of 456%, 1423%, 3,352%, and 2,465% more copies of <em>Ehrlichia</em> 16S rRNA at 24, 48, 72, and 96 h p.i., respectively, than mock-infected cells.</p>

<p>The uridine-cytidine kinase UCK2 is a conserved enzyme that is present in humans, as well as <em>D. melanogaster</em>. Therefore, to examine the contribution of the enzyme to <em>E. chaffeensis</em> growth in human cells, we used double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) to induce <em>UCK2</em> gene-specific silencing to inhibit translation of UCK2. We used three different RNA duplex sequences to define the specificity of the UCK2 knockdown. THP-1 cells are a promyelocytic cell line that was induced to differentiate into macrophages for 24 h using phorbol ester. After being silenced for 48 h, cells were infected with <em>E. chaffeensis</em>. Three different siRNA duplexes targeted to the <em>UCK2</em> transcript consistently inhibited <em>E. chaffeensis</em> growth by 48 h p.i. in the range of 47 to 69% compared to cells treated with a scrambled control siRNA (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>). We also detected improved <em>E. chaffeensis</em> growth in cells treated with siRNA for HPRT, which was used as a control for silencing-procedure effectiveness and to help show the specificity of the <em>UCK2</em>-specific duplexes.</p>

<p>Mitochondria surround morulae in <em>E. chaffeensis</em>-infected cells (<a href="#B49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B49">49</a>). Indeed, when we examined DH82 cells after infection with <em>E. chaffeensis</em> by TEM, one prominent feature of the infected cells was the clustering of large numbers of mitochondria around the morulae (<a href="#F8" class="usa-link">Fig. 8A</a> and <a href="#F8" class="usa-link">B</a>). It even appears that mitochondria were closely apposed to bacteria within the parasitophorous vacuole (<a href="#F8" class="usa-link">Fig. 8B</a>). This contrasts with the random distribution of mitochondria in uninfected cells (<a href="#F8" class="usa-link">Fig. 8C</a>).</p>

<p>We used <em>D. melanogaster</em> to investigate host factors that are important for the replication of <em>E. chaffeensis</em>. We combined microarray analysis and mutant fly screening to successfully identify 10 genes that contribute to the replication of <em>E. chaffeensis</em>
<em>in vivo</em>. Seven genes (<em>san</em>, <em>Cht11</em>, <em>Uck2</em>, <em>Echs1</em>, <em>whd</em>, <em>Ccdc58</em>, and <em>Apop1</em>) encode proteins that have mitochondrial functions, serve as substrates in the mitochondria, or are associated with proteins with mitochondrial functions. For example, the <em>Cht11</em> gene encodes a glycoside hydrolase with a chitinase active site (<a href="#B43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B43">43</a>). <em>Cht11</em> is orthologous to the <em>Chitinase 11</em> gene of <em>Tribolium castaneum</em>, which has been assigned to a separate classification group (group VII) from other identified chitinases (<a href="#B3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B3">3</a>) (Subbaratnam Muthukrishnan, Kansas State University, personal communication). This suggests that <em>Cht11</em> has a function other than those classically described for chitinases. A two-hybrid screen associated the protein with the protein product encoded by <em>Tiny tim 50</em> (<a href="#B22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B22">22</a>). <em>Ttm50</em> (also known as <em>Tim50</em>) encodes a protein that is part of a transporter located on the inner mitochondrial membrane (<a href="#B15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B15">15</a>) (<a href="#F9" class="usa-link">Fig. 9</a>). Chitins and their derivatives regulate cholesterol when used as diet supplements (<a href="#B29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B29">29</a>, <a href="#B74" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B74">74</a>). Mice with high blood cholesterol had more severe <em>Anaplasma phagocytophilum</em> (another tick-transmitted rickettsia pathogen closely related to <em>E. chaffeensis</em>) infections in their blood, livers, and spleens than mice with normal cholesterol levels (<a href="#B72" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B72">72</a>). <em>E. chaffeensis</em> and <em>A. phagocytophilum</em> cannot synthesize cholesterol, and they scavenge it from host cells during infections (<a href="#B34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B34">34</a>). Therefore, <em>Cht11</em> may function to regulate cholesterol levels to facilitate infections. Cholesterol can also affect both the inner and outer mitochondrial membranes and affect ATPase activity (<a href="#B10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B10">10</a>, <a href="#B19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B19">19</a>). A human homolog of <em>Cht11</em> has not been identified, but homologs have been identified in ticks and body lice (<a href="#B43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B43">43</a>). Both of these species serve as vectors for several different rickettsia bacteria (<a href="#B4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B4">4</a>), and it will be interesting to explore the function of the gene for the survival of <em>E. chaffeensis</em> and other rickettsial agents in their respective arthropod hosts.</p>

<figcaption><p>Identification of mitochondrion-associated genes that impact <em>E. chaffeensis</em> infection. <em>san</em> [<em>N</em>-(alpha) acetyltransferase 50] and <em>Uck2</em> (uridine/cytidine kinase) are needed for the synthesis of cardiolipin. Phosphatidic acid (PA), a product of <em>san</em> activity, and CTP, which is produced from CMP, a product of <em>Uck2</em>, are both necessary substrates for biosynthesis of cardiolipin, the major inner mitochondrial membrane lipid. <em>Cht11</em> (<em>chitinase 11</em>) encodes part of a protein transporter in the inner mitochondrial membrane. <em>whd</em> (<em>withered</em>) encodes carnitine palmitoyltransferase 1, and <em>Echs1</em> encodes enoyl-CoA hydratase; both enzymes are needed for the production of acetyl-CoA. <em>Ccdc58</em> encodes coiled-coil domain-containing protein 58, which is found in complex with the inner mitochondrial membrane molecules MRPS28 and SSBP1. <em>Apopt1</em> (<em>Apoptogenic 1</em>) induces the release of cytochrome <em>c</em> through the permeability transition pore and triggers apoptosis. <em>Nup37</em> (<em>Nucleoporin 37</em>) is not found in the mitochondrion but is part of a nucleopore complex that transports hexose molecules, including glucose, an important molecule in energy metabolism.</p></figcaption></figure><p>Several of the genes that impact <em>E. chaffeensis</em> infections encode key mitochondrial enzymes needed for fatty acid metabolism and acetyl-coenzyme A (CoA) production. The <em>whd</em> gene was originally associated with uridine kinase (<a href="#B56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B56">56</a>). However, recent annotation (<a href="#B43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B43">43</a>) and experimentation (<a href="#B58" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B58">58</a>) have identified <em>whd</em> as the <em>palmitoyltransferase 1</em> (<em>CPT 1</em>) gene. Palmitoyltransferase 1 is the rate-limiting enzyme for palmitoyl-CoA uptake into mitochondria (<a href="#B24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B24">24</a>) (<a href="#F9" class="usa-link">Fig. 9</a>). <em>Echs1</em> in <em>Drosophila</em> is an ortholog of the <em>ECHS1</em> gene in humans and other mammals (<a href="#B43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B43">43</a>), which encodes an enoyl-CoA hydratase in the second step of the β-oxidation pathway in the mitochondrion (<a href="#B27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B27">27</a>) (<a href="#F9" class="usa-link">Fig. 9</a>). Therefore, when we experimentally challenged flies mutant for the <em>Echs1</em> gene, the immediate downstream effect was on the supply of acetyl-CoA to the citric acid cycle, which implies that the supply of acetyl-CoA may be critical to <em>E. chaffeensis</em> replication. <em>san</em> is an ortholog of the human <em>NAA50</em> gene, which encodes <em>N</em>(alpha)-acetyltransferase 50 (<a href="#B43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B43">43</a>). These acetyltransferases are involved in the conversion of glycerol-3-phosphate to phosphatidic acid (<a href="#B13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B13">13</a>). This biochemical step is upstream of the CDP diacylglycerol (CDP-DAG) intermediate in the synthesis of cardiolipin on the inner mitochondrial membrane. It is also possible that the acetyltransferase could be active in the β-oxidation pathway. However, this has yet to be proven experimentally.</p>
<p><em>CG15881</em>, or <em>Ccdc58</em>, is an ortholog of the human gene known as <em>coiled-coil domain-containing protein 58</em> (<em>CCDC58</em>). Although the exact function of <em>Ccdc58</em> is not known, its structure likely allows it to interact with other coiled-coil molecules (<a href="#B41" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B41">41</a>). Indeed, the STRING association tool (<a href="#B60" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B60">60</a>) shows that <em>CCDC58</em> has a direct relationship with <em>mitochondrial ribosomal protein S28</em> (<em>MRPS28</em>) and <em>translocase of inner mitochondrial membrane 9 homolog</em> (<em>TIMM9</em>), which encode an inner mitochondrial membrane chaperone protein, SSBP1, a protein putatively thought to be involved in mitochondrial DNA replication (<a href="#B60" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B60">60</a>). The BioGraph gene association tool (<a href="#B33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B33">33</a>) assigned a rank of 23 out of 18,180 gene concepts (top 0.13%) when it was assigned to “mitochondria.” Other coiled-coil domain-containing proteins have been associated with the inner mitochondrial membrane (<a href="#B70" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B70">70</a>), and <em>CCDC58</em> has also been associated with the mitochondrial single-nucleotide polymorphisms that impact the progression of AIDS (<a href="#B26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B26">26</a>). Therefore, it is highly likely that the impact of disruption of <em>Ccdc58</em> on <em>E. chaffeensis</em> growth is due to its impact on mitochondrial function.</p>
<p>CG14806 or <em>Apopt1</em> has no known function in <em>Drosophila</em>. It is an ortholog of the <em>Apoptogenic 1</em> gene (<em>APOPT1</em> or <em>Apop1</em>) in humans and rodents (<a href="#B66" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B66">66</a>). It is involved in the release of cytochrome <em>c</em> through permeability transition pores in the outer membrane of the mitochondrion and the activation of apoptosis (<a href="#B59" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B59">59</a>, <a href="#B73" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B73">73</a>) (<a href="#F9" class="usa-link">Fig. 9</a>). It seems plausible that inhibiting apoptosis would be to the bacterium's advantage. There is increased transcription of some apoptotic genes during <em>Ehrlichia</em> infections (<a href="#B75" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B75">75</a>), and there is delayed apoptosis in neutrophils after infection with <em>Ehrlichia ewingii</em> (<a href="#B71" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B71">71</a>). It is possible that <em>Apop1</em> is needed to help in the release of bacteria at the end of the replicative phase. <em>E. chaffeensis</em> organisms disseminate by lysing host cells or by exocytosis in order to spread to uninfected cells (<a href="#B17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B17">17</a>). More empirical analyses will be needed to support this hypothesis.</p>
<p><em>Uck2</em> (<em>CG6364</em>) is orthologous to the mammalian <em>UCK2</em> gene with the molecular function of a uridine kinase (<a href="#B43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B43">43</a>). Functionally, it has been associated with the phagocytosis of <em>Candida albicans</em> (<a href="#B57" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B57">57</a>). Using the STRING association tool (<a href="#B60" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B60">60</a>), we found that its predicted functional partners are all involved in nucleotide/nucleoside modification. These functions include nucleoside di- and triphosphate activity (<em>CG5276</em>), hydrolase activity (<em>CG8891</em>), uracil phosphoribosyl transferase activity (<em>CG5537</em>), uridine phosphorylase (<em>CG3788</em>, <em>CG8349</em>), cytidylate/uridylate kinase activity (Dak1), uridine phosphorylase (<em>CG6330</em>), and cytidine deaminase activity (<em>CG8349</em>). The BioGraph gene association tool (<a href="#B33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B33">33</a>) assigned a rank of 12.63% (2,297th out of 18,180 gene objects) when <em>Uck2</em> was associated with mitochondria, indicating a highly probable association. Cytosine triphosphate, a downstream product of the uridine kinase (<a href="#B38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B38">38</a>, <a href="#B62" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B62">62</a>), is a substrate used for the conversion of phosphatidic acid to CDP-DAG (<a href="#B31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B31">31</a>, <a href="#B46" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B46">46</a>) (<a href="#F9" class="usa-link">Fig. 9</a>). CDP-DAG is synthesized in the inner mitochondrial membrane and is necessary for the synthesis of cardiolipin, a major component of the inner mitochondrial membrane (<a href="#B46" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B46">46</a>). Therefore, we reason that the uridine kinase must be essential to providing substrates for <em>E. chaffeensis</em>, because it cannot be provided by the <em>de novo</em> synthesis pathway. When we grew <em>E. chaffeensis</em> in the presence of CPEC, a CTP synthetase inhibitor (<a href="#B52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B52">52</a>, <a href="#B62" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B62">62</a>) that inhibits the synthesis of CTP from glutamate (<a href="#F5" class="usa-link">Fig. 5</a>), bacterial replication was significantly enhanced (<a href="#F6" class="usa-link">Fig. 6</a>). These data suggest that the bacterium does not require the host to use glutamine as a substrate during replication. Supplementation of cytosine in cells enhanced <em>Ehrlichia</em> replication, which further implicates the use of the salvage pathway through cytidine. The enhanced growth of another rickettsia, <em>Rickettsia felis</em>, from pyrimidines in tryptose phosphate broth is also consistent with the involvement of mitochondrial enzymes in obligate intracellular bacterial growth (<a href="#B50" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B50">50</a>). Moreover, cytidine is the least abundant nucleoside in cells (<a href="#B32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B32">32</a>). The depletion of cytidine pools can disrupt the balance of ribonucleotides in cells, leading to alterations in cellular homeostasis and apoptosis (<a href="#B52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B52">52</a>). These observations suggest that <em>E. chaffeensis</em> needs to regulate cytidine in order to control apoptosis until its replication is completed within a phagosome.</p>
<p>The enhanced growth of <em>E. chaffeensis</em> after treatment with CPEC instead of an inhibitory effect suggests that the bacterium requires the uridine/cytidine salvage pathway to phosphorylate cytidine and uridine (<a href="#B61" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B61">61</a>) for its replication. More importantly, the disruption of <em>E. chaffeensis</em> growth in <em>UCK2</em>-silenced THP-1 macrophages reinforces the fact that the <em>UCK2</em> target has human relevance. Therefore, we focused our attention on <em>Uck2</em> because it has the potential to be a target for chemotherapy. <em>UCK2</em> has been found to be more active in certain types of cancers (<a href="#B54" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B54">54</a>) and acts to phosphorylate nucleoside analog drugs used for treatment of cancers and hepatitis C virus infections (<a href="#B23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B23">23</a>, <a href="#B61" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B61">61</a>). Uninfected host cells may be unaffected by UCK2 inhibitors, since nucleotides could be synthesized by the <em>de novo</em> synthesis pathway.</p>
<p>The clustering of mitochondria adjacent to morulae in <em>E. chaffeensis</em>-infected macrophages supports the hypothesis that it has physiological relevance and is consistent with previous observations (<a href="#B37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B37">37</a>, <a href="#B49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B49">49</a>). Mitochondrial membrane permeability and membrane potential were not disrupted by either <em>E. chaffeensis</em> (<a href="#B37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B37">37</a>) or <em>E. ewengii</em> (<a href="#B71" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B71">71</a>) infection. However, Liu et al. found that mitochondrial biochemical activity was reduced, as evidenced from the measurement of mitochondrial DNA synthesis or the transcription of several mitochondrial genes (<a href="#B37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B37">37</a>). There is also evidence that the <em>E. chaffeensis</em> type IV secretion system is used to insert bacterial effector proteins into host mitochondria (<a href="#B36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B36">36</a>). These observations suggest that the organism inhibits mitochondrial activity, perhaps to inhibit the generation of mitochondrially produced oxidative products that would be harmful to the bacteria (<a href="#B5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B5">5</a>, <a href="#B68" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B68">68</a>). In contrast, we have disrupted several genes that are associated with mitochondrial function that negatively affect <em>E. chaffeensis</em> infections. The gene products of <em>san</em> and <em>Uck2</em> affect cardiolipin synthesis on the inner mitochondrial membrane (<a href="#F9" class="usa-link">Fig. 9</a>). <em>Cht11</em> encodes protein for part of a transport molecule on the same inner mitochondrial membrane. The gene products of <em>whd</em> and <em>Echs1</em> affect steroidogenesis, ketogenesis, and the Krebs cycle (<a href="#F9" class="usa-link">Fig. 9</a>), the last of which has clear mitochondrial dependence. Although <em>Ccdc58</em> and <em>Apopt1</em> are associated with mitochondria (<a href="#F9" class="usa-link">Fig. 9</a>), we have yet to fully understand their significance. It is also interesting that genes like <em>Nup37</em> and <em>dumpy</em>, which do not have direct mitochondrial connections, also affect <em>E. chaffeensis</em> infections. The <em>NUP37</em> gene product is part of nucleopores. One of the molecules it transports, glucose, is also important for mitochondrial functions. The <em>dumpy</em> gene has been characterized genetically and appears to impact wing and other body part development (<a href="#B11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B11">11</a>, <a href="#B12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B12">12</a>). It has no known mammalian orthologs (<a href="#B43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B43">43</a>), and <em>dumpy</em> encodes a large extracellular molecule needed for structural integrity in wing epithelial tissue (<a href="#B12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B12">12</a>, <a href="#B69" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B69">69</a>). Interestingly, one of the properties listed for <em>dumpy</em> is that it binds iron and sulfur (<a href="#B43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B43">43</a>), properties of mitochondrial proteins involved in oxidation-reduction reactions (<a href="#B6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B6">6</a>). Clearly, more work is necessary to understand these molecules and their host-bacterium interactions. Nevertheless, our findings provide clues about the mitochondrial processes that are needed by the bacteria. Interestingly, at least one alphaproteobacterium closely related to <em>Ehrlichia</em>, designated IricES1, infects the mitochondria of ovarian cells of <em>Ixodes ricinus</em> (<a href="#B7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B7">7</a>). Perhaps <em>E. chaffeensis</em> is one step away from a similar symbiotic relationship.</p></section><section id="sec24"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Supplementary Material</h2>
