</div></section></section><section aria-label="Article content"><section class="body main-article-body"><section class="abstract" id="abstract1"><h2>Abstract</h2>
<p id="P1">The oocytes of the African clawed frog (<em>Xenopus laevis</em>) comprise one of the most widely used membrane protein expression systems. While frequently used for studies of transporters and ion channels, the application of this system to the study of mechanosensitive ion channels has been overlooked, perhaps due to a relative abundance of native expression systems. Recent advances, however, have illustrated the advantages of the oocyte system for studying plant and bacterial mechanosensitive channels. Here we describe in detail the methods used for heterologous expression and characterization of bacterial and plant mechanosensitive channels in Xenopus oocytes.</p>
<section id="kwd-group1" class="kwd-group"><p><strong>Keywords:</strong> Mechanosensitive ion channel, Heterologous expression, <em>Xenopus laevis</em> oocytes, Patch-clamp electrophysiology, Pressure-clamp</p></section></section><section id="S1"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">1 Introduction</h2>
<p id="P2">Oocytes of the African clawed frog (<em>Xenopus laevis</em>) have been used for several decades for the expression of transporters and receptors from bacteria [<a href="#R1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R1">1</a>], plants [<a href="#R2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R2">2</a>, <a href="#R3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R3">3</a>], and mammals [<a href="#R4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R4">4</a>–<a href="#R6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R6">6</a>] due to the simplicity and reliability of this experimental system [<a href="#R7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R7">7</a>]. Relatively low background ion channel activity [<a href="#R8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R8">8</a>], large size (1–1.5 mm), low cost, and ease of handling make these cells a favored system for the expression of human, plant and bacterial channels. Xenopus oocytes have also been used as a model system for the study of processes unrelated to ion transport such as apoptosis [<a href="#R9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R9">9</a>, <a href="#R10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R10">10</a>], gene expression [<a href="#R11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R11">11</a>], the structure and function of the nuclear envelope [<a href="#R12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R12">12</a>] and many others [<a href="#R13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R13">13</a>].</p>
<p id="P3">While most mechanosensitive channels (MSCs) have been characterized under native conditions [<a href="#R14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R14">14</a>–<a href="#R16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R16">16</a>], we have recently expressed and characterized two MSCs in Xenopus oocytes, MscS from <em>Escherichia coli</em> and MSL10 from <em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em> [<a href="#R17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R17">17</a>, <a href="#R18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R18">18</a>]. Our analyses were made possible by previous studies showing that the endogenous MSCs known to be present in the oocyte membrane [<a href="#R8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R8">8</a>, <a href="#R19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R19">19</a>, <a href="#R20" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R20">20</a>] can be effectively blocked or inactivated [<a href="#R21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R21">21</a>, <a href="#R22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R22">22</a>]. A number of useful papers on frog maintenance [<a href="#R23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R23">23</a>], oocyte expression and transport systems [<a href="#R3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R3">3</a>, <a href="#R24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R24">24</a>–<a href="#R28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R28">28</a>], oocyte isolation, and the methods used for characterization of ligand- and voltage-gated channels in the Xenopus oocyte system [<a href="#R29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R29">29</a>–<a href="#R33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R33">33</a>] have been published to date. Here we focus on the methods we used for the heterologous expression and characterization of MscS and MSL10 at the single-channel level. We describe in detail the techniques for surgery, oocyte isolation and preparation, in vitro RNA production and injection into oocytes, and basics of MSC electrophysiology, including patch-clamp and pressure-clamp.</p></section><section id="S2"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">2 Materials</h2>
<p id="P4">Special attention must be paid to water quality at several stages in this procedure. The water used for surgery solutions should be the same water in which the frog is maintained in the animal facility. Water used in buffers for oocyte isolation and patch-clamp should be distilled and filter-sterilized (0.2 μm) or autoclaved. Water used for complimentary RNA (cRNA) production must be DNase- and RNase-free. All solutions (apart from those made right before the experiment or requiring freezer storage) should be kept in a refrigerator. Gentamycin-containing buffers should be stored together with oocytes at 16–18°C.</p>
<section id="S3"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">2.1 Frog Surgery</h3>
<ol id="L1" class="list" style="list-style-type:decimal">
<li><p id="P5">Anesthetic solution: 0.5-1 L of 1.5 g/L MS-222 (ethyl-3-aminobenzoate methanesulfonate) buffered with 840 mg/L NaHCO<sub>3</sub> and dissolved in pretreated water from the animal facility.</p></li>
<li><p id="P6">Complete N96 buffer: 96 mM NaCl, 2 mM KCl, 1.8 mM CaCl<sub>2</sub>, 1 mM MgCl<sub>2</sub>, 5 mM Hepes, pH 7.38.</p></li>
<li><p id="P7">Penicillin streptomycin (Pen Strep) solution (100× solution).</p></li>
<li><p id="P8">Sterile autoclaved forceps and scissors.</p></li>
<li><p id="P9">Sutures (2 pieces each of Monocryl undyed monofilament (Ethicon) and Ethilon black monofilament (Ethicon)).</p></li>
<li><p id="P10">Parafilm.</p></li>
<li><p id="P11">50-ml conical tube with 20 ml of ND96 buffer.</p></li>
<li><p id="P12">Lab coat, gloves and eye protection.</p></li>
<li><p id="P13">70 % ethanol or Lysol for benchtop sterilization.</p></li>
</ol></section><section id="S4"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">2.2 Oocyte Isolation</h3>
<ol id="L2" class="list" style="list-style-type:decimal">

<li><p id="P15">0.1 g collagenase A type I, in 20 ml Ca<sup>2+</sup> -free ND96.</p></li>
<li><p id="P16">250 ml of 0.1 % BSA in Ca<sup>2+</sup> -free ND96.</p></li>
<li><p id="P17">30 ml of 50 mM KH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>/50 mM K<sub>2</sub>HPO<sub>4</sub> with 0.01 % BSA in water, pH 6.5 (made in advance and stored as 45 mL aliquots at −20°C).</p></li>
<li><p id="P18">Complete ND96 buffer supplemented with 50 mg/ml gentamycin.</p></li>
<li><p id="P19">50-ml conical tubes.</p></li>
<li><p id="P20">100 mm × 15 mm petri dishes.</p></li>
<li><p id="P21">Plastic transfer pipettes (62 μl aperture, large bulb).</p></li>
<li><p id="P22">Incubator capable of maintaining 16–18°C (such as a wine cooler).</p></li>
</ol></section><section id="S5"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">2.3 RNA Preparation</h3>
<ol id="L3" class="list" style="list-style-type:decimal">

<li><p id="P24">RNase-free water.</p></li>
<li><p id="P25">RNase-free pipette tips and tubes.</p></li>
<li><p id="P26">Spectrophotometer suitable for measuring the concentration and purity of nucleic acids (e.g., NanoPhotometer, Implen).</p></li>
</ol></section><section id="S6"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">2.4 Oocyte Injection</h3>

<li><p id="P28">Mineral oil.</p></li>

<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1</a>).</p></li>
<li><p id="P30">Capillary glass for an injection pipette. We use a thin-walled 8 in.-long pipette.</p></li>
<li><p id="P31">Micropipette or automatic injection system (<em>see</em>
<a href="#F2" class="usa-link">Fig. 2</a>).</p></li>
<li><p id="P32">Pipette puller (P97, Sutter Instrument Co) (<em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN1" class="usa-link">Note 1</a></sup>).</p></li>
<li><p id="P33">RNase-free pipette tips.</p></li>
<li><p id="P34">6-well tissue culture plates.</p></li>
</ol>
<figure class="fig xbox font-sm" id="F1"><h4 class="obj_head">Fig. 1.</h4>

<ol id="L5" class="list" style="list-style-type:decimal">
<li><p id="P35">10 ml of hypertonic buffer: 200 mM K<sup>+</sup> -aspartate, 20 mM KCl, 1 mM MgCl<sub>2</sub>, 10 mM EGTA, 10 mM Hepes, pH 7.4.</p></li>

<p id="P44">Surgery is not performed until cRNA has been made and quantified (<em>see</em> Subheading 3.3). This prevents unnecessary surgeries. The surgery and oocyte isolation occur on the same day and injections are done the following day. Our protocol for surgery and oocyte isolation is based on [<a href="#R34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R34">34</a>].</p>
<section id="S9"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">3.1 Frog Surgery</h3>
<ol id="L6" class="list" style="list-style-type:decimal">
<li><p id="P45">Immerse a frog in the MS-222/NaHCO<sub>3</sub> solution for 10–15 min, until she does not respond to pinching of the skin between the toes. 13 min is optimal in our hands.</p></li>
<li><p id="P46">Rinse the frog with pretreated animal facility water to remove the anesthetic from its skin.</p></li>
<li><p id="P47">Put the frog on a wet paper towel and perform the surgery with sterile instruments, on a benchtop sterilized by spraying with 70 % ethanol, using sterile gloves. Keep the frog moist with sterile ND96 solution (<em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN4" class="usa-link">Note 4</a></sup>).</p></li>
<li><p id="P48">Make a small incision through the skin of the abdomen, using sharp scissors, followed by an incision of about 1 cm in the muscle layer. Rinse with ND96. (If the skin is slippery, grasp it with two fingers through a piece of clean paper towel). Place a sheet of Parafilm with a square cut in the middle over the incision, so that oocytes extracted from the abdomen will not come in contact with the surface of the frog’s skin.</p></li>
<li><p id="P49">Pull 2–4 ml of oocytes out of the body cavity with dull forceps and place in 25 ml of ND96 in a 50 ml conical tube.</p></li>
<li><p id="P50">Repair the incision with two sets of sutures, one for the muscle tissue (undyed) and one for skin (black). Each stitch is made with two loops of single knots and a third loop of double knots (three stitches) per 1–1.5 cm incision. The last knot should be pulled tight, and the ends trimmed closely.</p></li>
<li><p id="P51">Wash the frog with 0.5 L of clean water taken from the facility in order to get rid of any blood and anesthetic on her skin.</p></li>
<li><p id="P52">Allow the frog to recover in 0.5 L of Pen Strep solution (5 ml of 100× Pen Strep stock in 0.5 L of treated water from the frog facility) for 40–60 min. Tilt the plastic bath 45° from the vertical so that most of the frog’s body is submerged in the buffer. Care must be taken that the frog does not drown (keep her nostrils above the water level) but her skin stays wet. When she starts to move in response to pinching, she’s almost recovered. Once the frog recovers, return her to a clean cage with fresh water.</p></li>
<li><p id="P53">Depending on institutional rules, a frog could be operated on up to 5 times, with the last operation terminal. The operations should be 4–6 weeks apart and alternating sides of the abdomen used. After the last operation the frog may be euthanized according to the rules of your institution (<em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN5" class="usa-link">Note 5</a></sup>).</p></li>
</ol></section><section id="S10"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">3.2 Oocyte Isolation</h3>
<ol id="L7" class="list" style="list-style-type:decimal">
<li><p id="P54">Separate the ovarian tissue into clumps of 10–20 oocytes each in a petri dish, using forceps and working under a stereomicroscope. Transfer the clumps into Ca<sup>2+</sup> -free ND96. Try to avoid damage to the oocytes.</p></li>
<li><p id="P55">Incubate the oocytes in a petri dish with slow shaking for 25–40 min at room temperature in 20 ml of Ca<sup>2+</sup> -free ND96 with collagenase. Ca<sup>2+</sup> -free medium is used to slow down the collagenase reaction as Ca<sup>2+</sup> strongly activates collagenase [<a href="#R35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R35">35</a>]. Stop the process as soon as single oocytes are released.</p></li>
<li><p id="P56">Wash the oocytes 5 times with 25 ml ND96 + 0.1 % BSA in a 50 ml conical tube.</p></li>
<li><p id="P57">Add 25 ml 0.1 M potassium phosphate pH 6.5 buffer and incubate for 40–50 min on a slow shaker. At 15-min intervals, draw the oocytes into a wide-bore plastic pipette (made by trimming the tip) and pipette up and down to facilitate dispersal.</p></li>
<li><p id="P58">Wash the oocytes 5 times with 25 ml ND96 + 0.1 % BSA in a 50 ml conical tube.</p></li>
<li><p id="P59">Sort the oocytes in a petri dish under the microscope, selecting only mature oocytes (Dumont stage V-VI, [<a href="#R36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R36">36</a>]) with clear animal–vegetal pole divisions and without any spots, markings or residual ovarian tissue on the surface. Manipulate single oocytes with plastic transfer pipettes (<em>see</em> Subheading 2). Store the selected oocytes in complete ND96 with 50 mg/ml gentamycin at 16–18°C.</p></li>
</ol></section><section id="S11"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">3.3 RNA Preparation</h3>
<p id="P60">The sequence of the RNA of interest should first be cloned into an expression vector appropriate for cRNA production and its translation in oocytes. We use pOO2, a vector that allows cloning of a cDNA in between the 5′- and 3′-untranslated β-globin sequences from Xenopus [<a href="#R37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R37">37</a>].</p>
<ol id="L8" class="list" style="list-style-type:decimal">
<li><p id="P61">Linearize approximately 5 μg of template DNA in a 70 μl reaction. Use a restriction enzyme that recognizes a single site in the vector downstream of the sequence to be transcribed (<em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN6" class="usa-link">Note 6</a></sup>).</p></li>
<li><p id="P62">Run 4 μl of the sample on a gel to ensure linearity.</p></li>
<li><p id="P63">Add 5 μl 0.5 M EDTA, 4 μl 5 M NaCl, 25 μl RNase-free water and mix well.</p></li>
<li><p id="P64">Add 200 μl 100 % ethanol.</p></li>
<li><p id="P65">Mix well and place at −20°C for at least 20 min.</p></li>
<li><p id="P66">Spin for 15 min on a benchtop centrifuge at top speed.</p></li>
<li><p id="P67">Carefully remove supernatant and dry the pellet.</p></li>
<li><p id="P68">Resuspend the pellet in 25 μl RNase-free water and calculate the concentration of DNA by measuring the OD<sub>260</sub>.</p></li>
<li><p id="P69">Follow the protocol supplied with Ambion mMessage mMachine kit to produce the cRNA of interest (<em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN7" class="usa-link">Note 7</a></sup>).</p></li>
<li><p id="P70">Aliquot the cRNA solution (it should be approximately 1 μg/μl) into 3 μl aliquots and store at −20°C.</p></li>
</ol></section><section id="S12"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">3.4 Oocyte Injection</h3>
<ol id="L9" class="list" style="list-style-type:decimal">
<li><p id="P71">Place 10–40 oocytes into a gridded petri dish (<em>see</em>
<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1</a>) filled with complete ND96. Place each oocyte into a square of the plastic grid with the light side (vegetal pole) up.</p></li>
<li><p id="P72">Thaw RNase-free water and cRNA on ice.</p></li>
<li><p id="P73">Pull long needles from 8 in. capillary glass on a puller. For a Sutter P-97 the following program may be a good starting point: heat = 560, pull = none, velocity = 120, time = 100. Trim the end of each needle with scissors 5–6 mm before the point where the pulled portion of the pipette becomes flexible.</p></li>
<li><p id="P74">Backfill the needle with 13–15 μl of mineral oil using a micropipette, then apply the needle to the pipette or injector apparatus. Make sure there are no bubbles in the oil, then pull approximately 500 nl of RNase-free water into the tip from a drop placed on a petri dish, followed by all 3 μl of your cRNA sample (<em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN8" class="usa-link">Note 8</a></sup>).</p></li>
<li><p id="P75">Using a microinjector or a micropipette attached to the glass needle (<em>see</em>
<a href="#F2" class="usa-link">Fig. 2</a>), inject 50 nl of the cRNA solution into each oocyte, preferably into the light side and close to the animal/vegetative border (<em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN9" class="usa-link">Note 9</a></sup>).</p></li>
<li><p id="P76">Repeat <strong>steps 4</strong> and <strong>5</strong> above with fresh oocytes and RNase-free water as a negative control.</p></li>
<li><p id="P77">Place the oocytes into a 6-well plate filled with complete ND96 with gentamycin.</p></li>
<li><p id="P78">Change the solution and remove defective oocytes on a daily basis until analysis (<em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN10" class="usa-link">Notes 10</a> and <a href="#FN11" class="usa-link">11</a></sup>).</p></li>
</ol></section><section id="S13"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">3.5 Patch-Clamp</h3>
<p id="P79">The microscope stage setup, including a dish with bath medium, command and ground electrodes, patch pipette and a pressure clamp headstage, is presented in <a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Figs. 3a</a> (schematic view) and <a href="#F4" class="usa-link">4</a> (actual setup).</p>
<ol id="L10" class="list" style="list-style-type:decimal">
<li><p id="P80">Conventional single-channel patch-clamp is carried out using pipettes with BN (“bubble number”, <em>see</em> ref. <a href="#R38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R38">38</a>) of 4–7 in symmetric and asymmetric buffers.</p></li>
<li><p id="P81">Prior to patching, the transparent vitelline membrane of each oocyte should be removed with a pair of sharp forceps after 5–10 min incubation in hypertonic buffer, which makes the cell shrink and the vitelline membrane more visible (<em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN12" class="usa-link">Note 12</a></sup>). After removal of the vitelline membrane, the oocyte becomes very fragile, so it should not be exposed to the surface of the buffer and care should be taken when moving it. We suggest removing the vitelline membrane in the petri dish to be used for patch-clamp, already filled with patching bath buffer.</p></li>
<li><p id="P82">A patch-pipette should be fabricated using a pipette puller (see appropriate manual for measuring glass melting temperature and initial proxy settings that can be adjusted to get the pipette of desired size and shape). Typically, a pipette with tip size between 0.5 and 2 µm is required, which corresponds to BN 4–6 and a resistance of several MΩ, depending on the tips’ length and geometry.</p></li>
<li><p id="P83">The command (or pipette) electrode is a small piece of Ag wire that has been chlorinated either by immersion into 6 % bleach for 15–25 min or by subjecting it to proper potential in KCl buffer [ <a href="#R39" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R39">39</a> ].</p></li>
<li><p id="P84">The ground electrode is an Ag/AgCl pellet, connected to the ground input of the amplifier’s headstage through the common ground (such as a Faraday cage or anti-vibration table) via Ag wire (<em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN13" class="usa-link">Note 13</a></sup>, <a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Figs. 3a</a> and <a href="#F4" class="usa-link">4</a>). It does not matter where the electrode is grounded, only that everything metal be grounded at a single common point, with a stable electrical contact between the wires and grounding point. This can be tested with a standard multimeter. The ground electrode could be immersed into the bath solution directly or via an agar salt bridge (<em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN14" class="usa-link">Note 14</a></sup>). The Ag/AgCl pellet should be washed with distilled water after experiments are completed.</p></li>
<li><p id="P85">Back-fill the patch-pipette with buffer, making sure it is free of air bubbles. If bubbles form, they can sometimes be eliminated by tapping the pipette. Bubbles can be prevented in the first place by pulling some buffer into the empty pipette with a syringe before back-filling the rest of the pipette.</p></li>
<li><p id="P86">Before and during immersion of the pipette into the bath buffer, a positive pressure of 20–30 mmHg should be applied to it. After the pipette tip is immersed, the pressure may be lowered to 5–10 mmHg (<em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN15" class="usa-link">Note 15</a></sup>).</p></li>
<li><p id="P87">Bring the pipette tip close to the oocyte membrane. The state of the pipette tip should be monitored by periodic application of low amplitude square voltage pulses of approximately 5 mV to the command electrode. In Axopatch amplifiers, use the “seal test mode”, where steps are gated by the amplifier’s internal line frequency oscillator. If the tip resistance grows (the current decreases) before the tip touches the oocyte membrane, replace the pipette, as its tip is likely obstructed with debris.</p></li>
<li><p id="P88">The moment of touching the oocyte membrane is observed electrically as a decrease of the measured current. When it decreases to 2/3–1/2 of its initial value, stop pipette motion and immediately decrease the pressure in the pipette from slightly positive to about −10 mmHg.</p></li>
<li><p id="P89">Observe the gradual formation of the “gigaseal” between the membrane and the pipette as the resistance of the pipettemembrane contact increases to 1 GΩ or more (<em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN16" class="usa-link">Note 16</a></sup>). After a gigaseal is reached you have obtained the cell-attached configuration (<em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN17" class="usa-link">Note 17</a></sup>). Next, bring the pipette pressure to between −1 and −3 mm Hg.</p></li>
<li><p id="P90">Excise the patch by rapid removal of the pipette away from the oocyte membrane. If the patch is not stable, this may result in patch rupture. Alternatively a membrane vesicle may form in the pipette tip and block it (usually when the pipette is not moved away from the cell quickly enough, <em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN18" class="usa-link">Note 18</a></sup>).</p></li>
<li><p id="P91">Once the patch is in the “excised” (in this particular case, “inside-out”) patch configuration (<em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN19" class="usa-link">Notes 19</a> and <a href="#FN20" class="usa-link">20</a></sup> and <a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3b</a>), one can proceed to stimulation of MSCs. We recommend using a fixed transmembrane voltage of −20 to −40 mV and applying triangular 4- or 5-second-long symmetric ramps of negative pressure to the patch pipette, allowing for the gradual opening and closing of individual MSCs (<em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN21" class="usa-link">Note 21</a></sup>). A typical trace obtained from the membrane patch of an oocyte expressing MSL10 from <em>A. thaliana</em> [<a href="#R18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R18">18</a>] is presented in <a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3c</a> for reference.</p></li>
<li><p id="P92">Once a tension-induced channel activity is detected and recorded, be sure to repeat patch excision and current measurement on water-injected oocytes to ensure the absence of artifacts or unusually high endogenous channel activity.</p></li>
<li><p id="P93">We have observed that the localization of the channel usually is not uniform within the oocyte membrane and several patches from the same cell may be necessary to locate channel activity, especially if a small pipette diameter is used.</p></li>
<li><p id="P94">In our hands, the endogenous cation-selective MSCs that are often observed in the cell-attached configuration inactivate upon patch excision, especially in the presence of elevated MgCl<sub>2</sub> or in TEA-Cl buffer (<em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN22" class="usa-link">Notes 22</a>–<a href="#FN24" class="usa-link">24</a></sup>).</p></li>
<li><p id="P95">The rare appearance of an endogenous, large-conductance Ca<sup>2+</sup> -activated chloride channel (<em>see</em> ref. <a href="#R8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R8">8</a>) should also be taken into account (<em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN25" class="usa-link">Note 25</a></sup>). However, it usually does not present a serious problem as long as small patch-pipettes are used, due to its relatively low expression.</p></li>
<li><p id="P96">A good starting point for recording settings would include the following: (1) acquisition at 20 kHz with the amplifier’s low pass filter set to 5–10 kHz (depending on the available settings); (2) a 4–5 s pressure ramp of −20 to −80 mmHg (depending on the pipette size, <em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN26" class="usa-link">Note 26</a></sup>), which in most cases eliminates artifacts caused by a too rapid increase in tension; and (3) a holding potential of 20 mV to 40 mV (pipette voltage) for the inside-out configuration, which typically provides clean and artifact-free traces (<em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN26" class="usa-link">Notes 26</a> and <a href="#FN27" class="usa-link">27</a></sup>).</p></li>
<li><p id="P97">Once several channels are activated in the patch, one may study the channel’s voltage dependence. To do so, repeat the measurement with the same transmembrane pressure but at various potentials (<em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN28" class="usa-link">Note 28</a></sup>).</p></li>
<li><p id="P98">Pressure may be gradually increased by increments of −10 to −40 mmHg in order to activate more channels in the patch and obtain a dose-response curve (<em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN29" class="usa-link">Note 29</a></sup>).</p></li>
<li><p id="P99">For additional recommendations on data collection and statistical analysis, <em>see</em>
<sup><a href="#FN30" class="usa-link">Notes 30</a> and <a href="#FN31" class="usa-link">31</a></sup>.</p></li>
</ol></section></section><section id="floats-group1" class="floats-group"><figure class="fig xbox font-sm" id="F5"><h2 class="obj_head">Fig. 5.</h2>

<div class="fn p" id="FN8">

<div class="fn p" id="FN9">
<sup>9</sup><p class="display-inline">We have not observed a substantial difference in expression level depending on the location of cRNA injection. However, we do observe a higher number of channels per patch at the vegetal pole (light half).</p>

<div class="fn p" id="FN10">
<sup>10</sup><p class="display-inline">Analysis can take place as early as 24 h and as late as 14 (up to 21 in rare cases) days after injection. Dead or damaged oocytes release substances that appear to damage the remaining healthy cells, so it may be useful to split the cells into several groups for longer storage times. The indications that an oocyte is damaged include, in order of increasing severity: the appearance of bright spots on the animal pole (dark half), blurring of the animal/vegetal pole dividing line, substantially increased cell size, and the appearance of white material extruded from the cell. If the damage is not too severe, patching is still possible as only a small fraction of intact membrane is required. In the case of a heavily damaged oocyte it becomes impossible to remove the vitelline membrane without oocyte rupture.</p>
</div>
<div class="fn p" id="FN11">
<sup>11</sup><p class="display-inline">A good practice is to check for expression of the protein of interest. <a href="#F5" class="usa-link">Fig. 5</a>). This approach allows one to see if the fusion protein is localized to the plasma membrane. Another option is to use a tagged version and to detect it via Western blotting (using, for example, the isolation method described in ref. <a href="#R40" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R40">40</a>). This approach allows one to evaluate level of expression more quantitatively, but is more time-consuming.</p>
</div>
<div class="fn p" id="FN12">
<sup>12</sup><p class="display-inline">If proper care is taken, this procedure could be carried out in regular ND96 buffer.</p>
</div>
<div class="fn p" id="FN13">
<sup>13</sup><p class="display-inline">It is important that the anti-vibration table, Faraday cage, and microscope with its stage all be in good electric contact, which insures the absence of electric interference from external sources. Our recommendation is to physically connect all pieces with metal wires, preferably in a single place (<em>see</em>
<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4</a>). Noise levels may be monitored visually with a command electrode attached to the amplifier’s headstage via acquisition software; noise should be at least 2–3 times lower than the expected signal from a single channel opening (<em>see</em> patch-clamp amplifier manual for typical acceptable noise levels). Induced low-frequency modulations (50 or 60 Hz) are typically caused by loopbacks and can be eliminated by connecting all power cables to the same noise filter/surge suppressor. In many cases, noise can be reduced with acquisition software suites such as Clampfit (Molecular Devices). However, these approaches may result in partial data loss, and it is preferable to make the initial recording with minimal noise. Note that any additional equipment (such as peristaltic pumps for a perfusion system, manipulators, etc.) most likely will require grounding too. A detailed step-by-step procedure for testing the amplifier’s internal noise is usually described in the user manual and should be performed at least once during the initial amplifier setup. It is recommended to repeat this test from time to time during standard use.</p>
</div>
<div class="fn p" id="FN14">
<sup>14</sup><p class="display-inline">An agar bridge is an L-shaped glass capillary (the same diameter as used for patch-pipettes and bent in the flame of a gas burner) filled with 2–3 % agar in a 1 M KCl solution [<a href="#R41" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R41">41</a>]. A heated agar solution is sucked into the capillary and then left to cool. The agar salt bridge should be rinsed with water and kept in salt solution in a fridge as it subject to bacterial invasion. If any signs of bacterial growth are detected, the bridge should be discarded and a new one made.</p>
</div>
<div class="fn p" id="FN15">
<sup>15</sup><p class="display-inline">It is also often recommended that during immersion of the tip into the buffer, a stream of air should be applied to the buffer surface by blowing on it. This is intended to move away any debris and fragments of lipid monolayers from the water–air interface.</p>
</div>
<div class="fn p" id="FN16">
<sup>16</sup><p class="display-inline">If this does not happen in a reasonable period of time (30–60 s) or at least a slow but steady growth of patch resistance is not observed, additional suction may be applied to the pipette (usually −20 to −50 mmHg), but it should not be very high as high suction may result in patch rupture (or inactivation for some kinds of MSCs). Additionally, a slightly negative potential (−10 to −20 mV) may be applied to the command electrode to facilitate “sealing” of the patch.</p>
</div>
<div class="fn p" id="FN17">
<sup>17</sup><p class="display-inline">At this point, it is already possible to carry out measurements of currents in response to increased membrane tension. However, to remove the complicating contributions of the cell’s resting potential, series resistance, and endogenous MSCs we recommend proceeding to the excised patch configuration for further measurements.</p>
</div>
<div class="fn p" id="FN18">
<sup>18</sup><p class="display-inline">The production of such a vesicle typically can’t be seen through the microscope (unless high magnification and DIC are used), but its formation results in a drop in current in response to high command potentials and the appearance of slow capacitive transient currents in response to voltage steps. Lifting the pipette tip into the air and then immediately immersing it into the bath buffer can disrupt the vesicle. If the vesicle collapses and the new seal is good, one can observe a slight increase of conductance.</p>
</div>
<div class="fn p" id="FN19">
<sup>19</sup><p class="display-inline">The inside-out excised patch preserves the orientation of the membrane leaflets—i.e., it is the same as it was in the moment the pipette touched the cell membrane. In certain cases the opposite orientation (when the outer leaflet of the patch faces the bath solution but not the inner volume of the pipette (outside-out configuration)) may be of interest. In order to achieve the outside-out configuration, one should obtain a proper gigaseal in the cell-attached mode and then rupture the patch with a short pulse of either high voltage or negative pressure, thus obtaining direct electric access to the inner volume of the cell (whole-cell mode). Rapid backward removal of the pipette from the cell in this case typically results in the production of a membrane patch in the outside-out configuration. Outside-out patches are often less stable than inside-out patches.</p>
</div>
<div class="fn p" id="FN20">
<sup>20</sup><p class="display-inline">The whole-cell mode is not of particular interest for characterization of MSCs in ooctyes due to a very high level of noise (caused by a huge size of the oocyte membrane) and high probability of membrane rupture under applied tension.</p>
</div>
<div class="fn p" id="FN21">
<sup>21</sup><p class="display-inline">Membrane tension for the patch in the pipette obeys the law of Laplas and therefore is proportional to transmembrane pressure and curvature radius of the membrane patch. Large patches formed in large pipettes require lower transmembrane pressure in order to be exposed to the same tension as small patches formed in small pipettes.</p>
</div>
<div class="fn p" id="FN22">
<sup>22</sup><p class="display-inline">The amount of endogenous MSC activity present in oocytes varies depending on the batch of oocytes, the frog, and the season of the year. Some scientists report that the season with the highest background activity is summer. Endogenous cation-selective MSCs may be inhibited by high concentrations of Mg<sup>2+</sup> ions (<em>see</em> ref. <a href="#R22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R22">22</a>) or very low concentrations of lanthanides (approximately 10 μM La<sup>3+</sup> or Gd<sup>3+</sup>), strong blockers of calcium, potassium and many mechanosensitive channels (<em>see</em> ref. <a href="#R21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R21">21</a>). However, it has been shown in the latter case that the inhibition of at least some non-selective MSCs (like MscS) by gadolinium is mediated by membrane lipids and may affect all MSCs at high enough levels [<a href="#R42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R42">42</a>].</p>
</div>
<div class="fn p" id="FN23">
<sup>23</sup><p class="display-inline">If there are indications that the MSC of interest is non-selective or anion-selective, one may utilize tetraethylammonium chloride (TEA-Cl) buffer to investigate the ion selectivity of the channel. The TEA<sup>+</sup> molecule is a blocker of many potassium channels [<a href="#R43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R43">43</a>] and due to its size (approximately 8 Å) is not able to permeate smaller channels. In our hands it blocked endogenous potassium MSCs, permeated <em>E. coli</em> MscS and did not permeate nor block <em>A. thaliana</em> MSL10 [<a href="#R17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R17">17</a>, <a href="#R18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R18">18</a>]. Symmetric 100 mM TEA-Cl buffer usually works well and provides a very clean background, but it also requires the addition of Mg<sup>2+</sup> (or other divalent ions) to form a tight membraneglass seal. In extreme cases, when sealing does not occur, a buffer consisting of 50–60 mM MgCl<sub>2</sub> only (with no other ions) may be an option.</p>
</div>
<div class="fn p" id="FN24">
<sup>24</sup><p class="display-inline">The formation of a seal between the membrane patch and the pipette occurs more slowly in TEA-Cl buffer. In order to facilitate the process, the concentration of MgCl<sub>2</sub> in the buffer may be increased up to 20 mM.</p>
</div>
<div class="fn p" id="FN25">
<sup>25</sup><p class="display-inline">If this problem persists, the Ca<sup>2+</sup> -activated chloride channel may be inhibited by exclusion of Ca<sup>2+</sup> ions from the buffer, or the addition of chelators or specific blocking agents. If the channels of interest are expected to be cation-selective (or, in some cases, non-selective) large non-permeant molecules such as 2-(<em>N</em>-morpholino)-ethanesulfonic acid (MES) could be used instead of chloride.</p>
</div>
<div class="fn p" id="FN26">
<sup>26</sup><p class="display-inline">Membrane potential is defined as the potential of the inner monolayer minus the potential of the outer monolayer, so that the membrane potential of a live cell is negative. For an insideout patch, a positive command potential (i.e., the potential applied through the amplifier to the command electrode in the patch-pipette) corresponds to a negative membrane potential. In case of the outside-out configuration, when the orientation of the monolayers is reversed (i.e., the inside one faces patchpipette), a positive command potential corresponds to a positive membrane potential of the same amplitude.</p>
</div>
<div class="fn p" id="FN27">
<sup>27</sup><p class="display-inline">Higher membrane potentials (up to–100 mV for oocytes) on the one hand often result in better signal to noise ratio, but on the other hand may induce the appearance of multiple subconducting states of the channel and always reduce membrane stability. Positive membrane potentials, though they almost never appear in live cells (with an obvious exception of excitable ones), are still of interest as they allow one to characterize a channel’s behavior under asymmetric conditions and may provide information on its gating mechanism and even molecular structure.</p>
</div>
<div class="fn p" id="FN28">
<sup>28</sup><p class="display-inline">This may be done either by changing the amplifier settings manually before each recording, or by using the software suite programming capabilities. In the latter case, the program will automatically execute several consecutive measurements with different settings (in this case—by changing command potential) and record the data for further analysis. Subsequently, the channel characteristics (such as its conductance) may be extracted from the data file using dedicated or third party software and plotted against membrane potential to produce a current/voltage (I/V)-curve. If the curve goes through zero point (i.e., zero current at zero potential but not zero in the vicinity of 0 mV), the channel is not clearly voltage-gated. Otherwise, the channel requires a certain voltage threshold to open and is, to some degree, voltage-gated. In cases where the I/V-curve is not linear, the channel is considered to be a rectifier, i.e., membrane potential modulates its conductance.</p>
</div>
<div class="fn p" id="FN29">
<sup>29</sup><p class="display-inline">If the current saturates (does not increase with tension after certain tension value is reached) one may wish to evaluate the channel’s midpoint pressure, the pressure at which a half of the channel population is open, or its threshold tension, the tension at which the very first channel of the population opens. These are distinctive features of a mechanosensitive channel and generally speaking, characterize its tension sensitivity. It should be noted though, that these are statistical values, and several measurements under the same conditions (including membrane patch size) must be carried out in order to obtain an average value and standard deviation. Of course, patches containing more channels produce more reliable values. In our hands, oocytes can survive pressures as high as −160 mmHg with pipettes with BN 4–5, which is enough to reach saturation for MscS [<a href="#R17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R17">17</a>]. However, the oocyte membrane seems to be more fragile than the membranes of giant <em>E. coli</em> spheroplasts previously used for characterization of MscS [<a href="#R14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R14">14</a>].</p>
</div>
<div class="fn p" id="FN30">
<sup>30</sup><p class="display-inline">As channels may be distributed over the oocyte surface nonuniformly, several patches may be necessary to find them. Usually 3–4 patches per oocyte are enough to make sure that the oocyte is or is not expressing MSCs. Patching 4–5 oocytes from 2 batches isolated from different frogs should give reliable data on a particular channel’s presence and properties. For beginning stages of an investigation, we can also recommend expressing a previously characterized channel (like MscS, <em>see</em> ref. <a href="#R17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="R17">17</a>) for an introduction to the method and a positive control. Co-expression of the channel of interest with MscS as an internal standard may be also very useful.</p>
</div>
<div class="fn p" id="FN31">
<sup>31</sup><p class="display-inline">Recorded traces may be later processed using either dedicated software (e.g., Clampfit, Molecular Devices) or a number of third party suites. Typical procedures include data filtering at 0.5–2 kHz, which reduces noise level but may eliminate fast events, and slight baseline correction. After that one may proceed to a statistical analysis of the events in a trace, including single channel current amplitudes, threshold and midpoint pressures, dwell time in open or closed states, and so on. Typically dedicated software suites provide a wide set of tools for initial data analysis. For more specific aims one may be interested in using such software suites as Origin (<a href="http://originlab.com" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">http://originlab.com</a>), QtiPlot (<a href="http://www.qtiplot.ro" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">http://www.qtiplot.ro</a>) or QuB (<a href="http://www.qub.buffalo.edu" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">http://www.qub.buffalo.edu</a>).</p>
</div>
