</div></section></section><section aria-label="Article content"><section class="body main-article-body"><section class="abstract" id="abstract1"><hr class="headless">
<p><em>In the epidermis of Arabidopsis leaves, flavonols specifically accumulate in
guard cells with enhanced synthesis in the presence of ethylene, where they lower
the levels of reactive oxygen species and reduce the rate of stomatal
closure</em>.</p></section><section class="abstract" id="abstract2"><h2>Abstract</h2>
<p>Guard cell swelling controls the aperture of stomata, pores that facilitate gas
exchange and water loss from leaves. The hormone abscisic acid (<a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a>) has a central role in regulation of stomatal closure
through synthesis of second messengers, which include reactive oxygen species (<a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a>). <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> accumulation must be minimized by antioxidants to keep concentrations
from reaching damaging levels within the cell. Flavonols are plant metabolites that
have been implicated as antioxidants; however, their antioxidant activity in planta
has been debated. Flavonols accumulate in guard cells of <em>Arabidopsis
thaliana,</em> but not surrounding pavement cells, as visualized with a
flavonol-specific dye. The expression of a reporter driven by the promoter of
<em>CHALCONE SYNTHASE</em>, a gene encoding a flavonol biosynthetic
enzyme, in guard cells, but not pavement cells, suggests guard cell-specific
flavonoid synthesis. Increased levels of <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a>
were detected using a fluorescent <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> sensor
in guard cells of <em>transparent testa4-2</em>, which has a null mutation in
<em>CHALCONE SYNTHASE</em> and therefore synthesizes no flavonol
antioxidants. Guard cells of <em>transparent testa4-2</em> show more rapid
<a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a>-induced closure than the wild type,
suggesting that flavonols may dampen the <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a>-dependent <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> burst that drives
stomatal closing. The levels of flavonols are positively regulated in guard cells by
ethylene treatment in the wild type, but not in the
<em>ethylene-insensitive2-5</em> mutant. In addition, in both
<em>ethylene-overproducing1</em> and ethylene-treated wild-type plants,
elevated flavonols lead to decreasing <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> and
slower <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a>-mediated stomatal closure. These
results are consistent with flavonols suppressing <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> accumulation and decreasing the rate of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a>-dependent stomatal closure, with ethylene-induced increases
in guard cell flavonols modulating these responses.</p></section><hr class="headless">
<p>Guard cells use an extensive signal transduction pathway to regulate the aperture of
stomata, pores on the surface of leaves that facilitate gas exchange and water loss (<a href="#bib49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib49">Roelfsema and Hedrich, 2005</a>; <a href="#bib27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib27">Joshi-Saha et al., 2011</a>). CO<sub>2</sub>, a necessary reactant for
photosynthesis, enters leaves through stomata, whereas water can leave through these same
pores via transpiration. Excess water loss can lead to dehydration of the plant when there
is excess heat or drought. Guard cells therefore must modulate the stomatal aperture in
response to the changing environment (<a href="#bib23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib23">Hirayama and
Shinozaki, 2007</a>). In response to stimuli that close stomata, such as drought
stress, the hormone abscisic acid (<a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a>) is
increased in the cytosol of guard cells (<a href="#bib65" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib65">Zeevaart,
1980</a>; <a href="#bib23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib23">Hirayama and Shinozaki (2007)</a>).
<a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a> induces ion movements across the cell
membrane expelling K<sup>+</sup> and Cl<sup>−</sup> ions through ion efflux
channels. The decreased internal solute concentration drives water out of the cell via
osmosis (<a href="#bib55" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib55">Vahisalu et al., 2008</a>). The loss of water
from guard cells decreases the volume of the cell, with the resulting loss of turgor
closing the stoma (<a href="#bib27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib27">Joshi-Saha et al., 2011</a>).
Additional understanding of this elegant signaling pathway will provide insight into how
plant growth and development is modulated by changes in soil moisture, temperature, and
light levels.</p>
<p>Reactive oxygen species (<a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a>) are one important
class of signaling molecules that has been implicated in guard cell signaling (<a href="#bib18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib18">Guan et al., 2000</a>; <a href="#bib66" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib66">Zhang et al., 2001</a>; <a href="#bib25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib25">Jiang and Zhang,
2002</a>, <a href="#bib26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib26">2003</a>). The well-documented role of
<a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> in causing oxidative stress is now joined
with growing evidence that <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> may play integral
roles as secondary messengers in signal transduction pathways (<a href="#bib60" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib60">Wood et al., 2003</a>; <a href="#bib47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib47">Rhee,
2006</a>; <a href="#bib35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib35">Mittler et al., 2011</a>; <a href="#bib37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib37">Munné-Bosch et al., 2013</a>). At high
concentrations, <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a>, such as hydrogen peroxide
(<a href="#def3" class="usa-link">H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub></a>), can cause
oxidative damage to DNA and proteins that are integral to normal cellular processes (<a href="#bib4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib4">Asada, 2006</a>; <a href="#bib56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib56">Van
Breusegem and Dat, 2006</a>); however, at low levels, <a href="#def3" class="usa-link">H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub></a> is an effective signaling molecule in guard
cells that can induce stomatal closure (<a href="#bib43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib43">Pei et al.,
2000</a>; <a href="#bib38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib38">Murata et al., 2001</a>; <a href="#bib66" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib66">Zhang et al., 2001</a>; <a href="#bib28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib28">Kwak et al., 2003</a>). Elevated <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a> in
guard cells triggers <a href="#def3" class="usa-link">H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub></a>
production through activation of NADPH or respiratory burst oxidase enzymes located on the
plasma membrane (<a href="#bib39" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib39">Mustilli et al., 2002</a>; <a href="#bib63" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib63">Yoshida et al., 2002</a>) and mutants in
<em>Arabidopsis thaliana respiratory burst oxidase D</em>
(<em>AtrbohD</em>) and <em>AtrbohF</em>, show impaired <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a>-regulated stomatal closure (<a href="#bib28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib28">Kwak et al., 2003</a>).</p>
<p>The ability of signaling-regulated synthesis of <a href="#def3" class="usa-link">H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub></a> to alter the activity of specific target proteins
has been shown in mammalian systems (<a href="#bib47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib47">Rhee, 2006</a>).
This oxidant, generated from superoxide O<sub>2</sub><sup>.−</sup> molecules, can
modulate the activity of phosphatases and kinases, causing dynamic changes in signal
transduction pathways (<a href="#bib45" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib45">Poole et al., 2004</a>).
Specifically, <a href="#def3" class="usa-link">H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub></a> can
interact with specific Cys residues, transforming the functional group of the Cys into
sulfenic acid, altering formation of disulfide bonds between two separate proteins or
between nearby Cys residues of the same protein, in a transient, reversible fashion (<a href="#bib45" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib45">Poole et al., 2004</a>; <a href="#bib46" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib46">Poole and Nelson, 2008</a>). In guard cells, this <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> burst is believed to target calcium channels to promote the influx of
calcium across the plasma membrane and the release of calcium from internal stores (<a href="#bib33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib33">McAinsh et al., 1996</a>; <a href="#bib1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib1">Allen et al., 2000</a>; <a href="#bib12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib12">Cho et al.,
2009</a>). Increased cytosolic calcium levels induce activation of anion efflux
channels located on the plasma membrane (<a href="#bib20" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib20">Hedrich et al.,
1990</a>; <a href="#bib52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib52">Schroeder and Hagiwara, 1990</a>;
<a href="#bib11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib11">Chen et al., 2010</a>; <a href="#bib58" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib58">Wang et al., 2013</a>), triggering stomatal closure. The mechanism behind
<a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> activation of Ca<sup>2+</sup> channels in
mammalian systems has been well studied (<a href="#bib46" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib46">Poole and
Nelson, 2008</a>); however, less is known about this mechanism in plants.</p>
<p>When maintained at manageable levels, <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> are
efficient and reversible signaling molecules employed by cells to regulate the activity of
enzymes and transcription factors; however, if levels of these molecules become excessively
elevated, they can cause oxidative stress (<a href="#bib21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib21">Hernández et al., 2009</a>; <a href="#bib44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib44">Pollastri and
Tattini, 2011</a>). Therefore, mechanisms that keep their levels from becoming too
high and allow their accumulation to be transient are important features of <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> signaling. <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> homeostasis is tightly regulated in plants through the production of a
variety of enzymatic and small molecule antioxidants (<a href="#bib34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib34">Mittler et al., 2004</a>). Flavonols are plant metabolites that may function as
antioxidants in plants and in animals that consume them (<a href="#bib21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib21">Hernández et al., 2009</a>). Although their antioxidant activity in planta
has been debated, previous studies have shown that the antioxidant capacity for flavonols
in vitro is greater than that of vitamin C or E, two well-documented in planta antioxidants
(<a href="#bib48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib48">Rice-Evans et al., 1997</a>). Guard cells provide
an elegant system to test the antioxidant ability of flavonols, owing to the
well-documented function of <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> as a second
messenger in this cell type (<a href="#bib43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib43">Pei et al., 2000</a>;
<a href="#bib38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib38">Murata et al., 2001</a>; <a href="#bib28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib28">Kwak et al., 2003</a>; <a href="#bib12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib12">Cho et al.,
2009</a>).</p>
<p>This study examined the accumulation of flavonols in guard cells and the role of flavonol
accumulation in controlling the levels of <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a>. A
flavonol-specific dye and a reporter construct driven by a promoter from a gene encoding a
flavonol biosynthetic enzyme revealed the localized accumulation of flavonols and the
expression of this gene that drives their synthesis in guard cells, but not in surrounding
pavement cells. Mutants that make no flavonols are used to demonstrate the specificity of
the flavonol-specific dye, and to show the effect of flavonols on <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> status and stomatal aperture. Finally, the induction of flavonol
synthesis in guard cells in response to ethylene was examined, as well as the effect of
these regulated changes in flavonol concentration on stomatal aperture. These results
indicate that flavonols suppress <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> levels within
guard cell modulating stomatal aperture and with changing environmental conditions
modulating flavonol synthesis and guard cell signaling.</p>
<section id="s1"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">RESULTS</h2>
<section id="s2"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Flavonols and Their Biosynthetic Machinery Are Found in Guard Cells</h3>
<p>To determine whether flavonols accumulate in guard cells, we used diphenylboric acid
2-aminoethyl ester (<a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a>) dye to detect
flavonols. The specificity of <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a>
fluorescence was demonstrated in Arabidopsis (<em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em>)
roots using mutants in flavonol synthesis, with no signal observed in the
<em>transparent testa4-2</em> (<em>tt4-2</em>) mutant that makes
no flavonoids (<a href="#bib30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib30">Lewis et al., 2011</a>) because
of a mutation in the gene that encodes chalcone synthase (<a href="#def5" class="usa-link">CHS</a>), the first step in the flavonoid biosynthesis pathway. The
<a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a> fluorescence of the surface of an
Arabidopsis leaf, with and without a differential interference contrast (<a href="#def6" class="usa-link">DIC</a>) overlay of pavement cells, illustrated
localized accumulation of flavonols in guard cells with no signal in surrounding
pavement cells (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1, A and B</a>). This pattern
was observed in more than 50 leaves. We also asked whether the flavonol localization
was restricted to guard cells as a result of flavonol biosynthesis in those cells.
Epidermal leaf peels of transgenic plants with a <em>CHS</em> promoter
driving a GUS reporter were visualized by bright-field microscopy. GUS product
accumulated in the epidermal guard cells (<a href="#fig1" class="usa-link">Fig.
1C</a>), with no GUS product accumulation in surrounding pavement cells. This
reporter is expressed in additional cell layers in cross sections of whole leaves
(<a href="#bib13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib13">Chory and Peto, 1990</a>). These results
were consistent with tissue-specific signals, including transcriptional controls of
flavonol synthesis, restricting flavonol accumulation to guard cells.</p>

<p>To analyze the functional significance of flavonol accumulation in guard cells, we
compared <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a>-dependent stomatal closure in
wild-type and <em>tt4-2</em> guard cells. Because <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a> triggers a <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> burst
in guard cells and flavonols reduce <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a>
concentrations, we expected flavonol concentrations in guard cells to be inversely
proportional to the rates of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a>-induced
stomatal closure. Leaves were excised and incubated with 20 μ<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span>
<a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a> for 0, 45, 90, or 180 min. At the
indicated times after treatment, epidermal peels were generated and <a href="#def6" class="usa-link">DIC</a> images were captured and used for
measurement of stomatal aperture. Apertures were quantified in 30 stomata for three
separate experiments (<a href="#fig4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4</a>). Although
<em>tt4</em> and the wild type showed similar aperture in opening
solution, the initial <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a>-induced stomatal
closure response was greater in <em>tt4</em> guard cells than the control,
with all aperture values significantly different than the wild type
(<em>P</em> &lt; 0.05). The apertures of stomata were also quantified in
the absence of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a> under identical conditions
over the same time course in these two genotypes. The guard cells remained open under
these conditions and there were no detectable differences between the wild type and
<em>tt4-2</em> (<a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.113.233528/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Fig. S2</a>). We also examined the effect of 3 h of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a> treatment on <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a> fluorescence in guard cells, mirroring the time course of
<a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a> in the stomatal closure assays to ask
if <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a> altered flavonol synthesis. The <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a> signal was not altered by <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a> during this 3-h treatment (<a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.113.233528/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Fig. S3</a>). These results were consistent with the absence
of an antioxidant in <em>tt4-2</em> guard cells that leads to an increase in
signaling via <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a>.</p>

<p>Because elevated ethylene levels were previously shown to increase flavonol
accumulation in Arabidopsis roots (<a href="#bib30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib30">Lewis et al.,

<em>ethylene-overproducing1</em> (<em>eto1</em>) mutant (<a href="#bib9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib9">Chae et al., 2003</a>) and enhanced flavonol
accumulation was observed in the guard cells (<a href="#fig2" class="usa-link">Fig.
2</a>). The <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a> fluorescence intensity

<a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a> fluorescence in the cytosol and a

with the wild type (<a href="#fig3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3</a>; <em>P</em>
&lt; 0.001).</p>
<p>To further examine the effect of ethylene on flavonol synthesis in guard cells, we
treated plants with exogenous ethylene. The wild type and the
<em>ethylene-insensitive2-5</em> (<em>ein2-5</em>) mutant, an
ethylene signaling mutant with nearly complete ethylene insensitivity (<a href="#bib2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib2">Alonso et al., 1999</a>), were treated with 5
μL/L ethylene gas for 24 h, and then <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a> fluorescence in the guard cells was imaged (<a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">Fig. 5</a>). The <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a>
fluorescence was quantified in both the nucleus and cytosol, as described above, for
30 stomata per treatment in three separate experiments, and the average signal
relative to the signal in the cytosol of untreated <a href="#def8" class="usa-link">Col-0</a> was calculated (<a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">Fig. 5</a>).
Similar to <em>eto1,</em> the guard cells of ethylene-treated wild-type
plants had enhanced <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a> fluorescence in the
cytosol and nuclei compared with an untreated control (<em>P</em> &lt; 0.01).
A similar magnitude increase in <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a>
fluorescence was also evident after 3 h of ethylene treatment, as shown in <a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.113.233528/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Figure S3A</a>. Guard cells of <em>ein2-5</em> had
significantly lower <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a> fluorescence than
<a href="#def8" class="usa-link">Col-0</a> in both the cytosol and nucleus in
the absence of treatment (<em>P</em> &lt; 0.02) and showed no increase in
<a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a> fluorescence after ethylene treatment
(<a href="#fig5" class="usa-link">Fig. 5</a>).</p>

<a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> Accumulate in Guard Cells and Are
Affected by Flavonol Concentrations</h3>
<p>The concentrations of <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> are highly
regulated in guard cells and are an essential feature of rapid stomatal closure in
response to <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a> (<a href="#bib43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib43">Pei et al., 2000</a>; <a href="#bib28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib28">Kwak et
al., 2003</a>). <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> concentration
within guard cells was examined to determine whether flavonols are acting as
antioxidants in vivo. To visualize <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a>
accumulation in Arabidopsis leaf tissue, we used 2',7'
dihydrodichlorofluorescein diacetate (<a href="#def10" class="usa-link">H2DCF-DA</a>), a general <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a>
fluorescent sensor (<a href="#bib19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib19">Halliwell and Whiteman,
2004</a>). In the past, this fluorescent probe has been imaged with UV light to
visualize <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> in guard cells (<a href="#bib28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib28">Kwak et al., 2003</a>); however, we excited with a
488-nm laser, a wavelength that is more frequently used when imaging this probe
(<a href="#bib19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib19">Halliwell and Whiteman, 2004</a>). <a href="#def10" class="usa-link">H2DCF-DA</a> is taken up by cells, in which
internal cellular esterases cleave off the diacetate functional group, preventing
2',7'-dichlorofluorescein (DCF) from diffusing outside of the cell.
Upon oxidation by <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a>, DCF becomes
fluorescent. To minimize any dye uptake difference or light-induced fluorescence
increases that can occur with this dye (<a href="#bib38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib38">Murata et
al., 2001</a>), all incubation and imaging times and settings were carefully
maintained within each experimental comparison.</p>
<p>We used spectral separation through confocal microscopy to separate out chlorophyll
autofluorescence from DCF fluorescence in guard cells in epidermal leaf peels of the
wild type, <em>tt4-2</em>, and <em>eto1</em> (<a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">Fig. 6</a>). In all three genotypes, DCF fluorescence was at very
low levels in pavement cells, but accumulated in guard cells in both nuclei and the
cytosol, with greater fluorescence in the nuclei, mirroring the tissue and
subcellular accumulation of flavonols. The levels of DCF fluorescence in the cytosol
and nucleus of the wild type, <em>tt4</em>, and <em>eto1</em> were
quantified in 90 guard cells and values were normalized relative to the levels in the
cytosol of <a href="#def8" class="usa-link">Col-0</a> (<a href="#fig6" class="usa-link">Fig. 6B</a>). In the <em>tt4</em> mutant, which does not
synthesize flavonols, DCF fluorescence intensity was greater in both the cytosol and
the nucleus of guard cells compared with the wild type. Quantification of DCF
fluorescence indicated that there is a 2- and 2.5-fold increase in
<em>tt4-2</em> in the cytosol and nucleus, respectively, compared with
wild-type guard cells.</p>

<p>The effect of 24 h of ethylene treatment of the wild type and <em>tt4</em>
and in the untreated <em>eto1</em> mutant was examined using the same
above-described stomatal closure assay. The closure at time 0, 45, and 90 min after
<a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a> treatment for 90 stomata from three
experiments were quantified in these three genotypes (<a href="#fig7" class="usa-link">Fig. 7</a>). After incubation in the opening solution for 3 h, all stomata
were equivalently open and in the absence of added <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a>, remained equivalently open (<a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.113.233528/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Fig. S2</a>). Ethylene-treated wild-type and
<em>eto1</em> guard cells showed decreased closure in response to <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a>, whereas untreated <em>tt4</em>
shows the most stomatal closure at each time point. When wild-type plants were
treated with ethylene for 3 h (<a href="http://www.plantphysiol.org/cgi/content/full/pp.113.233528/DC1" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Supplemental Fig. S3C</a>), similar results were observed. We expected
that <em>tt4</em> would show no response to ethylene treatment, if the only
effect of ethylene is on flavonoid synthesis; however, we found that it does show a
reduced stomatal closure in the presence of 24 h of ethylene treatment, but not 3 h.
The stomatal aperture is still smaller in <em>tt4-2</em> with ethylene
treatment than in Col-0 with ethylene, consistent with a role for ethylene-mediated
flavonol synthesis in reducing the rate of stomatal closure through modulation of
<a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> signals. The difference in stomatal
aperture in <em>tt4</em> in the absence and presence of 24 h of ethylene
treatment suggests the presence of a separate flavonol-independent pathway by which
ethylene treatment facilitates stomatal closure. The absence of this effect at 3 h
suggests that this may well be a secondary effect of extended ethylene
treatments.</p>

<p>Guard cells control the aperture of stomatal pores in response to environmental
fluctuations, including variations in light intensity, moisture, and carbon dioxide
levels (<a href="#bib22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib22">Hetherington and Woodward, 2003</a>). In
response to environmental stress, the plant hormone <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a> signals stomatal closure (<a href="#bib65" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib65">Zeevaart,
1980</a>; <a href="#bib23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib23">Hirayama and Shinozaki, 2007</a>;
<a href="#bib12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib12">Cho et al., 2009</a>; <a href="#bib27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib27">Joshi-Saha et al., 2011</a>). This signaling pathway employs <a href="#def3" class="usa-link">H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub></a> as a <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> second messenger (<a href="#bib33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib33">McAinsh et al., 1996</a>; <a href="#bib38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib38">Murata et
al., 2001</a>; <a href="#bib66" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib66">Zhang et al., 2001</a>; <a href="#bib28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib28">Kwak et al., 2003</a>). During signaling of stomatal
closure, <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> must reach a certain threshold to
oxidize their targets (<a href="#bib28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib28">Kwak et al., 2003</a>; <a href="#bib12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib12">Cho et al., 2009</a>), but must not reach levels that
will cause oxidative damage (<a href="#bib37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib37">Munné-Bosch et
al., 2013</a>). To prevent oxidative damage, <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> signaling must be tightly regulated by small molecule and/or protein
antioxidants (<a href="#bib14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib14">Conklin et al., 1996</a>; <a href="#bib3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib3">Apel and Hirt, 2004</a>), with antioxidants including
glutathione and flavonoids. Recent reports demonstrated the altered redox state and
altered stomatal aperture in mutants defective in glutathione synthesis (<a href="#bib40" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib40">Okuma et al., 2011</a>; <a href="#bib36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib36">Munemasa et al., 2013</a>). In this study, we tested the hypothesis
that flavonols act as antioxidants that reduce <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> concentrations in guard cells, thus modulating stomatal closure.</p>
<p>We examined where and how flavonols accumulated in the epidermal layers of leaves in
order to better understand their role in cell signaling. Flavonol accumulation was
visualized through confocal microscopy, using a flavonol-specific fluorescent dye, <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a>. In the wild type, <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a> fluorescence was localized to guard cells but was absent in
surrounding pavement cells, whereas it was not detected in any cell types in the
flavonoid-deficient <em>tt4-2</em> mutant. Localized flavonol accumulation in
guard cells could be a result of localized synthesis or selective import of these
molecules into the cells. To examine whether flavonol biosynthetic enzymes are
synthesized in guard cells in planta, a <em>CHS</em>:<em>GUS</em>
transgenic line was utilized. <em>CHS</em> promoter-driven GUS product
accumulated specifically in guard cells and not in surrounding epidermal pavement cells,
which suggests that production of <a href="#def5" class="usa-link">CHS</a>, a key
flavonol biosynthesis enzyme, is localized to Arabidopsis guard cells. This result is
consistent with previous research that shows the presence of transcripts encoding
enzymes of flavonol biosynthesis in guard cell protoplasts, including chalcone
isomerase, flavanone 3-hydroxylase, flavonol synthase1, and flavonoid
3′hydroxylase (<a href="#bib42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib42">Pandey et al., 2010</a>).
This result extends previous study in two ways. First, it minimized the damage to cells
that is the result of creating protoplasts, which may increase the expression of
flavonoid biosynthesis genes in cells where they are not normally produced (<a href="#bib16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib16">Dixon and Paiva, 1995</a>; <a href="#bib59" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib59">Winkel-Shirley, 2002</a>). Second, the available microarray data do
not have a pavement cell transcriptome for comparison (<a href="#bib42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib42">Pandey et al., 2010</a>). Using very different experimental methods in
<em>Vicia faba</em>, flavonols were previously reported to accumulate in
guard cells and surrounding epidermal cells (<a href="#bib51" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib51">Schnabl
et al., 1986</a>; <a href="#bib53" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib53">Takahama, 1988</a>). Both
reports used spectrophotometric imaging of epidermal peels, which limits the resolution
of flavonols accumulation in individual cells. Using the highly localized accumulation
of <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a> fluorescence, our results demonstrate
that there is a guard cell-specific flavonol accumulation profile in Arabidopsis.</p>
<p>Because flavonols accumulate specifically in guard cells, we asked whether these
molecules function as antioxidants to reduce <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a>
concentrations. Flavonols have been shown to have antioxidant activity in vitro;
however, their antioxidant activity in planta has been debated (<a href="#bib48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib48">Rice-Evans et al., 1997</a>; <a href="#bib21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib21">Hernández et al., 2009</a>; <a href="#bib44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib44">Pollastri
and Tattini, 2011</a>). Because <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> has
been implicated as a second messenger in guard cell signaling (<a href="#bib33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib33">McAinsh et al., 1996</a>; <a href="#bib38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib38">Murata et
al., 2001</a>; <a href="#bib66" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib66">Zhang et al., 2001</a>; <a href="#bib28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib28">Kwak et al., 2003</a>), we asked whether the presence
of flavonols affected the <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> accumulation in
guard cells. To visualize <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> accumulation, we
used <a href="#def10" class="usa-link">DCFH-DA</a>, a general <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> sensor, and utilized spectral separation to remove chlorophyll
autofluorescence. DCF fluorescence was localized to guard cells, similar to the <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a> fluorescence pattern, indicating that both
flavonols and <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> accumulate specifically in
this cell type, but not in surrounding pavement cells. In addition, we saw increased DCF
fluorescence in <em>tt4-2</em> mutants that synthesize no flavonols, consistent
with the absence of flavonols resulting in increased <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> concentrations, which is also consistent with an antioxidant activity for
flavonols in vitro.</p>
<p><a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a> fluorescence were at higher levels in the
nuclei than the cytosol in all genotypes and treatments. This observation is consistent
with characteristics of other plant species that showed flavonoid and <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> accumulation in the nuclei (<a href="#bib17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib17">Feucht et al., 2004</a>; <a href="#bib32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib32">Mazars et al., 2010</a>; <a href="#bib30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib30">Lewis et
al., 2011</a>). Immunolocalization with antibodies against <a href="#def5" class="usa-link">CHS</a> and chalcone isomerase suggests that the enzymes of flavonoid
synthesis are localized to the nucleus in Arabidopsis roots (<a href="#bib50" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib50">Saslowsky et al., 2005</a>). The nuclear localization of flavonols is
consistent with the notion that <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> can regulate
gene expression through interaction with redox-sensitive transcription factors (<a href="#def11" class="usa-link">TF</a>s) in plants (<a href="#bib61" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib61">Wu et al., 2012</a>), such as the well-established redox-sensitive <a href="#def11" class="usa-link">TF</a> described in bacteria and mammals (<a href="#bib5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib5">Aslund et al., 1999</a>; <a href="#bib6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib6">Biswas et al., 2006</a>). Flavonols are a likely candidate to modulate
the activity of redox-sensitive <a href="#def11" class="usa-link">TF</a>s, because of
their increased accumulation in the nuclei; however, it is unlikely that they are the
only antioxidant present in this organelle. Flavonols have been shown to chelate metal
ions, such as Fe<sup>3+</sup>, which catalyze the production of hydroxyl radicals from
<a href="#def3" class="usa-link">H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub></a> (<a href="#bib21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib21">Hernández et al., 2009</a>). Thus, flavonol
accumulation in the nucleus would contribute to a decreased redox potential in the
nucleus, potentially decreasing the number of <a href="#def11" class="usa-link">TF</a> oxidation events. Combined with our data suggesting an in planta
antioxidant ability of flavonols, we hypothesize that flavonol accumulation in the
nuclei may modulate the activity of redox-sensitive <a href="#def11" class="usa-link">TF</a>s that affect gene expression in addition to preventing oxidative damage to
DNA, although the time scale of transcriptional effects may be beyond the rapid
regulation of guard cell aperture.</p>
<p>Because stomatal apertures are environmentally regulated, we hypothesized that
regulation of flavonol synthesis may be a way for plants to modulate the physiology of
guard cells. Previous studies have shown that ethylene increases flavonol accumulation
in Arabidopsis roots (<a href="#bib8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib8">Buer et al., 2007</a>; <a href="#bib30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib30">Lewis et al., 2011</a>), and we asked whether this
hormone may also increase flavonol accumulation in guard cells. Elevated levels of
ethylene through endogenous overproduction in the <em>eto1</em> mutant and
exogenous application of ethylene to wild-type leaves increased flavonol accumulation in
this cell type, as visualized by localized <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a>
fluorescence. We observed robust increases in <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a> fluorescence at both 3 and 24 h after treatment with ethylene gas. This
ethylene-enhanced flavonol accumulation pattern requires a functional ethylene signaling
pathway, because the ethylene-induced <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a>
fluorescence increases were lost in the <em>ein2</em> mutant. A previous study
showed that ethylene-induced flavonoid increases detected by <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a> can also be detected by <a href="#def9" class="usa-link">LC-MS</a> (<a href="#bib30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib30">Lewis et al., 2011</a>). We
therefore verified these flavonols increases in leaves using <a href="#def9" class="usa-link">LC-MS</a> and detected approximately 5-fold increases in quercetin and
a smaller, but still statistically significant, increase in kaempferol in whole leaves
treated with ethylene under similar conditions.</p>
<p>We also observed decreased DCF fluorescence in the wild type and <em>tt4</em>
treated with ethylene for 3 or 24 h as well as in <em>eto1</em> guard cells
compared with the wild type, suggesting that the increase in flavonols decreases <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> concentrations. In addition, ethylene-treated
wild-type guard cells and <em>eto1</em> exhibited a decreased rate of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a>-induced stomatal closure, a result that is
consistent with flavonols acting as antioxidants reducing the <a href="#def3" class="usa-link">H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub></a> burst that drives stomatal closure.</p>
<p>Ethylene has been shown to promote both stomatal closure in <em>Arachis
hypogaea</em> and Arabidopsis (<a href="#bib41" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib41">Pallas and Kays,
1982</a>; <a href="#bib15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib15">Desikan et al., 2006</a>) and
stomatal opening in <em>Dianthus caryophyllus</em>, <em>Solanum
lycopersicum</em>, <em>V. faba,</em> and Arabidopsis (<a href="#bib31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib31">Madhavan et al., 1983</a>; <a href="#bib29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib29">Levitt et al., 1987</a>; <a href="#bib54" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib54">Tanaka et
al., 2005</a>). Applied ethylene gas or its precursor,
1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylic acid, was shown to induce stomatal closure through
elevated <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> production generated by AtRBOHF, an
NADPH oxidase (<a href="#bib15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib15">Desikan et al., 2006</a>). In our
study, a previously reported <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> burst that was
observed upon ethylene treatment of wild-type guard cells (<a href="#bib15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib15">Desikan et al., 2006</a>) was masked by increased flavonol
accumulation, but we observed that a 24-h ethylene treatment of <em>tt4</em> led
to elevated <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> (although this increase was not
detected after 3 h of treatment), consistent with the absence of antioxidants. Other
researchers report that treatment of wild-type plants with ethylene or
1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylic acid or endogenous ethylene overproduction in
<em>eto1</em> inhibit stomatal closure in the presence of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a> in Arabidopsis (<a href="#bib54" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib54">Tanaka et al., 2005</a>) and in wheat (<em>Triticum
durum</em>; <a href="#bib10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib10">Chen et al., 2013</a>). These
findings are mirrored in our results, with decreased <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> and stomatal closure in response to ethylene treatment or the
<em>eto1</em> mutation. We predicted that ethylene-treated
<em>tt4</em> might be insensitive to the effect of ethylene on stomatal
aperture, because there will be no change in flavonoid antioxidants. We found that in
both the wild type and <em>tt4</em>, ethylene reduced the rate of stomatal
closure, consistent with a flavonoid- and <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-independent ethylene regulation of this process, although the
<em>tt4</em> mutant has a greater rate in both cases consistent with a second
<a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-dependent mechanism. A <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-independent pathway for <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a>-induced stomatal closure was previously suggested (<a href="#bib28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib28">Kwak et al., 2003</a>). <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a> treatment of <em>atrbohD/F</em> double mutant guard
cells resulted in no change in <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a>
concentrations, although cytosolic Ca<sup>2+</sup> elevations were observed in nearly
one-half of all guard cells (<a href="#bib28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib28">Kwak et al.,
2003</a>). We hypothesize that a 24-h ethylene treatment may inhibit <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a>-independent stomatal closure in the absence of
flavonols, which would explain the delayed closure that is observed in ethylene-treated
<em>tt4</em>. Our results suggest that ethylene induces flavonol
accumulation, which reduces both the concentration of <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> and the rate of <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a>-induced
stomatal closure.</p>
<p>We have constructed a model to synthesize these data, which is shown in <a href="#fig8" class="usa-link">Figure 8</a>. This model outlines the <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a> signaling pathway in which <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a> activates respiratory burst oxidases increasing
the levels of <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a>, which act as signaling
molecules to induce stomatal closure. Flavonoids act as antioxidants reducing the levels
of <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a>, which decrease the rate of stomatal
closure. Elevated levels of ethylene, both through elevated synthesis in
<em>eto1</em> or by treatment with exogenous ethylene, increase flavonol
accumulation and decrease <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a>, resulting in a
reduced rate of stomatal closure. When a flavonoid-deficient mutant is treated with
ethylene, we find that <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> levels increase after
24 h of treatment, but not after 3 h of treatment. These findings suggest that extended
ethylene elevated <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> accumulation (<a href="#fig8" class="usa-link">Fig. 8</a>, dotted line), but that the elevated ethylene
after <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> treatment in the wild type more than
compensates by increasing levels of flavonol antioxidants. We predicted that
<em>tt4</em> would be insensitive to the effect of ethylene on stomatal
closure, whereas we found that this treatment leads to a reduced rate of closure, which
is evident at 24 h, but not 3 h of ethylene treatment (<a href="#fig8" class="usa-link">Fig. 8</a>, dotted line). We hypothesize that ethylene may enhance the synthesis
of a second signaling molecule that may act to alter stomatal closure through a separate
mechanism in the presence of extended ethylene treatment. Yet the absence of ethylene
effects on <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> and stomatal aperture after 3-h
treatments suggests that the primary effects of ethylene are on synthesis of flavonol
antioxidants that suppress <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> levels.</p>

of ethylene treatment did not. The effects of ethylene observed at 24 h are
indicated with dashed lines. Arrows represent positive effects and bars represent
negative effects.</p></figcaption></figure></section><section id="s8"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">CONCLUSION</h2>
<p>Guard cell function is integral to plant adaptation to a changing environment. Much is
known about the role of <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> as a second
messenger in this cell type (<a href="#bib57" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib57">Wang and Song,
2008</a>); however, the role of antioxidants in guard cells to modulate <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> concentrations has not been well studied. We
observed flavonol and <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> accumulation in guard
cells and showed that the concentrations of flavonols in planta are inversely
proportional to <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> levels, consistent with in
vitro studies of flavonols acting as antioxidants (<a href="#bib48" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib48">Rice-Evans et al., 1997</a>; <a href="#bib21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib21">Hernández
et al., 2009</a>). This study showed that flavonol levels are ethylene regulated
in guard cells. Elevated levels of ethylene in response to exogenous treatment of the
wild type and endogenous synthesis in the <em>eto1</em> mutant increase flavonol
accumulation in guard cells, resulting in decreased <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> levels and decreased rate of stomatal closure. Together these results
indicate that flavonols suppress <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> levels in
guard cells and thereby modulate the dynamics of stomatal aperture.</p></section><section id="s9"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">MATERIALS AND METHODS</h2>
<section id="s10"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Plant Growth and Ethylene Treatment</h3>
<p>Arabidopsis (<em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em>) plants were germinated on
1× Murashige and Skoog medium, pH 5.6, Murashige and Skoog vitamins, and 0.8%
(w/v) agar, buffered with 0.05% (w/v) MES and supplemented with 1% (w/v) Suc. After
vernalization at 4°C for 48 h, plates were placed in a 12-h day growth chamber
under 100 <em>µ</em>mol m<sup>−2</sup> s<sup>−2</sup>
cool-white light. Seedlings were transferred to Metromix 360 soil 7 d after
germination. All experiments were conducted on leaves of plants that were 3 to 4
weeks after germination. This study utilized ecotype Columbia 0 of Arabidopsis (<a href="#def8" class="usa-link">Col-0</a>), <em>tt4-2</em> (<a href="#bib30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib30">Lewis et al., 2011</a>), <em>eto1</em>
(Chae et al., 2013), <em>ein2-5</em> (<a href="#bib2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib2">Alonso
et al., 1999</a><em>)</em>, and a
<em>CHS</em>:<em>GUS</em> reporter (<a href="#bib13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib13">Chory and Peto, 1990</a>).</p>
<p>For ethylene treatments, 4-week-old wild-type, <em>tt4-2</em>, and
<em>ein2-5</em> plants were placed in a clear plastic container that was
sealed with vacuum grease. Ethylene gas was added to achieve a 5 μL/L
concentration of ethylene within the chamber. Control plants were placed in identical
sealed containers without ethylene. After either 3 h or 24 h of incubation, leaves
were excised and used for imaging <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a> or DCF
fluorescence, or in stomatal closure assays.</p></section><section id="s11"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Flavonol Accumulation Using <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a>
</h3>
<p>Individual leaves were excised and submerged in an aqueous solution containing 0.01%
(v/v) Triton X-100 and 2.52 mg/mL<sup>−1</sup>
<a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a> for 2.5 h. Whole leaves were then washed
in deionized water for 1 min and mounted in deionized water between two coverslips. A
Zeiss 710 <a href="#def7" class="usa-link">LSCM</a> was used to excite the leaf
surfaces with 100% maximum laser power at 488 nm and a pinhole yielding a 4-μm
cross section. We optimized <a href="#def7" class="usa-link">LSCM</a> settings to
spectrally separate the fluorescence of flavonol-<a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a> and chlorophyll by capturing the emission spectrum for each compound
in which there is no overlap. <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a>
fluorescence emission was collected between 475 and 619 nm (<a href="#bib30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib30">Lewis et al., 2011</a>). The gain settings were selected to
maximize the total flavonol signal while preventing oversaturation. All micrographs
within each figure were acquired using identical offset, gain, and pinhole settings
using the same detectors. Postimage quantification of <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a> fluorescence intensities was done by placing a region of
interest (<a href="#def12" class="usa-link">ROI</a>) around the nuclei of each
guard cell and around an area in the cytosol with no chloroplasts present. The
average intensity values within each <a href="#def12" class="usa-link">ROI</a>
were recorded and averaged.</p></section><section id="s12"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Histochemical Staining and Imaging of <em>CHS</em>:<em>GUS</em>
Activity</h3>
<p>Whole leaves from the <em>CHS</em>:<em>GUS</em> transgenic line were
excised and submerged in a GUS staining solution (80 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> sodium phosphate
buffer, 0.4 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> potassium ferricyanide, 0.04 m<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> potassium
ferrocyanide, 0.05% [v/v] Triton X-100, 0.8 mg/mL
5-bromo-4-chloro-3-indolyl-β-<span class="font-variant-small-caps">d</span>-glucuronide, pH 7) at 37°C
for 18 h. Leaves were removed from staining solution and washed with 75% (v/v)
ethanol for 5 min. Epidermal strips were prepared by spraying a microscope slide with
a silicone-based medical adhesive (Hollister stock #7730). After 5 min, the basal
epidermis of the leaf was gently pressed into the dried adhesive coat, and the leaf
was gently scraped with a pipette tip until only the fixed epidermis remained (<a href="#bib64" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib64">Young et al., 2006</a>). Epidermal peels were
imaged using <a href="#def6" class="usa-link">DIC</a> imaging with a Zeiss Auxio
Observer D1 microscope with an AxioCam HRc color camera.</p></section><section id="s13"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Quantification of Flavonols by <a href="#def9" class="usa-link">LC-MS</a>
</h3>
<p>Samples were run on a Thermo LTQ Orbitrap XL with an electrospray ionization source,
coupled to a Thermo Accela 1250 pump and autosampler (Thermo Fisher), using a
Security Guard column in line with a Luna 150 × 3 C18 column, both from
Phenomenex. For flavonol analysis, 10 µl of each sample was injected with a
solvent of water:acetonitrile, both containing 0.1% (v/v) formic acid in the
following gradients: 95% to 40% (v/v) water from 0 to 5 min, 40% to 20% (v/v) water
from 5 to 20 min, and 20% to 95% (v/v) water from 20 to 23 min to recondition the
column. MS<sup>2</sup> fragmentation spectra of flavonols were induced using 30-kV
collision-induced dissociation. Spectra of samples were compared to those of
standards and those listed on the MassBank database (<a href="http://www.massbank.jp" class="usa-link usa-link--external" data-ga-action="click_feat_suppl" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">www.massbank.jp</a>). Data were
analyzed by quantifying peak areas using Thermo Xcalibur software, and with
normalization to the internal standard formononetin (Indofine Chemicals). Absolute
quantities of metabolites were found by comparing peak area data to standard curves
created using pure standards of quercetin and kaempferol (Indofine Chemicals).</p></section><section id="s14"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">DCF and Hoechst Staining and Quantification</h3>
<p><a href="#def10" class="usa-link">H2DCF-DA</a> was dissolved in dimethyl sulfoxide
to yield a 50-μ<span class="font-variant-small-caps">m</span> stock. This was diluted in deionized water to yield
a final concentration of 2.5 µM with 2% (v/v) dimethyl sulfoxide. The
epidermis was stained for 15 min with two drops of H2DCF-DA stain and washed with
deionized water. A Zeiss 710 <a href="#def7" class="usa-link">LSCM</a> was used to
excite the leaf surfaces with 1% maximum laser power at 488 nm with a 3.5 digital
gain. Settings were optimized to spectrally separate the fluorescence of DCF -<a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a> and chlorophyll by capturing the emission
spectrum for each compound in which there is no overlap. The DCF signal was collected
between 495 to 527 nm with a pinhole yielding a 4-μm cross section, making
sure to limit excess exposure to the laser that induces <a href="#def2" class="usa-link">ROS</a>. The gain settings were selected to maximize the total DCF
signal while preventing oversaturation. All micrographs within each figure were
acquired using identical offset, gain, and pinhole settings using the same detectors.
DCF fluorescence intensities were measured by placing an <a href="#def12" class="usa-link">ROI</a> around the nuclei of each guard cell and around an area in
the cytosol with no chloroplasts present. The average intensity values within each
<a href="#def12" class="usa-link">ROI</a> was recorded and averaged.</p>
<p>Hoechst imaging was performed on whole leaves that were previously stained with <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a>. After 2.5 h of <a href="#def4" class="usa-link">DPBA</a> staining, leaves were washed in deionized water for 1 min
and transferred to an aqueous solution containing Hoechst at a 1 µg/mL
concentration. The leaf surface was excited with 10% laser power at 405 nm and a
pinhole yielding a 4 μm cross section. We optimized <a href="#def7" class="usa-link">LSCM</a> settings to spectrally separate the fluorescence of the
Hoechst stain with cell wall autofluorescence by capturing the emission spectrum for
each compound in which there is no overlap. Hoechst fluorescence emission was
collected between 420 to 546 nm. The gain settings were selected to maximize the
total Hoechst signal while preventing oversaturation.</p></section><section id="s15"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Stomatal Closure Assay</h3>
<p><a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a>-induced stomatal closure assays were
performed with plants 3 to 4 weeks after germination. Leaves from untreated plants or
plants treated with ethylene gas for 24 h were excised and submerged in an aqueous
opening solution (5 mM KCl, 50 µM CaCl<sub>2</sub>, 10 mM MES buffer, pH 5.6)
and incubated under cool-white light for 3 h. To induce stomatal closure, leaves were
transferred to a similar opening solution with 20 µM <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a> added to induce closure (<a href="#bib24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib24">Jammes et al., 2009</a>). After incubation in the <a href="#def1" class="usa-link">ABA</a> solution under white light (100 µmol
m<sup>−2</sup> s<sup>−2</sup>) for 0, 45, 90, and 180 min, leaf
peels were prepared and imaged using <a href="#def6" class="usa-link">DIC</a>
imaging with a Zeiss Axio Observer D1 microscope with a Hamamatsu 1394 ORCA-ERA
monochromatic camera (<a href="#bib24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="bib24">Jammes et al., 2009</a>).
Apertures were measured using ImageJ software.</p>
<p>Sequence data from this article can be found in the GenBank/EMBL data libraries under
accession numbers. CHS (AT5G13930), <em>ETO1</em> (AT3G51770), and
<em>EIN2</em> (AT5G03280)</p></section><section id="s16"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Supplemental Data</h3>
