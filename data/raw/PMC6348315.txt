</div></section></section><section aria-label="Article content"><section class="body main-article-body"><section class="abstract" id="abstract1"><h2>Abstract</h2>
<p>A feature of the physiological adaptation to spaceflight in <em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em> (<em>Arabidopsis</em>) is the induction of reactive oxygen species (ROS)-associated gene expression. The patterns of ROS-associated gene expression vary among <em>Arabidopsis</em> ecotypes, and the role of ROS signalling in spaceflight acclimation is unknown. What could differences in ROS gene regulation between ecotypes on orbit reveal about physiological adaptation to novel environments? Analyses of ecotype-dependent responses to spaceflight resulted in the elucidation of a previously uncharacterized gene (<em>OMG1</em>) as being ROS-associated. The <em>OMG1</em> 5′ flanking region is an active promoter in cells where ROS activity is commonly observed, such as in pollen tubes, root hairs, and in other tissues upon wounding. qRT-PCR analyses revealed that upon wounding on Earth, OMG1 is an apparent transcriptional regulator of <em>MYB77</em> and <em>GRX480</em>, which are associated with the ROS pathway. Fluorescence-based ROS assays show that OMG1 affects ROS production. Phylogenetic analysis of OMG1 and closely related homologs suggests that OMG1 is a distant, unrecognized member of the CONSTANS-Like protein family, a member that arose via gene duplication early in the angiosperm lineage and subsequently lost its first DNA-binding B-box1 domain. These data illustrate that members of the rapidly evolving COL protein family play a role in regulating ROS pathway functions, and their differential regulation on orbit suggests a role for ROS signalling in spaceflight physiological adaptation.</p>
<section id="kwd-group1" class="kwd-group"><p><strong>Keywords:</strong> <em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em>, B-box domain, CONSTANS-Like, ecotype-specific, microgravity, reactive oxygen species, spaceflight, transcription factor, uncharacterized genes</p></section></section><section class="abstract" id="abstract2"><hr class="headless">
<p>Plants adjust to spaceflight by changing patterns of gene expression. The analysis of how different <em>Arabidopsis</em> plants respond to space resulted in the discovery of a previously uncharacterized gene (<em>OMG1</em>), which was consistently induced in space. This research revealed that OMG1 belongs to the CONSTANS-Like protein family and is involved in maintaining the reactive oxygen species pathway, which appears to be an important feature of the physiological adaptation of plants to the spaceflight environment. It also shows that spaceflight experiments can be used as a platform to discover novel gene function and provide further insights into terrestrial plant biology.</p></section><section id="s2"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Introduction</h2>
<p>The complex networks and signalling pathways that equip plants to cope with environmental challenge developed over evolutionary time in terrestrial environments. Exposing plants to novel environments presents opportunities to understand how plants adjust to conditions outside their evolutionary experience. Spaceflight is one such novel environment, and understanding how plants function outside of Earth’s boundaries is also integral to space exploration. Plants respond to spaceflight in ways that are dependent on species, ecotype, genetics and even organs (<a href="#CIT0009" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0009">Cowles <em>et al.</em> 1984</a>; <a href="#CIT0025" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0025">Hoson <em>et al.</em> 2002</a>; <a href="#CIT0079" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0079">Soga <em>et al.</em> 2002</a>; <a href="#CIT0064" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0064">Paul <em>et al.</em> 2013a</a>; <a href="#CIT0013" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0013">Ferl <em>et al.</em> 2014</a>). Indeed, even a single gene difference can influence the spaceflight response (<a href="#CIT0063" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0063">Paul <em>et al.</em> 2017</a>), thus investigating ecotype-dependent spaceflight transcriptomes can reveal strategies for physiological adaptation to spaceflight while expanding understanding of gene function in terrestrial environments.</p>
<p>The physiological impact of spaceflight is reflected in the patterns of gene expression. Irrespective of plant growth hardware, duration in space or age of plants, one consensus is that spaceflight elicits responses from pathways associated with cell wall remodelling, cell polarity, phytohormone-mediated processes, as well as general stress responses, such as wounding, pathogen defence, heat shock and reactive oxygen species (ROS) (<a href="#CIT0031" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0031">Joo <em>et al.</em> 2001</a>; <a href="#CIT0062" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0062">Paul <em>et al.</em> 2005</a>, <a href="#CIT0065" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0065">2012b</a>, <a href="#CIT0064" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0064">2013a</a>; <a href="#CIT0008" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0008">Correll <em>et al.</em> 2013</a>; <a href="#CIT0092" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0092">Zupanska <em>et al.</em> 2013</a>; <a href="#CIT0013" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0013">Ferl <em>et al.</em> 2014</a>; <a href="#CIT0052" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0052">Mazars <em>et al.</em> 2014</a>; <a href="#CIT0080" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0080">Sugimoto <em>et al.</em> 2014</a>; <a href="#CIT0091" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0091">Zhang <em>et al.</em> 2014</a>; <a href="#CIT0038" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0038">Kwon <em>et al.</em> 2015</a>; <a href="#CIT0071" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0071">Schüler <em>et al.</em> 2015</a>). In addition, a large proportion of genes differentially expressed in spaceflight encode unknown proteins.</p>
<p>Spaceflight-grown <em>Arabidopsis</em> roots tend to be smaller, have fewer lateral roots and shorter root hair development than their terrestrial controls (<a href="#CIT0060" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0060">Paul <em>et al.</em> 2012a</a>; <a href="#CIT0038" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0038">Kwon <em>et al.</em> 2015</a>). Aspects of these morphologies in ecotype Columbia (Col-0) suggest a relationship between the down-regulation of several peroxidase genes in spaceflight (<a href="#CIT0038" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0038">Kwon <em>et al.</em> 2015</a>).</p>
<p>In this study, a spaceflight-induced gene of unknown function identified from roots was investigated for its potential involvement in ROS-related functions. AT1G05290, which we have dubbed <em>Orbitally Manifested Gene 1</em> (<em>OMG1</em>), was one of the highest significantly (<em>P</em> = 0.0005) induced genes in the Advanced Plant Experiment 01 (APEX01) spaceflight data set (fold change = 12) (<a href="#CIT0064" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0064">Paul <em>et al.</em> 2013a</a>). Two distinct biochemical assays typically used to measure ROS activity indicated that knocking out OMG1 alters the production of ROS. Co-expression networks around <em>OMG1</em> show correlations to genes involved in ROS-associated oxidative phosphorylation pathways <a href="#CIT0057" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0057">Obayashi <em>et al</em>. 2014</a>. <em>OMG1</em> was also expressed in organs known to highly utilize ROS during development, such as root hairs and pollen tubes (<a href="#CIT0077" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0077">Shin and Schachtman 2004</a>; <a href="#CIT0068" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0068">Potocký <em>et al.</em> 2007</a>; <a href="#CIT0086" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0086">Winter <em>et al.</em> 2007</a>; <a href="#CIT0041" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0041">Lassig <em>et al.</em> 2014</a>). In addition, <em>OMG1</em> is an unrealized member of the CONSTANS-Like family of transcription factors. These findings, originating with the novel environment of spaceflight, further the understanding of plant growth and development and provide insight into the role of a heretofore unknown gene.</p></section><section id="s3"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Materials and Methods</h2>
<section id="s4"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">ROSMETER analysis</h3>
<p>The ROSMETER tool assesses the ROS-related transcriptomic signature in a large-scale <em>Arabidopsis</em> gene expression data set (<a href="#CIT0070" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0070">Rosenwasser <em>et al.</em> 2013</a>). <em>Arabidopsis</em> gene expression profiles from two independent spaceflight experiments, APEX01 and Advanced Plant Experiment 03-2 (APEX03-2), along with their corresponding ground controls, were used to correlate differential gene expression in spaceflight with specific ROS species and origins. The APEX01 experiment utilized two ecotypes and both were planted on each plate of the experiment. Since the entire plate was harvested on orbit to a single tube, the subsequent transcriptomic analyses comprised 60 % Wassilewskija (WS) and 40 % Col-0 (<a href="#CIT0066" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0066">Paul <em>et al.</em> 2013b</a>). In the APEX03-2 data set, WS and Col-0 were kept on separate plates, harvested into separate tubes and analysed individually. A data set from Col-0 compared to WS vertically grown in standard laboratory conditions was included as an additional control (<a href="#CIT0072" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0072">Schultz <em>et al.</em> 2017</a>). From each data set, Affymetrix Probeset-ID, Gene Locus ID, <em>P</em>-value and fold change were listed and submitted to ROSMETER. The heatmap was constructed using the GENE-E software with hierarchical clustering using the Matrix value offered in the software.</p></section><section id="s5"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Plant materials and growth conditions</h3>
<p>Seeds from both Col-0 and WS were prepared for APEX01 and APEX03-2 as previously described (<a href="#CIT0065" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0065">Paul <em>et al.</em> 2012b</a>, <a href="#CIT0066" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0066">2013b</a>; <a href="#CIT0078" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0078">Sng <em>et al.</em> 2014</a>; <a href="#CIT0014" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0014">Ferl and Paul 2016</a>). Briefly, plants were cultured on Phytagel™ 10 cm Petri plates and grown either in the Advanced Biological Research System (ABRS) hardware (APEX01), or in the Veggie hardware (APEX03-2) under continuous lighting of ~80–135 μmol m<sup>−2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> PAR. In this study, the effects of the spaceflight environment were evaluated, which includes ambient radiation, microgravity and attending effects on convection. <p>Protein sequences were identified by rpsblast search of the NR database (<a href="#CIT0047" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0047">Marchler-Bauer and Bryant 2004</a>; <a href="#CIT0048" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0048">Marchler-Bauer <em>et al</em>. 2015</a>; <a href="#CIT0093" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0093">NCBI Resource Coordinators 2016</a>); full-length protein sequences containing a single CCT domain (pfam06203) with <em>e</em>-value &lt; 0.01 were considered potential OMG1 homologs. Protein sequences were aligned using Clustal Omega v1.2.3 (<a href="#CIT0103" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0103">Sievers <em>et al</em>. 2011</a>), PROBALIGN (<a href="#CIT0101" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0101">Roshan and Livesay 2006</a>), MUSCLE v3.8.31 (<a href="#CIT0094" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0094">Edgar 2004</a>), MSAProbs v0.97 (<a href="#CIT0046" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0046">Liu <em>et al.</em> 2010</a>), PROBCONS (<a href="#CIT0012" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0012">Do <em>et al.</em> 2005</a>) and mafft-einsi v7.215 (<a href="#CIT0034" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0034">Katoh and Standley 2013</a>), with default parameters. Alignments were left unprocessed or processed by Gblocks v0.91 to remove potentially ambiguous regions (<a href="#CIT0105" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0105">Talavera and Castresana 2007</a>). The minimum number of sequences for a flank position (-b2) was 3/5 the total number of sequences. The maximum number of contiguous non-conserved positions (-b3) was 10. The minimum block length (-b4) was 5, and gap positions were allowed (-b5 = a). Other Gblocks parameters were left at default values. Initial maximum likelihood phylogenies were constructed from each alignment using FastTree v2.1.7 with default parameters (<a href="#CIT0106" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0106">Price <em>et al</em>. 2010</a>). Initial trees were used as starting trees for full maximum likelihood reconstruction using RAxML v8.2.8 (<a href="#CIT0104" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0104">Stamatakis 2014</a>). Maximum likelihood phylogenies produced from each alignment were converted to a clade presence-absence matrix using the Super Tree Toolkit v0.1.2 (<a href="#CIT0095" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0095">Hill and Davis 2014</a>), and a supertree was inferred from this matrix using the BINCAT model in RAxML (<a href="#CIT0102" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0102">Nguyen <em>et al</em>. 2012</a>). Additionally, all individual sequence alignments were concatenated into a single supermatrix, which was used to infer the maximum likelihood protein family phylogeny using RAxML, with the best-fit evolutionary model selected by AIC (<a href="#CIT0107" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0107">Wheeler <em>et al</em>. 1995</a>). A consensus of ‘supertree’ and ‘supermatrix’ results was plotted using FigTree v1.4.3.</p>
<p>Protein structural modelling of the B-box2 domain was done using both the Phyre2 and Chimera (<a href="#CIT0067" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0067">Pettersen <em>et al.</em> 2004</a>). The closest OMG1 homolog, c2junA of the human Midline-1 (MID1) with a 92 % confidence level was used to predict the overall folding of the B-box domains in OMG1. The predicted structural model of OMG1 was compared to the x-ray crystal structure of c2junA using Chimera and MatchMaker. Side chains positioned near the zinc ions were displayed to predict the potential of OMG1 binding to zinc ions.</p></section><section id="s10"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">35S promoter:OMG1cds-sGFPS65T fusion construct for subcellular localization</h3>

<section id="s12"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">ROS-producing genes are differentially expressed in response to spaceflight in an ecotype-specific manner</h3>
<p>The differential gene expression data sets of the three independent spaceflight experiments and their comparable ground controls were used in the ROSMETER analyses. The heatmap shows the positive-(red), negative-(blue) and no correlation-(black) between the ROS indices and the queried spaceflight transcriptomes (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1A</a>). A general positive correlation of ROS-producing genes was observed in all the spaceflight transcriptomes and hierarchical clustering displayed a higher similarity with the APEX03-2_WS data set compared to the APEX03-2_Col-0 data set. Two interesting ROS correlation groups were observed in the heatmap. Group A showed that only the spaceflight transcriptome of WS positively correlated with the cytoplasmic ascorbate peroxidase knockout (KO-<em>APX1</em>) experiments, which represent cytoplasmic H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> ROS indices, whereas in the Col-0 background cytosolic ROS shows no correlation. Group B showed ecotype-related differences in the correlations associated with mitochondrial stress. Specifically, TDNA-AOX-MLD and Retenone-3h indices showed that if one ecotype had a positive correlation the other had no correlation. In addition, in the Retenone-12h and TDNA-AOX indices, the mixed ecotype transcriptome shows a similar trend to one of the two ecotypes. The control data set, which reflects terrestrial ecotype differences between Col-0 and WS, showed that many of the genes differentially expressed between the two ecotypes are associated with the ROS-scavenging mechanism. A representative transcriptome heatmap from the APEX03-2 spaceflight experiment that compared several significantly (<em>P</em> &lt; 0.01) differentially expressed (&gt;1 or −1 &lt; log2 fold change) peroxidase genes between 4-day-old Col-0 and WS ecotypes illustrated that Col-0 alters the expression of many more peroxidase genes in spaceflight than does WS (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1B</a>). Peroxidases, an integral part of ROS signalling in cell wall remodelling, cell elongation, auxin catabolism, and response to abiotic and biotic stress, (<a href="#CIT0074" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0074">Shigeto and Tsutsumi 2016</a>) were found to be differentially expressed in spaceflight.</p>

<p>The predicted <em>OMG1</em> gene encodes a 369 amino acid protein that contains a C-terminal 43 amino acid sequence that is 84 % sequence similarity to the (CONSTANS (<span class="text-underline">C</span>O), CONSTANS-Like (<span class="text-underline">C</span>OL) and TIMING OF CAB EXPRESSION1 (<span class="text-underline">T</span>OC1)) ‘CCT’ domain (<a href="#CIT0003" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0003">Berardini <em>et al.</em> 2015</a>). OMG1 also contains two divergent B-box domains in the N-terminal region <strong>[see</strong><a href="#sup1" class="usa-link"><strong>Supporting Information—Fig. S2</strong></a><strong>]</strong>. The OMG1 B-box1 domain has 51 % similarity to the B-box1 consensus sequence, and the OMG1 B-box2 domain has a 94 % similarity to the B-box2 consensus sequence (<a href="#CIT0020" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0020">Griffiths <em>et al.</em> 2003</a>; <a href="#CIT0050" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0050">Masaki <em>et al.</em> 2005</a>; <a href="#CIT0017" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0017">Gangappa and Botto 2014</a>).</p>
<p>A consensus phylogeny suggests that OMG1 belongs to group II of the CO and COL family of transcriptional regulators (<a href="#F6" class="usa-link">Fig. 6</a>). Maximum likelihood phylogenies placed a clade of OMG1-related sequences from Brassicas and Monocots (SH-like aLRT &gt; 0.989) close to COL11-12 sequences from across flowering plants <strong>[see</strong><a href="#sup1" class="usa-link"><strong>Supporting Information—Table S3</strong></a><strong>]</strong>. OMG1 appears closely related to the COL11-12 and COL9-10 lineages, although the precise placement of the OMG1 group remains unresolved.</p>
<p>Both the OMG1 and the COL11-12 clades consist of sequences representing both monocots and dicots. In the consensus tree, a single COL11-12-like sequence from the basal flowering plant <em>Amborella trichopoda</em> rooted the OMG1 and COL11-12 clades (SH-like aLRT &gt; 0.912), suggesting that the OMG1 lineage arose via a gene-duplication event in flowering plants, prior to the divergence of monocots and dicots (<a href="#F7" class="usa-link">Fig. 7</a>). However, no OMG1-related sequences from Eudicots were observed outside the Brassicaceae family, suggesting that either OMG1 was lost from non-Brassica dicots, or the sequence has diverged so much in these lineages as to be unrecognizable. The different alignment and tree-reconstruction procedures support the early-duplication hypothesis, and make it unlikely that the grouping of monocot and dicot OMG1-related sequences is a phylogenetic artefact.</p>

<p>As sessile organisms, plants have developed sensitive, adaptive strategies to regulate their metabolism in a changing environment (<a href="#CIT0056" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0056">Noctor <em>et al.</em> 2007</a>; <a href="#CIT0073" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0073">Schwarzländer and Finkemeier 2012</a>). A strategy to avoid oxidative damage relies on the ability to deploy different antioxidants and ROS-scavenging/detoxifying enzymes to efficiently adjust their redox states. When subjected to the novel environment of spaceflight, plants respond by overexpressing more ROS- or redox-associated genes than comparable plants on the ground (<a href="#CIT0027" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0027">Im <em>et al.</em> 2011</a>; <a href="#CIT0065" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0065">Paul <em>et al.</em> 2012b</a>, <a href="#CIT0066" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0066">2013b</a>, <a href="#CIT0063" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0063">2017</a>; <a href="#CIT0008" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0008">Correll <em>et al.</em> 2013</a>; <a href="#CIT0080" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0080">Sugimoto <em>et al.</em> 2014</a>). ROS in its various forms and subcellular production sites can elicit distinct and diverse physiological, biochemical and molecular responses (<a href="#CIT0054" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0054">Mittler <em>et al.</em> 2004</a>; <a href="#CIT0015" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0015">Gadjev <em>et al.</em> 2006</a>; <a href="#CIT0082" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0082">Swanson and Gilroy 2010</a>; <a href="#CIT0081" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0081">Suzuki <em>et al.</em> 2012</a>). It is possible that plants drew from diverse ROS response strategies to cope with spaceflight, an environment that is outside their evolutionary experience and therefore potentially stressful. The data presented here suggest that <em>OMG1</em> is a putative regulator of the ROS signalling pathway associated with wounding and may play a prominent role in regulating the spaceflight ROS response, especially in the WS ecotype where it is strongly induced by spaceflight.</p>
<section id="s20"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Spaceflight induces the differential expression of ROS-producing genes in an ecotype-dependent manner</h3>
<p>In general, the differentially expressed genes in spaceflight showed a positive correlation to genes producing ROS (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1A</a>). Although it is known that ecotype differences can diversify the ROS signatures associated with stress responses on Earth (<a href="#CIT0033" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0033">Kalbina and Strid 2006</a>; <a href="#CIT0045" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0045">Li <em>et al.</em> 2006</a>, <a href="#CIT0043" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0043">2008</a>), these results are the first to demonstrate that different ecotypes use different specific subcellular ROS species to respond to the spaceflight environment (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1</a>).</p>
<p>Previous morphological differences between Col-0 and WS ecotypes in the spaceflight environment were primarily observed in the roots. Although the overall root length at 11 days on orbit between the two ecotypes showed no significant difference (<a href="#CIT0063" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0063">Paul <em>et al.</em> 2017</a>), it was noted that the Col-0 ecotype showed a dramatic lag in root development during the first 48-h post-germination in orbit when compared to the WS ecotype, and that there are also ecotype-specific differences in root growth behaviour (<a href="#CIT0060" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0060">Paul <em>et al.</em> 2012a</a>). One difference between the Col-0 and WS ecotypes is the regulation of class III peroxidase genes (<a href="#CIT0059" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0059">Passardi <em>et al.</em> 2007</a>). In fact, two genes <em>PE10</em> (AT1G49570) and <em>PE59</em> (AT5G19890) that were up-regulated in the APEX03-2 Col-0 but remained unchanged in APEX03-2 WS (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1B</a>) are involved in controlling the root cell elongation process (<a href="#CIT0049" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0049">Markakis <em>et al.</em> 2012</a>). The differentially expressed peroxidase genes between the two ecotypes in the APEX03-2 spaceflight transcriptomes (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1B</a>) reinforce the notion that different ecotypes utilize ROS differently to respond to their environment and suggest that in WS, these genes were already at a level appropriate for spaceflight acclimation and were therefore unchanged. In addition, the observed down-regulation of peroxidases in Col-0 was consonant with observations previously published (<a href="#CIT0038" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0038">Kwon <em>et al.</em> 2015</a>). Since peroxidases are necessary for the general oxidoreduction of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> in general, the down-regulation of peroxidases in spaceflight within Col-0 could contribute to Col-0-specific ROS signatures seen in clusters A and B of the ROSMETER analyses (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1A</a>).</p>
<p>The current ROSMETER analysis is not intended to define how specific ecotypes utilize peroxidases or regulate specific ROS indices in response to spaceflight. Rather, it is to illustrate that the genetic background of the spaceflight-grown plant had a substantial impact on the specific subcellular ROS species that were used in the physiological response.</p></section><section id="s21"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">OMG1 transcript is consistently up-regulated in WS ecotype across multiple spaceflight experiments and is induced rapidly upon wounding in both WS and Col-0</h3>
<p>
<em>OMG1</em> is consistently induced in spaceflight in WS but not Col-0. Comparisons of the 5′ upstream region, the coding region and the 3′ UTR of <em>OMG1</em> between the two ecotypes showed no differences in the primary sequence. However, in the intergenic region downstream of the <em>OMG1</em> stop codon, a point mutation is observed at positions 2197, 3327, and a two-nucleotide deletion is observed at position 3488 <strong>[see</strong><a href="#sup1" class="usa-link"><strong>Supporting Information—Fig. S4</strong></a><strong>]</strong>.</p>
<p>Wassilewskija and Col-0 do regulate <em>OMG1</em> differently. <em>OMG1</em> showed slight differences in basal expression between Col-0 and WS, and both ecotypes demonstrated immediate up-regulation of <em>OMG1</em> after wounding, along with the rapid diminishing of the signal afterwards <strong>[see</strong><a href="#sup1" class="usa-link"><strong>Supporting Information—Fig. S5A</strong></a><strong>]</strong>. However, Col-0 and WS responded very differently in a wounding dosage test, where leaves were subjected to various degrees of damage at the onset of wounding (‘immediate’ time point) <strong>[see</strong><a href="#sup1" class="usa-link"><strong>Supporting Information—Fig. S5B</strong></a><strong>]</strong>. Irrespective of the extent of the wounded surface area of the leaf, Col-0 expressed <em>OMG1</em> at a consistent level, whereas WS expressed <em>OMG1</em> in a dose-response manner <strong>[see</strong><a href="#sup1" class="usa-link"><strong>Supporting Information—Fig. S5C</strong></a><strong>]</strong>. These results suggest that OMG1 is involved in a rapid wounding response in both Col-0 and WS ecotypes but with different modes of regulation.</p>
<p>The WS and Col-0 ecotypes are genotypically distinct, and their transcriptomic responses to environmental and developmental cues can be dissimilar (e.g. <a href="#CIT0004" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0004">Chen <em>et al.</em> 2005</a>; <a href="#CIT0037" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0037">Kusunoki <em>et al.</em> 2017</a>). The overall spaceflight transcriptomes of WS and Col-0 are dramatically different (<a href="#CIT0063" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0063">Paul <em>et al.</em> 2017</a>). It is hypothesized that even though OMG1 is functional in both ecotypes upon wounding, other gene products or pathways present in Col-0 but not WS could compensate for the ‘need’ for OMG1 in space, or that these genes were already at a level appropriate for spaceflight acclimation and were therefore unchanged in the spaceflight transcriptome. Although OMG1’s role in the physiological adaptation of WS to spaceflight has yet to be discerned, its strong induction suggests that OMG1 contributes to a successful adjustment to that environment. Irrespective of OMG1’s specific role in spaceflight physiology, it is noteworthy that OMG1’s overall metabolic function, which was previously unknown, was only discovered because of its role in spaceflight physiology.</p></section><section id="s22"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">OMG1 is associated with the regulation of ROS</h3>
<p>
<em>OMG1</em> is associated with ROS regulation. The location of <em>OMG1</em> promoter region activity coincided with regions of dynamic ROS activity. <em>OMG1</em> specifically regulated the expression of two ROS-associated genes (<em>GRX480</em> and <em>MYB77</em>). And, loss of OMG1 protein in <em>omg1</em> resulted in deviations from WT in two different ROS assays.</p>
<p>The rapid induction of <em>OMG1</em> expression immediately after wounding was observed with a promoter-driven GUS reporter qRT-PCR analysis. Rapid induction of transcription (prior to 2-min post-stimulus) has been observed in gravitational and mechanical stimulation of <em>Arabidopsis</em> root apical tissues (<a href="#CIT0035" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0035">Kimbrough <em>et al.</em> 2004</a>). In addition, wounding <em>Arabidopsis</em> leaves produces ROS within minutes at the wound site (<a href="#CIT0053" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0053">Miller <em>et al.</em> 2009</a>; <a href="#CIT0002" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0002">Beneloujaephajri <em>et al.</em> 2013</a>) and this rapid response is necessary for plants to elicit immediate defence/repair mechanisms against herbivore and pathogen attacks. Wounded plants can transmit rapid RBOHD-dependent ROS signals of speeds up to 8.4 cm min<sup>−1</sup> to mediate long-distance cell-to-cell communication (<a href="#CIT0053" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0053">Miller <em>et al.</em> 2009</a>). However, since no statistical differences were seen in the expression level of <em>RbohD</em> in Col-0 WT plants and <em>omg1</em> KO plants (<a href="#F6" class="usa-link">Fig. 6D</a>), this suggests that the changes in extracellular or intracellular ROS are not a result of altered expression of <em>RbohD</em> in the <em>omg1</em> KO line.</p>
<p>Reactive oxygen species molecules are essential signalling molecules in plant growth and development but can also act as toxic stressors. Therefore, there exists an elaborate and highly redundant network of genes that maintain the delicate ROS homeostasis (<a href="#CIT0018" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0018">Gechev <em>et al.</em> 2006</a>). Functional OMG1 is necessary for rapid generation of intracellular ROS at the wound site (<a href="#F6" class="usa-link">Fig. 6A</a> and <a href="#F6" class="usa-link">B</a>). However, over time these ROS return to WT levels, presumably facilitated by other genes of redundant function <strong>[see</strong><a href="#sup1" class="usa-link"><strong>Supporting Information—Fig. S6</strong></a><strong>]</strong>.</p>
<p>In a recent review, it has been suggested that altered ROS signalling in response to the spaceflight environment could affect plant growth and development indirectly via changes in hormonal influx within the plants (<a href="#CIT0071" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0071">Schüler <em>et al.</em> 2015</a>). OMG1 was found to regulate the expression of ROS-associated genes <em>GRX480</em> and <em>MYB77</em>. GRX480/GRXC9 is a member of the glutaredoxin (GRX) family of small redox enzymes. GRX480 is a salicylic acid (SA)-responsive gene induced quickly and transiently by an NPR1-independent pathway, which is associated with the ROS-scavenging/antioxidant network (<a href="#CIT0040" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0040">Laporte <em>et al.</em> 2012</a>; <a href="#CIT0090" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0090">Zander <em>et al.</em> 2012</a>; <a href="#CIT0023" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0023">Herrera-Vásquez <em>et al.</em> 2015a</a>; <a href="#CIT0026" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0026">Huang <em>et al.</em> 2016</a>). <em>MYB77</em> is a member of the R2R3-type transcription factor family that is involved in ROS metabolism through the mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) cascade. The MAPK cascade is also known to play a role in plant development and hormone signalling and includes hormones such as jasmonic acid (JA), SA and ethylene (ET) (<a href="#CIT0077" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0077">Shin and Schachtman 2004</a>; <a href="#CIT0083" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0083">Teige <em>et al.</em> 2004</a>; <a href="#CIT0016" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0016">Galvez-Valdivieso <em>et al.</em> 2009</a>; <a href="#CIT0024" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0024">Herrera-Vásquez <em>et al.</em> 2015b</a>).</p>
<p>OMG1’s role in ROS signalling was established with two frequently used ROS biochemical assays. The DCF-DA assay showed that intracellular ROS production around the wound site was dampened in the <em>omg1</em> mutant, whereas flg22-induced extracellular ROS production was 20 % higher than WT in the <em>omg1</em> mutant. These conflicting results can be addressed by the different signalling pathways elicited by each stimulus. Responses to mechanical damage can be localized, systemic or both. In any case, the response involves the generation, translocation, perception and transduction of wound signals to activate the expression of wound‐inducible genes. In response to mechanical wounding, the central role for JA signalling is well established. However, many other compounds, such as oligopeptide systemin, oligosaccharides, phytohormones, hydraulic pressure or electrical pulses, have also been proposed to play a role in wound signalling (<a href="#CIT0042" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0042">León <em>et al.</em> 2001</a>). Early localized ROS induced by wounding, specifically O<sub>2</sub>•−, H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and singlet oxygen, have been suggested to originate intracellularly from the photosynthetic electron transport chain in chloroplasts (<a href="#CIT0055" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0055">Morker and Roberts 2011</a>).</p>
<p>On the other hand, flg22, a microbe-associated molecular pattern (MAMP), elicits an early response that includes SA-, JA- and ET-defence signalling pathways. Flg22 also elicits a late response that includes activation of defence-related senescence processes, SA-dependent secretory pathway genes and pathogenesis-related protein 1 (<em>PR1</em>) expression (<a href="#CIT0011" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0011">Denoux <em>et al.</em> 2008</a>). MAMP-induced ROS have been shown to originate from the apoplast through activation of NADPH oxidases as well as peroxidases (<a href="#CIT0032" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0032">Jwa and Hwang 2017</a>). Although there are crosstalks between signalling pathways induced by mechanical wounding and flg22, there are also unique pathways induced by each stimulus that could attribute to the different results observed. Details of these mechanisms are still being investigated; however, the results infer that within the Brassicaceae family OMG1 does play a role in the ROS signalling pathway.</p></section><section id="s23"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title">Phylogeny and protein architecture of OMG1</h3>
<p>Prior to this study, members of the <em>Arabidopsis</em> CO and COL family were thought to only function in photoperiod signalling that involves flowering, circadian rhythms, stomata opening and root-shoot branching (<a href="#CIT0017" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0017">Gangappa and Botto 2014</a>). Evidence presented here suggests that members of this family play a role in ROS signalling. This, in turn, could aid in the functional characterization of many of the other uncharacterized members such as COL11 and COL12. We suggest that divergent function of OMG1 can be attributed to the evolved B-box1 and B-box2 domains. This is in line with reports on the rapid evolution of COL genes within the Brassicaceae, in which the increased evolutionary rates were observed not only in the middle region of the protein but also in the conserved zinc-finger and basic CCT regions (<a href="#CIT0039" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="CIT0039">Lagercrantz and Axelsson 2000</a>).</p></section></section><section id="s24"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Conclusion</h2>
<p>OMG1 is a CCT domain transcriptional regulator of ROS homeostasis, one that is important to the physiological adaptation of <em>Arabidopsis</em> ecotype WS to spaceflight. While investigations are underway to determine the detailed mechanisms of how OMG1 regulates the ROS homeostasis in wounding, and its specific role in spaceflight, the results presented here demonstrate several important concepts for general consideration. First, spaceflight experiments are useful platforms to discover novel gene functions and provide fundamental insights into terrestrial plant biology. Second, the response to spaceflight is genotype-specific and this understanding should be taken into consideration when defining the overall plant spaceflight response. WS and Col-0 differ in induction of OMG1 in spaceflight. Third, the maintenance of ROS homeostasis plays an important role as plants physiologically adapt to spaceflight. And, last, the CONSTANS-Like family contains members that function well beyond the clock and timing roles typically associated with CCT domains, including ROS signalling.</p></section><section id="s101"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">Sources of Funding</h2>
