</div></section></section><section aria-label="Article content"><section class="body main-article-body"><section class="abstract" id="abstract1"><h2>Abstract</h2>
<p>Adipose tissue (AT), which typically comprises an increased percentage of body mass with advancing age, receives a large proportion of resting cardiac output. During exercise, an old age-associated inability to increase vascular resistance within the intra-abdominal AT may compromise the ability of the cardiovascular system to redistribute blood flow to the active musculature, contributing to the decline in exercise capacity observed in this population. We tested the hypotheses that <em>1</em>) there would be an elevated perfusion of AT during exercise with old age that was associated with diminished vasoconstrictor responses of adipose-resistance arteries, and <em>2</em>) chronic exercise training would mitigate the age-associated alterations in AT blood flow and vascular function. Young (6 mo; <em>n</em> = 40) and old (24 mo; <em>n</em> = 28) male Fischer 344 rats were divided into young sedentary (YSed), old sedentary (OSed), young exercise trained (YET), or old exercise trained (OET) groups, where training consisted of 10-12 wk of treadmill exercise. In vivo blood flow at rest and during exercise and in vitro α-adrenergic and myogenic vasoconstrictor responses in resistance arteries from AT were measured in all groups. In response to exercise, there was a directionally opposite change in AT blood flow in the OSed group (∼150% increase) and YSed (∼55% decrease) vs. resting values. Both α-adrenergic and myogenic vasoconstriction were diminished in OSed vs. YSed AT-resistance arteries. Exercise training resulted in a similar AT hyperemic response between age groups during exercise (YET, 9.9 ± 0.5 ml·min<sup>−1</sup>·100<sup>−1</sup> g; OET, 8.1 ± 0.9 ml·min<sup>−1</sup>·100<sup>−1</sup> g) and was associated with enhanced myogenic and α-adrenergic vasoconstriction of AT-resistance arteries from the OET group relative to OSed. These results indicate that there is an inability to increase vascular resistance in AT during exercise with old age, due, in part, to a diminished vasoconstriction of AT arteries. Furthermore, the results indicate that exercise training can augment vasoconstriction of AT arteries and mitigate age-related alterations in the regulation of AT blood flow during exercise.</p>
<section id="kwd-group1" class="kwd-group"><p><strong>Keywords:</strong> aging, adipose tissue, blood flow, α-adrenergic vasoconstriction, exercise</p></section></section><hr class="headless">
<p><span class="font-variant-small-caps">by the year 2030, individuals,</span> 85 years and older, will constitute the fastest-growing segment of the population in the United States (<a href="#B1" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B1">1</a>), and aging is associated with a decline in maximal exercise capacity (<a href="#B23" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B23">23</a>, <a href="#B41" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B41">41</a>, <a href="#B55" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B55">55</a>, <a href="#B57" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B57">57</a>, <a href="#B59" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B59">59</a>, <a href="#B72" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B72">72</a>), which may contribute to the adoption of a more sedentary lifestyle. During the transition from rest to exercise, a rapid redistribution of peripheral blood flow is essential to maintain central venous return and the instantaneous elevation of cardiac output (<a href="#B30" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B30">30</a>), as well as augment oxygen (O<sub>2</sub>) delivery to the active musculature to match the immediate increase (≤2 s) in O<sub>2</sub> consumption (<a href="#B7" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B7">7</a>). However, during exercise (<a href="#B52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B52">52</a>), heat stress (<a href="#B38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B38">38</a>), and orthostatic challenges (<a href="#B61" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B61">61</a>), whole-body hemodynamics (i.e., cardiac output distribution and perturbations in arterial pressure) are altered in old vs. young subjects. In particular, during exercise, old age-associated alterations in muscle blood flow (<a href="#B10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B10">10</a>, <a href="#B21" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B21">21</a>, <a href="#B46" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B46">46</a>, <a href="#B77" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B77">77</a>) result in a mismatching of O<sub>2</sub> delivery to O<sub>2</sub> consumption (<a href="#B9" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B9">9</a>, <a href="#B14" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B14">14</a>, <a href="#B22" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B22">22</a>), which would expectedly contribute to an age-related functional decline. Whether aging affects the distribution of blood flow to other tissues, such as adipose tissue (AT), during exercise remains to be determined.</p>
<p>We have demonstrated recently that arterial pressure in old rats decreases during an orthostatic challenge, which was associated with an inability to elevate vascular resistance in white AT (<a href="#B61" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B61">61</a>). Furthermore, there was a severely diminished α-adrenergic vasoconstriction in AT-resistance arteries with old age (<a href="#B61" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B61">61</a>). Given that AT makes up a greater proportion of body composition with aging in rats (<a href="#B18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B18">18</a>) and humans (<a href="#B11" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B11">11</a>), a reduced vasoconstriction of resistance vessels from this tissue could have significant ramifications on the ability to regulate AT blood flow in aged individuals to maintain arterial pressure and muscle blood flow during exercise (<a href="#B19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B19">19</a>).</p>
<p>The primary role of white AT is the storage and mobilization of lipids. However, white AT is also regarded as an endocrine organ (<a href="#B27" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B27">27</a>), as white adipocytes produce mediators that influence metabolism of lipids and glucose and immune responses (<a href="#B73" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B73">73</a>, <a href="#B74" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B74">74</a>). Regulation of AT blood flow is complex and largely influenced by pathologies {e.g., type II diabetes and insulin resistance (<a href="#B28" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B28">28</a>, <a href="#B36" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B36">36</a>), body composition [obesity (<a href="#B71" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B71">71</a>)], nutritional state [fasting vs. postprandial (<a href="#B70" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B70">70</a>)], and the anatomical location of AT [e.g., central vs. peripheral; for review, see Sotornik et al. (<a href="#B64" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B64">64</a>)]}. Although white AT lacks significant parasympathetic innervation (<a href="#B29" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B29">29</a>), there is substantial neuroanatomical and neurochemical evidence of a differential sympathetic nervous system (SNS) innervation depending on anatomical location [for review, see Bartness and Bamshad (<a href="#B5" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B5">5</a>)]. Electrical stimulation of nerves innervating white AT results in a vasoconstriction that is abolished by the α-adrenergic receptor blockade (<a href="#B54" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B54">54</a>), suggesting that the regulation of AT blood flow is influenced largely by the SNS. During short-duration exercise (&lt;30 min) in young, healthy subjects, there is an immediate decrease in blood flow to the viscera (<a href="#B2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B2">2</a>), as well as subcutaneous and epididymal AT (<a href="#B45" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B45">45</a>), likely consequent to activation of sympathetic vasoconstrictor neurons. The reduction of blood flow to these tissues during exercise contributes, in part, to the effective redistribution of cardiac output to maximize perfusion of the active musculature and maintenance of systemic vascular resistance (<a href="#B2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B2">2</a>, <a href="#B19" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B19">19</a>). However, given the old age-associated diminution of α-adrenergic vasoconstriction in resistance arteries from white AT (<a href="#B61" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B61">61</a>), it is likely that AT blood flow will either remain similar to resting values or possibly be elevated during the transition from rest to exercise with old age.</p>
<p>As AT receives a large proportion of cardiac output (∼8–12%) under normal resting conditions (<a href="#B18" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B18">18</a>), old age-associated alterations in AT blood flow could have significant consequences on the ability to redirect blood flow to active tissue during physical activity in the elderly population. However, the effects of aging and exercise on AT blood flow are unknown. Therefore, the purpose of this investigation was to test the hypotheses that with old age, <em>1</em>) there is an inability to modulate AT blood flow during short-duration dynamic exercise, and <em>2</em>) the altered adipose blood flow response with old age will be associated with a diminished α-adrenergic and myogenic vasoconstriction of resistance arteries from AT. Given the ability of exercise training to enhance adrenergic vasoconstriction in muscle that is not actively recruited during exercise (<a href="#B43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B43">43</a>), as well as myogenic vasoconstriction (<a href="#B50" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B50">50</a>), the second part of this study was to determine whether chronic exercise training can <em>1</em>) mitigate putative alterations in AT perfusion during exercise and <em>2</em>) improve α-adrenergic and myogenic vasoconstriction in adipose-resistance arteries.</p>
<section id="sec1"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">METHODS</h2>
<p>All procedures were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee at Texas A&amp;M University and the University of Florida. All methods complied fully with the National Institutes of Health's <em>Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals</em>, revised 1996. Male Fischer 344 rats were obtained at ages 4–5 mo and 22 mo and studied at 6 mo and 24 mo to represent young (<em>n</em> = 40) and old (<em>n</em> = 28) animals, respectively. The rats were housed individually at 23°C, maintained on a 12:12-h light-dark cycle, and provided rat chow and water ad libitum. This strain was chosen because cardiovascular function decreases with age in these rats without the development of hypertension and atherosclerosis (<a href="#B42" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B42">42</a>).</p>
<section id="sec2"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title"></h3>
<section id="sec3"><h4 class="pmc_sec_title">Endurance exercise training.</h4>
<p>Young and old rats were assigned randomly to a sedentary (Sed) control group [young (YSed), <em>n</em> = 21; old (OSed), <em>n</em> = 13] or an exercise-trained (ET) group [young (YET), <em>n</em> = 19; old (OET), <em>n</em> = 15]. ET rats were habituated to treadmill exercise, during which each rat walked on a motor-driven treadmill at 15 m/min (0° incline), 5 min/day for 3 days. After the habituation period, the incline was raised to 15° for the duration of the training period, whereas the 15 m/min speed was maintained. During the first 5 wk of training, the time of exercise was increased by 10 min/wk, until 60-min duration was reached by the 6th wk. The ET rats continued to exercise 5 days/wk for 60 min/day for the remainder of the 10- to 12-wk training period. As discussed by Musch et al. (<a href="#B52" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B52">52</a>), this training intensity [i.e., percent of maximal aerobic capacity (VO<sub>2</sub>max)] would correspond to ∼60–70% in young rats and ∼70–80% in old rats of their respective VO<sub>2</sub>max.</p></section><section id="sec4"><h4 class="pmc_sec_title">Study 1: AT blood flow at rest and during exercise.</h4>
<p>Blood flow at rest and during exercise to the intra-abdominal AT was determined in YSed (<em>n</em> = 13), YET (<em>n</em> = 8), OSed (<em>n</em> = 7), and OET (<em>n</em> = 5) animals using the radionuclide-tagged microsphere technique, as described previously (<a href="#B16" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B16">16</a>, <a href="#B33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B33">33</a>, <a href="#B44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B44">44</a>). Prior to the surgical procedure, Sed animals were familiarized with treadmill running. During the familiarization period (&lt;2 wk), animals exercised for 10 min/day at a speed of 15 m/min at a 15° incline, two to three times/wk. At least 24 h after the last exercise bout, during the familiarization or training period, animals were anesthetized with isoflurane (2–2.5%/O<sub>2</sub> balance), and a catheter [Silastic; Dow Corning, Midland, MI; inside diameter (ID) 0.6 mm; outside diameter (OD) 1.0 mm] filled with heparinized saline solution (Elkins-Sinn, Cherry Hill, NJ; 100 U/mL) was advanced into the ascending aorta via the right carotid artery. This catheter was used for infusion of radiolabeled microspheres for tissue blood flow measurements and for monitoring mean arterial pressure. The carotid catheter was externalized dorsally and secured to the skin. A second polyurethane catheter (Braintree Scientific, Braintree, MA; ID 0.36 mm; OD 0.84 mm) was implanted in the caudal tail artery and externalized at the tail. This catheter was used to obtain a reference blood sample, which serves as an artificial organ for calculating tissue flows. All surgical procedures, beginning with the initiation and ending with the withdrawal of anesthesia, lasted ≤30 min. After the closure of incisions, the animals were given ≥4 h to recover, as previous studies (<a href="#B24" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B24">24</a>) have demonstrated that circulatory dynamics, regional blood flow, arterial blood gases, and acid-base status are stable in the awake rat, 1–6 h after gas anesthesia.</p>
<p>After the recovery period, the rat was placed on the treadmill, and the tail artery catheter was connected to a 1-ml plastic syringe that was connected to a Harvard Apparatus (Holliston, MA) infusion/withdrawal pump (model 907), and the carotid artery catheter was connected to a blood-pressure transducer (MLT844; ADInstruments, Colorado Springs, CO). Exercise was initiated (15 m/min) at a 0° incline to assure that all animals ran continuously during the testing period without changing gait patterns (e.g., stop and start running on the treadmill). After 4 min of total exercise time, blood withdrawal from the caudal artery (0.25 ml/min) began. The right carotid artery catheter was disconnected from the pressure transducer, and a distinct radiolabeled (<sup>46</sup>Sc, <sup>113</sup>Sn, <sup>57</sup>Co, or <sup>85</sup>Sr) microsphere (15-μm diameter; DuPont/NEN, Boston, MA) was infused (∼2.5 × 10<sup>5</sup> in number) in random order into the ascending aorta and flushed with warmed saline to assure clearance of the beads. Blood withdrawal from the caudal artery continued for 45 s after microsphere infusion. After a 30-min recovery period, a second microsphere infusion was performed in conscious, standing animals following the same procedures as described above. This infusion strategy was used to minimize the pre-exercise, anticipatory response (<a href="#B3" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B3">3</a>) and facilitates an accurate “resting” measurement. Following the microsphere infusion, animals were euthanized with sodium pentobarbital (&gt;100 mg/kg ip). The intra-abdominal adipose pad—defined as the fat that lines the dorsal wall of the abdominal cavity, separate from the visceral organs, and lies bilaterally along the vertebral column, extending caudally from the diaphragm to the pelvis—and kidneys were removed. The radioactivity level of the tissues was then determined by a gamma-scintillation counter (Cobra II auto-gamma counter; Packard, Downers Grove, IL) set to record the peak energy activity of each isotope for 5 min. AT blood flow was calculated by the reference sample method (<a href="#B33" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B33">33</a>, <a href="#B44" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B44">44</a>) and expressed in ml·min<sup>−1</sup>·100<sup>−1</sup> g of tissue. To account for possible changes in perfusion, due to alterations in arterial pressure, vascular conductance was calculated (i.e., blood flow/mean arterial pressure) and expressed in ml·min<sup>−1</sup>·100<sup>−1</sup> g of tissue/mmHg. Data were only analyzed and reported for animals that displayed adequate mixing of the microspheres, as verified by a &lt;20% difference in blood flow between the right and left kidney.</p></section><section id="sec5"><h4 class="pmc_sec_title">Study 2: adipose microvessel vasoconstrictor responsiveness.</h4>
<p>Microvessel studies from the intra-abdominal white AT were performed in a separate group of YSed (<em>n</em> = 8), YET (<em>n</em> = 11), OSed (<em>n</em> = 6), and OET (<em>n</em> = 10) animals, according to the methods of Ramsey et al. (<a href="#B61" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B61">61</a>). After euthanasia (sodium pentobarbital &gt;100 mg/kg ip), the intra-abdominal adipose pad was carefully dissected free and placed in a 4°C filtered physiological saline solution (PSS) buffer (in mM: 147 NaCl, 4.7 KCl, 1.2 NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>2</sub>, 1.17 MgSO<sub>4</sub>, 5.0 glucose, 2.0 pyruvate, and 3.0 MOPS; pH 7.4). Resistance arteries (&lt;300 μm maximal luminal diameter) were isolated with the aid of a stereomicroscope (Olympus SVH10). Subsequently, both ends of the artery were cannulated with glass micropipettes filled with the filtered PSS and secured to the pipettes with 11-0 ophthalmic suture (Alcon Laboratories, Fort Worth, TX). After cannulation, each vessel in the tissue chamber was transferred to the stage of an inverted microscope (Olympus IX70), coupled to a video camera (Panasonic WV-BP310) and video caliper (307A; Colorado Video, Boulder, CO). Intraluminal pressure in the isolated artery was set at 75 cmH<sub>2</sub>O (<a href="#B61" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B61">61</a>). Vessels that exhibited leaks were discarded. Vessels free of leaks equilibrated for at least 1 h at 37°C, with the bathing solution replaced every 20 min during this period, and were allowed to develop spontaneous tone. To determine vasoconstrictor responsiveness to norepinephrine (NE) and phenylephrine (PE), cumulative additions of each drug (10<sup>−9</sup> to 10<sup>−4</sup> M) were performed (<a href="#B15" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B15">15</a>). Following assessment of vasoconstrictor responses, the vessels were incubated in Ca<sup>2+</sup>-free PSS containing 100 μM sodium nitroprusside (SNP) to determine maximal passive lumen diameter. In a separate group of abdominal AT arteries, myogenic vasoconstrictor responses were determined by increasing the intraluminal pressure from 0 to 135 cmH<sub>2</sub>O in 15-cmH<sub>2</sub>O increments. The diameter was recorded continuously for 5 min with each pressure change (<a href="#B51" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B51">51</a>). The passive pressure–diameter relation was then determined after the vessels were incubated at 37°C for 60 min in Ca<sup>2+</sup>-free PSS containing 100 μM SNP, with the bathing solution changed every 20 min. The same protocol, as described for the active myogenic response, was used to determine passive artery characteristics.</p></section><section id="sec6"><h4 class="pmc_sec_title">Muscle oxidative enzyme activity.</h4>
<p>Citrate synthase, a mitochondrial enzyme and marker of muscle oxidative potential, was measured in duplicate from soleus muscle homogenates, according to the method of Srere (<a href="#B65" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B65">65</a>). Citrate synthase activity, expressed as μmol·min<sup>−1</sup>·g<sup>−1</sup> wet weight, was measured spectrophotometrically using a SpectraMax M5 microplate (Molecular Devices, Sunnyvale, CA) in 300-μl aliquots at 30°C to determine the efficacy of the training protocol.</p></section><section id="sec7"><h4 class="pmc_sec_title">Data presentation.</h4>

<p>Dose-response curves were analyzed by two-way ANOVA with repeated measures to detect differences between (experimental groups) and within (concentration or pressure) factors. Post hoc analyses were performed using Duncan's multiple range test. Vascular sensitivity, the concentration of NE or PE exhibiting EC<sub>50</sub>, was determined by logarithmic curve-fitting equations. A one-way ANOVA was performed to determine the significance of differences among groups in vessel characteristics, body masses, and citrate synthase activity. All values are presented as means ± SE. <em>P</em> ≤ 0.05 was required for significance.</p></section></section></section><section id="sec9"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">RESULTS</h2>

<p>In the sedentary animals at rest, there was lower AT blood flow in the old group vs. younger counterparts (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1<em>A</em></a>). Exercise training significantly elevated adipose perfusion at rest in both age groups and abolished age-related differences in resting flow (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1<em>A</em></a>). Vascular conductance measured at rest paralleled blood flow measures in all groups (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1<em>B</em></a>). During the steady state of exercise, there was a significantly higher AT blood flow in OSed vs. YSed (<a href="#F2" class="usa-link">Fig. 2<em>A</em></a>). Exercise training resulted in an elevated adipose perfusion during exercise to YET and a reduced flow to OET vs. sedentary counterparts (<a href="#F2" class="usa-link">Fig. 2<em>A</em></a>); age-related differences in adipose blood flow were no longer present. Despite small differences in arterial pressure among groups during exercise (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>), vascular conductance measures paralleled those of blood flow among groups (<a href="#F2" class="usa-link">Fig. 2<em>B</em></a>). When comparing vascular conductance responses during the transition from rest to exercise, all groups showed significant differences, albeit there was a directionally opposite response in the OSed vs. YSed groups. Specifically, compared with resting values, there was an ∼55% decrease in vascular conductance in YSed and an ∼110% increase in vascular conductance in OSed during exercise (<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3</a>). After exercise training, both OET and YET demonstrated a similar decrease (∼50%) in vascular conductance from rest to exercise, and age-related differences were abolished (<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3</a>).</p>

Compared with YSed, there was a tendency for a greater maximal diameter and significantly less tone in intra-abdominal AT arteries from the OSed group. <p>NE elicited a concentration-dependent vasoconstriction in AT-resistance arteries from all groups (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4<em>A</em></a>). The constriction evoked by NE was significantly lower in OSed vs. YSed, and exercise training resulted in a significantly greater contractile response to NE in OET vs. OSed (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4<em>A</em></a>). There was a greater vasoconstriction after exercise training, only at the highest concentration (10<sup>−4</sup> M) in YET (59 ± 4%) vs. YSed (45 ± 4%, <em>P</em> &lt; 0.05) and OET (46 ± 6%, <em>P</em> &lt; 0.05). There was a lower sensitivity to NE in OSed vs. YSed (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>). There were no differences in sensitivity to NE between YET and OET, although there was an enhanced sensitivity in OET vs. OSed (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>). PE produced similar concentration-dependent, contractile responses in AT-resistance arteries from all groups (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4<em>B</em></a>). Vasoconstrictor responses to PE were diminished in OSed vs. YSed, and exercise training enhanced contractile responses to PE in the old group but did not significantly affect that of the young group (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4<em>B</em></a>) vs. age-matched sedentary counterparts. Although there were no group response differences to PE between YET and OET, the constriction elicited by 10<sup>−5</sup> and 10<sup>−4</sup> M PE was greater in the YET (55 ± 4 and 64 ± 5%, respectively) vs. OET (44 ± 4 and 49 ± 5%, respectively; <em>P</em> &lt; 0.05). There was a lower sensitivity to PE in OSed vs. YSed (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>). Exercise training did not affect sensitivity to PE in the young group but enhanced sensitivity significantly in OET vs. OSed (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>). There were no differences in sensitivity to PE between the trained groups (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>).</p>

<p>The overall purpose of this study was to test the hypothesis that old age-related vascular dysfunction in AT would adversely affect the ability to redistribute blood flow away from AT during exercise. In support of our hypothesis, there was an increase in AT blood flow and vascular conductance during exercise in old rats, whereas there was a decrease in AT blood flow and vascular conductance in young, healthy controls (<a href="#F2" class="usa-link">Figs. 2</a> and <a href="#F3" class="usa-link">3</a>). To investigate potential mechanisms for this disparate blood flow response to AT during exercise, we measured AT-resistance artery contractile responses to α-adrenergic and myogenic stimuli, both of which were found to be diminished in the OSed vs. YSed group (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Figs. 4</a> and <a href="#F5" class="usa-link">5</a>). After chronic aerobic training, the directional change and magnitude of AT perfusion and vascular conductance from rest to exercise were similar between young and old groups (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Figs. 1</a>–<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">3</a>). Furthermore, there was a significant improvement in both intra-abdominal AT arterial α-adrenergic and myogenic vasoconstriction with old age after exercise training. It is likely that the inability to rapidly divert blood flow away from AT with old age contributes to reductions in arterial pressure during orthostatic challenges (<a href="#B61" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B61">61</a>) and heat stress (<a href="#B38" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B38">38</a>), as well as altered whole-body hemodynamics during physical activity (<a href="#B39" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B39">39</a>). However, after exercise training, many of the old, age-associated alterations in AT blood flow and vasoconstrictor responses in adipose-resistance arteries were abolished. This suggests that an enhanced ability to centrally mobilize blood volume from AT after chronic training may be a contributing mechanism to the enhanced muscle blood flow during exercise (<a href="#B6" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B6">6</a>) and the increased ability to tolerate orthostatic challenges (<a href="#B49" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B49">49</a>) in older individuals.</p>
<section id="sec17"><h3 class="pmc_sec_title"></h3>
<section id="sec18"><h4 class="pmc_sec_title">Regulation of AT blood flow.</h4>
<p>AT blood flow is intricately linked to metabolism for adequate substrate and humoral delivery (<a href="#B64" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B64">64</a>) and is tightly regulated through autonomic and endocrine signaling, as well as intrinsic autoregulatory mechanisms of AT-resistance vessels (<a href="#B75" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B75">75</a>). Electrical stimulation of nerves innervating inguinal white AT elicits a robust vasoconstriction that is abolished by α-adrenergic receptor blockade (<a href="#B54" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B54">54</a>), suggesting an immediate decrease in AT vascular conductance with enhanced SNS activity. However, AT vascular responses to systemic intravenous infusion of NE produce conflicting results that appear to be time dependent. For example, experiments that elevate systemic NE concentrations in humans (all of which result in elevated arterial pressure) demonstrate a decreased (<a href="#B32" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B32">32</a>) or increased (<a href="#B40" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B40">40</a>) vascular conductance and augmented blood flow (<a href="#B60" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B60">60</a>) in subcutaneous AT. In the latter two studies, it should be noted that despite plasma NE concentrations reaching a steady state within 5–10 min, significant changes in AT blood flow did not occur until 15–30 min after NE infusion began, thus suggesting that a prolonged exposure is required to elicit changes in vascular conductance.</p>
<p>In the current study, we observe a robust vasoconstriction in resistance arteries from centrally located intra-abdominal AT in the young group to both NE and PE (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4</a>, <em>A</em> and <em>B</em>, respectively), which is in contrast to some human data showing an increased AT vascular conductance to systemically elevated NE levels (<a href="#B40" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B40">40</a>). The reasons for the disparate findings between the isolated vessel rapid contraction to NE and studies showing an increased adipose perfusion to elevated systemic NE over longer time frames (<a href="#B60" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B60">60</a>) are unknown but may be related to tissue-specific vascular properties (peripheral vs. central) or the large elevation in arterial pressure with systemic NE infusion (<a href="#B40" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B40">40</a>, <a href="#B60" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B60">60</a>) concomitant with the loss of myogenic autoregulation at higher transmural pressures in AT-resistance vessels (<a href="#B75" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B75">75</a>).</p>
<p>The effect of exercise on AT blood flow appears to be dependent largely on the duration of exercise. In the current study, there was a reduction in blood flow (<a href="#F2" class="usa-link">Fig. 2<em>A</em></a>) and vascular conductance (<a href="#F3" class="usa-link">Fig. 3</a>) when transitioning from rest to exercise in young, healthy subjects, due, in part, to an active vasoconstriction in AT. This observation is consistent with other studies that demonstrate an immediate (≤5 min) decrease in perfusion of epididymal (<a href="#B45" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B45">45</a>) adipose depots and subcutaneous fat [(<a href="#B45" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B45">45</a>), and see Armstrong et al. (<a href="#B2" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B2">2</a>) for exception] during dynamic whole-body exercise. However, with longer durations of exercise, blood flow to AT increases, likely consequent to an increased delivery of free fatty acids from AT (<a href="#B25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B25">25</a>), suggesting that any initial vasoconstriction during exercise onset is modified later by circulating humoral or endocrine factors (<a href="#B25" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B25">25</a>) and possibly tachyphylaxia of adrenergic receptors (<a href="#B66" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B66">66</a>). Given that stimulation of nerves innervating white AT simultaneously enhance lipolysis rate and induce a strong vasoconstriction (<a href="#B54" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B54">54</a>), there may be an impaired release of free fatty acids during exercise. Indeed, upon the cessation of exercise, fatty acids are suddenly “washed out” of AT, which may be related to the release of vasoconstrictor tone manifest during exercise [reviewed in Frayn et al. (<a href="#B26" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B26">26</a>)].</p></section><section id="sec19"><h4 class="pmc_sec_title">Aging, exercise training, and AT function.</h4>
<p>In the current study, adipose blood flow was significantly lower at rest in old vs. young subjects. There are several potential mechanisms for the lower flow at rest with old age, including <em>1</em>) diminished vascular endothelial function (<a href="#B8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B8">8</a>, <a href="#B17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B17">17</a>, <a href="#B63" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B63">63</a>); <em>2</em>) elevated oxidative stress from AT (<a href="#B4" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B4">4</a>), which may diminish the bioavailabilty of nitric oxide; and <em>3</em>) significantly elevated basal SNS activity with old age (<a href="#B53" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B53">53</a>), offsetting a diminished α-adrenergic sensitivity (<a href="#T1" class="usa-link">Table 1</a>). During submaximal exercise, the plasma NE responses are either similar (<a href="#B37" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B37">37</a>, <a href="#B39" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B39">39</a>) or smaller (<a href="#B31" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B31">31</a>) in old vs. young subjects, which in combination with a diminished α-adrenergic vasoconstriction (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4</a>), likely contributes to the large increase in blood flow to AT during exercise in the OSed group. After exercise training, the AT blood flow response between age groups was largely normalized (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Figs. 1</a> and <a href="#F2" class="usa-link">2</a>), which is associated with an elevated blood flow to oxidative skeletal muscle during exercise (<a href="#B10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B10">10</a>). Without measurement of cardiac output, the precise relationship between the reduced AT observed herein and the elevated skeletal muscle blood flow with aging after training (<a href="#B10" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B10">10</a>) cannot be determined and warrants future investigations. In healthy, young men, exercise training does not appear to alter adipose perfusion, as there are no differences in AT blood flow between sedentary and endurance-trained subjects at rest (<a href="#B67" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B67">67</a>), despite a greater blood flow response to AT with adrenaline infusion after training (<a href="#B68" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B68">68</a>). In the current study, at rest, there was an enhanced blood flow to AT in both age groups after endurance training vs. age-matched sedentary counterparts (<a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Fig. 1<em>A</em></a>). Although not measured in the current study, there are several potential mechanisms responsible for changes in AT blood flow after training, including <em>1</em>) alterations in plasma NE concentrations, <em>2</em>) changes in the expression or location of adipose vascular adrenergic receptors, and <em>3</em>) enhanced adipose vessel endothelial function. Changes in sympathetic nerve activity or differences in plasma NE concentrations affecting resistance artery function are unlikely, as endurance training has little effect on resting sympathetic nerve activity (<a href="#B62" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B62">62</a>) with lower (<a href="#B34" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B34">34</a>) or unchanged (<a href="#B12" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B12">12</a>, <a href="#B58" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B58">58</a>) plasma NE concentrations after training. Given that AT blood flow is influenced largely by α-adrenergic receptor function (1a), differences in the expression of adrenergic receptors (e.g., diminished α<sub>1</sub>-receptor density with old age) among groups could contribute to differences in adipose blood flow. Given that both aging (<a href="#B8" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B8">8</a>, <a href="#B17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B17">17</a>, <a href="#B63" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B63">63</a>) and obesity (<a href="#B20" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B20">20</a>) diminish resistance artery endothelial function, it is likely that improvements in endothelial function after exercise training (<a href="#B47" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B47">47</a>, <a href="#B56" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B56">56</a>, <a href="#B63" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B63">63</a>) contributed to the greater AT perfusion at rest.</p>
<p>During the transition from rest to exercise with old age, the AT blood flow response was directionally opposite (reduction in flow) in the exercise-trained vs. sedentary group (increased flow vs. rest; <a href="#F1" class="usa-link">Figs. 1<em>A</em></a> and <a href="#F2" class="usa-link">2<em>A</em></a>). The AT blood flow response in old age after training suggests an enhanced vasoconstriction within the AT-resistance vasculature. Indeed, in AT-resistance arteries, there was an enhanced α-adrenergic (<a href="#F4" class="usa-link">Fig. 4</a>, <em>A</em> and <em>B</em>) and myogenic (<a href="#F5" class="usa-link">Fig. 5<em>A</em></a>) vasoconstriction after training with old age. We are unaware of any other studies that have looked specifically at the effects of aging and/or exercise training on adipose-resistance artery function. However, exercise training has been shown to enhance α-adrenergic-mediated vasoconstriction within tissue that is not recruited during exercise (i.e., shows no net hyperemic response) (<a href="#B43" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B43">43</a>), suggesting a possible systemic effect on adrenergic receptor function. There is also evidence that exercise training enhances myogenic vasoconstriction (<a href="#B17" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B17">17</a>), which was demonstrated in the old group after training (<a href="#F5" class="usa-link">Fig. 5<em>A</em></a>). The precise mechanisms for the altered AT-resistance artery vasoconstriction observed herein remain to be determined but likely contribute to an increased ability to mobilize free fatty acids after exercise training (<a href="#B13" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B13">13</a>, <a href="#B69" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B69">69</a>).</p></section><section id="sec20"><h4 class="pmc_sec_title">Conclusions.</h4>
<p>In summary, α-adrenergic receptor-mediated and myogenic vasoconstriction in AT-resistance arteries are diminished with aging, which was associated with an increase in AT blood flow during exercise in the OSed group. Exercise training augmented adrenergic vasoconstriction in adipose arteries with old age, which likely contributed, in part, to the reduction in adipose blood flow during exercise relative to sedentary counterparts. An age-associated deficit in α-adrenergic vasoconstriction in AT may contribute to the impaired baroreflex buffering with old age (<a href="#B35" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B35">35</a>, <a href="#B76" class="usa-link" aria-describedby="B76">76</a>) and result in a spatial overperfusion of AT during exercise. Whereas the possibility that a blunted adipose vasoconstriction with old age diminishes the ability to redistribute blood flow to the active muscles during exercise in humans is likely, elegant studies are required to address this issue mechanistically.</p></section></section></section><section id="sec21"><h2 class="pmc_sec_title">GRANTS</h2>
